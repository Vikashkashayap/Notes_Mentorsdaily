1. Protected Area Categories

Why this topic matters

This is the consolidated prelims fact-bank — the single sheet UPSC mines every year for one-liners: first/largest/floating national park, species–park pairs, Ramsar firsts & Montreux Record, the 4 hotspots, biosphere numbers, and the keystone/flagship/umbrella terminology. High recall load, low conceptual difficulty — the marks are pure memory.

India's Protected Area (PA) network is governed by the Wildlife (Protection) Act 1972 (Topic 29). Four legal categories, plus non-statutory designations:

CategoryLegal basisKey feature
National ParkWPA 1972Highest protection; no human activity/grazing/rights permitted
Wildlife SanctuaryWPA 1972Some regulated human activity/rights may be allowed
Conservation ReserveWPA 2002 amendmentBuffer/corridor areas, govt-owned, with local community involvement
Community ReserveWPA 2002 amendmentPrivate/community land, voluntary conservation
Tiger ReserveWPA 2006 (NTCA)Core (critical tiger habitat) + buffer; notified by NTCA
Biosphere ReserveUNESCO MAB (non-statutory)Core + buffer + transition zones
As of recent counts, India has 100+ national parks, 560+ wildlife sanctuaries, 50+ tiger reserves, and 18 biosphere reserves. Exact numbers change; UPSC tests relative facts and named examples more than exact totals.

2. National Parks — Key Facts

  • First national park: Jim Corbett (Uttarakhand, 1936; originally Hailey National Park) — also the first Tiger Reserve under Project Tiger (1973).
  • Largest national park: Hemis (Ladakh) — snow leopard habitat.
  • State with most national parks: Madhya Pradesh has among the highest counts.

4. Tiger Reserves

  • Project Tiger launched 1973; tiger reserves are notified by the NTCA (Topic 35), each with a core (critical tiger habitat) and buffer zone.
  • First 9 tiger reserves (1973): Corbett, Bandipur, Kanha, Manas, Melghat, Palamau, Ranthambore, Simlipal, Sunderbans.
  • Largest tiger reserve (area): Nagarjunsagar-Srisailam (Andhra Pradesh/Telangana). Smallest: Bor (Maharashtra) / Pench-Orang variants (small ones vary).
  • Newest additions: Guru Ghasidas-Tamor Pingla (Chhattisgarh) and others — India crossed 50+ tiger reserves; the All India Tiger Estimation 2022 put the tiger population at ~3,682.
  • Notable: Sariska & Panna — famous for tiger relocation/reintroduction after local extinction; Kaziranga — highest tiger density.

5. Elephant Reserves & Project Elephant

  • Project Elephant launched 1992 to protect elephants, their habitat and corridors; elephant is India's National Heritage Animal.
  • India has 30+ elephant reserves; Karnataka holds the largest elephant population, followed by Assam and Kerala.
  • MIKE (Monitoring the Illegal Killing of Elephants) is a CITES programme India participates in; Gaj Yatra is an awareness campaign.
  • Project Tiger and Project Elephant were administratively merged into "Project Tiger and Elephant Division" under MoEFCC.

6. Biosphere Reserves

  • Non-statutory; based on the UNESCO Man and the Biosphere (MAB) programme; three zones — core (legally protected), buffer, transition.
  • India has 18 biosphere reserves; 12 are on the UNESCO World Network of Biosphere Reserves.
  • First biosphere reserve: Nilgiri (1986).

7. Ramsar Wetlands (India)

  • India has 85+ Ramsar sites (the highest count in Asia), covering the largest Ramsar wetland network in the region (Topic 31).
  • First Ramsar sites (1981): Chilika (Odisha) and Keoladeo (Rajasthan).
  • Largest Ramsar site: Sundarbans (West Bengal). Smallest: Renuka (Himachal Pradesh).
  • State with most Ramsar sites: Tamil Nadu / Uttar Pradesh (these two lead).
  • On the Montreux Record (wetlands of concern): Keoladeo and Loktak.

8. Conservation & Community Reserves

  • Introduced by the 2002 amendment to the WPA to bring corridors, buffers, and community-owned land under conservation.
  • First Conservation Reserve: Tiruppadaimarudur (Tamil Nadu).
  • First Community Reserve: associated with Keshopur-Miani / Kokkarebellur type community efforts; Kokkarebellur (Karnataka) is a well-known community-protected bird site.

9. Biodiversity Hotspots (India)

  • Four of the world's 36 biodiversity hotspots extend into India: The Himalaya, Indo-Burma, Western Ghats-Sri Lanka, and Sundaland (Nicobar Islands).
  • Criteria (Norman Myers): must have ≥1,500 endemic vascular plant species AND have lost ≥70% of its primary vegetation.
  • The Western Ghats and the Eastern Himalaya are recognised megadiverse zones within India (Topic 06/07 cross-reference).

11. Glossary of Terms

Endemic species

Found naturally in only one geographic region and nowhere else (e.g., lion-tailed macaque in Western Ghats).

Keystone species

A species whose impact on its ecosystem is disproportionately large relative to its abundance (e.g., tiger, fig trees).

Flagship species

A charismatic species used to anchor a broader conservation campaign (e.g., tiger, elephant).

Umbrella species

Protecting it indirectly protects many co-occurring species by conserving large habitats (e.g., tiger, snow leopard).

Indicator species

Its presence/absence signals environmental conditions (e.g., lichens for air quality).

Endemic vs Exotic vs Invasive

Exotic = introduced from elsewhere; Invasive = exotic that spreads harmfully (e.g., Lantana, water hyacinth, Prosopis juliflora).

In-situ vs Ex-situ conservation

In-situ = in natural habitat (parks, reserves); Ex-situ = outside (zoos, gene/seed banks, botanical gardens).

Ecotone / Edge effect

Transition zone between two ecosystems, often with higher species richness (edge effect).

13. Prelims PYQs

UPSC Prelims 2015

Q: Which one of the following is the only ‘floating national park’ in the world?

  • (a) Keibul Lamjao National Park
  • (b) Keoladeo National Park
  • (c) Bhitarkanika National Park
  • (d) Kaziranga National Park

Ans: (a) — Keibul Lamjao (Manipur) floats on the phumdis of Loktak lake; last natural refuge of the sangai deer.

UPSC Prelims 2017

Q: The first two wetlands in India to be designated Ramsar sites (1981) were:

  • (a) Chilika and Keoladeo
  • (b) Wular and Loktak
  • (c) Sambhar and Bhoj
  • (d) Point Calimere and Kolleru

Ans: (a) — Chilika (Odisha) and Keoladeo (Rajasthan), both designated 1981; India now leads Asia with 85+ sites.

UPSC Prelims 2019

Q: Which of the following global biodiversity hotspots is/are located wholly or partly in India?

  • (a) The Himalaya only
  • (b) Western Ghats and Indo-Burma only
  • (c) The Himalaya, Indo-Burma, Western Ghats–Sri Lanka and Sundaland
  • (d) Sundaland only

Ans: (c) — All four extend into India; Sundaland enters only via the Nicobar Islands.

UPSC Prelims 2013

Q: Which one of the following is India’s National Aquatic Animal?

  • (a) Saltwater crocodile
  • (b) Olive ridley turtle
  • (c) Gangetic river dolphin
  • (d) Gharial

Ans: (c) — The Gangetic river dolphin; the focus of Project Dolphin (2020).

UPSC Prelims 2020

Q: Which one of the following pairs of national park and its characteristic species is correctly matched?

  • (a) Gir — One-horned rhinoceros
  • (b) Dachigam — Hangul
  • (c) Kaziranga — Asiatic lion
  • (d) Sundarbans — Sangai deer

Ans: (b) — Dachigam (J&K) protects the hangul (Kashmir stag); Gir=lion, Kaziranga=rhino, Sundarbans=Bengal tiger.

UPSC Prelims 2018

Q: A ‘keystone species’ is best described as one that:

  • (a) Is the most abundant species in an ecosystem
  • (b) Has a disproportionately large effect on its ecosystem relative to its abundance
  • (c) Is always at the top of the food chain
  • (d) Is used only to raise conservation funds

Ans: (b) — Impact out of proportion to numbers (tiger, fig trees); contrast flagship (fund-raising charisma) and umbrella (large-habitat) species.

UPSC Prelims 2014

Q: Consider the following statements about India’s biosphere reserves:  1. They are notified under the Wildlife (Protection) Act, 1972.  2. They have a legally protected core zone surrounded by buffer and transition zones.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

  • (a) 1 only
  • (b) 2 only
  • (c) Both 1 and 2
  • (d) Neither 1 nor 2

Ans: (b) — Biosphere reserves are non-statutory (UNESCO MAB), not WPA notifications; only the core zone is legally protected, so statement 2 alone is correct.

UPSC Prelims 2022

Q: With reference to India’s tiger conservation, which one of the following statements is correct?

  • (a) Wildlife sanctuaries notify tiger reserves
  • (b) Tiger reserves are notified by the NTCA and comprise a core (critical tiger habitat) and a buffer zone
  • (c) Project Tiger was launched in 1992
  • (d) Corbett is India’s largest tiger reserve by area

Ans: (b) — NTCA notifies reserves (core+buffer); Project Tiger began 1973; largest by area is Nagarjunsagar-Srisailam.

14. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

Questions below are original, exam-style formulations built from real UPSC GS-III themes — paraphrased to be copyright-safe. Each framework = intro angle → structured body → way-forward, with examples to quote.

Mains GS-III — style 2018 15 marks · 250 words

Q: How does India’s network of protected areas — national parks, sanctuaries, tiger reserves and biosphere reserves — contribute to in-situ biodiversity conservation? Discuss the gaps.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — define: In-situ = conserving species in their natural habitat; the PA network under WPA 1972 is its backbone, covering ~5% of India’s land area.
  2. Scale & categories: 100+ national parks, 560+ wildlife sanctuaries, 50+ tiger reserves, 18 biosphere reserves, plus conservation/community reserves and 85+ Ramsar wetlands.
  3. Contributions: protect flagship/keystone species (tiger, elephant, rhino, lion); maintain corridors; deliver ecosystem services (water, carbon); community reserves add a participatory model; ecotourism supports livelihoods.
  4. Gaps: habitat fragmentation, human–wildlife conflict, encroachment, invasive species, under-funding/staffing, weak corridors, tourism pressure, linear-infrastructure diversions.
  5. Way forward: landscape-level planning, eco-sensitive-zone & corridor protection, FRA-based community stewardship, technology (M-STrIPES), climate resilience, restoration.
  6. Conclusion: The PA network is central to India’s megadiversity, but effectiveness hinges on connectivity, funding and local participation.
Mains GS-III — style 2020 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Human–wildlife conflict is intensifying around India’s protected areas. Examine the causes and suggest a corridor-based conservation strategy.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — frame: As PA cores fill to capacity, wildlife spills into human landscapes — crop raiding by elephants, cattle-lifting by leopards, tiger dispersal deaths.
  2. Causes: habitat fragmentation & corridor loss, shrinking buffers, linear infrastructure (roads/rail/canals), monoculture plantations, prey depletion, encroachment.
  3. Impacts: loss of human life & crops, retaliatory killings, negative conservation sentiment, animal mortality on highways/rail.
  4. Corridor strategy: notify & legally protect wildlife corridors and eco-sensitive zones; wildlife under/over-passes; secure elephant corridors (Right of Passage); Project Elephant/Tiger landscape planning.
  5. Community & tech measures: quick ex-gratia, insurance, early-warning (SMS/AI cameras), predator-proof enclosures, FRA co-management, eco-development committees.
  6. Conclusion: Connectivity, not just protection, is the key — landscape-level, community-anchored corridors reconcile conservation with livelihoods.
Mains GS-III — style 2016 15 marks · 250 words

Q: India hosts Asia’s largest network of Ramsar wetlands. Discuss their ecological and economic significance and the threats they face.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — scale: 85+ Ramsar sites (Asia’s most); first two Chilika & Keoladeo (1981); largest Sundarbans; Montreux Record = Keoladeo & Loktak.
  2. Ecological value: biodiversity & migratory-bird habitat (Central Asian Flyway), groundwater recharge, flood buffering, carbon & nutrient cycling, water purification.
  3. Economic value: fisheries, agriculture, tourism (Chilika, Bharatpur), livelihoods for wetland-dependent communities.
  4. Threats: encroachment & urbanisation, pollution/eutrophication, siltation, invasive species (water hyacinth), catchment change, climate stress.
  5. Way forward: Wetland (Conservation & Management) Rules 2017, Amrit Dharohar, catchment-scale management, community stewardship, brief-document/integrated management plans.
  6. Conclusion: Wetlands are ‘kidneys of the landscape’ — Ramsar status must translate into on-ground catchment governance, not just listing.
Mains GS-III — style 2022 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Fifty years after Project Tiger, evaluate its achievements and the emerging challenges for big-cat conservation in India.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: Project Tiger (1973) is among the world’s most successful species-recovery programmes; India holds ~75% of wild tigers (~3,682 in 2022).
  2. Achievements: 50+ tiger reserves; NTCA institutional framework; M-STrIPES monitoring; recoveries at Sariska & Panna after local extinction; umbrella-species benefits.
  3. Challenges: saturation of source populations, corridor loss, human–tiger conflict, poaching & illegal trade, genetic isolation, uneven distribution.
  4. Widening the lens: Project Lion (Gir), Project Snow Leopard, Project Dolphin, Project Cheetah (Kuno), International Big Cat Alliance (IBCA).
  5. Way forward: corridor & landscape connectivity, second homes for lions/cheetahs, conflict mitigation, community benefit-sharing, curb wildlife trade.
  6. Conclusion: The next 50 years demand a shift from reserve-centric to landscape-level, coexistence-based big-cat conservation.
Mains GS-III — style 2023 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Critically examine the objectives and controversies of India’s Project Cheetah, the world’s first intercontinental large-carnivore translocation.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: The Asiatic cheetah went extinct in India (1952); Project Cheetah (2022) reintroduced African cheetahs to Kuno NP (MP) — a first-of-its-kind translocation.
  2. Objectives: restore a lost apex predator & grassland/open-forest ecosystem function, revive degraded grasslands, boost eco-tourism & conservation prestige.
  3. Achievements: successful translocation from Namibia (2022) & South Africa (2023); cub births in India; monitoring protocols developed.
  4. Controversies: mortalities (adults & cubs), Kuno’s carrying-capacity & prey-base doubts, sub-species substitution (African vs Asiatic), habitat suitability, second-home (Gandhi Sagar) delay.
  5. Way forward: secure additional sites, strengthen prey base & grasslands, veterinary/monitoring capacity, transparent science-led review.
  6. Conclusion: A bold conservation experiment whose success needs adaptive, evidence-based management rather than symbolic milestones.
