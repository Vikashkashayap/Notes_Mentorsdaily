Conceptual Clarity — Why this Topic Matters

Biodiversity and chemical-waste treaties are a Prelims goldmine because they are so easy to confuse — UPSC exploits exactly that. Three patterns recur:

  • Definitional / static: year of a convention, its subject, its parent body (e.g. Nagoya = ABS under CBD, CITES = wildlife trade, Minamata = mercury). Pure recall — a convention↔year↔subject table settles it.
  • Statement-elimination (2–4 statements): matching convention↔protocol↔mechanism — e.g. Cartagena = biosafety/LMOs, Nagoya = access & benefit-sharing, Basel = hazardous-waste movement. One wrong pairing sinks the option.
  • Applied / current: newest outcomes (Kunming-Montreal GBF “30×30,” High Seas/BBNJ Treaty), India’s ratifications & NBSAP updates. Mains uses these for biodiversity-governance answers.

Highest-frequency themes: CBD↔Cartagena↔Nagoya chain · Aichi → Kunming-Montreal GBF (30×30) · CITES appendices · CMS/Bonn · Basel-Rotterdam-Stockholm chemical trio · Minamata mercury · GEF as funding mechanism.

1. Mapping the Biodiversity & Chemical Treaties

Topic 31 covered the climate–atmosphere–land–water cluster (UNFCCC, Kyoto, Paris, Ozone, UNCCD, Ramsar). Topic 32 completes the map with the biodiversity cluster (CBD family + CITES + CMS) and the chemicals & waste cluster (Basel–Rotterdam–Stockholm + Minamata). Together the two topics cover every Multilateral Environmental Agreement (MEA) UPSC has ever asked.

2. Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD), 1992

The CBD is the umbrella biodiversity treaty — one of the three “Rio Conventions” opened for signature at the 1992 Earth Summit (alongside UNFCCC and UNCCD). It is legally binding but framed as an enabling convention: specifics come through its protocols and COP decisions.

The three objectives (memorise verbatim)

ObjectiveMeaningDelivered through
1. Conservation of biological diversityProtect genes, species and ecosystemsProtected areas, in-situ & ex-situ measures
2. Sustainable use of its componentsUse resources without long-term depletionNational Biodiversity Strategies (NBSAPs)
3. Fair & equitable sharing of benefits from genetic resourcesBenefit-sharing with resource/knowledge holdersNagoya Protocol (ABS)
  • Membership: near-universal — the USA has signed but not ratified (a favourite Prelims fact, mirroring its Kyoto/CBD non-ratification pattern).
  • Secretariat: Montreal, Canada. Supreme body = Conference of the Parties (COP), meeting biennially.
  • India’s enabling law: the Biological Diversity Act, 2002 (NBA–SBB–BMC three-tier structure) domesticates CBD & Nagoya.
check for latest update or data India’s updated NBSAP was released to align national biodiversity targets with the Kunming-Montreal GBF — verify the latest version & target figures before the exam.

3. Cartagena Protocol (2000) & Nagoya Protocol (2010)

The CBD’s two binding protocols are the single most-confused pair in the syllabus. One governs living modified organisms; the other governs who profits from genetic resources.

FeatureCartagena ProtocolNagoya Protocol
Adopted / in force2000 / 20032010 / 2014
SubjectBiosafety — safe transfer, handling & use of Living Modified Organisms (LMOs/GMOs)Access & Benefit-Sharing (ABS) of genetic resources & associated traditional knowledge
Core mechanismAdvance Informed Agreement (AIA) before first trans-boundary LMO movement; precautionary principlePrior Informed Consent (PIC) + Mutually Agreed Terms (MAT); ABS Clearing-House
SupplementNagoya–Kuala Lumpur Supplementary Protocol on Liability & Redress (2010)—
Hook“CartaGena = GMO safety”“NagoYA = sharing the benefits”
  • Both are protocols under the CBD — only CBD parties can join.
  • India ratified both. Nagoya feeds directly into India’s Biological Diversity Act, 2002 (benefit-sharing via NBA).
  • Cartagena’s guiding idea is the precautionary principle — lack of full scientific certainty is no reason to allow a risky LMO.
One-line separator: Cartagena = biosafety (keep dangerous LMOs out); Nagoya = biofairness (share the money from genetic resources).

4. Aichi Targets & the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework (GBF), 2022

The CBD sets time-bound targets under a rolling “Strategic Plan.” The 2010–2020 plan carried the 20 Aichi Biodiversity Targets; its successor is the Kunming-Montreal GBF, adopted at CBD COP15 (2022) — the biodiversity world’s answer to the Paris Agreement.

Aichi Targets (2010–2020) — the predecessor

  • Adopted at CBD COP10 in Nagoya, Japan (2010) — 20 targets under 5 Strategic Goals (A–E).
  • None was fully achieved globally by 2020 (a key evaluative fact) — only partial progress on 6.
  • Aichi Target 11 (17% terrestrial / 10% marine protected) is the direct ancestor of GBF’s “30×30.”

Kunming-Montreal GBF (2022) — the current framework

ElementDetailExam hook
AdoptionCBD COP15, presidency China (Kunming), hosted in Montreal, Dec 2022“Kunming names it, Montreal hosts it”
Headline “30×30”Protect 30% of land, inland water, coastal & marine areas by 2030Target 3 — the flagship
RestorationRestore 30% of degraded ecosystems by 2030Target 2
FinanceMobilise US$200 bn/yr by 2030; US$30 bn/yr international flows to developing nationsTarget 19
Structure4 long-term Goals (2050) + 23 action-oriented Targets (2030)“4 goals, 23 targets”
check for latest update or data A dedicated GBF Fund was established under the GEF to finance implementation. Track India’s revised national targets & latest COP16 (Cali, 2024 / resumed 2025) decisions before the exam.

5. CITES, 1973 — Trade in Endangered Species

CITES (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora), signed in Washington DC in 1973 (a.k.a. the Washington Convention), regulates cross-border trade in wildlife so that trade does not threaten survival. It does not ban domestic use — only international movement.

The three Appendices (the heart of every CITES question)

AppendixLevel of threatTrade ruleExample species
IMost endangered; threatened with extinctionCommercial trade banned (permitted only in exceptional cases with export + import permits)Tiger, Asian elephant, Great Indian Bustard, gharial
IINot now threatened but may become so without controlTrade allowed but regulated (export permit; no import permit needed)Corals, many reptiles, mahogany
IIIProtected in at least one country asking others for helpTrade regulated only in the listing country’s specimensSpecies listed unilaterally by a member
  • Legally binding, but implemented through national legislation — in India via the Wildlife (Protection) Act, 1972 & Foreign Trade rules.
  • Appendix I & II changes need a two-thirds vote at the Conference of the Parties; Appendix III can be amended unilaterally.
  • Secretariat administered by UNEP, based in Geneva.
Fig 32.2 — CITES appendices pyramid: strictness falls from Appendix I (trade banned) down to Appendix III (single-country listing); species count rises going down.

6. CMS / Bonn Convention, 1979 — Migratory Species

The Convention on Migratory Species (CMS), adopted in Bonn, Germany (1979), protects migratory wild animals — species that cross national boundaries — through international cooperation across their entire range.

FeatureDetail
Appendix IMigratory species threatened with extinction — range states must strictly protect them (e.g. Siberian crane, dugong).
Appendix IISpecies with unfavourable status needing international agreements — basis for daughter agreements (e.g. AEWA for waterbirds; MoUs on dugongs, sharks, raptors).
India’s roleIndia is a party; hosted CMS COP13 in Gandhinagar (Feb 2020) — logo the Great Indian Bustard, mascot “Gibi.” Theme: “Migratory species connect the planet and we welcome them home.”
NicknameThe Bonn Convention; often called an “umbrella/framework” convention for its daughter agreements.
CITES vs CMS one-liner: CITES stops harmful trade in species; CMS secures safe migration routes for species that move across borders. Different problems, different treaties.
Fig 32.3 — The chemicals & waste trio (Basel–Rotterdam–Stockholm) share one Geneva secretariat; Minamata (mercury, 2013) is a separate stand-alone convention.

7. The Chemicals & Waste Trio: Basel, Rotterdam, Stockholm

Three sister conventions govern hazardous chemicals and waste across their life-cycle — movement, trade and elimination. They share a joint Geneva secretariat (the “BRS synergies” process), which is why they are examined as a set.

ConventionYearFocusCore mechanism
Basel1989Trans-boundary movement & disposal of hazardous wastesPrior Informed Consent for waste exports; Ban Amendment bars OECD→non-OECD hazardous-waste exports
Rotterdam1998 (in force 2004)Trade in hazardous chemicals & pesticidesPrior Informed Consent (PIC) procedure — importers must consent before shipment
Stockholm2001 (in force 2004)Elimination of Persistent Organic Pollutants (POPs)Ban/restrict the “dirty dozen” POPs (e.g. DDT, PCBs, dioxins); precautionary principle
  • Hook chain: Basel = waste movement · Rotterdam = chemical trade consent · Stockholm = POP elimination. Chronology 1989 → 1998 → 2001.
  • DDT nuance: Stockholm restricts DDT but allows its limited use for disease-vector (malaria) control — a classic statement-based trap.
  • India is a party to all three; POPs are regulated domestically under the Environment (Protection) Act, 1986 framework.
Basel ≠ Bamako: the Bamako Convention (1991) is a stricter African regional treaty banning hazardous-waste imports into Africa — sometimes offered as a distractor. Basel is the global one.

8. Minamata Convention on Mercury, 2013

The Minamata Convention (adopted 2013, in force 2017) is the global treaty to protect human health and the environment from anthropogenic emissions and releases of mercury. It is named after Japan’s Minamata disease — mercury poisoning from industrial effluent in Minamata Bay.

FeatureDetail
CoverageControls mercury across its life-cycle — mining, use in products & processes, emissions, storage & waste.
Bans / phase-outsPhase-out of many mercury-added products (some batteries, thermometers, certain lamps) by target dates; controls on Artisanal & Small-scale Gold Mining (ASGM), a major release source.
IndiaRatified in 2018; secured flexibility on phasing out mercury-based thermal power & products.
SecretariatUNEP, Geneva.
Mercury = Minamata. If a question mentions mercury, the answer is Minamata — never Basel (waste), Stockholm (organic pollutants) or Rotterdam (chemical trade).

9. Funds & Scientific Bodies Behind the Conventions

MEAs need money and science. A cluster of financial mechanisms and expert bodies serve the biodiversity & chemical conventions — UPSC pairs them frequently with their parent treaties.

BodyRoleServes
GEF (Global Environment Facility, 1991)Chief financial mechanism for MEAs; funds projectsCBD, UNFCCC, UNCCD, Stockholm, Minamata + GBF Fund
GCF (Green Climate Fund, 2010)Climate finance for mitigation & adaptationUNFCCC / Paris (climate, not biodiversity)
IUCN (1948)Publishes the Red List of Threatened Species (the global conservation-status standard)Advisory to CITES, CMS, CBD; a hybrid govt–NGO union
IPBES (2012)Intergovernmental science-policy platform — the “IPCC for biodiversity”Assessments for CBD & policymakers
  • GEF vs GCF trap: GEF is the multi-convention financial mechanism (including biodiversity & chemicals); GCF is climate-specific under the UNFCCC.
  • IUCN Red List categories (recall order): Extinct → Extinct in the Wild → Critically Endangered → Endangered → Vulnerable → Near Threatened → Least Concern (plus Data Deficient / Not Evaluated).
  • IPBES is not a UN body per se — it is an independent intergovernmental body, established 2012, secretariat in Bonn.

11. 11. Prelims PYQs

UPSC Prelims 2016

Q: The Cartagena Protocol is associated with which of the following?

  • (a) Access and benefit-sharing of genetic resources
  • (b) Biosafety and trans-boundary movement of living modified organisms
  • (c) Trade in endangered species of wild fauna and flora
  • (d) Elimination of persistent organic pollutants

Ans: (b) — Cartagena (2000), under the CBD, governs biosafety and LMOs. ABS is Nagoya; trade is CITES; POPs is Stockholm.

UPSC Prelims 2019

Q: Consider the following pairs:  1. Nagoya Protocol — Access and Benefit-Sharing  2. Kigali Amendment — Hydrofluorocarbons  3. Minamata Convention — Mercury.  Which of the pairs is/are correctly matched?

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Ans: (d) — All three are correct — Nagoya (ABS under CBD), Kigali (HFC phase-down under the Montreal Protocol), Minamata (mercury).

UPSC Prelims 2015

Q: With reference to CITES, which statement is correct?

  • (a) It bans all forms of domestic use of listed species.
  • (b) Appendix I species face the strictest control, with commercial international trade generally prohibited.
  • (c) It is a non-binding voluntary code.
  • (d) Appendix III listings require a two-thirds vote of the Conference of the Parties.

Ans: (b) — Appendix I = strictest, commercial trade banned. CITES regulates international trade (not domestic use), is legally binding, and Appendix III can be amended unilaterally.

UPSC Prelims 2023

Q: The “30×30” target — protecting 30% of land and sea by 2030 — is the flagship of which framework?

  • (a) The Aichi Biodiversity Targets
  • (b) The Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework
  • (c) The Paris Agreement
  • (d) The Sendai Framework

Ans: (b) — “30×30” (Target 3) is the flagship of the Kunming-Montreal GBF adopted at CBD COP15 (2022). Aichi was its predecessor (2010–2020).

UPSC Prelims 2014

Q: The Convention on Migratory Species (CMS) is also known as the:

  • (a) Washington Convention
  • (b) Bonn Convention
  • (c) Ramsar Convention
  • (d) Rotterdam Convention

Ans: (b) — CMS = Bonn Convention (1979). Washington = CITES; Ramsar = wetlands; Rotterdam = hazardous chemical trade (PIC).

UPSC Prelims 2018

Q: Consider the following statements about the Basel, Rotterdam and Stockholm Conventions:  1. They share a joint secretariat.  2. The Stockholm Convention deals with persistent organic pollutants.  3. The Rotterdam Convention operates a Prior Informed Consent procedure.  Which is/are correct?

  • (a) 2 only
  • (b) 1 and 2 only
  • (c) 2 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Ans: (d) — All three are correct. The BRS conventions share a Geneva secretariat; Stockholm = POPs; Rotterdam = PIC.

UPSC Prelims 2020

Q: The Global Environment Facility (GEF) serves as a financial mechanism for which of the following?

  • (a) Only the UNFCCC
  • (b) Only the CBD
  • (c) Multiple conventions including the CBD, UNCCD, Stockholm and Minamata
  • (d) Only climate-adaptation projects under the Green Climate Fund

Ans: (c) — GEF is the multi-convention financial mechanism. The GCF is the climate-specific fund under the UNFCCC.

UPSC Prelims 2024

Q: The High Seas Treaty (BBNJ), adopted in 2023, primarily aims to:

  • (a) Regulate deep-sea mineral mining royalties
  • (b) Conserve and sustainably use marine biodiversity in areas beyond national jurisdiction
  • (c) Set binding fishing quotas within Exclusive Economic Zones
  • (d) Replace the CBD for coastal waters

Ans: (b) — BBNJ enables marine protected areas and biodiversity conservation on the high seas — beyond EEZs — supporting the 30×30 marine goal.

12. 12. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

Questions below are original, exam-style formulations built from real UPSC GS-III themes — paraphrased to be copyright-safe. Each framework = intro angle → structured body → way-forward, with examples to quote.

Mains GS-III — style 2019 15 marks · 250 words

Q: “The Convention on Biological Diversity and its protocols form an integrated regime for conserving and equitably using the planet’s genetic wealth.” Discuss.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: Define CBD (1992, Rio) and its three objectives — conservation, sustainable use, benefit-sharing.
  2. The integrated regime:
    • CBD as the umbrella framework convention.
    • Cartagena Protocol (biosafety/LMOs) — safeguards the “conserve” goal.
    • Nagoya Protocol (ABS) — operationalises “equitable sharing.”
    • Kunming-Montreal GBF (30×30) — time-bound targets.
  3. India’s domestication: Biological Diversity Act 2002 (NBA–SBB–BMC), 2023 Amendment, updated NBSAP.
  4. Gaps: weak Aichi delivery, finance shortfalls, DSI benefit-sharing unresolved.
  5. Conclusion: An integrated but under-implemented regime; success hinges on finance & national target-setting.
Mains GS-III — style 2020 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Distinguish between CITES and the Convention on Migratory Species. How do they complement India’s domestic wildlife-protection framework?

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: Both are global species-protection treaties addressing different threats — trade vs migration.
  2. CITES (1973): regulates international trade; three appendices; implemented via the Wildlife (Protection) Act 1972.
  3. CMS/Bonn (1979): protects migratory species across range states; Appendices I/II; daughter agreements; India hosted COP13 (Gandhinagar 2020).
  4. Complementarity with India: WPA schedules, protected areas, Great Indian Bustard/Siberian crane conservation, flyway protection.
  5. Conclusion: Trade control + migratory-route security together plug India’s cross-border conservation gaps.
Mains GS-III — style 2018 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Evaluate the effectiveness of the Basel–Rotterdam–Stockholm regime in curbing the global movement of hazardous chemicals and waste.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: Three sister conventions covering waste (Basel), chemical trade (Rotterdam), and POPs (Stockholm), with a joint secretariat.
  2. Strengths: PIC procedures, Basel Ban Amendment, POP phase-outs, synergies process reducing duplication.
  3. Weaknesses: illegal e-waste & plastic-waste dumping, weak enforcement, non-ratification of the Ban Amendment by some states, DDT exemptions.
  4. India context: e-waste & hazardous-waste rules under EPA 1986; import-control challenges.
  5. Conclusion: A robust normative architecture undermined by enforcement gaps; needs stronger monitoring & producer responsibility.
Mains GS-III — style 2023 15 marks · 250 words

Q: “The Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework is to biodiversity what the Paris Agreement is to climate.” Critically examine.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: GBF (2022) as the successor to the failed Aichi Targets; the “Paris moment” for nature.
  2. Parallels: global goals + national targets (like NDCs), finance mobilisation, monitoring framework, 2030/2050 horizons.
  3. Flagship targets: 30×30, restoration of 30% degraded ecosystems, US$200 bn/yr, GBF Fund.
  4. Critique: voluntary national targets, finance shortfalls, DSI unresolved, measurement difficulties, Aichi’s failure as a warning.
  5. Conclusion: Ambitious and Paris-like in design, but delivery depends on national follow-through & predictable finance.
Mains GS-III — style 2016 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Discuss the role of financial mechanisms and scientific bodies (GEF, GCF, IUCN, IPBES) in strengthening global biodiversity governance.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: Effective MEAs need money and science — hence a supporting ecosystem of funds and expert bodies.
  2. Finance: GEF (multi-convention mechanism + GBF Fund); GCF (climate, complementary).
  3. Science: IUCN Red List (conservation-status standard); IPBES (science-policy assessments, the “IPCC for nature”).
  4. Synergy: data → targets → funded projects → monitored outcomes.
  5. Conclusion: Their coordination determines whether GBF targets translate into results on the ground.

Must-Remember Facts — International Treaties & Conventions (II)

CBD family & species treaties
  • CBD 1992 (Rio): 3 objectives — conserve, sustainable use, benefit-sharing. USA signed, not ratified. Secretariat Montreal.
  • Cartagena 2000: biosafety / LMOs (AIA, precaution). Nagoya 2010: Access & Benefit-Sharing (PIC + MAT).
  • Aichi (2010–20): 20 targets, none fully met. Kunming-Montreal GBF 2022: 4 goals + 23 targets; 30×30; GBF Fund under GEF.
  • CITES 1973 (Washington): international trade; Appendix I (banned) > II (regulated) > III (country-specific). Implemented via WPA 1972.
  • CMS/Bonn 1979: migratory species; India hosted COP13 Gandhinagar 2020 (GIB “Gibi”).
Chemicals, funds & current
  • BRS trio: Basel 1989 (waste movement) · Rotterdam 1998 (chemical trade PIC) · Stockholm 2001 (POPs, “dirty dozen,” DDT allowed for malaria). Joint Geneva secretariat.
  • Minamata 2013: mercury (India ratified 2018). Mercury = Minamata, always.
  • Funds/science: GEF = multi-convention finance + GBF Fund; GCF = climate; IUCN = Red List; IPBES = “IPCC for biodiversity.”
  • Current: BBNJ / High Seas Treaty 2023 (marine, needs 60 ratifications); DSI “Cali Fund” at COP16.
