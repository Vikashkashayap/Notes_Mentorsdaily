Topic 36: Environmental Funds & Indian NGOs

The money and the movements that drive environmental action in India — a Prelims matching field and a rich Mains resource on climate finance and civil society. Scoring core: the domestic funds (NCEF, CAMPA, NAFCC, National Ganga Fund); the new fiscal instruments (coal cess, sovereign green bonds, Green Credit Programme, carbon market); the international funds India taps via NABARD & SIDBI (GEF, GCF, Adaptation Fund); and the big NGOs and think tanks (CSE, TERI, CEEW, WWF-India, BNHS, ATREE, WTI) with their signature publications.

Conceptual Clarity — Why this Topic Matters

Funds and NGOs are tested because each has a crisp identity — a source of money or a signature publication. Three patterns recur:

  • Definitional / static: a fund’s source and purpose — e.g. the National Clean Energy Fund is financed by a cess on coal; CAMPA holds forest-diversion levies. Pure recall — a fund↔source↔purpose table settles it.
  • Statement-elimination (2–4 statements): matching an NGO/think tank to what it publishes — “State of India’s Environment by CSE,” “Living Planet Report by WWF,” “BNHS runs bird conservation.” One wrong pairing sinks the option.
  • Applied / current: sovereign green bonds, the Green Credit Programme, the carbon market (CCTS) and India’s access to the GCF via NABARD. Mains uses these for climate-finance and public-private-partnership answers.

Highest-frequency themes: NCEF (coal cess) · CAMPA · NAFCC · sovereign green bonds · Green Credit Programme · carbon market CCTS · GEF/GCF access via NABARD/SIDBI · CSE & Down To Earth · TERI · BNHS (oldest) · WWF-India · ATREE · Wildlife Trust of India.

1. Mapping Green Finance & Civil Society

Environmental action in India is powered by two engines: money (public funds, fiscal instruments, international flows) and civil society (NGOs, think tanks, grassroots movements). Sorting a body into its bucket tells you what it does and where its resources come from.

BucketWhat it isExamples
Domestic public fundsGovernment-held corpora for clean energy, forests, adaptationNCEF, CAMPA, NAFCC, National Ganga Fund
Fiscal & market instrumentsCesses, bonds and markets that raise/steer green moneyCoal cess, sovereign green bonds, Green Credit, carbon market (CCTS)
International fundsGlobal money India accesses through accredited agenciesGEF, GCF, Adaptation Fund (via NABARD/SIDBI)
NGOs & think tanksConservation, research and advocacy bodiesCSE, TERI, CEEW, WWF-India, BNHS, ATREE, WTI
  • Funds differ by source, not just purpose: a cess-financed fund (NCEF) is legally distinct from a budget-financed one (NAFCC) or a market-raised one (green bonds). UPSC probes exactly this source.
  • NGOs are not implementing government bodies — they advise, research, campaign and run field projects, often in partnership with MoEFCC but without statutory power.
  • Access architecture matters: India cannot draw GCF money directly — it flows through accredited National Implementing Entities such as NABARD.
Sorting hook: public corpus → NCEF/CAMPA/NAFCC; instrument that raises money → cess/bond/market; global money → GEF/GCF; body that does the work → NGO/think tank. Fix the bucket, then recall the source or publication.

2. Domestic Environmental Funds

These are the government-held corpora that bankroll clean energy, afforestation, adaptation and river cleaning. Learn each by its source and administering agency.

FundSource & yearPurpose
NCEF / NCEEF — National Clean Energy (& Environment) FundCess on coal (2010); cess later subsumed into GST Compensation Cess (2017)Funds clean-energy & environment research/projects
CAMPA — Compensatory Afforestation FundForest-diversion levy + Net Present Value; CAF Act 2016Afforestation, forest & wildlife management (90% States : 10% Centre)
NAFCC — National Adaptation Fund for Climate ChangeUnion budget, 2015; implemented by NABARDState-level adaptation in vulnerable sectors/regions
National Ganga Fund / Clean Ganga FundVoluntary donations + budget, 2014Supplements Namami Gange under NMCG
  • NCEF and the coal cess: the Clean Energy Cess on coal, lignite and peat funded the NCEF from 2010; after GST rollout in 2017 the coal cess was replaced by the GST Compensation Cess, complicating fresh inflows — a nuanced current-affairs point.
  • NAFCC vs SAPCC: the NAFCC funds projects that operationalise State Action Plans on Climate Change (SAPCC); NABARD is its National Implementing Entity.
  • Clean Ganga Fund accepts donations from residents, NRIs, PIOs and corporates (CSR), with tax benefits — a trust chaired by the Finance Secretary.
Source test: coal cess → NCEF; forest-diversion levy → CAMPA; budget via NABARD → NAFCC; donations → Clean Ganga Fund. The examiner almost always tests the source, not the purpose.

3. Fiscal & Market Instruments

Beyond dedicated funds, India now raises and steers green money through cesses, bonds and emerging markets. These are the freshest, most current-affairs-heavy items in the topic.

InstrumentNatureKey fact
Coal / Clean Energy CessLevy on coal production & importFunded NCEF; subsumed into GST Compensation Cess (2017)
Sovereign Green Bonds (SGrBs)Government market borrowing earmarked for green projectsFirst issued in FY 2022–23 by RBI on behalf of the Centre
Green Credit Programme (GCP)Market-based incentive for voluntary green actionsNotified 2023 under EPA 1986; starts with tree plantation
Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS)India’s compliance carbon marketUnder Energy Conservation (Amendment) Act 2022; BEE + Grid regulator
PAT / ESCerts & RECEnergy-saving & renewable-energy certificatesRun by BEE; market-based efficiency drivers
  • Sovereign green bonds let the government borrow specifically for projects like solar, metro rail and afforestation; proceeds are ring-fenced and reported under a Green Finance Framework — a landmark first for India in 2023.
  • Green Credit Programme (GCP) issues tradable “green credits” for verified activities (initially tree plantation on degraded land) — distinct from carbon credits, which count emissions avoided.
  • CCTS is the national compliance carbon market that will replace the older PAT/ESCerts and REC framework with a unified carbon-credit market, administered with the BEE and the grid regulator.
  • Green Credit vs Carbon Credit: green credit rewards a positive environmental action; a carbon credit represents one tonne of CO₂ avoided/removed. A frequent conceptual trap.
Freshness flag: SGrBs, GCP and CCTS are all post-2022 and high-yield for Prelims. Verify the latest issuance size and the current status of the carbon-market rollout before the exam.

4. International Funds Accessed by India

The global climate funds studied in Topic 34 do not hand money to countries directly — they route it through accredited agencies. For India, the gateway institutions are NABARD and SIDBI, and the focal ministry is MoEFCC.

FundWhat it financesIndia’s access route
GEF — Global Environment FacilityMulti-convention: biodiversity, climate, land, chemicalsMoEFCC focal point; national & multilateral agencies
GCF — Green Climate FundClimate mitigation & adaptation in developing countriesNABARD (accredited National Implementing Entity); SIDBI accredited
Adaptation FundConcrete adaptation projects (Kyoto/Paris)NABARD is the National Implementing Entity
  • National Implementing Entity (NIE): NABARD was India’s first NIE for the Adaptation Fund and is accredited to the GCF — it lets India access money without an international intermediary taking a cut.
  • SIDBI is accredited to the GCF for MSME-focused energy-efficiency and clean-energy projects.
  • Direct access matters: accreditation gives India ownership of project design and faster disbursal — a Mains point on climate-finance sovereignty.

5. Major Indian Conservation NGOs

A cluster of long-standing NGOs does India’s hands-on conservation, research and species protection. Each pairs with a signature focus or founding year that UPSC likes to test.

NGOFounded & baseKnown for
BNHS — Bombay Natural History Society1883, MumbaiIndia’s oldest conservation NGO; birds; IBA programme; Salim Ali legacy
WWF-India1969, New DelhiSpecies & habitat conservation; environmental education
ATREE — Ashoka Trust for Research in Ecology & the Environment1996, BengaluruBiodiversity & ecosystem research; policy engagement
Wildlife Trust of India (WTI)1998, NoidaWildlife rescue, rehabilitation & anti-poaching; elephant corridors
Kalpavriksh1979, PuneEnvironmental action & documentation; community conservation
Nature Conservation Foundation (NCF)1996, MysuruScience-based conservation of wildlife & habitats
  • BNHS is the anchor fact: founded 1883, it is India’s oldest and runs the Important Bird & Biodiversity Areas (IBA) programme; ornithologist Salim Ali is its most famous figure. It partners on vulture conservation.
  • WTI’s field signature is securing elephant corridors and running rapid wildlife-rescue teams — often in partnership with State forest departments.
  • ATREE and NCF are research-led, feeding peer-reviewed ecology into policy; Kalpavriksh is known for community-conserved-area advocacy and documentation.
Pairing hook: oldest & birds → BNHS (1883); species & education → WWF-India; rescue & corridors → WTI; ecology research → ATREE/NCF; community conservation → Kalpavriksh.

Below the national NGOs sit grassroots bodies that mobilise villages, revive commons and pioneer decentralised green livelihoods — the “last-mile” of India’s environmental action.

Pairing hook: seeds → Navdanya; solar women → Barefoot College; Ice Stupa → SECMOL; commons → FES; johad/water → TBS (Rajendra Singh).

10. Current Affairs Link (2024–2026)

The freshest, most exam-relevant developments in green finance and civil society. Confirm the latest figures before the exam.

  • Green Credit Programme (GCP): rules operationalised from 2023–24, starting with tree plantation on degraded/notified land; methodology and credit issuance are evolving. check for latest update or data

11. Prelims PYQs

UPSC Prelims 2015

Q: The National Clean Energy Fund (NCEF) was financed primarily through:

  • (a) A surcharge on income tax
  • (b) A cess on coal, lignite and peat
  • (c) Voluntary corporate donations
  • (d) Market borrowing through green bonds

Ans: (b) — the Clean Energy Cess on coal, lignite and peat (2010) funded the NCEF; the cess was later subsumed into the GST Compensation Cess in 2017.

UPSC Prelims 2014

Q: India’s oldest conservation NGO, associated with ornithologist Salim Ali and the Important Bird & Biodiversity Areas programme, is:

  • (a) WWF-India
  • (b) Bombay Natural History Society (BNHS)
  • (c) Wildlife Trust of India
  • (d) ATREE

Ans: (b) — BNHS, founded in 1883, is India’s oldest conservation NGO and runs the IBA programme; Salim Ali is its most famous figure.

UPSC Prelims 2019

Q: Consider the following pairs:  1. Living Planet Report — WWF  2. Red List of Threatened Species — IUCN  3. State of India’s Environment — CSE.  Which of the pairs is/are correctly matched?

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Ans: (d) — all three are correctly matched: the Living Planet Report (WWF), the Red List (IUCN) and the State of India’s Environment (CSE).

UPSC Prelims 2021

Q: Which of the following is India’s National Implementing Entity accredited to access the Green Climate Fund (GCF)?

  • (a) NABARD
  • (b) RBI
  • (c) NITI Aayog
  • (d) MoEFCC

Ans: (a) — NABARD is India’s accredited National Implementing Entity for the GCF and Adaptation Fund; MoEFCC is the national focal point, not an implementing entity.

UPSC Prelims 2017

Q: Funds under the Compensatory Afforestation Fund (CAMPA) are raised chiefly from:

  • (a) A cess on petroleum products
  • (b) Levies and Net Present Value paid for diversion of forest land
  • (c) International climate grants
  • (d) Corporate Social Responsibility contributions

Ans: (b) — CAMPA holds compensatory-afforestation levies and Net Present Value collected when forest land is diverted; 90% goes to the States, 10% to the Centre.

UPSC Prelims 2023

Q: With reference to India’s Green Credit Programme (GCP), consider the following statements:  1. It was notified under the Environment (Protection) Act, 1986.  2. A green credit is identical to a carbon credit.  3. Its first activity is tree plantation on degraded land.  Which of the statements is/are correct?

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 1 and 3 only
  • (c) 2 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Ans: (b) — the GCP was notified under EPA 1986 (2023) and begins with tree plantation; a green credit rewards a positive environmental action, unlike a carbon credit which represents a tonne of CO₂ avoided.

UPSC Prelims 2016

Q: The green-building rating system GRIHA (Green Rating for Integrated Habitat Assessment) is associated with which organisation?

  • (a) CSE
  • (b) TERI
  • (c) CEEW
  • (d) WRI India

Ans: (b) — GRIHA was developed by TERI (with MNRE) as India’s national green-building rating; CSE is known for Down To Earth and the State of India’s Environment report.

UPSC Prelims 2024

Q: Sovereign Green Bonds (SGrBs) in India are:

  • (a) Issued by private banks for CSR projects
  • (b) Government borrowings, issued by the RBI, earmarked for green projects
  • (c) Grants from the Green Climate Fund
  • (d) Tradable carbon credits under the CCTS

Ans: (b) — SGrBs are Union-government market borrowings, issued by the RBI on the Centre’s behalf (from FY 2022–23), with proceeds ring-fenced for green projects under a Green Finance Framework.

12. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

Questions below are original, exam-style formulations built from real UPSC GS-III themes — paraphrased to be copyright-safe. Each framework = intro angle → structured body → way-forward, with examples to quote.

Mains GS-III — style 2019 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Examine the role of dedicated environmental funds in financing India’s climate and conservation goals. Are they adequately utilised?

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — money as the enabler: Frame funds as the fiscal backbone of India’s environmental commitments.
  2. The fund architecture: NCEF (coal cess), CAMPA (forest levy, 90:10), NAFCC (budget via NABARD), Clean Ganga Fund (donations).
  3. Utilisation gaps: under-spending and diversion of NCEF, slow CAMPA disbursal, thin State capacity, monitoring weaknesses.
  4. New instruments: sovereign green bonds, Green Credit Programme and CCTS widen the resource base.
  5. Way forward: ring-fence corpora, transparent MRV, State capacity-building, blended finance.
  6. Conclusion: Funds exist in plenty; the deficit is in timely, accountable utilisation.
Mains GS-III — style 2021 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Discuss the contribution of non-governmental organisations and civil society to environmental governance in India.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — the third sector: Position NGOs as a bridge between the state, science and communities.
  2. Roles: field conservation (BNHS, WTI, WWF-India), research (ATREE, NCF), advocacy & data (CSE, TERI), grassroots mobilisation (Navdanya, TBS).
  3. Impact: PILs and public-interest litigation, policy inputs, species recovery, watershed revival, awareness.
  4. Challenges: FCRA and funding constraints, capacity, occasional state-NGO friction.
  5. Way forward: enabling regulation, partnership models, data-sharing with government.
  6. Conclusion: Civil society supplies reach, expertise and accountability that the state alone cannot.
Mains GS-III — style 2023 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Evaluate India’s emerging market-based instruments — sovereign green bonds, the Green Credit Programme and the carbon market — for mobilising green finance.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — beyond budgets: Note the shift from pure public funds to market-based green finance.
  2. The instruments: SGrBs (ring-fenced borrowing), GCP (rewards green actions), CCTS (compliance carbon market under EC Act 2022).
  3. Strengths: crowd in private capital, price externalities, signal policy intent.
  4. Risks: greenwashing, thin market depth, MRV credibility, green-credit vs carbon-credit confusion.
  5. Way forward: robust standards, third-party verification, integration with global markets (Article 6).
  6. Conclusion: Promising tools, but their credibility hinges on rigorous measurement and governance.
Mains GS-III — style 2018 10 marks · 150 words

Q: How does India access international climate finance, and why does “direct access” matter?

Model Answer Framework
  1. Access routes: GEF (MoEFCC focal point), GCF and Adaptation Fund via accredited entities NABARD and SIDBI.
  2. Direct access: accreditation as a National Implementing Entity lets India design and disburse projects without an international intermediary.
  3. Why it matters: ownership, faster disbursal, lower overhead, alignment with national priorities.
  4. Conclusion: Strengthening domestic accredited entities is central to climate-finance sovereignty.
Mains GS-III — style 2016 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Distinguish between a green credit and a carbon credit, with reference to India’s recent initiatives.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Green credit: a tradable incentive for a verified positive environmental action (e.g. tree plantation) under the Green Credit Programme (EPA 1986, 2023).
  2. Carbon credit: represents one tonne of CO₂ avoided or removed, traded under the Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (EC Act 2022).
  3. Key difference: action-based reward vs emission-based accounting; a single activity may earn both but they are not interchangeable.
  4. Conclusion: Both are market instruments, but conflating them is a common and costly error.
Mains GS-III — anticipated theme 15 marks · 250 words

Likely: Assess whether India’s carbon market (CCTS) and Green Credit Programme can deliver credible, additional emission reductions, or risk becoming greenwashing instruments. check for latest update or data
