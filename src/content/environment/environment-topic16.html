Conceptual Clarity — Why this Topic Matters

UPSC tests India's biodiversity in three distinct ways:

  • Definitional / static: direct-recall facts — number of biogeographic zones (10), largest zone (Deccan Peninsula), which classification (Rodgers & Panwar 1988), megadiverse-country criteria, number of hotspots in India (4 of 36).
  • Statement-elimination: multi-statement questions mixing one true + one false claim — swapping Gadgil vs Kasturirangan coverage figures, mis-pairing a zone with the wrong region, or the cultural-vs-natural landscape distinction.
  • Applied / current: Western Ghats ESA notification delays, Wayanad landslides, bamboo-sector policy — feeding prelims news-recall + Mains GS-III development-vs-ecology answers (see Current Affairs section).

Focus especially on the 10 zones + their representative regions, Gadgil vs Kasturirangan differences, and the 2017 bamboo "grass" reclassification — the three highest-frequency themes.

1. India's Biodiversity — Key Statistics

  • India occupies just ~2.4% of world's land area, harbours ~7-8% of all recorded global species — one of 17 megadiverse countries (Topic 11).
  • Hosts 4 of the world's 36 biodiversity hotspots (Western Ghats, Eastern Himalaya, Indo-Burma, Sundaland — via Nicobar Islands; Topic 13) and 10 distinct biogeographic zones.
  • Significant endemism across flora and fauna, particularly amphibians and freshwater fish in the Western Ghats.

Prelims hook: "Megadiverse" (17 nations, UNEP-WCMC criteria) ≠ "biodiversity hotspot" (36 regions, Conservation International criteria of ≥1,500 endemic plants + ≥70% habitat loss). UPSC swaps the two counts and criteria — keep them separate.

India's Centres of Endemism & Notable Endemic Species

Endemic = found nowhere else on Earth. India's endemism concentrates in three regions — the two "cradles" (Western Ghats, NE India/Eastern Himalaya) plus the isolated islands (A&N). Know at least one flagship endemic per region:

Centre of endemismNotable endemic species
Western GhatsPurple/pig-nosed frog (Nasikabatrachus sahyadrensis), Nilgiri tahr, lion-tailed macaque, Malabar civet, Nilgiri langur
Eastern Himalaya / North-EastHoolock gibbon (India's only ape), pygmy hog, Namdapha flying squirrel, golden langur, white-winged wood duck
Andaman & Nicobar IslandsNicobar megapode, Andaman wild pig, Nicobar tree shrew, Andaman day gecko
Peninsular / otherAsiatic lion (Gir — only wild population), one-horned rhino (Kaziranga), Kashmir stag/hangul, Sangai deer (Loktak)
Do not confuse endemic vs indigenous vs exotic: Endemic = native AND restricted to one region only (lion-tailed macaque). Indigenous/native = naturally occurring but may also live elsewhere (tiger). Exotic/alien = introduced from outside (water hyacinth, lantana).

2. Conceptual Clarity — Zone, Biome, Hotspot

Don't confuse these three

  • Biogeographic zone: administrative/scientific unit for conservation planning within a country, based on plant/animal communities + physiography (India: 10 zones, Rodgers & Panwar 1988).
  • Biome (Topic 05): global-scale classification by climate + dominant vegetation (e.g., tropical rainforest, desert), transcends national boundaries.
  • Biodiversity hotspot (Topic 13): Conservation-International-defined global priority area meeting strict endemism + habitat-loss criteria.
  • India's biogeographic zones are the finest-grained of the three — basis for protected area network planning.

3. India's 10 Biogeographic Zones

  • Rodgers & Panwar classification (1988), adopted by Wildlife Institute of India, divides country into 10 biogeographic zones for systematic conservation planning.
  • Ensures protected area network represents India's full ecological diversity rather than clustering in a few regions.

4. Gadgil Commission (WGEEP, 2011)

  • Western Ghats Ecology Expert Panel (WGEEP), chaired by Madhav Gadgil, submitted report in 2011.
  • Recommended entire Western Ghats (~1,64,280 sq km) be declared Ecologically Sensitive Area (ESA), divided into 3 zones (ESZ I, II, III) with graded restrictions.
  • ESZ I (highest sensitivity) faced strictest curbs on mining, quarrying, polluting industries.
  • Proposed bottom-up, participatory governance — empowering local Panchayats and Gram Sabhas.
The Gadgil report faced strong political and industry pushback from state governments (Kerala, Karnataka, Goa) over its broad ESA coverage and perceived restrictions on development/mining — leading to the constitution of the Kasturirangan panel to review it.

5. Kasturirangan Report (2013)

  • High Level Working Group, chaired by K. Kasturirangan (former ISRO chief), constituted to review Gadgil report after state objections.
  • Recommended narrower approach — only ~37% of Western Ghats (~60,000 sq km) classified ecologically sensitive.
  • Based on satellite-mapped split: "cultural landscape" (human-dominated, e.g. plantations/settlements) vs "natural landscape" (largely undisturbed forest).
  • Restrictions applied mainly to natural landscape portion.

7. National Bamboo Mission

  • National Bamboo Mission (NBM) promotes holistic bamboo sector development — cultivation, processing, market linkages, value-added products.
  • Recognises bamboo's dual role: rural livelihoods (fast-growing, high-yield cash crop) + ecological restoration (soil binding, degraded land rehab, carbon sequestration).
  • 2017 amendment to Indian Forest Act, 1927 reclassified bamboo on non-forest land as "grass" not "tree" — removed felling/transit permit requirements, eased farmer cultivation.

8. Current Affairs (2024–2026)

The living, fastest-changing edge of this topic — verify each against the latest notification before your attempt.

Western Ghats ESA notification — still pending check for latest update or data

Through 2024–2025 the final Ecologically Sensitive Area notification for the Western Ghats (balancing Gadgil/Kasturirangan) remained pending after repeated draft extensions — the 6th draft was issued by MoEFCC, still awaiting the six states' concurrence (Kerala, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Maharashtra, Goa, Gujarat).

Wayanad landslides — renewed ESA urgency (2024) check for latest update or data

The catastrophic July 2024 Wayanad (Kerala) landslides — hundreds dead — intensified scientific and political debate over delayed ESA implementation and unregulated land-use change (quarrying, plantations, construction) in Western Ghats sensitive zones.

Continued 2024–2025 expansion of bamboo-based industries (handicrafts, construction, engineered bamboo, biofuel/ethanol) as a climate-resilient rural livelihood, building on the 2017 IFA "grass" reclassification.

10. Mains PYQs

GS-III 2023

Q. Compare and contrast the Gadgil Committee and Kasturirangan Committee recommendations on Western Ghats conservation. Which approach do you consider more sustainable, and why?

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: Both panels addressed the same problem — protecting the Western Ghats hotspot — but differed sharply on how much to protect and how, shaping India's still-unresolved ESA debate.
  2. Body — Compare:
    • Coverage: Gadgil — entire Ghats (~100%) as ESA; Kasturirangan — only ~37% (natural landscape).
    • Zoning: Gadgil — 3-tier ESZ I/II/III graded restrictions; Kasturirangan — binary cultural vs natural landscape.
    • Governance: Gadgil — bottom-up, Gram Sabha/Panchayat empowered; Kasturirangan — more top-down, state-implemented.
    • Reception: Gadgil rejected by states as too restrictive; Kasturirangan seen as a "workable" compromise but still contested.
  3. Body — Which is more sustainable:
    • Argue Gadgil's ecological rigour + participatory governance is scientifically sounder for long-term hotspot integrity (Wayanad 2024 vindicates stronger safeguards).
    • But Kasturirangan's realism improves political feasibility — sustainability needs implementability, not just ambition.
  4. Conclusion: A balanced path — Gadgil's graded, participatory framework applied to a scientifically-defined core — best reconciles ecology with livelihoods.

GS-III 2021

Q. Discuss the significance of India's biogeographic zone classification for conservation planning and Protected Area network design.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: The Rodgers & Panwar (1988) classification divides India into 10 biogeographic zones — the scientific backbone of the Wildlife Institute of India's protected-area planning.
  2. Body — Significance:
    • Representativeness: ensures the PA network samples every ecological zone, not just charismatic forests — from Trans-Himalayan cold desert to Islands.
    • Gap analysis: identifies under-protected zones (e.g. grasslands, semi-arid) for new sanctuaries/reserves.
    • Finest-grained unit: more actionable for domestic planning than global biomes or hotspots.
    • Corridor & landscape design: guides connectivity across zones for wide-ranging species.
  3. Body — Limitations: zones are broad; sub-zonal micro-endemism (Western Ghats) needs finer tools; climate change is shifting zone boundaries.
  4. Conclusion: The 10-zone framework remains the organising principle for systematic, representative conservation — but must be updated with climate-shift and connectivity data.

GS-III 2019

Q. The Western Ghats face increasing ecological stress from unregulated development. Critically examine the delay in implementing Ecologically Sensitive Area notifications.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: Despite two expert reports (2011, 2013), the final Western Ghats ESA notification remains unissued after multiple draft extensions — a decade-long governance failure.
  2. Body — Drivers of delay:
    • State resistance (Kerala, Karnataka, Goa, Maharashtra) over restrictions on mining, quarrying, plantations, tourism.
    • Livelihood & land-rights concerns of Ghats communities.
    • Centre-state coordination gaps; repeated re-drafting to accommodate objections.
  3. Body — Consequences:
    • Continued unregulated land-use change → landslides (Wayanad 2024), biodiversity loss, altered hydrology.
    • Erodes ecological resilience of a global hotspot supplying peninsular India's rivers.
  4. Conclusion: A scientifically-defined, participatory ESA with livelihood safeguards must be finalised urgently — delay converts a policy gap into recurring human and ecological disaster.

GS-III 2017

Q. Discuss the role of the National Bamboo Mission in promoting sustainable rural livelihoods and ecological restoration in India.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: The National Bamboo Mission (NBM) promotes holistic bamboo-sector development, leveraging bamboo's dual value — livelihood + ecology — as a climate-resilient crop.
  2. Body — Livelihood role:
    • Fast-growing, high-yield cash crop; supports handicrafts, construction, engineered bamboo, agarbatti, biofuel.
    • 2017 IFA amendment reclassified bamboo on non-forest land as "grass" → no felling/transit permit → eased farmer cultivation and trade.
    • Value-chain, processing and market-linkage support under the Mission.
  3. Body — Ecological role:
    • Soil binding, degraded-land rehabilitation, watershed protection.
    • Rapid carbon sequestration; substitutes wood/plastic (lower footprint).
  4. Body — Challenges: quality planting material, industry-scale processing gaps, market volatility.
  5. Conclusion: NBM links green livelihoods with ecological restoration — a model of the bio-economy if processing and market bottlenecks are resolved.

GS-I 2015

Q. "India's status as a megadiverse nation is closely tied to its diverse biogeographic zones." Elaborate with suitable examples.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: India is 1 of 17 megadiverse nations — holding ~7–8% of global species on ~2.4% of land — a richness rooted in its 10 distinct biogeographic zones spanning cold desert to tropical islands.
  2. Body — Zone-diversity link (examples):
    • Trans-Himalaya (Ladakh) — snow leopard, Tibetan antelope; Himalaya — red panda, musk deer.
    • Western Ghats — amphibian/fish endemism (purple frog); North-East (Indo-Burma) — hoolock gibbon.
    • Desert — GIB, chinkara; Coasts/Islands — mangroves, coral, Nicobar megapode.
  3. Body — Why zones drive diversity: varied climate, altitude, physiography → distinct habitats → high beta-diversity + endemism; 4 of 36 global hotspots lie within these zones.
  4. Conclusion: India's megadiversity is a direct expression of its biogeographic variety — conserving each zone is essential to sustaining that status.
