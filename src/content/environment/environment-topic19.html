Conceptual Clarity — Why this Topic Matters

UPSC tests water & ocean impacts of climate change in three distinct ways:

  • Definitional / static: precise distinctions — water stress vs scarcity vs depletion, the ocean-acidification chemistry chain (CO2 → carbonic acid → lower pH → carbonate depletion), methane-hydrate clathrate structure.
  • Statement-elimination: matching each water scheme to its correct feature — Jal Shakti Abhiyan (campaign), Jal Jeevan Mission (Har Ghar Jal/FHTC), National Water Mission (NAPCC mission), Atal Bhujal Yojana (World Bank, 7 states).
  • Applied / current: marine heatwaves & coral bleaching, JJM coverage, groundwater-depletion geography, arsenic/fluoride contamination belts (see Current Affairs section).

Focus especially on the water-scheme architecture, ocean-acidification chemistry, and the NWM-as-NAPCC-mission link — the three highest-frequency themes.

1. Groundwater — Status & Contamination

  • India: world's largest groundwater extractor, more than USA + China combined, mainly for irrigation.
  • Excessive extraction caused sharp water-table decline in Punjab, Haryana, Rajasthan, parts of Tamil Nadu.
  • Arsenic contamination: Ganga-Brahmaputra floodplains (West Bengal, Bihar, parts of UP/Assam).
  • Fluoride contamination: Rajasthan, Gujarat, parts of south India — causes fluorosis.
  • Climate change alters monsoon recharge patterns, deepening groundwater dependence in erratic rainfall years.

2. Conceptual Clarity — Water Stress vs Scarcity vs Depletion

Don't confuse these three

  • Water stress: demand approaches/exceeds supply (per capita availability <1,700 m³/yr).
  • Water scarcity: acute shortfall — physical shortage + poor governance/access; <1,000 m³/capita/yr = "water scarce."
  • Groundwater depletion: extraction > natural recharge over time — physical, measurable water-table decline, distinct from demand-supply concepts.

3. Jal Shakti Abhiyan

  • Launched 2019 under new Ministry of Jal Shakti (merged Water Resources + Drinking Water & Sanitation).
  • Time-bound, mission-mode campaign — water conservation in 250+ water-stressed districts.
  • 5 interventions: rainwater harvesting, renovating traditional water bodies, borewell reuse/recharge, watershed development, intensive afforestation.

4. Jal Jeevan Mission

  • Launched 2019 — aims to provide Functional Household Tap Connections (FHTC) to every rural household, "Har Ghar Jal."
  • Decentralised, community-participation model: village-level Water and Sanitation Committees (Paani Samitis/VWSCs) oversee local infrastructure.
  • Also focuses on source sustainability + greywater management.

5. National Water Mission & Atal Bhujal Yojana

  • National Water Mission (NWM): 1 of 8 NAPCC missions (Topic 20) — integrated water resource management, minimise wastage, equitable distribution across/within states.
  • Targets 20% improvement in water-use efficiency.
  • Atal Bhujal Yojana (ABY): World Bank-supported Central Sector Scheme, launched 2020.
  • Covers 7 water-stressed states (Gujarat, Haryana, Karnataka, MP, Maharashtra, Rajasthan, UP).
  • Combines community-led demand-side management + supply-side infra, with performance-based state incentive grants.

6. Ocean Acidification

  • Ocean acidification: ongoing ocean pH decrease from absorbing anthropogenic atmospheric CO2 (oceans absorbed ~1/4-1/3 of all human-emitted CO2 since Industrial Revolution).
  • Dissolved CO2 + seawater → carbonic acid → lowers pH, reduces carbonate ion availability.
  • Threatens calcifying organisms (corals, molluscs, pteropods, foraminifera) building CaCO3 shells/skeletons.
  • Directly threatens coral reefs (already bleaching-stressed) and base of marine food webs.

Coral Reefs & Coral Bleaching — the Mechanism

  • Corals live in symbiosis with zooxanthellae — microscopic algae (dinoflagellates) inside coral tissue that photosynthesise, giving corals colour + up to ~90% of their energy.
  • Bleaching = under thermal/acidification/pollution stress, corals expel the zooxanthellae, turn white, and lose their food source. Prolonged bleaching → coral death.
  • Main trigger: sea-surface temperature ~1–2°C above normal summer maximum for sustained weeks (marine heatwaves); worsened by acidification, UV, sedimentation, low salinity.
  • Reefs need warm (23–29°C), shallow, clear, sunlit, saline water — hence mostly tropical.

India's 4 major reef areas: Gulf of Mannar, Gulf of Kutch, Lakshadweep, Andaman & Nicobar. Darwin's subsidence theory explains atoll formation: fringing → barrier → atoll as the volcanic island subsides.

Sea-Level Rise (SLR) — Two Drivers

  • Thermal expansion of warming ocean water (seawater expands as it heats) — historically the largest single contributor.
  • Land-ice melt — melting glaciers + ice sheets (Greenland, Antarctica) adding new water to oceans.
  • Impacts: coastal inundation, saltwater intrusion into aquifers/farmland, loss of mangroves & deltas, displacement (e.g. Sundarbans islands, Lakshadweep).
Prelims trap: Melting sea ice (Arctic floating ice) does NOT raise sea level — it already displaces its volume (Archimedes). Only melting land ice (glaciers, ice sheets) + thermal expansion raise sea level.

7. Methane Hydrates

  • Methane hydrates (clathrates): ice-like crystals — methane trapped in water-molecule lattice under high pressure/low temp.
  • Found in deep ocean sediments + Arctic/sub-Arctic permafrost.
  • Potential future energy resource — India's eastern continental margin surveyed under National Gas Hydrate Program.
  • Climate risk: warming oceans/thawing permafrost could destabilise deposits, releasing methane (potent GHG, Topic 17) — "clathrate gun hypothesis" feedback loop.
Methane hydrate destabilisation is considered a low-probability but high-impact climate tipping point — abrupt large-scale release could trigger rapid additional warming, though current scientific consensus treats near-term catastrophic release as unlikely this century.

8. Current Affairs (2024–2026)

4th Global Coral Bleaching Event confirmed check for latest update or data

In April 2024, NOAA + ICRI declared the 4th global mass coral-bleaching event (the 2nd in a decade); >60% of reef areas affected. India's Gulf of Mannar, Lakshadweep & Andaman reefs recorded bleaching stress amid record 2023–24 ocean surface temperatures — compounding thermal + acidification pressure.

Jal Jeevan Mission coverage & extension check for latest update or data

FHTC coverage crossed ~75%+ of rural households; the mission timeline was extended (2028) in Union Budget 2025 to close remaining gap areas, with focus on source sustainability & quality (arsenic/fluoride-affected habitations).

CGWB groundwater assessment & Atal Bhujal review check for latest update or data

Central Ground Water Board's latest Dynamic Groundwater Resource assessment shows marginal improvement in extraction stage; Atal Bhujal Yojana mid-term review reports water-level gains in participating stressed blocks across the 7 states.

10. Mains PYQs

GS-III, 2023

Q. Describe the various causes and effects of ocean acidification on marine biodiversity and coral reef ecosystems, along with international efforts to address it.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: Define ocean acidification — fall in ocean pH from absorbing ~1/4–1/3 of anthropogenic CO2; often called "the other CO2 problem."
  2. Body — causes:
    • Rising atmospheric CO2 → dissolves in seawater → carbonic acid → lower pH, reduced carbonate ions
    • Local aggravators — coastal pollution, nutrient runoff (eutrophication)
  3. Body — effects:
    • Calcifiers (corals, molluscs, pteropods, foraminifera) can't build CaCO3 shells
    • Coral reef degradation (compounded by warming-driven bleaching), disrupted marine food webs, fisheries decline
    • Threat to livelihoods (Gulf of Mannar, Lakshadweep reefs)
  4. Body — international efforts: UNFCCC/Paris CO2 cuts, SDG-14 (Life Below Water), ICRI, Global Ocean Acidification Observing Network (GOA-ON), MPAs.
  5. Conclusion: Only deep global CO2 mitigation truly addresses acidification — a shared ocean-climate imperative.

GS-III, 2019

Q. Discuss the causes of depletion of groundwater and mention the ways to replenish it through the schemes launched by the Government of India in this regard.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: India is the world's largest groundwater extractor (more than USA + China combined) — over-reliance is now a resource crisis.
  2. Body — causes of depletion:
    • Over-extraction for irrigation (~60% of irrigation), free/subsidised power
    • Green-Revolution water-intensive cropping (paddy in Punjab/Haryana)
    • Urbanisation reducing recharge, erratic monsoon lowering natural recharge
  3. Body — replenishment schemes:
    • Atal Bhujal Yojana (community-led groundwater management, 7 states)
    • Jal Shakti Abhiyan (recharge + water-body renovation)
    • National Water Mission (20% WUE), MGNREGA water structures, Per Drop More Crop (PMKSY)
  4. Conclusion: Demand-side management (crop shift, pricing) + supply-side recharge together break the depletion spiral.

GS-I, 2020

Q. Bring out the causes for urban flooding in major Indian cities. Discuss the mechanisms for preparedness to reduce the risks, with reference to groundwater recharge and water conservation.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: Urban floods (Chennai 2015, Mumbai 2005, Bengaluru 2022) increasingly frequent as climate change intensifies rainfall bursts.
  2. Body — causes:
    • Encroachment on natural drains/wetlands, unplanned concretisation reducing infiltration
    • Inadequate/clogged stormwater drains, loss of urban water bodies
    • Intense short-duration cloudbursts (climate variability)
  3. Body — preparedness & recharge:
    • Sponge-city design, rooftop rainwater harvesting, permeable pavements, recharge pits/wells
    • Restore urban lakes/wetlands, desilt drains, real-time flood early-warning
    • AMRUT/Smart Cities stormwater components
  4. Conclusion: Flood water is a recharge opportunity — integrate drainage with harvesting to convert a hazard into a resource.

GS-III, 2020

Q. What is water-use efficiency? Describe the role of micro-irrigation in increasing water-use efficiency in India.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: Water-use efficiency (WUE) = crop output per unit water; India's flood irrigation wastes vast water.
  2. Body — micro-irrigation's role:
    • Drip/sprinkler deliver water to root zone — cuts evaporation/runoff, raises WUE 40–60%
    • Fertigation, "Per Drop More Crop" (PMKSY), reduced groundwater draw
    • Prevents waterlogging/salinisation, supports water-stressed regions
  3. Body — constraints: high upfront cost, small landholdings, clogging/maintenance, awareness gaps.
  4. Conclusion: Subsidy support + FPO-led adoption can mainstream micro-irrigation as a water-security cornerstone.

GS-III, 2016

Q. "In the villages of India, the growing water crisis is largely a governance crisis." Critically examine in the context of Jal Shakti Abhiyan and related interventions.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: India's water crisis is not merely physical scarcity but poor management — supporting the "governance crisis" view.
  2. Body — governance failures:
    • Fragmented authority (water = state subject), free-power-driven over-extraction
    • Weak enforcement, poor demand management, neglect of traditional water bodies
  3. Body — governance responses:
    • Jal Shakti Abhiyan (mission-mode, district-level campaign), Ministry of Jal Shakti consolidation
    • Atal Bhujal Yojana (community participation), Jal Jeevan Mission (Paani Samitis)
  4. Body — counterpoint: physical drivers (climate, geography, aquifer type) also matter — not purely governance.
  5. Conclusion: Crisis is largely but not solely governance — decentralised, community-led management is the durable fix.

Quick Reference — Ocean Acidification

  • Chemistry: CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻ → pH falls
  • Ocean pH dropped ~8.2 → ~8.1 (≈30% more acidic since pre-industrial)
  • Aragonite saturation falls → corals/shellfish can't build shells
  • Hits coral reefs, pteropods, molluscs, plankton
  • Distinct from warming — a separate CO₂ effect

Key Concepts to Remember

  • Sea-level rise = thermal expansion + land-ice melt (not sea-ice)
  • Coral bleaching — heat stress expels zooxanthellae → white coral
  • Thermohaline circulation — density-driven global ocean conveyor
  • Methane hydrates — frozen CH₄ in seabed; thaw = feedback
  • Water stress <1700 m³/cap/yr; scarcity <1000 m³
