Conceptual Clarity — Why this Topic Matters

Global environmental treaties are among the densest static blocks in the paper — and almost always tested. Three patterns recur:

  • Definitional / static: year of a treaty, where it was signed, what it covers, the parent body (e.g. UNFCCC 1992, Montreal Protocol phases out ODS, Ramsar covers wetlands). Pure recall — a treaty↔year↔subject table settles it.
  • Statement-elimination (2–4 statements): matching convention↔protocol↔mechanism — e.g. Kyoto had legally-binding targets on developed countries only, Paris uses NDCs for all, Kigali targets HFCs (not the original ODS). One wrong pairing sinks the option.
  • Applied / current: the latest COP outcomes (loss & damage fund, global stocktake), India’s positions (CBDR, Panchamrit). Mains GS-III uses these for climate-justice & diplomacy answers.

Highest-frequency themes: UNFCCC↔Kyoto↔Paris chain · CBDR-RC principle · Montreal/Kigali on ozone & HFCs · IPCC role · UNCCD & land degradation neutrality · Ramsar & Montreux Record.

1.

Classifying Multilateral Environmental Agreements (MEAs) & CBDR

Before the individual treaties, fix the grammar of international environmental law — the difference between a framework convention and a protocol, hard vs soft law, and the equity principle (CBDR) that runs through climate diplomacy.

1.1 Key legal concepts

TermMeaningExample
Framework ConventionSets broad goals & principles; details filled later.UNFCCC (1992), Vienna Convention (1985).
ProtocolLegally-operational instrument under a convention, with specific commitments.Kyoto Protocol, Montreal Protocol.
AmendmentAdds/changes obligations within an existing protocol.Kigali Amendment (2016) to Montreal.
Hard vs Soft lawBinding treaty obligations vs non-binding declarations/principles.Kyoto targets (hard) vs Rio Declaration (soft).
COPConference of Parties — supreme decision-making body of a convention.UNFCCC COP, CBD COP.

1.2 CBDR-RC — the equity principle

  • Common But Differentiated Responsibilities & Respective Capabilities — all states share responsibility for the environment but developed nations, having contributed more historical emissions & having greater capacity, must lead.
  • Origin: Rio Declaration, 1992 (Principle 7); embedded in the UNFCCC & reaffirmed (as “CBDR-RC in light of national circumstances”) in the Paris Agreement.
  • India’s core negotiating plank, alongside climate justice, equity & historical responsibility.
Prelims trap: a convention/framework sets direction; a protocol creates binding commitments. Kyoto & Montreal are protocols; UNFCCC & Vienna are their parent conventions. Paris is an agreement under the UNFCCC (not a “protocol”).
Fig 31.1 — MEAs are built in layers: a framework convention sets goals, a protocol adds binding commitments, and amendments extend them — all resting on principles like CBDR-RC.
2.

Stockholm 1972, UNEP & the Rio 1992 Foundations

Modern environmental diplomacy begins with the 1972 Stockholm Conference and matures at the 1992 Rio Earth Summit, which spawned three “Rio Conventions.”

MilestoneYearOutcome
Stockholm Conference (UN Conference on the Human Environment)1972First global environment meet; Stockholm Declaration; led to UNEP (HQ Nairobi) & India’s Water Act 1974/Air Act 1981.
Brundtland Report (“Our Common Future”)1987Defined sustainable development.
Rio Earth Summit (UNCED)1992Rio Declaration, Agenda 21, and the 3 Rio Conventions: UNFCCC, CBD, UNCCD (UNCCD adopted 1994).
Rio+202012“The Future We Want”; seeded the SDGs.
Stockholm+502022Reaffirmed a healthy environment as a human right.
Three Rio Conventions (memorise): UNFCCC (climate), CBD (biodiversity), UNCCD (desertification). All trace to the 1992 Rio Summit — a classic matching question.
3.

UNFCCC, 1992 — the Parent Climate Treaty

The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change is the umbrella under which all climate negotiations (Kyoto, Paris, every COP) sit. Objective: stabilise greenhouse-gas concentrations to prevent “dangerous anthropogenic interference.”

FeatureDetail
Adopted / in forceAdopted 1992 (Rio); entered into force 1994.
PrincipleCBDR-RC — differentiates Annex I (developed) from non-Annex.
Party categoriesAnnex I (developed + economies in transition), Annex II (developed funders), Non-Annex I (developing, incl. India).
Supreme bodyConference of Parties (COP) — meets annually (COP1 = Berlin 1995).
NatureFramework convention — no binding emission targets itself; those come via protocols/agreements.
Key distinction: the UNFCCC sets no numeric targets; it is the framework. Binding/quantified action came through the Kyoto Protocol (1997) and the Paris Agreement (2015).
4.

Kyoto Protocol, 1997

The first treaty to set legally-binding emission-reduction targets — but only on developed (Annex I) countries, embodying strict CBDR.

FeatureDetail
Adopted / in forceAdopted 1997 (Kyoto, Japan); in force 2005.
TargetsBinding cuts (~5.2% below 1990 levels) for Annex I only; no targets for developing countries.
Commitment periods1st: 2008–2012; 2nd (Doha Amendment, 2012): 2013–2020.
GasesCO₂, CH₄, N₂O, HFCs, PFCs, SF₆ (later NF₃).
Flexible mechanismsCDM (Clean Development Mechanism), JI (Joint Implementation), Emissions Trading (carbon market).
WeaknessUSA never ratified; Canada withdrew; covered a shrinking share of global emissions.
CDM & India: the Clean Development Mechanism let developed countries earn Certified Emission Reductions (CERs) by funding clean projects in developing countries — India was a major CDM host. This is the ancestor of today’s carbon markets (Article 6, Paris).
Fig 31.2 — The climate-treaty timeline: Stockholm (1972) → UNFCCC (1992) → Kyoto (1997) → Paris (2015) → first Global Stocktake (2023).
5.

Paris Agreement, 2015

The landmark climate treaty adopted at COP21 (Paris) — universal (all countries act) and built bottom-up on Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs).

FeatureDetail
Adopted / in forceAdopted 2015; in force Nov 2016.
Temperature goalHold warming well below 2°C, pursue efforts to limit to 1.5°C above pre-industrial.
ArchitectureBottom-up NDCs (self-set, updated every 5 years, expected to progress — “no backsliding”).
Global StocktakeEvery 5 years (first in 2023) to assess collective progress.
FinanceDeveloped countries to mobilise climate finance (the US$100 bn/yr goal); new goal (NCQG) negotiated later.
Other elementsAdaptation goal, Loss & Damage, Article 6 (carbon markets), transparency framework.

5.1 India’s Paris commitments & Panchamrit

  • At COP26 (Glasgow, 2021) India announced Panchamrit: 500 GW non-fossil capacity by 2030; 50% energy from renewables by 2030; cut projected emissions by 1 bn tonnes by 2030; reduce emissions intensity of GDP by 45% (2030 vs 2005); net-zero by 2070.
  • India’s updated NDC (2022) formalised the 45% intensity cut & 50% non-fossil target.
Kyoto vs Paris (most-tested contrast): Kyoto = top-down, binding targets on developed only; Paris = bottom-up NDCs for all, legally binding process but nationally-determined ambition. Both under the UNFCCC.
6.

The Ozone Regime — Vienna, Montreal & Kigali

The most successful environmental treaty regime — universally ratified and demonstrably healing the ozone layer.

InstrumentYearWhat it does
Vienna Convention1985Framework convention for ozone-layer protection (no specific targets).
Montreal Protocol1987Phases out Ozone-Depleting Substances (ODS) — CFCs, halons, HCFCs. Universally ratified.
Kigali Amendment2016Phases down HFCs — potent greenhouse gases (not ozone-depleting), a climate win.
  • Multilateral Fund helps developing countries meet phase-out costs.
  • India ratified the Kigali Amendment (2021) with its own HFC phase-down schedule.
Prelims trap: HFCs (targeted by Kigali) do NOT deplete ozone — they replaced CFCs but are strong greenhouse gases, so Kigali is really a climate measure bolted onto an ozone treaty. CFCs/HCFCs (ODS) are handled by Montreal itself.
Fig 31.3 — The ozone regime: Vienna Convention (1985) framework → Montreal Protocol (1987) phases out ODS → Kigali Amendment (2016) phases down HFCs.
7.

IPCC & the Science Behind the Treaties

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change supplies the scientific consensus that drives climate negotiations — it does not conduct research itself but assesses published science.

FeatureDetail
Set up byWMO & UNEP, 1988.
RoleAssesses climate-change science, impacts & mitigation; produces Assessment Reports (ARs).
Working GroupsWG-I (physical science), WG-II (impacts/adaptation), WG-III (mitigation).
Latest cycleSixth Assessment Report (AR6) — Synthesis Report 2023 check latest.
RecognitionNobel Peace Prize 2007 (shared with Al Gore).
Related science bodies: IPBES (biodiversity & ecosystem services — the “IPCC for biodiversity”), WMO (weather/climate data), and GEO/Earth observations. IPCC assesses; it does not fund or regulate.
8.

UNCCD — Combating Desertification, 1994

The third Rio Convention — the only legally-binding international agreement linking environment, development and sustainable land management, focused on drylands.

FeatureDetail
Adopted1994 (following the Rio Summit); Secretariat at Bonn.
FocusDesertification, land degradation & drought (DLDD), especially in Africa & drylands.
Key targetLand Degradation Neutrality (LDN) — SDG target 15.3, no net loss of healthy land.
COP14 (2019)Hosted by India (New Delhi); “Delhi Declaration”; India pledged to restore 26 m ha of degraded land by 2030.
InstrumentsNational Action Programmes; links to the Bonn Challenge & Great Green Wall (Africa).
India link: India hosted UNCCD COP14 in 2019 and targets restoring 26 million hectares of degraded land by 2030 — a frequent factual hook. LDN (SDG 15.3) is the core UNCCD goal.
9.

Ramsar Convention on Wetlands, 1971

The oldest of the modern MEAs — an intergovernmental treaty for the conservation & wise use of wetlands, signed in Ramsar, Iran.

FeatureDetail
Adopted / in force1971; in force 1975. NOT a Rio Convention (predates Rio).
Core idea“Wise use” of wetlands; designate Ramsar Sites (Wetlands of International Importance).
CriteriaNine criteria (e.g. supports vulnerable species, >20,000 waterbirds).
Montreux RecordRegister of Ramsar sites where ecological character is threatened. India: Keoladeo & Loktak have been listed.
India80+ Ramsar sites — the most in Asia check latest; implemented via Wetlands (C&M) Rules 2017 & Amrit Dharohar.
Prelims trap: Ramsar (1971) is NOT one of the three Rio Conventions — it predates the 1992 Earth Summit. The Montreux Record flags threatened Ramsar sites (a favourite distractor with “World Heritage in Danger,” which is UNESCO).
10.

Current Affairs & Recent Developments

Climate diplomacy moves at every COP — these dated hooks are the highest-yield applied material for both Prelims and Mains.

  • 2023 COP28 (Dubai): concluded the first Global Stocktake; historic call to “transition away from fossil fuels”; operationalised the Loss & Damage Fund; pledge to triple renewables & double energy efficiency by 2030.
  • 2023 IPCC AR6 Synthesis Report warned the 1.5°C window is closing; called for deep, immediate cuts check latest.
  • 2022 COP27 (Sharm el-Sheikh): agreed in principle to establish the Loss & Damage Fund; India pushed “phase-down of all fossil fuels.”
  • 2022 India’s Long-Term Low-Emission Development Strategy (LT-LEDS) submitted to UNFCCC, elaborating the net-zero-2070 pathway.
  • 2021 India ratified the Kigali Amendment; announced Panchamrit at COP26 (Glasgow); Glasgow Climate Pact used “phase-down” of coal.
  • 2019 UNCCD COP14 hosted by India (New Delhi); Delhi Declaration.
  • Ongoing Article 6 (Paris) carbon-market rules & the New Collective Quantified Goal (NCQG) on finance under negotiation check latest.
Exam angle: expect a Prelims Q on which COP delivered the Loss & Damage Fund / Global Stocktake, and a Mains hook linking India’s Panchamrit & CBDR stance to climate-justice diplomacy.

11. Prelims PYQs

UPSC Prelims 2016

Q: Which three conventions are known as the “Rio Conventions,” tracing to the 1992 Earth Summit?

  • (a) UNFCCC, Ramsar, Montreal Protocol
  • (b) UNFCCC, CBD, UNCCD
  • (c) CBD, Ramsar, CITES
  • (d) UNFCCC, Kyoto, Paris

Ans: (b) — The three Rio Conventions are the UNFCCC (climate), CBD (biodiversity) and UNCCD (desertification). Ramsar (1971) predates Rio; Kyoto/Paris are instruments under the UNFCCC.

UPSC Prelims 2021

Q: Consider the following about the Kyoto Protocol and the Paris Agreement:  1. Kyoto set binding targets only on developed countries.  2. Paris relies on Nationally Determined Contributions for all parties.  3. Both are instruments under the UNFCCC.  Which are correct?

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Ans: (d) — All three are correct — Kyoto (top-down, Annex I only), Paris (bottom-up NDCs for all), both under the UNFCCC framework.

UPSC Prelims 2018

Q: The Kigali Amendment (2016) to the Montreal Protocol targets:

  • (a) Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs)
  • (b) Hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs)
  • (c) Carbon dioxide
  • (d) Methane

Ans: (b) — Kigali phases down HFCs — potent greenhouse gases that are NOT ozone-depleting; the Montreal Protocol itself handles ODS like CFCs/HCFCs.

UPSC Prelims 2023

Q: The concept of “Land Degradation Neutrality (LDN)” is associated with which convention?

  • (a) UNFCCC
  • (b) Ramsar Convention
  • (c) UNCCD
  • (d) CBD

Ans: (c) — LDN (SDG target 15.3) is the central goal of the UNCCD, the desertification/land convention. India hosted UNCCD COP14 in 2019.

UPSC Prelims 2014

Q: The IPCC was established by which two organisations?

  • (a) UNEP and UNESCO
  • (b) WMO and UNEP
  • (c) UNFCCC and WMO
  • (d) UNDP and UNEP

Ans: (b) — The IPCC was set up by the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) and UNEP in 1988. It assesses science; it does not conduct original research.

UPSC Prelims 2017

Q: The “Montreux Record” is maintained under which convention?

  • (a) CITES
  • (b) World Heritage Convention
  • (c) Ramsar Convention
  • (d) CBD

Ans: (c) — The Montreux Record lists Ramsar wetland sites whose ecological character is threatened. India’s Keoladeo & Loktak have featured on it.

UPSC Prelims 2015

Q: Which of the following institutions/outcomes resulted from the 1972 Stockholm Conference?

  • (a) IPCC
  • (b) United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP)
  • (c) Green Climate Fund
  • (d) Global Environment Facility

Ans: (b) — The Stockholm Conference (1972) led to the creation of UNEP (HQ Nairobi). The IPCC (1988) and GCF/GEF came much later.

UPSC Prelims 2022

Q: India’s “Panchamrit” commitments were announced at:

  • (a) COP21 Paris
  • (b) COP27 Sharm el-Sheikh
  • (c) COP26 Glasgow
  • (d) COP28 Dubai

Ans: (c) — India announced Panchamrit (incl. net-zero by 2070, 500 GW non-fossil by 2030) at COP26 in Glasgow (2021).

12. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

Questions below are original, exam-style formulations built from real UPSC GS-III themes — paraphrased to be copyright-safe. Each framework = intro angle → structured body → way-forward, with examples to quote.

Mains GS-III — style 2018 15 marks · 250 words

Q: “The shift from the Kyoto Protocol to the Paris Agreement reflects a fundamental change in the architecture of global climate governance.” Discuss.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — Kyoto model: top-down, legally-binding targets on Annex I only; CDM/JI/ET mechanisms.
  2. Limits: US non-ratification, narrow coverage, no developing-country action.
  3. Paris shift: universal participation, bottom-up NDCs, 1.5°C goal, 5-yearly stocktake.
  4. Continuity: CBDR-RC retained; finance & transparency.
  5. Critique: ambition gap, weak enforceability of NDCs.
  6. Conclusion: flexibility gained universality but at the cost of legal bindingness.
Mains GS-III — style 2016 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Why is the Montreal Protocol regarded as the most successful environmental treaty? What lessons does it offer for climate negotiations?

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — success markers: universal ratification, measurable ozone recovery, Multilateral Fund.
  2. Design features: clear targets, differentiated timelines, financial & technical support, adjustable science-based schedules.
  3. Kigali extension: HFC phase-down — climate co-benefit.
  4. Lessons for climate: concrete targets, funding, technology transfer, flexibility.
  5. Caveats: ozone problem was narrower & had ready substitutes vs the economy-wide energy transition.
  6. Conclusion: a template but not a direct transplant.
Mains GS-III — style 2020 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Examine the principle of Common But Differentiated Responsibilities and its relevance to India’s climate diplomacy.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — origin: Rio 1992 (Principle 7); in UNFCCC & Paris (“in light of national circumstances”).
  2. Rationale: historical responsibility, capacity, equity, right to develop.
  3. India’s stance: per-capita emissions, climate justice, finance & technology from developed nations.
  4. Evolving reality: pressure on major emerging emitters; India’s Panchamrit & net-zero-2070.
  5. Tensions: dilution of firewall between developed/developing.
  6. Conclusion: CBDR remains India’s anchor while it raises domestic ambition.
Mains GS-III — style 2023 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Discuss the significance of the Loss and Damage Fund agreed at recent COPs.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — concept: compensation/support for climate impacts beyond adaptation.
  2. Milestones: agreed in principle COP27; operationalised COP28.
  3. Significance: recognises historical responsibility, aids vulnerable/LDCs & SIDS.
  4. Challenges: funding scale, contributors, governance (host: World Bank interim).
  5. Conclusion: a milestone in climate justice, execution pending.
Mains GS-III — style 2019 15 marks · 250 words

Q: How do international conventions on desertification and wetlands support India’s domestic environmental goals?

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — UNCCD: LDN, India’s 26 m ha restoration pledge, COP14 host; links to GIM/CAMPA.
  2. Ramsar: 80+ sites, wise use, Montreux Record; links to Wetland Rules 2017 & Amrit Dharohar.
  3. Co-benefits: water security, biodiversity, carbon sinks, livelihoods.
  4. Gaps: encroachment, monitoring, funding.
  5. Conclusion: conventions provide targets & legitimacy that anchor domestic schemes.

15-Minute Revision Box

Must-Remember Facts — International Treaties & Conventions (I)

Grammar, milestones & climate regime
  • MEA grammar: convention = framework (goals); protocol = binding action; amendment extends it. CBDR-RC from Rio 1992.
  • Milestones: Stockholm 1972 (→UNEP), Brundtland 1987, Rio 1992 (3 Rio Conventions: UNFCCC, CBD, UNCCD).
  • UNFCCC (1992): parent climate treaty, no targets; Annex I / Non-Annex; COP is supreme body.
  • Kyoto (1997): binding on developed only; CDM/JI/ET; Doha Amendment (2nd period).
  • Paris (2015): NDCs for all; well below 2°C, aim 1.5°C; Global Stocktake every 5 yrs (first 2023).
Ozone, land, wetlands & India
  • Ozone: Vienna 1985 (framework) → Montreal 1987 (ODS/CFCs) → Kigali 2016 (HFCs).
  • IPCC: WMO + UNEP (1988); assesses science; AR6 2023.
  • UNCCD (1994): desertification; LDN (SDG 15.3); India hosted COP14 (2019).
  • Ramsar (1971): wetlands; NOT a Rio Convention; Montreux Record = threatened sites; India 80+ sites.
  • India: Panchamrit (COP26, 2021); net-zero 2070; ratified Kigali 2021; LT-LEDS 2022.
Highest-frequency themes since 2014: Rio-Convention identification (UNFCCC/CBD/UNCCD vs Ramsar); Kyoto-vs-Paris architecture; ozone treaty ladder (Vienna→Montreal→Kigali); IPCC parentage (WMO+UNEP); and India’s Panchamrit / net-zero-2070 pledges.
