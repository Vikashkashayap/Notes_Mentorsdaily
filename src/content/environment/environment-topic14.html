Conceptual Clarity — Why this Topic Matters

UPSC tests ex-situ conservation & forest governance in three distinct ways:

  • Definitional / static: exact difference between zoo / gene bank / botanical garden, the regulator of each (CZA), the founding Acts & years (NFP 1988, CAMPA Act 2016, FCA 1980), and social-forestry sub-types — a fact-heavy Prelims zone.
  • Statement-elimination: multi-statement sets on CAMPA (national vs state levels, fund use), ex-situ scope, and JFM structure where one clause is deliberately reversed.
  • Applied / current: CAMPA fund-utilisation scrutiny, Van (Sanrakshan Evam Samvardhan) Adhiniyam 2023, agroforestry push — direct Mains GS-III fodder (see Current Affairs section).

Focus especially on the zoo/gene-bank/botanical-garden distinction & their regulators, CAMPA structure & critiques, and NFP 1988 shift + JFM — the three highest-frequency themes.

1. Ex-Situ Conservation Concept

Ex-situ conservation ("off-site") protects species and genetic material outside their natural habitat, in controlled/managed conditions. It serves as a critical safety net when in-situ conservation (Topic 13) alone cannot guarantee survival — particularly for species with critically small wild populations, or when habitat loss is too severe/rapid for natural recovery. It also supports captive breeding for eventual reintroduction, genetic material banking, and research/education.

2. Conceptual Clarity — Zoo vs Gene Bank vs Botanical Garden

Three distinct ex-situ tools

Zoo/Aquarium: holds live animals for captive breeding, public education, and research — regulated in India by the Central Zoo Authority. Gene/Seed Bank: stores genetic material (seeds, tissue, semen, embryos, DNA) in a dormant/frozen state for long-term preservation and future use, without maintaining live breeding populations. Botanical/Horticultural Garden: maintains living plant collections for conservation, taxonomic research, and education — distinct from a gene bank because plants are grown and displayed live rather than stored dormant.

Prelims hook: A gene bank stores material dormant; a botanical garden keeps plants living. Cryopreservation belongs to gene banks, NOT zoos/gardens — a favourite statement-reversal trap.

3. Zoos & Aquariums

India's zoos are regulated by the Central Zoo Authority (CZA), a statutory body under the Wildlife (Protection) Act, 1972, responsible for zoo recognition, coordinated captive breeding programmes for endangered species, and enforcing minimum standards of animal housing/care. Coordinated breeding programmes exist for species including the Asiatic Lion, tiger, and various endangered deer and bird species, often linked to eventual reintroduction efforts.

4. Seed & Gene Banks

Seed/gene banks preserve genetic diversity of crop varieties, wild relatives, and endangered species' genetic material for long-term security against extinction, disease, and climate change impacts.

InstitutionFocus
National Bureau of Plant Genetic Resources (NBPGR), New DelhiIndia's national seed gene bank; conserves crop and wild relative germplasm
National Bureau of Animal Genetic Resources (NBAGR), KarnalConserves livestock/animal genetic resources including semen/embryo cryopreservation
Svalbard Global Seed Vault (Norway)International "doomsday vault" — backup seed storage for global food security, holds duplicate samples from national gene banks worldwide including India's
Cryopreservation (freezing at ultra-low temperatures, e.g., liquid nitrogen at -196°C) is the standard technique enabling long-term dormant storage of seeds, semen, and embryos in gene banks.

Orthodox vs Recalcitrant seeds — a classic trap: Orthodox seeds survive drying & freezing → can be seed-banked (most cereals, pulses). Recalcitrant seeds (mango, jackfruit, cocoa, many tropical trees) die if dried/frozen → cannot be seed-banked, so they need field gene banks, tissue culture, or cryopreservation of embryos instead.

5. Botanical & Horticultural Gardens

Botanical gardens maintain living plant collections, often including rare/endangered/endemic species, for conservation, research and public education. India's premier institutions include the Indian Botanic Garden (Acharya Jagadish Chandra Bose Indian Botanic Garden), Howrah, and the National Botanical Research Institute (NBRI), Lucknow. Horticultural gardens focus more on cultivated ornamental/economic plant species but also contribute to ex-situ conservation of cultivar diversity.

6. National Wildlife Action Plan

India's National Wildlife Action Plan (NWAP), periodically revised by the Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change, sets the strategic framework for wildlife conservation — the most recent iteration (NWAP 2017-2031) shifted focus toward landscape-level conservation (beyond isolated PAs), climate change adaptation for wildlife, and increased community/private sector participation, moving away from the earlier species/PA-centric approach of previous plans.

7. CAMPA

CAMPA (Compensatory Afforestation Fund Management and Planning Authority) manages funds collected from project developers for diverting forest land to non-forest use (e.g., mining, infrastructure) under the Forest (Conservation) Act, 1980. Under the Compensatory Afforestation Fund Act, 2016, these funds — collected as Net Present Value (NPV) of diverted forest land plus compensatory afforestation costs — are used for afforestation, forest regeneration, wildlife habitat improvement, and infrastructure development in forest areas, administered at both national (National CAMPA) and state (State CAMPA) levels.

Fig 14.1 — CAMPA fund flow: forest land diversion to compensatory afforestation
CAMPA has drawn criticism for compensatory afforestation often failing to replicate the ecological value of lost natural/old-growth forest, and for delays/mismanagement in fund utilisation by states — a recurring current affairs and mains theme.

8. National Forest Policy & Joint Forest Management

India's National Forest Policy, 1988 marked a fundamental shift from the earlier revenue/commercial-timber orientation (1952 policy) toward environmental stability, conservation, and meeting local community needs — setting a national target of 33% of geographical area under forest/tree cover (partially achieved only in some states; national figure remains below target per ISFR data, Topic 05).

Joint Forest Management (JFM), launched following the 1988 policy (formalised via a 1990 government circular), institutionalises partnership between State Forest Departments and local communities (through Forest Protection Committees/JFM Committees) for protecting and regenerating degraded forest land, with communities receiving a share of usufruct benefits (non-timber forest produce, share of timber revenue) in return for protection responsibilities.

The Forest-Law Ladder — FCA 1980, FRA 2006, Van Sanrakshan 2023

Governance of India's forests rests on three statutes UPSC repeatedly cross-tests. Do not confuse who they empower:

LawYearCore purposeKey feature
Forest (Conservation) Act1980Restrict diversion of forest land to non-forest useCentral government approval mandatory for diversion; source of NPV & CAMPA funds
Forest Rights Act (Scheduled Tribes & Other Traditional Forest Dwellers — Recognition of Forest Rights Act)2006Recognise & vest forest rights of STs and OTFDs living in forestsIndividual + Community Forest Rights; Gram Sabha is the authority; consent needed for diversion

FRA is a rights law, not a green law: the Forest Rights Act 2006 empowers the Gram Sabha (not the Forest Department) to grant/reject forest diversion consent and manage Community Forest Resources — a frequent statement-attribution trap. It recognises rights of both Scheduled Tribes and Other Traditional Forest Dwellers (residence pre-13 Dec 2005).

9. Social Forestry

Social forestry refers to tree-planting programmes on non-forest/community/private/degraded land (village commons, farm bunds, roadsides, wastelands) to meet local fuelwood, fodder, and small timber needs — reducing pressure on natural forests. It is broadly categorised into:

  • Farm forestry: tree planting by farmers on their own agricultural land.
  • Community forestry: tree planting on community/common land, managed collectively.
  • Extension forestry: planting on roadsides, canal banks, railway lines, and other government wastelands.
  • Agroforestry: integration of trees with crops/livestock on the same land for combined production and environmental benefits.

11. Prelims PYQs

UPSC CSE 2021

Q. With reference to CAMPA (Compensatory Afforestation Fund Management and Planning Authority), consider the following statements:

1. It manages funds collected for diversion of forest land for non-forest purposes.
2. It operates only at the national level with no state-level component.
3. Funds are used for afforestation and forest regeneration activities.

Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

  • (a)1 and 2 only
  • (b)1 and 3 only
  • (c)2 and 3 only
  • (d)1, 2 and 3

Ans: (b). CAMPA operates at BOTH National and State levels (statement 2 false); it manages forest-diversion funds used for afforestation/regeneration — statements 1 and 3 true.

UPSC CSE 2019

Q. The National Forest Policy of 1988 primarily emphasised which of the following?

  • (a)Maximising revenue from commercial timber extraction
  • (b)Environmental stability, conservation, and meeting local community needs
  • (c)Complete prohibition of any forest produce collection by local communities
  • (d)Privatisation of all forest land

Ans: (b). NFP 1988 shifted from the 1952 revenue/timber orientation to ecological stability, conservation and local livelihood needs.

UPSC CSE 2017

Q. Joint Forest Management (JFM) in India involves:

  • (a)Management of forests solely by the State Forest Department
  • (b)Partnership between State Forest Departments and local communities for forest protection and regeneration
  • (c)Management of forests exclusively by private corporations
  • (d)Transfer of all forest ownership to local communities

Ans: (b). JFM is a Forest Department–community partnership (via JFM/Forest Protection Committees) with usufruct benefit-sharing; ownership is not transferred.

UPSC CSE 2015

Q. Consider the following statements regarding ex-situ conservation:

1. Zoos and gene banks are both examples of ex-situ conservation.
2. Ex-situ conservation always requires the species to be removed permanently from the wild.
3. Cryopreservation is a technique used in gene banks.

Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

  • (a)1 and 2 only
  • (b)1 and 3 only
  • (c)2 and 3 only

Ans: (b). Ex-situ often supports captive breeding for eventual reintroduction — it does NOT require permanent removal (statement 2 false); zoos+gene banks are ex-situ and cryopreservation is a gene-bank technique.

12. Mains PYQs

GS-III 2021

Q. Distinguish between in-situ and ex-situ conservation. Discuss the role of gene banks and zoos as complementary tools in biodiversity conservation.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: Define in-situ (conserving species in natural habitat — PAs, sacred groves) vs ex-situ (conserving outside habitat — zoos, gene banks, botanical gardens). Both mandated under CBD Articles 8 and 9.
  2. Body — Key distinctions:
    • Scale & ecology: in-situ conserves whole ecosystems + evolutionary processes; ex-situ conserves individuals/germplasm only, no ecological interactions.
    • Cost & capacity: in-situ cheaper per species at scale; ex-situ costly, limited numbers, but precise control.
    • Genetic risk: ex-situ risks inbreeding, adaptation to captivity, loss of wild fitness.
  3. Body — Gene banks & zoos as complements:
    • Gene banks (NBPGR Delhi, NBAGR Karnal, Svalbard Vault): store seeds/germplasm/cryopreserved material as insurance against extinction & crop-diversity loss.
    • Zoos & captive breeding (CZA-regulated): breed critically endangered species for reintroduction — e.g. Gir lion, Manipur brow-antlered deer, gharial, vulture (Jatayu centres).
    • Complementarity: ex-situ acts as a backup + source population; success is measured only when animals/plants are reintroduced back in-situ (e.g. cheetah, Great Indian Bustard conservation breeding).
  4. Conclusion: Neither is a substitute; an integrated strategy — in-situ as primary, ex-situ as insurance and reintroduction pipeline — best secures biodiversity.

GS-III 2018

Q. What is the CAMPA mechanism? Critically examine its effectiveness in achieving genuine compensatory afforestation in India.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: CAMPA = Compensatory Afforestation Fund Management and Planning Authority; funds collected when forest land is diverted (NPV + compensatory afforestation cost), given statutory backing by the CAF Act 2016 with National + State authorities.
  2. Body — Design/objectives:
    • Ensure afforestation of an equivalent non-forest / degraded forest area for every diversion.
    • Channel Net Present Value toward forest regeneration, wildlife management, and forest-dependent communities.
  3. Body — Criticism / effectiveness gaps:
    • Poor fund utilisation: large unspent balances; CAG flagged idle funds.
    • Monoculture plantations replace biodiverse natural forests — no ecological equivalence.
    • Land shortfall & conflict: afforestation on community/forest-dweller land, violating FRA 2006 consent.
    • Monitoring weak: survival rates of plantations often unverified.
  4. Conclusion: CAMPA is a sound financing idea but delivers "notional" afforestation; genuine compensation needs FRA compliance, natural-forest restoration over monoculture, and transparent survival audits.

GS-III 2016

Q. Discuss the objectives and outcomes of Joint Forest Management in India. What challenges limit its effectiveness?

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: JFM (national resolution 1990) = partnership between State Forest Department and local communities via JFM Committees / Van Suraksha Samitis to protect and regenerate degraded forests in exchange for usufruct benefit-sharing.
  2. Body — Objectives:
    • Regenerate degraded forests through community participation.
    • Share timber/non-timber produce and share of final harvest with communities.
    • Reduce Forest Department–community conflict; build local stewardship.
  3. Body — Outcomes:
    • ~100,000+ JFM committees managing millions of hectares; improved canopy in several states (e.g. West Bengal Arabari model, Odisha).
    • Higher NTFP incomes and reduced encroachment in successful zones.
  4. Body — Challenges:
    • No statutory backing (resolution, not law) — weak, reversible tenure.
    • Forest Department dominance; token community role; elite capture.
    • Overlap/conflict with FRA 2006 community forest rights.
    • Benefit-sharing delays; unequal gender participation.
  5. Conclusion: JFM pioneered participatory forestry but needs statutory tenure security and FRA alignment to move from state-led to genuine community-led governance.

GS-III 2014

Q. Examine the shift in India's forest policy from the 1952 policy to the 1988 National Forest Policy. What were the key drivers of this shift?

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction: India's forest policy evolved from a revenue/production orientation (1894, 1952) to an ecological/conservation orientation (1988), reflecting a paradigm change in how forests are valued.
  2. Body — 1952 policy:
    • Treated forests as a national revenue and industrial-timber resource.
    • Set the 33% forest-cover target (60% hills, 20% plains) but prioritised commercial exploitation over local needs.
  3. Body — 1988 National Forest Policy (shift):
    • Primary aim redefined as ecological stability and conservation, not revenue.
    • Prioritised meeting the fuel/fodder/NTFP needs of forest-dependent communities.
    • Restricted diversion of forest land; introduced participatory management (basis of JFM 1990).
  4. Body — Key drivers:
    • Alarming deforestation & declining forest cover through the 1970s–80s.
    • Environmental awakening — Chipko movement, Forest (Conservation) Act 1980, Silent Valley.
    • Recognition of tribal/community rights and rising soil-erosion, flood, drought concerns.
  5. Conclusion: The 1952→1988 shift marked a move from forests-as-revenue to forests-as-ecological-infrastructure, laying the foundation for JFM, FRA 2006, and today's conservation-led governance.
