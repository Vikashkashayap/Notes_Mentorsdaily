Conceptual Clarity — Why this Topic Matters

This topic is where UPSC tests whether you can link a human practice to environmental degradation. Three question patterns recur:

  • Definitional / static: "what is desertification?", define biopiracy, salinisation, eutrophication-from-agrochemicals; identify causes of soil erosion. Pure recall — a practice↔impact table settles it.
  • Statement-elimination (2–4 statements): matching practice↔consequence↔region — e.g. overgrazing→desertification (Thar fringe), sand mining→riverbed/aquifer damage, overfishing→stock collapse. One wrong pairing sinks the option.
  • Applied / current: sand-mining bans, groundwater over-exploitation (CGWB categories), UNCCD desertification data, illegal wildlife-trade seizures. Mains GS-III links these to the tragedy of the commons and sustainable-development trade-offs.

Highest-frequency themes: deforestation drivers · desertification & UNCCD · sand mining · groundwater over-extraction · overfishing · monoculture/agrochemicals · illegal wildlife trade & biopiracy.

1. Exploitative Practices — Concept

An exploitative practice is any use of a natural resource faster than it can regenerate — extraction beyond the sustainable yield. It is the root cause behind pollution, biodiversity loss and climate change.

1.1 Sustainable vs Exploitative Use

AspectSustainable useExploitative useExample
RateWithin regeneration limitBeyond regenerationSelective vs clear-felling
Time horizonLong-term, inter-generationalShort-term profitRotational grazing vs overgrazing
OutcomeResource persistsDepletion & degradationSustainable vs over-fishing

1.2 The Tragedy of the Commons

  • Idea (Garrett Hardin, 1968): when a resource is shared and open-access, each user maximises personal gain, but the collective over-use ruins the resource for all.
  • Examples: common grazing land, ocean fisheries, groundwater aquifers, the atmosphere (GHGs).
  • Solutions: regulation, property rights, community management (Elinor Ostrom's work on commons governance), pricing externalities.

1.3 Underlying Drivers

  • Population & consumption: rising demand for food, water, minerals, energy.
  • Market failure: environmental costs not priced (externalities).
  • Weak governance: open access, poor enforcement, corruption.
  • Poverty & livelihood pressure: dependence on forests, fisheries, grazing.
Fig 27.1 — The tragedy of the commons: open-access resources are over-used because each user maximises private gain, leading to collapse — broken only by regulation, property rights or community governance.
Exam angle: "Tragedy of the commons" (Hardin, 1968) explains groundwater depletion, overfishing & GHG emissions. Elinor Ostrom (Nobel) showed community-based governance can manage commons without privatisation or state control.

2. Deforestation & Forest Degradation

Deforestation is permanent removal of forest for other land use; degradation is a decline in forest quality/density without full clearance.

2.1 Drivers

DriverHowExample
AgricultureClearing for cropland/plantationShifting cultivation (jhum), palm oil
InfrastructureRoads, dams, mining, urban sprawlLinear projects in forests
Fuelwood & timberLogging, firewood collectionPressure on open forests
Forest firesNatural + man-madeUttarakhand, Similipal fires

2.2 Consequences

  • Soil erosion & landslides; loss of watershed regulation → floods/droughts.
  • Carbon loss: forests are carbon sinks; clearing releases CO₂.
  • Biodiversity & habitat loss; human-wildlife conflict.
  • Livelihood loss for forest-dependent communities.
Takeaway: India tracks forest cover via the biennial FSI India State of Forest Report (ISFR). Compensatory afforestation (CAMPA) and the Forest (Conservation) Act regulate diversion — but "green cover" gains often come from plantations, not dense natural forest (a key critique).

3. Overgrazing & Desertification

Overgrazing is grazing livestock beyond the land's carrying capacity, stripping vegetation and compacting soil — a leading cause of desertification.

3.1 Desertification

  • Definition: land degradation in arid, semi-arid & dry sub-humid areas from climatic variation & human activity (UNCCD).
  • Not the same as natural desert spread — it is degradation of productive dryland.
  • Scale: nearly a third of India's land is degraded/undergoing desertification (ISRO Desertification Atlas).

3.2 Causes & Effects

CauseEffectRegion example
OvergrazingVegetation loss, soil compactionThar fringe, Deccan
DeforestationErosion, moisture lossHimalayan foothills
Over-irrigationWaterlogging & salinisationPunjab, Haryana canal belts
Wind/water erosionLoss of topsoilWestern India
Prelims trap: UNCCD (1994) is the desertification convention — India hosted COP14 (2019) and set a Land Degradation Neutrality (LDN) target. Do not confuse with UNFCCC (climate) or CBD (biodiversity) — the Rio trio.

4. Mining & Sand Mining

Mining extracts minerals but disturbs land, water and forests. Sand mining — sand is the world's second-most extracted resource after water — is an acute, under-regulated problem in India.

4.1 Impacts of Mining

ImpactDetail
Land degradationOverburden, tailings, subsidence
Water pollutionAcid mine drainage, heavy metals
DeforestationForest diversion (e.g. coal in central India)
Air & dustRespiratory disease, silicosis
DisplacementTribal & forest communities

4.2 Sand Mining

  • Use: construction boom drives huge demand for river/beach sand.
  • Harm: deepens riverbeds, lowers water table, damages aquifers, erodes banks & bridges, destroys aquatic habitat & turtle-nesting beaches.
  • Regulation: Sustainable Sand Mining Guidelines (MoEFCC); requires environmental clearance; NGT has repeatedly cracked down on illegal mining.
  • Governance gap: "sand mafia", illegal night mining, weak state enforcement.
Prelims trap: even minor minerals like sand now need environmental clearance (post-2012 Supreme Court order). Sand mining lowers the water table by exposing/deepening the aquifer — a common statement-elimination link.

5. Overfishing & Marine Over-use

Overfishing is harvesting fish faster than stocks can replenish, collapsing fisheries and marine ecosystems.

5.1 Drivers & Practices

PracticeHarm
Bottom trawlingDestroys seabed habitat, high bycatch
Use of fine/banned netsCatches juveniles & non-targets
Overcapacity of fleetsEffort exceeds sustainable yield
IUU fishingIllegal, unreported, unregulated catch

5.2 Consequences & Response

  • Stock collapse & loss of fisher livelihoods; disrupted food chains.
  • Bycatch: turtles, dolphins killed incidentally — hence Turtle Excluder Devices (TEDs).
  • Management tools: fishing bans in breeding season, mesh-size rules, Marine Protected Areas, WTO Agreement on Fisheries Subsidies (curbing harmful subsidies).
  • Blue economy: sustainable-fisheries push (PM Matsya Sampada Yojana) balances livelihood & conservation.
Takeaway: ocean fisheries are the classic open-access commons — without catch limits and MPAs, individual over-fishing collapses the shared stock. TEDs and seasonal bans are the practical fixes UPSC likes.

6. Groundwater Over-extraction

India is the world's largest groundwater user. Pumping beyond natural recharge is depleting aquifers — a slow-motion commons tragedy driven by free/subsidised power and canal-less irrigation.

6.1 The Problem

  • Drivers: Green-Revolution cropping (paddy/sugarcane), free electricity, unregulated borewells.
  • Effects: falling water table, drying wells, land subsidence, salinity ingress in coastal aquifers, arsenic/fluoride concentration.
  • CGWB categories: assessment units classed as Safe, Semi-critical, Critical, Over-exploited (extraction > recharge).

6.2 Management

MeasureDetail
Atal Bhujal YojanaCommunity-led groundwater management in stressed blocks
Jal Shakti AbhiyanRainwater harvesting, recharge, "Catch the Rain"
MGNREGA worksCheck dams, ponds, watershed structures
Micro-irrigationDrip/sprinkler (Per Drop More Crop)
Crop diversificationShift from water-guzzling paddy
Prelims trap: an "over-exploited" CGWB unit is one where annual groundwater extraction exceeds annual recharge (stage of extraction >100%). Atal Bhujal Yojana targets such water-stressed blocks via community participation.

7. Monoculture & Agrochemical Overuse

Intensive agriculture — large single-crop fields plus heavy fertiliser/pesticide use — boosts yields but degrades soil, water and biodiversity.

7.1 Monoculture

  • What: growing one crop variety over large areas (e.g. Green-Revolution wheat-paddy).
  • Problems: loss of agro-biodiversity, greater pest/disease vulnerability, soil-nutrient exhaustion, dependence on inputs.
  • Alternatives: crop rotation, mixed/inter-cropping, agro-forestry, millets ("Shree Anna").

7.2 Agrochemical Overuse

InputHarmExample
Chemical fertiliser (N,P)Soil acidification, eutrophication of waterNitrate runoff, algal blooms
PesticidesBiomagnification, pollinator loss, resistanceDDT legacy, bee decline
Excess irrigationWaterlogging & salinisationCanal-command salinity

7.3 Sustainable Alternatives

  • Organic & natural farming (e.g. Zero-Budget Natural Farming / PKVY).
  • Integrated Nutrient & Pest Management (INM/IPM); balanced fertilisation, bio-pesticides.
  • Soil Health Cards, micro-irrigation, millet promotion.
Takeaway: the Green Revolution solved hunger but created a soil-water-biodiversity debt. UPSC frames the fix as a shift to sustainable/regenerative agriculture — diversification, natural farming and precise input use.

8. Illegal Wildlife Trade & Biopiracy

Over-exploitation of species for trade drives many toward extinction; misuse of biological resources & traditional knowledge is biopiracy.

8.1 Illegal Wildlife Trade

  • Products: tiger & leopard parts, rhino horn, elephant ivory, pangolin scales, star tortoises, exotic birds, red sanders.
  • Drivers: demand for traditional medicine, luxury, pets; high profit, low risk.
  • Controls: CITES (global trade convention), Wildlife (Protection) Act 1972, WCCB (Wildlife Crime Control Bureau), TRAFFIC monitoring.

8.2 Biopiracy

AspectDetail
DefinitionUnauthorised commercial use of bio-resources/traditional knowledge without benefit-sharing
Famous casesNeem, turmeric, Basmati patent challenges (revoked)
Legal shieldBiological Diversity Act 2002, National Biodiversity Authority (NBA)
Global frameworkNagoya Protocol on Access & Benefit-Sharing (ABS)
Defensive toolTraditional Knowledge Digital Library (TKDL)
Prelims trap: CITES regulates international trade in species (Appendices I/II/III); CBD/Nagoya governs access & benefit-sharing of genetic resources. India's Biological Diversity Act + NBA operationalise the anti-biopiracy regime.

9. Governance & Sustainable Alternatives

Curbing exploitative practices needs a mix of law, economic incentives, community rights and sustainable technology.

9.1 Governance Tools

ToolFunction
Regulation & clearancesEIA, forest/wildlife clearances, mining leases
Economic instrumentsPolluter-pays, green tax, subsidy reform
Property/community rightsForest Rights Act, JFM, community fisheries
InstitutionsNGT, CPCB/SPCBs, CGWB, WCCB, NBA
InternationalUNCCD, CBD, CITES, Ramsar, WTO fisheries

9.2 The Sustainable-Development Frame

  • Sustainable use — within regeneration limits; inter-generational equity.
  • Circular economy — reduce virgin-resource extraction (links to Topic 24).
  • Community stewardship — Ostrom-style commons management; decentralised control.
  • Aligning with SDGs — SDG 12 (responsible consumption), 15 (life on land), 14 (life below water), 6 (water).
Integrated takeaway: every exploitative practice is a commons problem — the durable answer combines enforceable limits, right pricing of externalities and community ownership, not just bans. This is the GS-III reasoning chain UPSC rewards.

11. Prelims PYQs

UPSC Prelims 2016

Q: The phrase "tragedy of the commons" best explains:

  • (a) Private ownership improving resource use
  • (b) Over-use of a shared, open-access resource
  • (c) Government monopoly over minerals
  • (d) Equitable distribution of common land

Ans: (b) — Hardin's idea: individually rational over-use of a shared resource ruins it for all — e.g. groundwater, fisheries.

UPSC Prelims 2015

Q: Desertification, as defined by the UNCCD, refers to land degradation in:

  • (a) Only true deserts
  • (b) Arid, semi-arid & dry sub-humid areas
  • (c) Coastal wetlands
  • (d) Tropical rainforests

Ans: (b) — Desertification = degradation of drylands (arid/semi-arid/dry sub-humid), not the natural spread of deserts.

UPSC Prelims 2020

Q: Consider illegal sand mining. Which statements are correct? 1. It can lower the local water table.  2. Minor minerals like sand require environmental clearance.  3. It damages riverbeds and aquatic habitat.

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Ans: (d) — All correct — sand mining deepens riverbeds, harms aquifers/habitat, and now needs environmental clearance.

UPSC Prelims 2019

Q: A groundwater assessment unit classified as "over-exploited" by CGWB is one where:

  • (a) Extraction is far below recharge
  • (b) Extraction equals half the recharge
  • (c) Annual extraction exceeds annual recharge
  • (d) No extraction is permitted

Ans: (c) — "Over-exploited" = stage of extraction >100% (extraction exceeds recharge). Atal Bhujal Yojana targets such blocks.

UPSC Prelims 2017

Q: The Turtle Excluder Device (TED) is associated with reducing harm from:

  • (a) Oil spills
  • (b) Plastic pollution
  • (c) Bycatch in fishing (trawling)
  • (d) Coral bleaching

Ans: (c) — TEDs let sea turtles escape trawl nets, cutting bycatch mortality — a fix for over-fishing's collateral damage.

UPSC Prelims 2021

Q: Biopiracy in India is primarily countered by:

  • (a) Wildlife (Protection) Act 1972
  • (b) Biological Diversity Act 2002 & the NBA
  • (c) Air (Prevention) Act 1981
  • (d) Forest (Conservation) Act 1980

Ans: (b) — The Biological Diversity Act 2002 and National Biodiversity Authority regulate access & benefit-sharing of bio-resources. (TKDL is a defensive tool.)

UPSC Prelims 2014

Q: Which convention regulates international trade in endangered species?

  • (a) UNCCD
  • (b) Ramsar
  • (c) CITES
  • (d) UNFCCC

Ans: (c) — CITES governs cross-border trade via Appendices I/II/III. UNCCD = desertification; Ramsar = wetlands; UNFCCC = climate.

UPSC Prelims 2023

Q: "Land Degradation Neutrality (LDN)" is a target under the:

  • (a) Convention on Biological Diversity
  • (b) UN Convention to Combat Desertification
  • (c) Paris Agreement
  • (d) Montreal Protocol

Ans: (b) — LDN is a UNCCD goal (SDG 15.3); India committed to it, notably at UNCCD COP14 (2019).

Prelims 2026 — anticipated themes

Likely: UNCCD LDN & India's degraded-land restoration pledge, CGWB "over-exploited" block metrics, WTO fisheries-subsidies agreement, and Biological Diversity (Amendment) Act 2023 changes are live hooks — expect Act-to-purpose and convention-mapping items. check for latest update or data

12. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

Questions below are original, exam-style formulations built from real UPSC GS-III themes — paraphrased to be copyright-safe. Each framework = intro angle → structured body → way-forward, with examples to quote.

Mains GS-III — style 2018 15 marks · 250 words

Q: "The tragedy of the commons underlies most of India's resource-degradation problems." Examine with examples and solutions.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — define: tragedy of the commons (Hardin, 1968) — open-access over-use.
  2. Examples: groundwater depletion, overfishing, overgrazing, atmospheric GHGs.
  3. Solutions:
    • Regulation (limits), pricing externalities, property rights, community management (Ostrom), technology.
  4. Conclusion: Atal Bhujal (community groundwater), MPAs, JFM — blended governance.
Mains GS-III — style 2016 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Discuss the causes and consequences of desertification in India and the measures to combat it.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — define: desertification (UNCCD) — dryland degradation.
  2. Causes: overgrazing, deforestation, over-irrigation/salinisation, erosion, climate.
  3. Consequences: lost productivity, migration, food/water insecurity.
  4. Conclusion: LDN target, afforestation, watershed & grazing management, UNCCD COP14 commitments.
Mains GS-III — style 2020 10 marks · 150 words

Q: "Unregulated sand mining is an ecological and governance crisis." Comment.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — context: construction boom, sand = 2nd-most extracted resource.
  2. Ecological harm: riverbed deepening, water-table fall, bank erosion, habitat loss.
  3. Governance gap: sand mafia, weak enforcement, illegal night mining.
  4. Conclusion: sustainable-mining guidelines, monitoring/GPS, alternatives (M-sand), NGT oversight.
Mains GS-III — style 2019 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Examine how groundwater over-extraction threatens India's water and food security, and suggest remedies.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — scale: largest groundwater user; many over-exploited blocks (CGWB).
  2. Drivers: free power, paddy/cane monoculture, unregulated borewells.
  3. Effects: falling table, salinity, dry wells, quality decline.
  4. Conclusion: Atal Bhujal, micro-irrigation, crop diversification, power-pricing reform, recharge.
Mains GS-III — style 2022 15 marks · 250 words

Q: "Illegal wildlife trade and biopiracy are twin threats to India's biological heritage." Discuss the legal and institutional response.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — threats: trade in tiger parts, pangolin, red sanders; biopiracy of neem/turmeric knowledge.
  2. Legal shield: Wildlife (Protection) Act 1972, Biological Diversity Act 2002, CITES, Nagoya Protocol.
  3. Institutions: WCCB, NBA, TRAFFIC, TKDL (defensive).
  4. Conclusion: stronger enforcement, community incentives, demand-reduction, benefit-sharing.
Mains GS-III — anticipated theme 15 marks · 250 words

Q: India has pledged to restore degraded land under UNCCD's Land Degradation Neutrality goal. Examine the drivers of land degradation and the institutional roadmap to meet this target. check for latest update or data

15-Minute Revision Box

Must-Remember Facts — Environmental Exploitative Practices

Concepts & land/water
  • Exploitative use: extraction beyond regeneration — root of pollution & biodiversity loss.
  • Tragedy of commons: Hardin 1968; open-access over-use; Ostrom → community governance.
  • Deforestation: agriculture, infra, fire; erosion + carbon loss; FSI/ISFR, CAMPA.
  • Desertification: dryland degradation; UNCCD (1994), LDN, COP14 India 2019; ~30% land degraded.
  • Groundwater: over-exploited = extraction > recharge (CGWB); Atal Bhujal, micro-irrigation.
Mining, fisheries & biodiversity
  • Sand mining: 2nd-most extracted resource; lowers water table; needs env. clearance; NGT.
  • Overfishing: bottom-trawling, bycatch; TEDs, seasonal bans, MPAs, WTO subsidies deal.
  • Monoculture/agrochemicals: soil/water damage; fix = diversification, natural farming, IPM/INM.
  • Wildlife trade/biopiracy: CITES + WCCB (trade); Biodiversity Act 2002 + NBA + Nagoya + TKDL (biopiracy).
  • Frame: limits + pricing externalities + community ownership — not bans alone.
Highest-frequency themes since 2014: tragedy of the commons, desertification/UNCCD/LDN, CGWB over-exploited groundwater, sand-mining governance, CITES & Biological Diversity Act mapping, TED/overfishing — WTO fisheries subsidies and BDA-2023 amendments are current-affairs hooks.
