1. Why Environmental Movements Matter

  • Environmental movements shaped India's conservation laws, forest policy, and the doctrine of public participation in environmental decision-making.
  • Many embody ecofeminism (women's central role), Gandhian methods (satyagraha, non-violence), and the idea that livelihoods of forest/river-dependent communities are inseparable from ecology.
  • Movements have directly influenced legislation and projects — e.g., Silent Valley's abandonment, the Forest Conservation Act 1980 momentum, and rehabilitation norms for large dams.

2. Bishnoi Movement

  • Earliest recorded conservation movement in India — 1730, Khejarli village, Marwar (Rajasthan).
  • Led by Amrita Devi Bishnoi, who along with 363 Bishnois sacrificed their lives hugging Khejri trees to prevent the Maharaja of Jodhpur's men from felling them.
  • The Bishnoi sect (founded by Guru Jambheshwar) follows 29 tenets protecting trees and wildlife (blackbuck, chinkara) — the philosophical ancestor of Chipko.
  • The Amrita Devi Bishnoi Wildlife Protection Award is given for community conservation.

3. Chipko Movement

  • Began 1973 in Chamoli district, Uttarakhand (then Uttar Pradesh, Garhwal Himalaya); "chipko" = "to hug/embrace" — villagers hugged trees to prevent commercial felling.
  • Key figures: Sunderlal Bahuguna (gave it national reach and the slogan "ecology is permanent economy"), Chandi Prasad Bhatt (founded DGSM), and Gaura Devi (led the women of Reni village in the iconic 1974 action).
  • Strongly ecofeminist and Gandhian; led to a 15-year ban on green felling in the Himalayan forests of UP and influenced national forest policy.
Chipko's "embrace-the-tree" tactic was consciously revived from the Bishnoi tradition, linking the two movements across 240 years.

4. Appiko Movement

  • The "southern Chipko" — began 1983 in the Uttara Kannada district of Karnataka (Western Ghats).
  • "Appiko" = "to hug" in Kannada; led by Pandurang Hegde, protesting commercial felling and the loss of tropical forest to monoculture plantations.
  • Three-fold message: save (Ulisu), grow (Belesu), use rationally (Balasu) the forests.

5. Save Silent Valley Movement

  • Campaign (late 1970s-1980s) to protect the Silent Valley tropical rainforest in the Palakkad district, Kerala, from a proposed hydroelectric dam on the Kunthipuzha river.
  • Spearheaded by the Kerala Sasthra Sahithya Parishad (KSSP) and poets/scientists; the valley is a habitat of the endangered lion-tailed macaque.
  • Success: the project was abandoned; the area was declared Silent Valley National Park (1984/1985) — a landmark victory of science-based conservation advocacy.

6. Narmada Bachao Andolan (NBA)

  • Movement (from the mid-1980s) against large dams on the Narmada river, especially the Sardar Sarovar Dam (Gujarat) — raising displacement, rehabilitation, and ecological concerns across Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat, Maharashtra.
  • Led by Medha Patkar (with Baba Amte's support and, at one stage, Arundhati Roy's advocacy).
  • Raised the doctrine of rehabilitation & resettlement (R&R) of oustees and displaced tribals; the Supreme Court's Narmada judgments allowed dam height increases with R&R conditions.
  • A defining case in the debate between large-dam development and displacement/environment.

7. Other Indian Movements

  • Jungle Bachao Andolan: 1980s, Singhbhum (Bihar/Jharkhand) — tribal resistance to replacing natural sal forests with commercial teak.
  • Tehri Dam Movement: opposition (Sunderlal Bahuguna) to the Tehri dam on the Bhagirathi, on seismic and displacement grounds.
  • Navdanya: Vandana Shiva's seed-saving and organic-farming/biodiversity movement.
  • Chilika Bachao Andolan: against commercial prawn aquaculture in Odisha's Chilika lake.

9. International Campaigns

Earth Day

Observed annually on 22 April (since 1970). A global day of environmental awareness; the 1970 first Earth Day is credited with catalysing modern environmental regulation (e.g., US EPA).

Earth Hour

Organised by WWF (started Sydney, 2007). Held on the last Saturday of March — people switch off non-essential lights for one hour (8:30-9:30 pm local) as a symbol of commitment to the planet.

World Environment Day

5 June — led by UNEP since 1974 (mandated by the 1972 Stockholm Conference); the flagship global environment-awareness day with a host country and annual theme.

10. Blue Flag Certification

Blue Flag

An international eco-label awarded by the Foundation for Environmental Education (FEE), Denmark, to beaches, marinas, and sustainable boating operators that meet ~33 stringent criteria across environmental education, water quality, environmental management, and safety/services.

  • India's beaches are certified under the MoEFCC's BEAMS (Beach Environment & Aesthetics Management Services) programme within the ICZM (Integrated Coastal Zone Management) project.
  • Shivrajpur (Gujarat), Ghoghla (Diu), Kasarkod & Padubidri (Karnataka), Kappad (Kerala), Rushikonda (Andhra Pradesh), Golden (Odisha), Radhanagar (Andaman) were among the first eight Indian beaches to receive Blue Flag in 2020, with more added later (e.g., Minicoy Thundi & Kadmat in Lakshadweep).

Q: With reference to the ‘Chipko Movement’, which of the following statements is correct?

  • (a) It was a movement against commercial deforestation in the Himalayan region

  • (c) It sought to protect coastal beaches
  • (d) It opposed mining in the Western Ghats

Ans: (a) — Chipko (1973, Chamoli) was a Gandhian, ecofeminist movement against commercial felling in the Himalayan forests.

13. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

Questions below are original, exam-style formulations built from real UPSC GS-I / GS-III themes — paraphrased to be copyright-safe. Each framework = intro angle → structured body → way-forward, with examples to quote.

Mains GS-I — style 2020 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Examine the role of grassroots environmental movements in shaping India’s forest and conservation policy, with reference to the Chipko and Silent Valley movements.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — bottom-up ecology: Frame grassroots movements as ecological assertion linking livelihoods, conservation & rights.
  2. Chipko (1973, Chamoli): Sunderlal Bahuguna, Chandi Prasad Bhatt, Gaura Devi; Gandhian, ecofeminist; tree-hugging against commercial felling → influenced the 1980 Forest (Conservation) Act & a 15-year Himalayan green-felling ban.
  3. Save Silent Valley (Kerala, KSSP): stopped a hydro dam → Silent Valley National Park (1984/85); protected the lion-tailed macaque & rainforest; science-society activism.
  4. Policy impacts: strengthened EIA culture, forest-conservation law, PIL/environmental jurisprudence, community-forest rights (later FRA 2006).
  5. Broader significance & limits: gave voice to women/tribals; but localised, personality-driven, unevenly institutionalised.
  6. Conclusion: These movements were catalytic in mainstreaming conservation in Indian policy & law.
Mains GS-III — style 2016 15 marks · 250 words

Q: “Large dams involve a trade-off between development and displacement.” Discuss with reference to the Narmada Bachao Andolan.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — the NBA lens: Narmada Bachao Andolan (1985, Medha Patkar, Baba Amte) opposed Sardar Sarovar & other Narmada dams over displacement.
  2. Development case: irrigation (Gujarat/Rajasthan), drinking water, hydropower, flood control, drought relief.
  3. Displacement case: ~200,000+ oustees (largely tribals/Dalits); submergence of forests/farmland; inadequate Rehabilitation & Resettlement (R&R); cultural loss.
  4. Judicial/institutional: SC 2000 allowed construction with R&R conditions & height caps; catalysed the World Commission on Dams debate; land-acquisition reform (LARR 2013).
  5. Balancing: cost-benefit including social/ecological costs; free prior informed consent; just R&R; decentralised water options.
  6. Conclusion: NBA reframed “development” to include displaced people’s rights — participatory planning is the way forward.
Mains GS-I — style 2021 15 marks · 250 words

Q: “Indian environmental movements have been deeply shaped by women and Gandhian methods.” Elaborate with examples.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — ecofeminism & satyagraha: Note the twin threads of women’s leadership and non-violent Gandhian protest.
  2. Women’s role: Amrita Devi (Bishnoi), Gaura Devi (Chipko’s Reni action), Medha Patkar (NBA), Vandana Shiva (Navdanya).
  3. Gandhian methods: tree-hugging, padyatras, fasts, satyagraha; “ecology is permanent economy” (Bahuguna).
  4. Why women lead: forest/water dependence for fuel, fodder and household needs makes degradation a lived, gendered burden.
  5. Way forward: institutionalise community and women-led conservation (JFM, FRA gram sabhas, SHGs).
  6. Conclusion: ecofeminism and Gandhian ethics remain the moral core of India’s green movements.
Mains GS-III — style 2019 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Assess how international awareness campaigns and eco-labels complement grassroots movements in advancing environmental goals.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Introduction — two scales of action: Contrast local movements with global campaigns and standards.
  2. Global campaigns: Earth Day (22 April), Earth Hour (WWF), World Environment Day (5 June, UNEP) — mass awareness.
  3. Eco-labels/standards: Blue Flag (FEE, Denmark) via BEAMS/ICZM raises beach-management standards and green tourism.
  4. Complementarity: campaigns build public pressure and norms; grassroots movements deliver on-ground change; labels create measurable benchmarks.
  5. Limits: risk of tokenism/greenwashing without enforcement.
  6. Conclusion: the two scales reinforce each other when linked to policy and accountability.
Mains GS-I — style 2018 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Trace the continuity between the Bishnoi tradition and the Chipko Movement.

Model Answer Framework
  1. Bishnoi roots: 1730 Khejarli sacrifice (Amrita Devi, 363 martyrs) to save Khejri trees; Guru Jambheshwar’s 29 tenets.
  2. Chipko revival: 1973 Chamoli tree-hugging consciously drew on the Bishnoi “embrace-the-tree” ethic across 240 years.
  3. Shared values: reverence for trees/wildlife, community sacrifice, non-violence.
  4. Conclusion: Chipko institutionalised an ancient ecological ethic into a national conservation movement.
