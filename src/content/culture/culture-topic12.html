Topic 12: Indian Languages

India's languages are its deepest cultural bedrock — the Constitution recognises 22 languages in the Eighth Schedule, the 2011 Census counted 121 languages and 1,369 mother tongues, and the country hosts four major language families. The exam tests this as family–language–fact matching plus policy: the Eighth Schedule, the Classical Language status, and the official-language provisions. This book-depth file covers the Indo-Aryan, Dravidian, Austroasiatic and Tibeto-Burman families, the Eighth Schedule (22), the Classical Languages (Tamil to the 2024 additions), scripts from Brahmi, and language policy — with PYQs and model answers.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames Languages

Languages are tested on two axes: family–language matching (static: "Which of these is Austroasiatic?") and constitutional/policy facts (the Eighth Schedule, classical status, official-language articles). Sort your prep:

  • Static/factual — four families and which language belongs where; the 22 Eighth-Schedule languages (and which came in via which amendment); the classical languages and their years. High recall.
  • Institutional/policy — Articles 343–351 (official language, Hindi + English), the Eighth Schedule (Article 344/351), classical-language criteria and benefits, the Official Languages Act 1963. Reform architecture.
  • Analytical (GS-I / GS-II) — linguistic diversity as unity-in-diversity, the Hindi–regional-language debate, language endangerment and preservation. Needs idea + example + judgement.

Note: Two facts anchor everything — the Eighth Schedule has 22 languages (English is not in it), and Classical status is a Union-government recognition (Tamil first, 2004) separate from the Schedule. Keep the two lists distinct.

1. Overview & Language Families

India's languages descend from four major families (plus small isolates like the Andamanese and Great Andamanese). A "family" means genetically related languages from a common ancestor — the single most tested static grid in this topic.

  • Indo-Aryan (Indo-European): the largest family (~78% of Indians), spoken across the North, West and East; descends from Sanskrit via Prakrit and Apabhramsha.
  • Dravidian: the four literary South Indian languages plus tribal tongues (Gondi, Tulu, Brahui in Pakistan) — an indigenous family unrelated to Indo-Aryan.
  • Austroasiatic (Austric): the Munda branch (Santhali, Mundari, Ho) of central-east India and the Khasi–Mon-Khmer of Meghalaya — India's oldest linguistic layer.
  • Sino-Tibetan (Tibeto-Burman): the North-East and Himalayan languages — Bodo, Manipuri (Meitei), Naga, Mizo, Ladakhi.
One-line frame: India's languages = four families — Indo-Aryan (North), Dravidian (South), Austroasiatic (tribal Central-East) and Tibeto-Burman (North-East) — a single family grid answers most matching questions.

2. Indo-Aryan Languages

The Indo-Aryan branch of the Indo-European family is India's largest, descending in a clear line from Vedic/Classical Sanskrit → Prakrits → Apabhramsha → modern languages. It is usually split into stages and regional groups.

Stage / GroupExamplesNote
Old Indo-AryanVedic Sanskrit, Classical SanskritThe classical root language
Middle Indo-AryanPali, Prakrits, ApabhramshaPali = Buddhist canon; Prakrit = Jain texts & drama
New Indo-Aryan (North)Hindi, Urdu, Punjabi, RajasthaniLargest speaker bloc
New Indo-Aryan (East)Bengali, Assamese, Odia, MaithiliMagadhan group
New Indo-Aryan (West)Marathi, Gujarati, Konkani, Sindhi—
  • Sanskrit is the fountainhead — the language of the Vedas, epics and classical literature, and a Classical Language (2005); it is spoken as a mother tongue by a tiny population but has huge cultural weight.
  • Pali and Prakrit (Middle Indo-Aryan) carried the Buddhist and Jain canons respectively; both were granted Classical status in 2024.
  • Hindi and Urdu share the Khari Boli base and grammar but differ in script (Devanagari vs Perso-Arabic) and vocabulary register — a "single spoken, two literary" case.
Prelims trap: Indo-Aryan is a branch of Indo-European, not a separate family. Sanskrit → Prakrit/Pali → Apabhramsha → modern languages is the descent line. Konkani, Sindhi, Maithili are Indo-Aryan and in the Eighth Schedule.

3. Dravidian Languages

The Dravidian family is indigenous to South India and unrelated to Indo-Aryan. It has four major literary languages — each also a Classical Language — plus many tribal tongues, and even an outlier (Brahui) in Balochistan.

LanguageStateClassical year
TamilTamil Nadu2004 (first ever)
TeluguAndhra Pradesh & Telangana2008
KannadaKarnataka2008
MalayalamKerala2013
Tulu, Gondi, Kodava, KurukhVarious (tribal/regional)Not in Eighth Schedule
  • Tamil is the oldest — with a continuous literary tradition from the Sangam age (c. 300 BCE) — and was the first language to be declared Classical (2004).
  • Telugu ("Italian of the East" for its vowel-ending mellifluousness) and Kannada both received Classical status in 2008; both have literature over a thousand years old.
  • Tulu and Gondi are major Dravidian tongues not in the Eighth Schedule — a live demand for inclusion.
Prelims trap: All four literary Dravidian languages (Tamil, Telugu, Kannada, Malayalam) are both Eighth-Schedule and Classical. Tamil was first (2004). Tulu is Dravidian but NOT in the Eighth Schedule.

4. Austroasiatic & Tibeto-Burman Families

Two smaller families complete India's linguistic map: the very old Austroasiatic (tribal central-east and Meghalaya) and the Sino-Tibetan / Tibeto-Burman of the North-East and Himalaya. Only a few of their languages reach the Eighth Schedule.

Family / BranchLanguagesRegion
Austroasiatic — MundaSanthali, Mundari, Ho, KorkuJharkhand, Odisha, Chhattisgarh, WB
Austroasiatic — Mon-KhmerKhasi, NicobareseMeghalaya, Nicobar
Tibeto-Burman — Bodo-GaroBodo, Garo, TripuriAssam, Meghalaya, Tripura
Tibeto-Burman — Kuki-Chin / NagaMizo, Naga groups, Manipuri (Meitei)Mizoram, Nagaland, Manipur
Andamanese (isolate)Great Andamanese, Onge, JarawaAndaman Islands (highly endangered)
  • Santhali is the flagship Austroasiatic language — the only Munda language in the Eighth Schedule (added 2003) and the only one with its own script, Ol Chiki.
  • Bodo and Manipuri (Meitei) are the Tibeto-Burman languages in the Eighth Schedule; Manipuri now uses both Bengali script and the revived Meitei Mayek.
  • Andamanese languages are linguistic isolates — among the world's most endangered, with Great Andamanese nearly extinct.
Prelims trap: Santhali = Austroasiatic (Munda), Ol Chiki script, in Eighth Schedule. Bodo & Manipuri = Tibeto-Burman, in Eighth Schedule. Khasi & Mizo are NOT in the Eighth Schedule.

5. The Eighth Schedule (22 Languages)

The Eighth Schedule lists languages the Union is duty-bound to develop (Articles 344(1) and 351). It began with 14 languages in 1950 and now has 22, added through four constitutional amendments. This is the single highest-yield fact set in the topic.

AddedAmendmentLanguages
1950 (original 14)ConstitutionAssamese, Bengali, Gujarati, Hindi, Kannada, Kashmiri, Malayalam, Marathi, Odia, Punjabi, Sanskrit, Tamil, Telugu, Urdu
196721st AmendmentSindhi
199271st AmendmentKonkani, Manipuri, Nepali
200392nd AmendmentBodo, Dogri, Maithili, Santhali
  • Original 14 (1950): note Urdu and Sanskrit are in from the start; Odia and Assamese too.
  • The four add-ons: Sindhi (1967) → Konkani, Manipuri, Nepali (1992) → Bodo, Dogri, Maithili, Santhali (2003) — a memorised 1+3+4 sequence gives all 22.
  • Not included: English (an "associate/official" language but not in the Schedule), and demand languages like Tulu, Bhojpuri, Rajasthani, Garhwali — 38+ languages await inclusion.
Prelims high-frequency: Eighth Schedule = 22 languages; English is NOT one of them. Remember the amendment sequence: 21st (Sindhi) · 71st (Konkani, Manipuri, Nepali) · 92nd (Bodo, Dogri, Maithili, Santhali).

6. Classical Languages

"Classical Language" is a status conferred by the Union Government (through the Ministry of Culture, on the advice of a Linguistic Experts Committee) — entirely separate from the Eighth Schedule. It brings scholarly awards, Centres of Excellence and university chairs.

Criteria (as revised)Requirement
AntiquityHigh antiquity of early texts — recorded history of 1500–2000 years
Heritage bodyA body of ancient literature/texts considered a valuable heritage by generations
Original traditionAn original literary tradition, not borrowed from another speech community
DistinctnessThe classical language and literature may be distinct from the modern form
  • The list (chronological grant): Tamil (2004), Sanskrit (2005), Kannada & Telugu (2008), Malayalam (2013), Odia (2014).
  • 2024 additions: the Union Cabinet approved Marathi, Pali, Prakrit, Assamese and Bengali as Classical Languages — taking the total to 11.
  • Benefits: international/national awards for scholars, a Centre of Excellence for Studies, and professional chairs in central universities.
Prelims trap: Classical status is a Union recognition, NOT part of the Constitution/Eighth Schedule. Tamil was first (2004). After the 2024 additions the count is 11 (Tamil, Sanskrit, Kannada, Telugu, Malayalam, Odia, Marathi, Pali, Prakrit, Assamese, Bengali). check for latest update or data

8. Scripts & Their Evolution

Most Indian scripts descend from the ancient Brahmi script (attested in Ashokan edicts, 3rd c. BCE). Understanding the Brahmi family — and the exceptions — is a recurring prelims theme.

ScriptDescent / UseNote
BrahmiParent of most Indian scriptsDeciphered by James Prinsep (1837); Ashokan edicts
KharosthiNW India (Gandhara)Right-to-left; Aramaic-derived; died out
DevanagariHindi, Sanskrit, Marathi, NepaliFrom Brahmi via Nagari/Gupta scripts
Southern Brahmi groupTamil, Telugu, Kannada, Malayalam, GranthaRounded forms (to suit palm-leaf writing)
Ol Chiki / Meitei MayekSanthali / ManipuriModern indigenous-revival scripts (non-Brahmi origin claims)
Perso-ArabicUrdu, Kashmiri, SindhiNot Brahmi-derived
  • Brahmi is the ancestor of the Devanagari, Bengali-Assamese, Gurmukhi, Gujarati, Odia and all four South Indian scripts — a single tree with a northern and southern branch.
  • Kharosthi (used in the north-west, Aramaic-influenced, written right-to-left) is the famous exception that did not survive.
  • Southern scripts became rounded because they were incised on palm leaves — straight lines would split the leaf.
Prelims trap: Brahmi (left-to-right) is the parent of most Indian scripts and was deciphered by James Prinsep; Kharosthi is the right-to-left NW exception. Urdu/Kashmiri/Sindhi use Perso-Arabic, not Brahmi-derived scripts.

9. Language Policy & Current Affairs

The Constitution's language provisions (Part XVII, Articles 343–351) balance a national link-language with regional autonomy. Policy debates — Hindi imposition, mother-tongue education, endangered languages — are the GS-II/GS-I handles.

  • Official language: Article 343 makes Hindi in Devanagari the official language of the Union, with English continued (Official Languages Act, 1963) — English is an associate, not an Eighth-Schedule, language.
  • Key articles: 344 (Official Language Commission & Committee), 345–347 (state official languages), 348 (language of courts), 350A (mother-tongue primary education), 351 (directive to develop Hindi).
  • NEP 2020: emphasises mother-tongue/regional-language instruction up to at least Grade 5 and a flexible three-language formula — reviving the Hindi-imposition debate.
  • Endangerment: UNESCO lists nearly 200 Indian languages as endangered; the People's Linguistic Survey of India (Ganesh Devy) documented 780 languages; the Andamanese isolates are near extinction.
  • 2024 development: five new Classical Languages (Marathi, Pali, Prakrit, Assamese, Bengali) approved — the current-affairs peg for this topic.
Current-affairs frame: language policy is a federal balancing act — a link language for unity versus mother-tongue rights for diversity; endangered-language preservation is the newer, quieter priority. check for latest update or data

10. Prelims PYQs

Languages appear as family matching and Eighth-Schedule/classical facts. Practice MCQs below mirror the UPSC pattern; blocks without a genuine dated PYQ are honestly labelled "concept practice".

Concept practice

Q: How many languages are currently listed in the Eighth Schedule of the Constitution of India?

  • (a) 18
  • (b) 20
  • (c) 22
  • (d) 24

Answer: (c) 22 — from an original 14 (1950), reaching 22 via the 21st, 71st and 92nd Amendments. English is not among them.

Concept practice

Q: Which one of the following languages belongs to the Austroasiatic (Munda) family?

  • (a) Bodo
  • (b) Santhali
  • (c) Manipuri
  • (d) Konkani

Answer: (b) Santhali — a Munda (Austroasiatic) language with its own Ol Chiki script. Bodo and Manipuri are Tibeto-Burman; Konkani is Indo-Aryan.

Concept practice

Q: Which language was the first to be accorded the status of a Classical Language of India?

  • (a) Sanskrit
  • (b) Tamil
  • (c) Telugu
  • (d) Kannada

Answer: (b) Tamil (2004). Sanskrit followed in 2005; Telugu and Kannada in 2008.

Concept practice

Q: Consider the following statements:
1. English is listed in the Eighth Schedule of the Constitution.
2. Pali and Prakrit have been granted Classical Language status.
Which of the statements is/are correct?

  • (a) 1 only
  • (b) 2 only
  • (c) Both 1 and 2
  • (d) Neither 1 nor 2

Answer: (b) 2 only. English is NOT in the Eighth Schedule (it is an associate official language). Pali & Prakrit were granted Classical status in 2024.

Concept practice

Q: Most modern Indian scripts are derived from which ancient script, deciphered by James Prinsep?

  • (a) Kharosthi
  • (b) Brahmi
  • (c) Grantha
  • (d) Devanagari

Answer: (b) Brahmi — parent of most Indian scripts, deciphered by James Prinsep (1837) from Ashokan edicts. Kharosthi (right-to-left) is the NW exception that died out.

Concept practice

Q: Which of the following pairs (language — family) is correctly matched?

  • (a) Telugu — Indo-Aryan
  • (b) Khasi — Tibeto-Burman
  • (c) Marathi — Indo-Aryan
  • (d) Bodo — Dravidian

Answer: (c) Marathi is Indo-Aryan. Telugu is Dravidian; Khasi is Austroasiatic (Mon-Khmer); Bodo is Tibeto-Burman.

11. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

Model GS-I / GS-II questions on Indian languages. Each carries a full skeleton — Introduction → body → Conclusion.

Model question — GS-I 15 marks · 250 words

Q: "India's linguistic diversity is both a cultural treasure and a governance challenge." Discuss.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: With four language families, 22 scheduled languages and over 120 living tongues, India's linguistic plurality is unmatched — a heritage that is also an administrative test.
  2. Cultural treasure: each language carries a distinct literature, folklore and worldview; classical languages like Tamil and Sanskrit anchor a civilisational continuity.
  3. Governance challenge (the core): a link language is needed for administration and mobility, but Hindi-imposition fears, court/education language and equitable representation of smaller tongues create friction.
  4. Constitutional balance: Articles 343–351, the Eighth Schedule, the three-language formula and the Official Languages Act try to reconcile unity with regional autonomy.
  5. Critical edge: success has been uneven — scheduled languages thrive while tribal and isolate languages (Andamanese) face extinction, needing active preservation.
  6. Conclusion: India's model shows diversity can be managed as strength through federal accommodation — but mother-tongue rights and endangered languages demand fresh attention.
Model question — GS-I 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Trace the evolution of Indian languages from their families and discuss the significance of the Classical Language status.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: India's languages spring from four families, with Indo-Aryan and Dravidian dominant; recognising the oldest as "Classical" honours this deep literary continuity.
  2. Family evolution: Indo-Aryan flows Sanskrit→Prakrit/Pali→Apabhramsha→modern tongues; Dravidian is an independent southern family; Austroasiatic and Tibeto-Burman form older/eastern layers.
  3. Classical status (the core): conferred by the Union on antiquity, an original heritage literature and a distinct tradition — Tamil (2004) to the 2024 five (Marathi, Pali, Prakrit, Assamese, Bengali), now eleven.
  4. Significance: it funds scholarship, Centres of Excellence and university chairs, protects ancient texts and gives cultural pride and soft power.
  5. Critical edge: the tag can become competitive and political; benefits must translate into real research, not just prestige.
  6. Conclusion: classical recognition, used well, preserves India's textual heritage — a bridge between an ancient literary past and a living multilingual present.
Model question — GS-II 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Examine the constitutional provisions relating to official language and the safeguards for linguistic minorities.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Part XVII (Articles 343–351) frames India's language policy, balancing a Union link-language with protection for linguistic minorities.
  2. Official language: Article 343 makes Hindi (Devanagari) the Union's official language, with English continued via the Official Languages Act, 1963.
  3. Machinery (the core): Article 344 provides a Commission and Parliamentary Committee; 345–347 let states choose their own official languages.
  4. Minority safeguards: Article 29 (protection of language/script/culture), 350A (mother-tongue primary instruction), 350B (Special Officer for Linguistic Minorities), and Eighth-Schedule development duties.
  5. Critical edge: implementation gaps — weak mother-tongue schooling and Hindi-imposition anxieties — keep the debate alive.
  6. Conclusion: the framework is accommodative; the task is faithful implementation so that no language community feels marginalised.
Model question — GS-I 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Why are many Indian languages endangered, and what steps can preserve them?

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: UNESCO lists nearly 200 Indian languages as endangered, with tribal and isolate tongues (Andamanese) most at risk.
  2. Causes: dominance of link and scheduled languages, migration and urbanisation, absence of scripts/schooling, and inter-generational shift to majority tongues.
  3. Consequence (the core): each lost language erases unique oral literature, ecological knowledge and identity — an irreversible cultural loss.
  4. Preservation steps: documentation (People's Linguistic Survey), mother-tongue education (NEP 2020), script development, digital archives, and community-led revival.
  5. Critical edge: preservation must be community-driven, not merely archival; a language survives only if it is spoken by the young.
  6. Conclusion: safeguarding endangered languages protects India's intangible heritage — it needs living speakers, not just records.
