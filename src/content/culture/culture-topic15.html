Topic 15: Indian Philosophy

Indian philosophy (Darshana, "seeing") is a 3,000-year debate on reality, knowledge and liberation, organised into six orthodox (astika) and three heterodox (nastika) systems. The exam tests it as school–founder–text–core-idea matching plus GS-IV/GS-I ethics links. This book-depth file covers the six astika schools (Nyaya, Vaisheshika, Samkhya, Yoga, Mimamsa, Vedanta), the nastika systems (Charvaka, Buddhist, Jain), the Vedanta sub-schools (Advaita, Vishishtadvaita, Dvaita), key concepts (pramana, purushartha), and modern thinkers — with comparison grids and PYQs with model answers.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames Philosophy

Philosophy is tested as school–founder–text–core-idea matching ("Samkhya is dualistic — of which two principles?" / "Who founded Advaita?"). The productive skill is a nine-school grid with each system's founder, text and one-line idea. Sort your prep:

  • Static/factual — the 6 astika + 3 nastika schools, their founders/foundational sutras and core ideas; the three Vedanta sub-schools and their acharyas. High recall.
  • Thematic/doctrinal — astika (accept Veda) vs nastika (reject Veda); dualism (Samkhya) vs monism (Advaita); the traditional pairing of schools. Structure.
  • Analytical (GS-I/IV) — philosophy as a source of ethics (karma, purushartha), the Yoga/Vedanta influence on modern thought, and its relevance to well-being. Needs idea + example + judgement.

Note: Remember the astika/nastika split is about Vedic authority, not belief in God — Samkhya and Mimamsa are astika yet essentially non-theistic, while nastika simply means "rejects the Vedas" (Charvaka, Buddhism, Jainism).

1. Overview: Astika & Nastika

Indian philosophy divides into astika ("it exists" — those accepting the authority of the Vedas) and nastika ("it does not" — those rejecting it). This single distinction, based on Vedic authority rather than theism, is the backbone of the topic.

  • Six astika schools accept Vedic authority and are traditionally studied in three pairs: Nyaya–Vaisheshika (logic + atomism), Samkhya–Yoga (dualism + practice), Mimamsa–Vedanta (ritual + knowledge).
  • Three nastika schools reject the Vedas: Charvaka (materialist), Buddhism and Jainism.
  • Shared goal: most schools seek liberation (moksha/nirvana/kaivalya/apavarga) from the cycle of rebirth — philosophy is soteriological, not merely academic.
One-line frame: Indian philosophy = 6 astika (accept the Veda) + 3 nastika (reject it) schools, each with a founder, sutra-text and one core idea — a nine-school grid answers most questions.

2. Nyaya, Vaisheshika & Samkhya

The first cluster covers the logical, atomistic and dualist systems — the analytical backbone of Indian thought, dealing with valid knowledge, the physical world and the fundamental duality of matter and spirit.

SchoolFounder / textCore idea
NyayaGautama (Akshapada); Nyaya SutraLogic & epistemology; four pramanas (means of knowledge)
VaisheshikaKanada; Vaisheshika SutraAtomism (paramanu); reality classed into categories (padartha)
SamkhyaKapila; Samkhya Karika (Ishvarakrishna)Dualism of Purusha (consciousness) & Prakriti (matter); non-theistic
  • Nyaya is the school of logic — it developed the theory of valid knowledge (pramana), holding that liberation comes through correct reasoning; it accepts four pramanas: perception, inference, comparison and testimony.
  • Vaisheshika is the oldest atomistic system — Kanada held that all matter is made of eternal atoms (paramanu); it complements Nyaya's logic with physics/metaphysics.
  • Samkhya is the oldest school — a dualism of eternal Purusha (pure consciousness, plural) and Prakriti (primal matter with three gunas: sattva, rajas, tamas); liberation is discriminative knowledge separating the two. It is famously non-theistic.
Prelims trap: Nyaya = logic (Gautama/Akshapada, NOT the Buddha); Vaisheshika = atomism (Kanada); Samkhya = dualism (Kapila), oldest & non-theistic, three gunas. Samkhya provides the metaphysics that Yoga supplies the practice for.

3. Yoga, Mimamsa & Vedanta

The second cluster covers the practical, ritualist and knowledge-oriented systems — how to discipline the mind (Yoga), how to interpret Vedic ritual (Mimamsa), and how to know ultimate reality (Vedanta).

SchoolFounder / textCore idea
YogaPatanjali; Yoga SutraEight-limbed path (ashtanga) to still the mind; adds Ishvara to Samkhya
Mimamsa (Purva)Jaimini; Mimamsa SutraCorrect interpretation & performance of Vedic ritual (dharma)
Vedanta (Uttara Mimamsa)Badarayana; Brahma SutraNature of Brahman & Atman via the Upanishads
  • Yoga (Patanjali's Yoga Sutra) shares Samkhya's metaphysics but adds a personal God (Ishvara) and, above all, a practical eight-limbed method (yama, niyama, asana, pranayama, pratyahara, dharana, dhyana, samadhi) to still the mind.
  • Mimamsa (Purva Mimamsa, Jaimini) is the philosophy of Vedic ritual — it treats the Veda as eternal and authoritative and focuses on dharma through correct action.
  • Vedanta (Uttara Mimamsa, Badarayana's Brahma Sutra) is the "end of the Vedas" — the inquiry into Brahman and the self, the most influential school, branching into several sub-schools.
Prelims trap: Yoga = Patanjali (Yoga Sutra, ashtanga); Mimamsa = Jaimini (ritual/dharma); Vedanta = Badarayana (Brahma Sutra, Brahman). Mimamsa = Purva; Vedanta = Uttara Mimamsa. Yoga adds Ishvara to otherwise-atheistic Samkhya.

4. The Vedanta Sub-schools

Vedanta became the dominant strand of Indian philosophy and split into rival interpretations of the relation between the individual soul (Atman/Jiva), the world and the Absolute (Brahman) — the great medieval theological debate.

Sub-schoolAcharyaPosition
AdvaitaAdi Shankara (8th c.)Non-dualism: Brahman alone is real; world is Maya; Atman = Brahman
VishishtadvaitaRamanuja (11th–12th c.)Qualified non-dualism: souls & world are real attributes of Brahman
DvaitaMadhva (13th c.)Dualism: God, souls & matter eternally distinct; devotion to Vishnu
DvaitadvaitaNimbarkaDualistic non-dualism
ShuddhadvaitaVallabhaPure non-dualism; Krishna bhakti (Pushtimarga)
  • Advaita (Shankara) holds Brahman is the only reality and the perceived world is maya (appearance); liberation is realising Atman = Brahman — the summit of monism.
  • Vishishtadvaita (Ramanuja) affirms a personal God (Vishnu) whose "body" is the real world and souls; it grounds Bhakti with philosophical rigour.
  • Dvaita (Madhva) insists on an eternal difference between God, souls and matter — a robust theism supporting devotional worship.
Prelims trap: Advaita = Shankara (non-dual, maya); Vishishtadvaita = Ramanuja (qualified non-dual); Dvaita = Madhva (dual). These three plus Nimbarka (Dvaitadvaita) and Vallabha (Shuddhadvaita) are the Vedanta acharyas that anchor the later Bhakti movement.

5. Nastika (Heterodox) Schools

The nastika schools reject Vedic authority. Charvaka is the radical materialist outlier, while Buddhism and Jainism developed sophisticated philosophies alongside their religious teaching.

SchoolAttributed toCore idea
Charvaka / LokayataBrihaspati (tradition)Materialism; only perception is valid; no soul/rebirth/afterlife
Buddhist philosophyGautama BuddhaAnatta (no-self), impermanence (anicca), dependent origination
Jain philosophyMahavira / TirthankarasAnekantavada (many-sidedness); soul (jiva) & matter (ajiva)
  • Charvaka (Lokayata) is India's materialist-hedonist school — it accepts only perception (pratyaksha) as valid knowledge, denies soul, God, karma and rebirth, and is preserved mainly through its critics.
  • Buddhist philosophy teaches anatta (no permanent self), anicca (impermanence) and pratityasamutpada (dependent origination), later branching into schools like Madhyamaka (Nagarjuna) and Yogachara.
  • Jain philosophy is realist and pluralist — Anekantavada (reality is many-sided) and Syadvada (conditioned assertion) counsel intellectual humility.
Prelims trap: Charvaka/Lokayata = materialist, only perception valid, denies rebirth. Buddhist "no-self" (anatta) contrasts with Hindu Atman. Nastika means "rejects the Veda," not "atheist" — Samkhya (astika) is non-theistic while remaining Vedic-accepting.

7. Key Concepts & Epistemology

Beyond the schools, UPSC tests shared conceptual vocabulary — the means of knowledge (pramana), the goals of life (purushartha) and core metaphysical terms that recur across systems.

ConceptMeaning
PramanaMeans of valid knowledge — pratyaksha (perception), anumana (inference), upamana (comparison), shabda (testimony), arthapatti, anupalabdhi
PurusharthaFour aims of life: dharma, artha, kama, moksha
GunaThree qualities of Prakriti: sattva, rajas, tamas
Moksha termsMoksha (Vedanta), kaivalya (Samkhya-Yoga/Jain), nirvana (Buddhism), apavarga (Nyaya)
Karma & SamsaraAction-consequence & the cycle of rebirth (shared across schools)
  • Pramanas: schools differ on how many they accept — Charvaka only perception (1), Vaisheshika/Buddhism two, Samkhya three, Nyaya four, Mimamsa/Vedanta six.
  • Purushartha: the four legitimate human goals balance worldly life (artha, kama) within moral order (dharma) toward liberation (moksha).
  • Different liberations, one aim: the words differ (moksha, kaivalya, nirvana, apavarga) but each names freedom from the bondage of rebirth.
GS-IV hook: Indian philosophy supplies an ethical vocabulary — purushartha, karma, dharma, ahimsa, nishkama karma (Gita) — that underlies Indian conceptions of duty, virtue and the good life.

8. Modern Indian Philosophy

Nineteenth- and twentieth-century thinkers reinterpreted classical philosophy for the modern age — reconciling Vedanta with reason, social reform and nationalism.

ThinkerKey idea
Swami VivekanandaPractical Vedanta; service as worship; India's spiritual mission
Sri AurobindoIntegral Yoga; evolution of consciousness (the Supermind)
Mahatma GandhiSatyagraha, ahimsa; ethics rooted in the Gita & Jain thought
Rabindranath TagoreUniversal humanism; religion of man
S. RadhakrishnanNeo-Vedanta; comparative philosophy; "Idealist View of Life"
B. R. AmbedkarNavayana Buddhism; rational, egalitarian ethics
  • Vivekananda turned Advaita into "practical Vedanta" — service of the poor (daridra narayana) as worship — and carried it to the West at the 1893 Parliament of Religions.
  • Aurobindo reworked Yoga into a philosophy of spiritual evolution; Gandhi built a political ethics of truth and non-violence from classical roots.
  • Ambedkar's Navayana reinterpreted Buddhism as a rational, this-worldly ethic of equality, guiding his 1956 mass conversion.
Prelims trap: Vivekananda = practical Vedanta (Ramakrishna's disciple, 1893 Chicago); Aurobindo = Integral Yoga; Radhakrishnan = neo-Vedanta (his birthday is Teachers' Day); Ambedkar = Navayana Buddhism.

9. Relevance & Current Affairs

Classical Indian philosophy is not a museum piece — Yoga, Vedanta and Buddhist thought inform global well-being, ethics and India's cultural diplomacy, giving the topic a current-affairs edge.

  • International Day of Yoga (21 June): declared by the UN in 2014 (India-led resolution) — Patanjali's Yoga philosophy as global soft power and public-health tool.
  • Mindfulness & well-being: Buddhist meditation (vipassana) and yogic practice underpin worldwide mental-health and stress-management movements.
  • Ethics & governance: concepts like nishkama karma, dharma and trusteeship (Gandhi) feature in GS-IV ethics and public-service values.
  • Neo-Vedanta & identity: Vivekananda's ideas remain influential in debates on Indian identity, service and national purpose.
  • Preservation & scholarship: Sanskrit-philosophy scholarship gains from the Classical-language framework and institutes like the Indian Council of Philosophical Research (ICPR).
Current-affairs frame: Indian philosophy today is projected as living wisdom — Yoga diplomacy, mindfulness science and ethics-in-governance — blending heritage preservation with contemporary relevance. check for latest update or data

10. Prelims PYQs

Philosophy is tested as school–founder–idea matching. Practice MCQs below mirror the UPSC pattern; blocks without a genuine dated PYQ are honestly labelled "concept practice".

Concept practice

Q: Which pair of Indian philosophical schools is characterised as dualistic (Purusha–Prakriti) and its practical counterpart of mind-discipline?

  • (a) Nyaya & Vaisheshika
  • (b) Samkhya & Yoga
  • (c) Mimamsa & Vedanta
  • (d) Charvaka & Lokayata

Answer: (b) Samkhya (dualist metaphysics) & Yoga (its practical discipline). Nyaya-Vaisheshika = logic+atomism; Mimamsa-Vedanta = ritual+knowledge.

Concept practice

Q: The doctrine of Advaita (non-dualism), holding Brahman as the sole reality and the world as maya, was systematised by —

  • (a) Adi Shankara
  • (b) Ramanuja
  • (c) Madhva
  • (d) Vallabha

Answer: (a) Adi Shankara. Ramanuja = Vishishtadvaita; Madhva = Dvaita; Vallabha = Shuddhadvaita.

Concept practice

Q: Consider the following pairs (school — founder):
1. Nyaya — Gautama (Akshapada)
2. Vaisheshika — Kanada
3. Yoga — Patanjali
Which of the pairs are correctly matched?

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Answer: (d) All correct. Nyaya (Gautama/Akshapada), Vaisheshika (Kanada), Yoga (Patanjali).

Concept practice

Q: The Charvaka (Lokayata) school is best described as —

  • (a) Idealist and theistic
  • (b) Materialist, accepting only perception as valid knowledge
  • (c) A sub-school of Vedanta
  • (d) A ritualist Vedic system

Answer: (b) Charvaka is materialist — it accepts only pratyaksha (perception), denying soul, God, karma and rebirth.

Concept practice

Q: Which of the following is the foundational text of the Vedanta (Uttara Mimamsa) school?

  • (a) Yoga Sutra
  • (b) Nyaya Sutra
  • (c) Brahma Sutra
  • (d) Samkhya Karika

Answer: (c) Brahma Sutra (Badarayana). Yoga Sutra = Patanjali; Nyaya Sutra = Gautama; Samkhya Karika = Ishvarakrishna.

Concept practice

Q: With reference to Indian philosophy, "nastika" schools are those that —

  • (a) Deny the existence of God
  • (b) Do not accept the authority of the Vedas
  • (c) Reject the theory of karma
  • (d) Believe only in ritual

Answer: (b) Nastika = reject Vedic authority (Charvaka, Buddhism, Jainism). It is not about theism — astika Samkhya/Mimamsa are essentially non-theistic yet Veda-accepting.

11. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

Model GS-I / GS-IV questions on Indian philosophy. Each carries a full skeleton — Introduction → body → Conclusion.

Model question — GS-I 15 marks · 250 words

Q: "The six systems of Indian philosophy represent complementary, not merely competing, approaches to reality." Discuss.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: The astika schools are traditionally studied in pairs, suggesting they were seen as mutually completing facets of one quest rather than rival dogmas.
  2. Logic & physics: Nyaya's theory of valid knowledge and Vaisheshika's atomism together supply epistemology and metaphysics.
  3. Metaphysics & practice (the core): Samkhya's dualist analysis of Purusha and Prakriti is put into practice by Yoga's discipline of the mind.
  4. Ritual & knowledge: Mimamsa secures the authority and method of Vedic action, while Vedanta pursues the knowledge of Brahman that action prepares for.
  5. Critical edge: genuine disagreements remained (theism, the reality of the world), so complementarity is a later harmonising reading, not total unity.
  6. Conclusion: the six schools form a graded map — from reasoning to realisation — reflecting Indian thought's synthetic, pluralistic temper.
Model question — GS-I 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Examine the philosophical debate within Vedanta and its impact on the Bhakti movement.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Vedanta's internal debate over the soul–God–world relation shaped not only theology but the devotional movements that followed.
  2. Advaita: Shankara's non-dualism (Brahman alone real, world as maya) offered intellectual unity but limited scope for a personal, loving God.
  3. Theistic Vedanta (the core): Ramanuja's Vishishtadvaita and Madhva's Dvaita affirmed a real, personal God and real souls — the philosophical ground for devotion.
  4. Bhakti link: these acharyas legitimised Bhakti; Ramanuja inspired the Sri Vaishnava and later Ramananda–Kabir–Tulsidas streams; Vallabha and Nimbarka fuelled Krishna bhakti.
  5. Critical edge: the movement also drew on regional and folk devotion beyond Vedanta, so philosophy enabled but did not wholly create Bhakti.
  6. Conclusion: Vedantic theism gave Bhakti a doctrinal backbone, marrying high philosophy with popular devotion.
Model question — GS-IV 10 marks · 150 words

Q: How can concepts from Indian philosophy inform contemporary ethics and public life?

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Classical Indian ethical concepts remain a living resource for personal virtue and public conduct.
  2. Duty & detachment: the Gita's nishkama karma (selfless action) models integrity and non-attachment to results in public service.
  3. Non-violence & truth (the core): ahimsa and satya, from Jain and Gandhian thought, ground tolerance, conflict resolution and ethical means.
  4. Balance of goals: the purushartha scheme legitimises material aims within a moral order, offering a balanced view of development.
  5. Critical edge: concepts must be applied critically — not to justify fatalism or hierarchy, but to promote duty, equality and well-being.
  6. Conclusion: Indian philosophy offers a tested ethical vocabulary that, applied thoughtfully, enriches modern governance and civic life.
Model question — GS-I 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Assess the contribution of modern thinkers to the revival of Indian philosophy.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Nineteenth- and twentieth-century thinkers reinterpreted classical philosophy to meet the needs of a modernising, colonised India.
  2. Practical Vedanta: Vivekananda recast Advaita as service and self-confidence, projecting Indian thought globally at Chicago (1893).
  3. New syntheses (the core): Aurobindo's Integral Yoga and Radhakrishnan's comparative neo-Vedanta bridged Indian and Western philosophy.
  4. Ethics in action: Gandhi built satyagraha and trusteeship from classical roots; Ambedkar reinterpreted Buddhism for social justice.
  5. Critical edge: revival sometimes idealised the past; the enduring value lies in adapting concepts to equality and reason.
  6. Conclusion: modern thinkers kept Indian philosophy alive and relevant — a resource for identity, ethics and global dialogue.
