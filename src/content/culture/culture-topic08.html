Topic 8: Indian Dance

Dance (nritya) in India is codified worship in motion — its grammar fixed in Bharata's Natyashastra and carried into eight recognised classical forms and countless folk traditions. The exam tests it as dance–state–feature–exponent chains. This book-depth file covers the Natyashastra grammar (Nritta, Nritya, Natya; Tandava & Lasya; Rasa & Abhinaya), the eight classical dances (Bharatanatyam, Kathak, Kathakali, Kuchipudi, Odissi, Manipuri, Mohiniyattam, Sattriya), their comparison, and major folk & tribal dances — with tables, diagrams and PYQs with model answers.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames Indian Dance

Dance is tested as form–state–feature matching ("which is correctly paired?") plus a few discrimination traps (which is a Kerala form, which is solo-female, which uses elaborate mask-like make-up). The productive skill is holding accurate dance–region–marker chains. Sort your prep:

  • Static/factual — dance–state pairs (Bharatanatyam/TN, Odissi/Odisha, Sattriya/Assam), signature markers (Kathakali = green make-up & masks; Kathak = spins/footwork; Mohiniyattam = solo female Lasya), key exponents. High recall.
  • Thematic/stylistic — Tandava (vigorous, masculine) vs Lasya (graceful, feminine); Nritta (pure) vs Nritya (expressive) vs Natya (dramatic); temple origin vs court origin. Feature-to-form mapping.
  • Analytical (GS-I) — dance as living heritage and devotional expression, the Devadasi–to–stage revival, syncretism (Kathak's Hindu–Persian court fusion), and gender/patronage debates. Needs idea + example + judgement.

Note: The Sangeet Natak Akademi recognises eight classical dances; Chhau is sometimes counted as a ninth (semi-classical). All descend from the common grammar of the Natyashastra, differing by region, patron (temple vs court) and emphasis.

1. Overview & Natyashastra Grammar

All Indian classical dance flows from one source-text — Bharata's Natyashastra (c. 2nd BCE–2nd CE), the "fifth Veda" of performing arts. Its vocabulary is the grammar every classical form obeys, so it must be grasped before the individual dances.

  • Nritta / Nritya / Natya: Nritta = pure decorative movement (no meaning); Nritya = expressive dance conveying emotion; Natya = the dramatic element (dance-drama with story). Every classical form blends the three.
  • Tandava & Lasya: the twin modes — Tandava is the vigorous, energetic (masculine, Shiva) aspect; Lasya is the graceful, gentle (feminine, Parvati) aspect.
  • Abhinaya (expression): four kinds — Angika (body/limbs), Vachika (speech/song), Aharya (costume/make-up), Sattvika (emotion/psychological). Communicated through mudras/hastas (hand gestures).
  • Rasa & Bhava: the goal is to evoke rasa (aesthetic flavour) in the audience through bhava (emotional states) — the Natyashastra lists nine rasas (Navarasa).
  • Two origins: most forms began in the temple (devotional, e.g. Bharatanatyam, Odissi) or the court (entertainment, e.g. Kathak) — a useful sorting axis.
One-line frame: Indian classical dance is the Natyashastra made visible — a shared grammar of Nritta–Nritya–Natya and Tandava–Lasya, realised in eight regional styles.

2. Bharatanatyam

The oldest and most codified classical dance, Bharatanatyam is the temple dance of Tamil Nadu — often called the "mother of Indian classical dance" for its close fidelity to the Natyashastra. Once performed by devadasis, it was revived in the 20th century as a stage art.

  • State: Tamil Nadu; temple (Devadasi) origin, systematised in the Tanjore court by the Tanjore Quartet.
  • Features: geometric, fire-like postures (called the "fire dance"); the araimandi half-sitting stance; sharp lines, strong Nritta; solo, traditionally female.
  • Repertoire (Margam): alarippu → jatiswaram → shabdam → varnam → padam → tillana; accompanied by Carnatic music, mridangam and the nattuvanar (conductor).
  • Revival & exponents: Rukmini Devi Arundale (founded Kalakshetra, made it "respectable"), Balasaraswati, Yamini Krishnamurthy.
Prelims trap: Bharatanatyam is the "fire dance" of Tamil Nadu, revived by Rukmini Devi Arundale (Kalakshetra). It is a solo, temple-origin form set to Carnatic music.

3. Kathak

Kathak is the classical dance of North India and the only major form with a strong Hindu–Muslim, temple–to–court trajectory — making it the exam's favourite example of syncretism in dance. Its name comes from the kathakars (story-tellers) of temple courtyards.

  • State: North India (UP — Lucknow & Jaipur gharanas; Banaras, Raigarh).
  • Evolution: began as temple storytelling (Radha–Krishna); under Mughal/Nawabi courts it moved indoors and absorbed Persian costume, footwork and abstraction — the classic syncretic form.
  • Features: intricate footwork (tatkar) with ankle-bells, rapid chakkars (spins), and expressive bhava; performed standing, not in a bent stance; set to Hindustani music & tabla.
  • Gharanas: Lucknow (grace/expression), Jaipur (footwork/vigour), Banaras. Exponents: Birju Maharaj, Sitara Devi, Shambhu Maharaj.
GS-I hook: Kathak is the danced form of India's Ganga–Jamuni culture — a Hindu temple narrative refashioned in the Persianised Mughal court and performed by Hindu and Muslim masters alike.

4. Kathakali & Mohiniyattam

Kerala gave India two contrasting classical forms — the thunderous, masculine dance-drama Kathakali and the gentle, solo-female Mohiniyattam. Pairing them shows the Tandava–Lasya polarity within one region.

FeatureKathakaliMohiniyattam
NatureDance-drama (Natya); vigorous (Tandava)Solo lyrical dance; graceful (Lasya)
PerformerTraditionally male; elaborate costumeSolo female ("dance of the enchantress")
Make-upElaborate mask-like face paint (green = noble)Simple; white/off-white costume with gold
ThemesEpics (Ramayana, Mahabharata)Devotional, Vishnu/Mohini
MusicSopana style; Chenda & Maddalam drumsCarnatic; soft, swaying movements
  • Kathakali: Kerala's spectacular dance-drama — huge costumes, towering head-gear, and colour-coded facial make-up (green = heroic/divine, red = evil), performed with vachika replaced by singers; a form of "story-play".
  • Mohiniyattam: "the dance of Mohini (the enchantress)" — a soft, swaying, feminine solo, all Lasya, with gentle circular movements; revived by Vallathol Narayana Menon at Kerala Kalamandalam.
Prelims trap: Both are from Kerala. Kathakali = male dance-drama with elaborate make-up (Tandava); Mohiniyattam = solo female, graceful (Lasya). Don't confuse either with Kuchipudi (Andhra).

5. Kuchipudi & Odissi

Kuchipudi (Andhra) and Odissi (Odisha) are two temple-born forms famous for a distinctive signature act — Kuchipudi's brass-plate dance and Odissi's sculpturesque tribhanga pose.

FeatureKuchipudiOdissi
StateAndhra PradeshOdisha
OriginBhagavata Mela dance-drama; Siddhendra YogiTemple (Devadasi/Maharis & Gotipuas); Jagannath cult
SignatureTarangam — dancing on the rim of a brass plate, balancing a potTribhanga (three-bend) & chowk stance; sculpturesque poses
CharacterFast, fluid, dramatic; blends Nritta & NatyaLyrical, graceful; "mobile sculpture"
ExponentsVempati Chinna Satyam, Raja & Radha ReddyKelucharan Mohapatra, Sonal Mansingh
  • Kuchipudi: arose from the male Bhagavata Mela dance-drama tradition of Andhra villages; systematised by Siddhendra Yogi; famed for the Tarangam (brass-plate) act and quick rhythmic footwork.
  • Odissi: among the oldest surviving forms, seen in Udayagiri caves and Konark reliefs; the tribhanga pose makes it look like temple sculpture come alive; devoted to Lord Jagannath, carried by Maharis (temple dancers) and Gotipuas (boy dancers).
GS-I hook: Odissi is "sculpture in motion" — its tribhanga pose mirrors the temple carvings of Konark and Bhubaneswar, showing how India's dance and architecture share one aesthetic language.

6. Manipuri & Sattriya

The Northeast contributes two devotional (Vaishnava) forms — the gentle, spinning Manipuri and the monastery-born Sattriya of Assam, the most recent addition to the classical list.

FeatureManipuriSattriya
StateManipurAssam
ThemeVaishnava; Radha–Krishna Raas LeelaVaishnava; life of Krishna (Bhakti)
OriginAncient; Vaishnavism (15th–18th c.)Sankaradeva in Assam's Sattras (monasteries)
CharacterGentle, graceful; no ankle-bells; slow rounded movementsCombination of dance, drama & music; monastic discipline
SignatureCostume: stiff cylindrical skirt (Kumil); veiled faceRecognised as classical only in 2000 (8th form)
  • Manipuri: a soft, devotional form built on the Raas Leela of Radha–Krishna; the dancer glides with rounded movements and no jingling ankle-bells; the elaborate Kumil (barrel-shaped skirt) is its visual signature.
  • Sattriya: created by the saint-reformer Sankaradeva as part of Assam's Neo-Vaishnava Bhakti movement and preserved for centuries in the Sattras (monasteries); it is a combination of music, dance and drama, recognised by the Sangeet Natak Akademi as a classical form only in 2000.
Prelims trap: Sattriya (Assam) is a living tradition of Vaishnavites, created by Sankaradeva in the Sattras — NOT based on songs of Tulsidas/Kabir/Mirabai. It is the most recently recognised (2000) of the eight classical dances.

8. Folk & Tribal Dances

Beyond the classical eight is a vast, participatory world of folk and tribal dances tied to harvest, festival and worship. UPSC tests these as dance–state pairs, so the region is the key.

  • Chhau (Seraikella/Mayurbhanj/Purulia) is a semi-classical martial mask dance inscribed on the UNESCO ICH list — a bridge between folk and classical.
  • Function: folk dances are communal and participatory — marking the agrarian calendar (Bhangra, Bihu), worship (Garba/Navratri) and community identity, unlike the solo classical concert.
  • Tribal forms: Cheraw (Mizoram), Karma (central India), Hozagiri (Tripura) express tribal life and cosmology.
Prelims trap: Chhau is a UNESCO-listed semi-classical mask dance of the Odisha–Jharkhand–Bengal belt. Garba/Dandiya = Gujarat/Navratri; Bhangra = Punjab/harvest.

9. Institutions & Current Relevance

Dance in India is sustained by state academies, gurukuls and festivals — and its history of revival (from stigmatised Devadasi art to national stage) makes it a rich analytical theme.

  • Sangeet Natak Akademi (1953): the national academy for music, dance & drama — it defines the classical canon and confers awards & fellowships on dancers.
  • Custodian institutions: Kalakshetra (Rukmini Devi, Bharatanatyam), Kerala Kalamandalam (Vallathol, Kathakali/Mohiniyattam) — formalised training that saved dying forms.
  • Revival story: the 20th-century "anti-nautch" stigma nearly killed temple dance; reformers reframed it as respectable classical art — a case study in reclaiming heritage while confronting its problematic Devadasi past.
  • Global recognition: Chhau on the UNESCO ICH list; Indian classical dance is a major cultural-diplomacy export (ICCR tours).
  • Challenges: shrinking audiences and patronage, the pull of popular/film dance, commercialisation, and transmitting an oral, guru-based art — the core of any "safeguarding intangible heritage" answer.
One-line frame: India's classical dances survived because reformers rebuilt their institutions and dignity — preservation means sustaining the living guru–shishya practice, not merely staging performances. check for latest update or data

10. Prelims PYQs

A genuine dated UPSC PYQ plus concept-practice MCQs on Indian dance, built on genuine UPSC themes. Each carries a worked rationale.

UPSC Prelims 2014

Q: With reference to the famous Sattriya dance, consider the following statements:
1. Sattriya is a combination of music, dance and drama.
2. It is a centuries-old living tradition of Vaishnavites of Assam.
3. It is based on classical Ragas and Talas of devotional songs composed by Tulsidas, Kabir and Mirabai.
Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Answer: (a) 1 and 2 only. Sattriya is indeed a combination of music, dance and drama and a living Vaishnavite tradition of Assam (created by Sankaradeva). Statement 3 is wrong — it is based on Sankaradeva's own devotional compositions (Borgeet), not the songs of Tulsidas, Kabir or Mirabai.

Concept practice

Q: Consider the following pairs of classical dance and state:
1. Odissi — Odisha
2. Mohiniyattam — Karnataka
3. Kuchipudi — Andhra Pradesh
Which of the pairs are correctly matched?

  • (a) 1 and 3 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 2 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Answer: (a). Odissi–Odisha and Kuchipudi–Andhra Pradesh are correct. Mohiniyattam belongs to Kerala, not Karnataka — so pair 2 is wrong.

Concept practice

Q: Which classical dance form is a male-dominated dance-drama known for its elaborate, mask-like facial make-up and towering costumes?

  • (a) Mohiniyattam
  • (b) Kathakali
  • (c) Manipuri
  • (d) Bharatanatyam

Answer: (b) Kathakali (Kerala) — a vigorous dance-drama with colour-coded make-up (green = noble). Mohiniyattam is the gentle solo-female Kerala form.

Concept practice

Q: In the vocabulary of the Natyashastra, the pure, decorative rhythmic movement that carries no specific meaning is called —

  • (a) Nritta
  • (b) Nritya
  • (c) Natya
  • (d) Abhinaya

Answer: (a) Nritta. Nritya adds expression/emotion, Natya adds drama/story, and Abhinaya is the art of expression itself.

Concept practice

Q: The Kathak dance form is best described as —

  • (a) A South Indian temple dance set to Carnatic music
  • (b) A North Indian form that evolved from temple storytelling into a Mughal court art
  • (c) A Kerala dance-drama based on the epics
  • (d) A Vaishnavite monastery tradition of Assam

Answer: (b). Kathak began with the kathakars (temple storytellers) of North India and was transformed in the Mughal/Nawabi courts — the classic syncretic form with gharanas (Lucknow, Jaipur).

Concept practice

Q: Which one of the following is a semi-classical mask dance of the Odisha–Jharkhand–Bengal belt, inscribed on the UNESCO Representative List of Intangible Cultural Heritage?

  • (a) Lavani
  • (b) Ghoomar
  • (c) Chhau
  • (d) Garba

Answer: (c) Chhau (Seraikella/Mayurbhanj/Purulia) — a martial mask dance on the UNESCO ICH list. Lavani = Maharashtra, Ghoomar = Rajasthan, Garba = Gujarat.

11. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

Model GS-I questions on Indian dance. Each carries a full skeleton — Introduction → body → Conclusion.

Model question — GS-I 15 marks · 250 words

Q: "Indian classical dance is codified devotion." Discuss with reference to the Natyashastra and the temple origins of the classical forms.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Indian classical dance rests on a single codifying text — the Natyashastra — and most of its forms began as temple worship, making it a disciplined, sacred art rather than mere entertainment.
  2. Codification: the Natyashastra fixes the grammar — Nritta, Nritya, Natya; Tandava–Lasya; abhinaya and the navarasa — that every classical form obeys, giving dance a scientific, teachable structure.
  3. Devotional origin (the core): Bharatanatyam and Odissi were temple (Devadasi/Mahari) arts; Kuchipudi and Sattriya arose from Bhagavata/Vaishnava dance-drama — the dancer's body was an offering to the deity.
  4. Expression of theology: repertoires narrate Krishna's Raas Leela (Manipuri), the epics (Kathakali) and bhakti poetry — dance as visual scripture.
  5. Critical edge: the "codified devotion" ideal coexisted with the exploitation of Devadasis; 20th-century reform reclaimed the art while confronting that past.
  6. Conclusion: classical dance is devotion given precise form — a fusion of spiritual intent and rigorous grammar that makes it India's most complete performing art.
Model question — GS-I 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Kathak is often cited as the finest example of syncretism in the Indian performing arts. Examine.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Kathak is the only classical dance whose very history — from Hindu temple storytelling to Mughal court art — embodies India's composite culture.
  2. Hindu roots: it began with the kathakars narrating Radha–Krishna tales in temple courtyards, deeply devotional in origin.
  3. Persianisation (the core): under Mughal and Nawabi patronage it moved indoors, adopting Persian costume, abstract footwork, spins and a court aesthetic — a genuine fusion, not mere borrowing.
  4. Shared practice: Hindu and Muslim gharanas (Lucknow, Jaipur) and masters (Birju Maharaj) carry it together, and its themes span both bhakti and courtly love.
  5. Critical edge: the syncretism is real yet vulnerable to communal reinterpretation and commercialisation; safeguarding it means protecting its plural memory.
  6. Conclusion: Kathak dances India's Ganga–Jamuni tehzeeb — proof that the country's cultural masterpieces are collaborative achievements.
Model question — GS-I 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Distinguish between the classical and folk dance traditions of India, bringing out the significance of each.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Indian dance runs on two tracks — the codified, individual classical forms and the communal, participatory folk traditions — together mapping the nation's cultural range.
  2. Classical: governed by the Natyashastra, learnt through the guru–shishya system, largely solo and devotional (the eight recognised forms) — a refined, elite art.
  3. Folk (the core): unwritten and communal, tied to harvest and festival (Bhangra, Garba, Bihu), open to all — a democratic, grassroots expression of regional identity.
  4. Overlap: semi-classical forms like Chhau (UNESCO ICH) bridge the two, showing the categories are a spectrum.
  5. Significance: classical dance preserves codified heritage and cultural prestige; folk dance preserves community memory and inclusivity — both need documentation and support.
  6. Conclusion: the two traditions are complementary faces of one dance culture — the temple stage and the village ground — and preserving both preserves India's plural memory.
Model question — GS-I 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Assess the role of 20th-century reformers and institutions in reviving India's classical dance forms.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Several classical dances were near extinction in the early 20th century under colonial "anti-nautch" stigma; reformers and new institutions rescued and re-dignified them.
  2. Rukmini Devi Arundale: founded Kalakshetra and reframed Bharatanatyam as respectable classical art, decoupling it from the Devadasi stigma.
  3. Vallathol Narayana Menon (the core): established Kerala Kalamandalam, reviving Kathakali and Mohiniyattam through formal training.
  4. State support: the Sangeet Natak Akademi (1953) codified the canon, gave awards and institutionalised patronage; Sattriya's 2000 recognition extended it.
  5. Critical edge: revival sometimes "sanitised" and elitised the forms, and the underlying gender/caste history remains contested.
  6. Conclusion: reformers and institutions turned dying temple arts into a living national heritage — a model of cultural revival, though not without its tensions.
