Topic 2: Indian Sculpture

From the ~4,500-year-old bronze Dancing Girl of Mohenjo-daro to the cosmic Nataraja cast by Chola guilds, Indian sculpture is a continuous, richly tested tradition. This book-depth file covers materials & techniques, Harappan and Mauryan sculpture, the three great post-Mauryan schools — Gandhara, Mathura and Amaravati, the Gupta classical style (Sarnath Buddha), Buddha iconography & mudras, and the bronze (lost-wax) tradition from Chola to Pala — with labelled diagrams, comparison tables and genuine dated PYQs with model answers.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames Sculpture

Sculpture is tested overwhelmingly as school-discrimination (which style?) and iconography (read the gesture/symbol). The single most productive skill is telling Gandhara from Mathura from Amaravati at a glance. Sort your prep:

  • Static/terminology — the mudras (abhaya, dhyana, bhumisparsha, dharmachakra, varada), lakshanas (ushnisha, urna, elongated ears), lost-wax steps, material–school pairs. High recall.
  • Stylistic discrimination — Gandhara (Greco-Roman realism, grey schist, spiritual calm) vs Mathura (indigenous, red sandstone, energetic) vs Amaravati (white marble, narrative, dynamic). Feature-to-school mapping.
  • Analytical (GS-I) — cross-cultural influence (Greco-Bactrian in Gandhara), the Gupta "classical synthesis", bronze as technological achievement, sculpture as embodiment of philosophy. Needs idea + example + judgement.

Note: India's sculptural heritage spans every region — from Gandhara (in undivided India's north-west) to the Tamil bronzes of the south; recovered antiquities are protected under the Antiquities & Art Treasures Act 1972, and their repatriation is a live matter of cultural sovereignty.

1. Sculpture — Materials & Overview

Sculpture in India runs unbroken for over four millennia, and the material is often the first clue to date and school. The examiner rewards a candidate who can move from "what is it made of" to "which age and school".

Framing line for GS-I: Indian sculpture evolves from the naturalism of the Indus and early folk art, through the experiments of the three regional schools, to the idealised classical calm of the Gupta age — the aesthetic that then radiated across Asia.

2. Harappan Sculpture

Despite the absence of monumental temples or royal statues, the Harappans left small sculptures of astonishing sophistication — naturalistic, confident and technically advanced.

  • Bronze "Dancing Girl" (Mohenjo-daro): a ~10.5 cm lost-wax bronze of a naked girl in a relaxed, self-assured tribhanga-like pose with bangles — proof of mastery of metal casting c. 2500 BCE.
  • "Priest-King" (Mohenjo-daro): a steatite bust with a trefoil-patterned robe, half-closed meditative eyes and a fillet — the finest Harappan stone figure.
  • Male torso (Harappa): a polished red-sandstone torso of remarkable anatomical naturalism.
  • Terracotta & seals: mother-goddess figurines, toy carts, and the famous steatite seals (the Pashupati/proto-Shiva seal, the humped bull, the unicorn) — miniature relief carving of great precision.
Prelims trap: the Dancing Girl is bronze (lost-wax), not stone; the Priest-King is steatite. The Harappans mastered lost-wax casting ~2,000 years before the Cholas famous for it.

3. Mauryan Sculpture

Mauryan sculpture divides neatly into court art (royal, polished, imperial) and popular art (folk, unpolished, by local guilds) — a distinction the examiner likes.

GS-I hook: the Didarganj Yakshi — a life-size, sensuously modelled female chauri-bearer with the mirror Mauryan polish — shows folk (yakshi) subject matter meeting court-level finish, bridging the two streams.

4. The Three Schools: Gandhara, Mathura, Amaravati

In the post-Mauryan centuries (c. 1st–3rd CE), three regional schools independently created the first anthropomorphic images of the Buddha and defined Indian sculpture. Discriminating between them is the highest-yield sculpture skill.

Fig 2.1 — The diagnostic contrasts: Gandhara (Hellenistic realism, grey schist), Mathura (indigenous vigour, red sandstone), Amaravati (narrative dynamism, white marble).

Prelims lock: grey schist + Greek realism = Gandhara; red sandstone + indigenous vigour + Hindu/Jain images = Mathura; white marble + crowded narrative panels = Amaravati. All three first show the Buddha in human form (end of aniconism).

5. Gupta Classical Sculpture

The Gupta age (4th–6th CE) achieves a classical synthesis — it absorbs Gandhara's technique and Mathura's spiritual warmth into a serene, idealised, restrained ideal that becomes the "golden mean" of Indian art.

  • The Sarnath Buddha (seated, first sermon, dharmachakra mudra): downcast meditative eyes, a plain "wet" transparent robe (no folds), the halo richly carved — the definitive classical image.
  • Refinements: restrained ornament, spiritual inwardness, perfect proportion; the schools of Sarnath and Mathura lead.
  • Beyond the Buddha: the great Hindu reliefs — Vishnu Anantasayana (Deogarh), Varaha (Udayagiri) — monumental narrative sculpture in stone.
GS-I hook: the Gupta Buddha is India's "classical moment" — where technical mastery and spiritual serenity meet; this idealised calm, not Gandhara's realism, became the template exported to South-East and East Asia.

6. Buddha Iconography & Mudras

Reading a Buddha image — its mudra (hand gesture) and lakshanas (auspicious marks) — is a recurring, high-scoring prelims skill.

MudraGestureMeaning / event
DhyanaBoth hands in lap, palms upMeditation
BhumisparshaRight hand touching the earthCalling the earth to witness — the moment of enlightenment (defeat of Mara)
DharmachakraBoth hands at chest, "turning the wheel"First sermon at Sarnath
AbhayaRight hand raised, palm outFearlessness, protection
VaradaRight hand down, palm outCharity, boon-giving
  • Lakshanas (marks): ushnisha (cranial bump = wisdom), urna (forehead dot/whorl), elongated earlobes, webbed fingers, the dharmachakra on palms/soles.
Prelims lock: earth-touching = Bhumisparsha (enlightenment); wheel-turning at chest = Dharmachakra (first sermon); lap-meditation = Dhyana; raised palm = Abhaya (fearlessness).

7. Bronze Sculpture & the Lost-Wax Technique

India's bronze tradition — from Harappa to the Cholas — uses the lost-wax (cire perdue / madhuchhishta vidhana) method, and reaches its artistic summit in the Chola Nataraja.

Fig 2.2 — The solid-cast lost-wax process: each bronze is unique because the clay mould must be broken to release it.
  • Chola Nataraja: Shiva as Lord of the Dance in the Ananda Tandava within a ring of fire (prabhamandala) — the drum (creation), fire (destruction), abhaya hand (protection), the dwarf Apasmara (ignorance) crushed underfoot: the cosmic cycle in bronze.
  • Other Chola bronzes: Somaskanda, Ardhanarishvara, the graceful tribhanga Parvati/Uma figures — a school of unmatched refinement.
  • Pala bronzes (Bengal-Bihar): black-metal Buddhist images (Nalanda/Kurkihar) that shaped Tibetan and SE-Asian art.
GS-I hook: the Nataraja is the perfect fusion of science, art and philosophy — precise metallurgy serving a profound cosmology; a version stands at CERN, Geneva, as a symbol of cosmic dance and modern physics.

8. Medieval & Regional Sculpture

Medieval sculpture is increasingly architectural — integrated into temple walls — and regionally distinct, from the sensuous figures of Khajuraho to the jewel-like Hoysala carving.

10. Prelims PYQs

Genuine dated UPSC questions plus concept-practice MCQs on sculpture. Each carries a worked rationale.

UPSC Prelims 2014

Q: Lord Buddha's image is sometimes shown with the hand gesture called 'Bhumisparsha Mudra'. It symbolizes —

  • (a) Buddha's calling of the Earth to witness the truth of his words at the moment of enlightenment
  • (b) Buddha's calling of the Earth to witness his purity and chastity despite the temptations of Mara
  • (c) Buddha's reminder to his followers that they all arise from the Earth and finally dissolve into it
  • (d) Both (a) and (b)

Answer: (a). Bhumisparsha ("earth-touching") marks the moment of enlightenment — the Buddha calls the earth-goddess to witness the truth of his words against Mara.

Concept practice

Q: Buddha images in bluish-grey schist, with wavy hair, muscular bodies and toga-like drapery, belong to which school?

  • (a) Gandhara
  • (b) Mathura
  • (c) Amaravati
  • (d) Sarnath (Gupta)

Answer: (a) Gandhara — grey schist + Greco-Roman realism (Hellenistic drapery, wavy hair) is the signature of the NW frontier school under the Kushanas.

Concept practice

Q: The "Dancing Girl" of Mohenjo-daro is made of —

  • (a) steatite
  • (b) red sandstone
  • (c) bronze (lost-wax casting)
  • (d) terracotta

Answer: (c) bronze, cast by the lost-wax method — evidence that Harappans mastered metal casting c. 2500 BCE. (The "Priest-King" is steatite.)

Concept practice

Q: Which school of sculpture, using red spotted sandstone, produced not only Buddha images but also early Hindu and Jain images?

  • (a) Gandhara
  • (b) Mathura
  • (c) Amaravati
  • (d) Pala

Answer: (b) Mathura — the indigenous school in red spotted sandstone carved Buddhist, Hindu (e.g. early Vishnu) and Jain (tirthankara) images alike.

Concept practice

Q: In the Chola Nataraja bronze, the figure of the dwarf trampled underfoot (Apasmara) symbolizes —

  • (a) creation
  • (b) ignorance / illusion being conquered
  • (c) the river Ganga
  • (d) the demon Mara

Answer: (b). Apasmara (Muyalaka) is ignorance/forgetfulness crushed by Shiva's dancing foot; the drum = creation, fire = destruction, abhaya = protection.

Concept practice

Q: The Gupta-period Buddha at Sarnath is best identified by —

  • (a) heavy toga-like folds and a muscular body
  • (b) a plain transparent "wet" robe, downcast eyes and a richly carved halo
  • (c) white marble narrative panels
  • (d) a bronze ring of fire

Answer: (b). The classical Gupta (Sarnath) Buddha has a foldless "wet" robe, serene downcast eyes and an ornate halo — idealised spiritual calm.

11. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

Genuine dated UPSC GS-I questions plus model questions on sculpture. Each carries a full skeleton — Introduction → body → Conclusion.

UPSC Mains GS-I 2019 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Highlight the Central Asian and Greco-Bactrian elements in the Gandhara art.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Gandhara art (1st–5th CE), flourishing under the Kushanas on the NW trade routes, was the meeting ground of Indian Buddhist content and Hellenistic-Central Asian form.
  2. Greco-Bactrian (Hellenistic) elements: the realistic, muscular anatomy; wavy, Apollo-like hair; toga-like drapery with deep, naturalistic folds; the Buddha modelled on the Greek god-figure; acanthus and Corinthian pilasters.
  3. Central Asian elements: Kushana patronage and iconography, use of stucco alongside schist, and motifs transmitted along the Silk Road (winged figures, garland-bearers).
  4. Indian core (the balance): the subject remained wholly Indian and Buddhist — Jataka scenes, the lakshanas (ushnisha, urna), mudras; the foreign form served Indian meaning.
  5. Significance (critical edge): Gandhara gave the world one of its first anthropomorphic Buddhas and carried the image across Central Asia to China — a two-way cultural highway, not mere borrowing.
  6. Conclusion: Gandhara art is the classic case of syncretism — Hellenistic-Central Asian technique fused with Indian spiritual content into a distinctive, exportable style.
Model question — GS-I 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Compare and contrast the Gandhara and Mathura schools of sculpture, and assess why the Gupta age is called a classical synthesis.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Gandhara and Mathura, both Kushana-era, independently created the first Buddha images but from opposite aesthetic roots — foreign realism vs indigenous vigour.
  2. Gandhara: grey schist, Greco-Roman realism, spiritual restraint, Buddhist-only, foreign influence.
  3. Mathura: red spotted sandstone, robust indigenous energy derived from the yaksha, a smiling warmth, and a wider repertoire including Hindu and Jain images.
  4. Points of contrast (the core): material, influence, mood (ascetic calm vs earthy vitality), thematic range — two answers to the same problem of depicting the divine.
  5. Gupta synthesis (the resolution): the Sarnath Buddha fuses Gandhara's technical finish with Mathura's spiritual warmth into an idealised, foldless-robe serenity — balanced, restrained, "classical".
  6. Conclusion: the Gupta ideal, not either regional extreme, became the pan-Asian template — the reason the age is called India's classical high point.
Model question — GS-I 10 marks · 150 words

Q: The Chola bronze Nataraja is a supreme example of the union of art, technology and philosophy. Discuss.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: The Chola Nataraja (10th–12th CE) is India's most celebrated bronze — a work in which metallurgy, aesthetics and metaphysics are inseparable.
  2. Technology: the solid-cast lost-wax method demanded exact wax-carving, controlled alloying and single-pour precision — a high metallurgical achievement.
  3. Art: perfect balance and rhythm in the Ananda Tandava, the flying jata, the ring of fire — dynamic yet serene.
  4. Philosophy (the core): the drum (creation), fire (destruction), abhaya hand (protection/preservation), raised foot (release) and trampled Apasmara (ignorance) encode the cosmic cycle — the universe as Shiva's dance.
  5. Legacy (critical edge): its placement at CERN shows how a medieval religious icon speaks to modern cosmology — art as a bridge across a millennium.
  6. Conclusion: the Nataraja proves that in Indian art technique was never an end in itself but a vehicle for profound philosophical meaning.
Model question — GS-I 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Trace the evolution of Indian sculpture from the Harappan to the Gupta age, noting continuities and breaks.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Indian sculpture evolves over ~3,000 years from Harappan naturalism to Gupta idealism, with recurring themes and decisive innovations along the way.
  2. Harappan: small-scale naturalism — Dancing Girl (bronze), Priest-King (steatite), terracotta — then a long gap.
  3. Mauryan: court art (polished pillar capitals) vs popular folk art (yaksha/yakshi) — the Mauryan polish.
  4. Post-Mauryan schools (the break): Gandhara, Mathura, Amaravati create the anthropomorphic Buddha — the end of aniconism.
  5. Gupta (the synthesis): idealised, serene classical images (Sarnath) that balance realism and spirituality.
  6. Continuities & conclusion: the yaksha vitality, love of the human body, and lost-wax bronze recur throughout; the arc runs from naturalism, through experiment, to classical calm — a tradition of remarkable continuity.
