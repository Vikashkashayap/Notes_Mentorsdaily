Topic 24: Awards & Recognition

India honours excellence through a tiered system — the civilian awards (Bharat Ratna, Padma Vibhushan/Bhushan/Shri), gallantry awards (Param Vir Chakra and its peers), and a rich set of cultural/arts honours (the three Akademi awards, the Dadasaheb Phalke Award, the Jnanpith). The exam tests this as award–field–rank matching (which is the highest civilian award? which is for literature?) and occasionally as GS-I discussion of recognition and merit. This book-depth file maps the whole honours system — ranks, fields, founding years and famous firsts — with tables and PYQs + model answers.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames Awards

Awards are tested as award–field–rank matching ("The highest civilian award is?", "Jnanpith is for which field?"). The productive skill is a grid keyed by award, its rank/order, its field, its instituting year and the conferring body. Sort your prep:

  • Static/factual — the order of civilian awards (Bharat Ratna > Padma Vibhushan > Padma Bhushan > Padma Shri), the gallantry order (Param Vir Chakra highest wartime), and the field of each cultural prize (Jnanpith = literature; Phalke = cinema). High recall.
  • Thematic/structural — grouping: civilian → gallantry (wartime vs peacetime) → arts/Akademi → literary → international. Structure and hierarchy.
  • Analytical (GS-I) — the purpose and limits of a national honours system, controversies (politicisation, refusals), and recognition as nation-building. Needs idea + example + judgement.

Note: Two orders to lock down — civilian: Bharat Ratna (highest) > Padma Vibhushan > Padma Bhushan > Padma Shri; gallantry (wartime): Param Vir Chakra > Maha Vir Chakra > Vir Chakra, and their peacetime peers Ashoka Chakra > Kirti Chakra > Shaurya Chakra.

1. Overview & Hierarchy

A national honours system recognises exceptional service, valour and achievement, and signals the values a nation prizes. India's awards fall into clear families — civilian, military/gallantry, and specialised arts and literary honours — each with its own hierarchy and conferring authority. Getting the order within each family right answers most MCQs.

  • Conferred by: civilian and gallantry awards are conferred by the President of India (announced on Republic Day/Independence Day); arts awards by the respective Akademis; literary prizes by trusts/foundations.
  • Not a "title": Padma/Bharat Ratna cannot be used as a prefix/suffix to the name (Article 18 abolishes titles) — a favourite MCQ.
  • Family-first memory: fix the hierarchy inside each family, then attach names/firsts.
One-line frame: civilian honours reward service/achievement, gallantry honours reward valour (wartime vs peacetime), and cultural honours reward the arts and letters — each with a strict internal order.

2. Civilian Awards

The civilian awards are the apex of national recognition for distinguished service in any field. Their hierarchy, instituting year and a few landmark firsts are the most tested facts in the topic.

  • Bharat Ratna: the highest civilian award (instituted 1954), for exceptional service/performance of the highest order in any field of human endeavour. Not restricted to Indian citizens (e.g. Mother Teresa, Nelson Mandela — honorary). No monetary grant; a maximum of usually 3 in a year.
  • Padma Awards (1954): in three descending grades — Padma Vibhushan (exceptional and distinguished service), Padma Bhushan (distinguished service of high order), Padma Shri (distinguished service). Across all fields including art, social work, science, sports, trade.
  • First Bharat Ratna (1954): C. Rajagopalachari, S. Radhakrishnan and C. V. Raman.
  • Youngest/notable: Sachin Tendulkar was the first sportsperson to receive the Bharat Ratna (2014).
  • Article 18 caveat: these are awards, not hereditary titles; misuse as a name-prefix can lead to forfeiture.

3. Gallantry & Service Awards

Military honours reward valour, and split into wartime and peacetime series — a distinction the exam loves to test. Learn the two parallel ladders and the top award of each.

  • Wartime gallantry (in the face of the enemy): Param Vir Chakra (PVC) — highest > Maha Vir Chakra (MVC) > Vir Chakra (VrC).
  • Peacetime gallantry (away from the battlefield): Ashoka Chakra — highest > Kirti Chakra > Shaurya Chakra.
  • First PVC: Major Somnath Sharma (1947 J&K operations, posthumous).
  • Distinguished service (non-combat): Param Vishisht Seva Medal > Ati Vishisht Seva Medal > Vishisht Seva Medal.
  • Note: both series were instituted in 1950 (with Independence-era origins); all conferred by the President.

Trap-buster: Param Vir Chakra = highest wartime; Ashoka Chakra = highest peacetime gallantry. Don't confuse Ashoka Chakra (the award) with the Ashoka Chakra on the national flag.

4. National Arts & Akademi Awards

The arts have their own national honours, conferred by the three Akademis and the film establishment. These are the culturally specific awards most likely to appear in an Art & Culture paper.

  • Sangeet Natak Akademi Award & Fellowship: the highest national recognition for performing artists (music, dance, drama); the Fellowship (Ratna Sadsya) is the most prestigious, limited in number.
  • Sahitya Akademi Award & Fellowship: the top literary honour by the national academy of letters, given in 24 languages annually; the Sahitya Akademi Fellowship is its highest honour.
  • Lalit Kala Akademi Award: for visual/fine arts (painting, sculpture, graphics), including the National Exhibition awards.
  • Dadasaheb Phalke Award (1969): the highest award for lifetime contribution to Indian cinema; first winner Devika Rani. (Distinct from the annual National Film Awards.)
  • Tansen Samman, Kalidas Samman: state honours (Madhya Pradesh) for classical music and the classical arts, respectively.
GS-I hook: the Akademi Fellowships (limited, lifelong) rank above the annual Akademi awards — the state's way of conferring the highest, most enduring recognition on living masters of the arts.

5. Literary & Other Honours

Beyond the Akademis, private trusts confer India's most prestigious literary prizes. Learn the field, the founder/trust and any language restriction — the Jnanpith is the most frequently tested.

  • Jnanpith Award (1961/first awarded 1965): India's highest literary award, by the Bharatiya Jnanpith trust, for outstanding contribution to literature in any of the recognised Indian languages (including English since 2013). First winner: G. Sankara Kurup (Malayalam).
  • Vyas Samman & Saraswati Samman: literary awards by the K. K. Birla Foundation — Saraswati Samman for a work in any of the 22 scheduled languages; Vyas Samman for Hindi literature.
  • Moortidevi Award: another Bharatiya Jnanpith literary award.
  • Vachaspati / Bihari Samman: K. K. Birla Foundation awards for Sanskrit and Rajasthani/Hindi respectively.
  • Gandhi Peace Prize: an international award by the Government of India for social, economic and political transformation through non-violence.
Prelims cluster: Jnanpith = highest literary award (Bharatiya Jnanpith); Saraswati Samman & Vyas Samman = K. K. Birla Foundation. Match the prize to its trust and field.

6. Major International Awards

Several global honours won by Indians recur in Prelims. Learn the field of each and a few Indian laureates — especially the "Asian Nobel" Magsaysay and the Indian Nobel laureates.

  • Nobel Prize — Indian/Indian-origin laureates: Rabindranath Tagore (Literature, 1913 — first Asian), C. V. Raman (Physics, 1930), Mother Teresa (Peace, 1979), Amartya Sen (Economics, 1998), Kailash Satyarthi (Peace, 2014); Indian-origin: Har Gobind Khorana, S. Chandrasekhar, V. Ramakrishnan, Abhijit Banerjee.
  • Ramon Magsaysay Award ("Asia's Nobel"): for outstanding public/community service in Asia — Indian awardees include Vinoba Bhave (first, 1958), Jayaprakash Narayan, M. S. Subbulakshmi, Kiran Bedi, Arvind Kejriwal, Sandeep Pandey, Ravish Kumar.
  • Right Livelihood, Templeton, Booker: the Booker Prize for fiction (Arundhati Roy 1997, Aravind Adiga 2008; Geetanjali Shree's Tomb of Sand won the International Booker 2022).
  • Oscar (honorary): Satyajit Ray (1992); Bhanu Athaiya (costume, 1983) and A. R. Rahman (music, 2009) among competitive Indian Oscar winners.
GS-I hook: Tagore (1913, first Asian Nobel laureate) and Ray's honorary Oscar (1992) are recurring soft-power markers — useful to show India's cultural standing on the world stage.

8. Significance & Debates

A national honours system does more than reward individuals — it publicly defines a nation's values and role-models. But it also invites controversy over selection, politicisation and refusals, making it a live GS-I/ethics theme.

  • Nation-building: awards recognise excellence across fields and hold up exemplars — artists, scientists, soldiers, social workers — as national role-models.
  • Inclusion & grassroots recognition: recent Padma awards to unsung grassroots achievers ("People's Padma") broadened recognition beyond elites — a positive shift.
  • Soft power & morale: gallantry awards sustain military morale; cultural awards affirm the value of the arts.
  • Controversies (critical): allegations of political favouritism, lobbying, posthumous timing, and high-profile refusals/returns (e.g. writers returning Sahitya Akademi awards in protest) periodically dent credibility.
  • Constitutional debate: the Padma awards were once challenged as "titles" barred by Article 18; the Supreme Court upheld them as awards, not titles, provided they are not used as name-affixes.
Essay/GS-I frame: a national honours system is a mirror of a nation's values — its credibility rests on transparent, merit-based, apolitical selection; the goal is to celebrate excellence without turning recognition into patronage.

9. Current Affairs & Firsts

Awards generate steady current affairs each Republic Day (Padma/Bharat Ratna) and awards season (Jnanpith, Magsaysay, Nobel). Keep a running list of recent laureates and notable firsts.

  • Republic Day Padma list: announced annually; watch for grassroots ("People's Padma") awardees and posthumous honours.
  • Bharat Ratna: conferred periodically (not every year); recent honorees have included figures from politics, agriculture and public life.
  • Notable firsts to remember: Tagore (first Asian Nobel, 1913), Sachin Tendulkar (first sportsperson Bharat Ratna, 2014), Geetanjali Shree (first Hindi International Booker, Tomb of Sand, 2022).
  • Sahitya/Jnanpith: annual literary announcements — note the language and author.
  • Digital transparency: the Padma awards now use an open online nomination portal to widen and de-elitise the pool.
How to use in the exam: pair a fixed fact (award rank/field/first winner) with a recent laureate to show both static mastery and current awareness — and keep the "firsts" list ready for MCQs.

10. Prelims Practice Questions

Concept-practice MCQs modelled on the UPSC Prelims pattern for this topic (award ranks, fields, firsts and conferring bodies). Attempt before revealing the answer.

Q1. Which of the following is the highest civilian award of India?

  • a) Padma Vibhushan
  • b) Bharat Ratna
  • c) Padma Bhushan
  • d) Param Vir Chakra

Answer: b) Bharat Ratna — the highest civilian award (1954), above Padma Vibhushan/Bhushan/Shri; Param Vir Chakra is the highest wartime gallantry award.

Q2. Consider the following pairs of award and its field:

1. Dadasaheb Phalke Award — Cinema
2. Jnanpith Award — Literature
3. Param Vir Chakra — Peacetime gallantry

Which of the pairs given above are correctly matched?

  • a) 1 and 2 only
  • b) 2 and 3 only
  • c) 1 and 3 only
  • d) 1, 2 and 3

Answer: a) 1 and 2 only — Param Vir Chakra is the highest wartime gallantry award; the highest peacetime gallantry award is the Ashoka Chakra, so pair 3 is wrong.

Q3. With reference to the Bharat Ratna, consider the following statements:

1. It can be awarded to a person who is not an Indian citizen.
2. It carries a monetary grant along with the medallion.
3. It can be used as a prefix or suffix to the recipient's name.

Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

  • a) 1 only
  • b) 1 and 2 only
  • c) 2 and 3 only
  • d) 1, 2 and 3

Answer: a) 1 only — the Bharat Ratna can be conferred on non-citizens (e.g. Mother Teresa), but it carries no monetary grant and cannot be used as a title/name-affix (Article 18). So only statement 1 is correct.

Q4. The highest gallantry award given for valour other than in the face of the enemy (peacetime) is the:

  • a) Param Vir Chakra
  • b) Ashoka Chakra
  • c) Kirti Chakra
  • d) Maha Vir Chakra

Answer: b) Ashoka Chakra — the highest peacetime gallantry award (above Kirti Chakra and Shaurya Chakra); the Param Vir Chakra is its wartime equivalent.

Q5. The Jnanpith Award is conferred for outstanding contribution to:

  • a) Cinema
  • b) Classical music
  • c) Literature
  • d) Science

Answer: c) Literature — India's highest literary award, given by the Bharatiya Jnanpith trust; first awarded to G. Sankara Kurup (Malayalam).

Q6. Which of the following was the first Indian to receive the Ramon Magsaysay Award?

  • a) Jayaprakash Narayan
  • b) Vinoba Bhave
  • c) Mother Teresa
  • d) Kiran Bedi

Answer: b) Vinoba Bhave — he was the first Indian recipient of the Ramon Magsaysay Award (1958), often called "Asia's Nobel Prize."

11. Mains Practice Questions + Model Answers

GS-I model questions on awards and recognition, each with a 6-point analytical skeleton (intro → four substantive points including the core and a critical edge → conclusion).

Model Answer 1

Q. "A national honours system reflects a nation's values." Examine the purpose and criticisms of India's awards system. (250 words)

  • Intro: India's honours — civilian, gallantry and cultural — publicly recognise excellence and, in doing so, define the values and role-models the nation prizes.
  • Purpose: the Bharat Ratna and Padma awards reward achievement across all fields; gallantry awards honour valour and sustain military morale; Akademi and literary awards affirm the worth of the arts.
  • Democratisation (the core): the recent "People's Padma" trend — honouring unsung grassroots achievers via an open nomination portal — has broadened recognition beyond elites, aligning honours with an inclusive national ethos.
  • Nation-building: by celebrating diverse exemplars, awards foster shared pride and aspiration.
  • Criticisms (critical): allegations of political favouritism, lobbying, posthumous timing and high-profile award-returns in protest periodically dent credibility.
  • Conclusion: The system's value depends on transparent, merit-based, apolitical selection — recognition must celebrate excellence, not dispense patronage.
Model Answer 2

Q. Discuss the role of arts and literary awards in preserving and promoting India's cultural heritage. (250 words)

  • Intro: Beyond the civilian honours, a dedicated set of arts and literary awards sustains India's living cultural traditions and their practitioners.
  • Performing & visual arts: the Sangeet Natak, Sahitya and Lalit Kala Akademi awards and Fellowships recognise masters of music, dance, drama, literature and the visual arts, encouraging continuity.
  • Cinema & literature (the core): the Dadasaheb Phalke Award honours lifetime cinematic contribution, while the Jnanpith and Saraswati/Vyas Samman celebrate literature across India's many languages — validating linguistic diversity as national wealth.
  • Livelihood & morale: recognition (and its stipends/fellowships) supports artists economically and lifts the status of endangered art-forms.
  • Critical edge: awards reach only the visible few; grassroots folk and tribal artists remain under-recognised, and controversies can politicise even cultural prizes.
  • Conclusion: Arts and literary awards are vital instruments of cultural preservation — most effective when inclusive of regional, folk and tribal traditions.
Model Answer 3

Q. Distinguish between wartime and peacetime gallantry awards in India. (150 words)

  • Intro: India's gallantry awards recognise valour in two contexts — in the face of the enemy (wartime) and away from the battlefield (peacetime) — through two parallel ladders.
  • Wartime series: Param Vir Chakra (highest) > Maha Vir Chakra > Vir Chakra, for conspicuous bravery against an enemy.
  • Peacetime series (the core): Ashoka Chakra (highest) > Kirti Chakra > Shaurya Chakra, for courage in counter-insurgency, rescue and similar non-war situations.
  • Common features: both are conferred by the President and can be awarded posthumously.
  • Critical edge: the Ashoka Chakra (award) must not be confused with the Ashoka Chakra of the national flag.
  • Conclusion: The dual system honours valour comprehensively, in both war and peace.
Model Answer 4

Q. "Awards to unsung grassroots achievers have democratised the Padma honours." Comment. (150 words)

  • Intro: In recent years the Padma awards have increasingly recognised little-known achievers from villages and marginalised communities — the "People's Padma."
  • The shift: tribal healers, folk artists, organic farmers, teachers and social workers have been honoured alongside celebrities.
  • Enablers (the core): an open online nomination portal widened the pool beyond elite networks, making selection more participatory and transparent.
  • Significance: it affirms that service and excellence exist at the grassroots, inspiring ordinary citizens and dignifying folk traditions.
  • Critical edge: concerns remain about consistency, adequate vetting and whether the trend is sustained across fields.
  • Conclusion: Recognising grassroots achievers is a welcome democratisation of national honours — to be institutionalised with transparent criteria.
Note: These are model questions in the UPSC GS-I pattern with full answers; they are not reproductions of dated past-year papers.
