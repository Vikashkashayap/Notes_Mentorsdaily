Topic 4A: Indian Paintings — Prehistoric to Medieval

From the ochre bison of Bhimbetka, painted 10,000 years ago, to the jewel-like Bani Thani of Kishangarh, Indian painting is a story of two great streams — the monumental mural and the intimate miniature. This book-depth file covers prehistoric rock art, the mural tradition (Ajanta, Bagh, Sittanavasal, Badami, Lepakshi), the miniature schools (Pala, Western Indian/Jain), Mughal painting, and the Rajput, Pahari and Deccan schools — with labelled diagrams, comparison tables and PYQs with model answers.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames Painting

Painting is tested as school-discrimination (which style/site?) and technique/theme recognition (fresco vs tempera; Krishna-bhakti vs court portraiture). The productive skill is placing a described image in its school at a glance. Sort your prep:

  • Static/terminology — Shadanga (six limbs of painting), fresco-secco vs true fresco, site–dynasty pairs (Ajanta–Vakataka, Badami–Chalukya, Lepakshi–Vijayanagara), miniature-school markers (Jain "protruding eye", Kishangarh "Bani Thani"). High recall.
  • Stylistic discrimination — mural vs miniature; Mughal (naturalism, portraiture, muted realism) vs Rajput (flat colour, bhakti themes, symbolic) vs Pahari (lyrical, delicate). Feature-to-school mapping.
  • Analytical (GS-I) — Ajanta as the fountainhead; Mughal painting as Indo-Persian synthesis; Rajput/Pahari as vehicles of bhakti; patronage shaping style. Needs idea + example + judgement.

Note: India's painting heritage spans the whole subcontinent — from the Pahari valleys of the northern hills and Kashmir to the Deccan sultanates and the Tamil south; recovered manuscripts and murals are protected under the Antiquities & Art Treasures Act 1972.

1. Overview & the Two Streams

Indian painting divides into two great traditions defined by scale and support: the monumental mural (on walls of caves and temples) and the intimate miniature (on palm-leaf, paper, cloth). Reading any painting question well means first placing it in one of these streams, then in a school.

  • Mural — large, on prepared wall surfaces; narrative and religious; Ajanta is the fountainhead.
  • Miniature — small, on portable supports; began on palm-leaf (Pala, Jain) and flowered on paper under the Mughals and Rajput courts.
  • The Shadanga (six limbs) is the classical Indian canon of what makes a good painting — a favourite one-mark fact.
GS-I hook: Painting tracks patronage — monastic (Ajanta), imperial (Mughal), and princely/devotional (Rajput-Pahari); each patron reshaped subject, scale and style.

2. Prehistoric Rock Art (Bhimbetka)

The oldest Indian paintings are the rock shelters of Bhimbetka (Madhya Pradesh, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, 2003), where hunter-gatherers painted from the Upper Palaeolithic into historical times — a continuous record of prehistoric life.

PeriodColours / StyleThemes
Upper PalaeolithicGreen & dark red; large linear figuresBison, tigers, rhinos — big game
MesolithicSmaller figures; most numerousHunting scenes, group dances, animals
ChalcolithicContact with settled farmersFarming, pottery motifs
Early Historic / laterRed, white, yellowRiders, religious symbols, scripts
  • Pigments were natural mineral ochres — red (haematite), green (from a green stone), white (limestone) — mixed with binders; the durability comes from mineral colour on quartzite.
  • Other sites: Jogimara caves (Chhattisgarh, some of the earliest), and rock art across the Vindhyas — but Bhimbetka is the exam anchor.
Prelims trap: Bhimbetka = Madhya Pradesh, UNESCO 2003; the most numerous paintings are Mesolithic. The largest (big-game) figures are the earliest (Upper Palaeolithic).

3. The Mural Tradition & Ajanta

The mural tradition reaches its summit at Ajanta (Maharashtra, UNESCO), a set of 30 Buddhist rock-cut caves whose paintings (c. 2nd BCE–6th CE, mainly under the Vakatakas) are the fountainhead of Indian painting — the source that later murals and even miniatures echo.

secco/tempera (paint on dry ground), not true (buon) fresco
  • Technique: pigment applied to a dry lime-plaster ground (fresco-secco / tempera), not wet-plaster "true fresco" — a very common prelims discriminator.
  • Themes: the Jataka tales (previous lives of the Buddha), the life of the Buddha, and a rich secular world of courts, processions and daily life.
  • Famous panels: the Padmapani (Bodhisattva with a lotus) and Vajrapani bodhisattvas (Cave 1); the "Dying Princess"; Mara's temptation. Note the "Indian bend" (tribhanga) and expressive hand-gestures.
  • Compared with Ellora: Ellora paintings are later and include Hindu and Jain themes; Ajanta is wholly Buddhist and earlier.
GS-I hook: Ajanta fuses spiritual serenity with worldly richness — the Bodhisattva's compassion and the court's opulence on one wall — the defining Indian mural aesthetic that radiates to Bagh, Sittanavasal and beyond.

4. Other Mural Sites

After Ajanta the mural impulse spread across regions and religions, each site adding a local accent. Knowing the site–dynasty–religion triad is the exam skill here.

SitePatron / PeriodNote
Bagh Caves (MP)Buddhist, c. 5th–6th CEMore worldly, secular; sister to Ajanta
Sittanavasal (TN)Jain, Pandya (over Pallava)Lotus-pond (samavasarana) ceiling; dancers
Badami (Karnataka)Chalukya, 6th CEEarliest surviving Hindu (& Jain) murals
Ellora (Kailasa)RashtrakutaHindu & Jain themes; later than Ajanta
Brihadisvara, ThanjavurChola, 11th CEShaiva murals rediscovered under Nayaka layer
Lepakshi (AP)Vijayanagara, 16th CEVirabhadra temple ceiling; linear, decorative
Kerala muralsLater medievalRich, saturated; Mattancherry, temple walls
  • The tradition is unbroken but the faith shifts — Buddhist (Ajanta, Bagh), Jain (Sittanavasal), and increasingly Hindu (Badami, Chola, Lepakshi) as royal patronage moves.
  • Style drift: the plastic, rounded Ajanta figure gives way over time to flatter, more linear and decorative forms (Lepakshi, Kerala) — anticipating the miniature line.
Prelims trap: Sittanavasal = Jain (Tamil Nadu); Badami = earliest Hindu murals (Chalukya); Lepakshi = Vijayanagara. Match site to religion and dynasty.

5. The Miniature Tradition (Pala & Jain)

The miniature — small-format painting on a portable support — begins in eastern India under the Palas (on palm-leaf) and in western India in the Jain manuscript tradition, long before the Mughals. These are the indigenous roots the Mughal atelier later transformed.

SchoolWhere / WhenSignature
PalaBengal-Bihar, 8th–12th CEBuddhist palm-leaf manuscripts; sinuous line; linked to Nalanda
Western Indian / Jain (Apabhramsa)Gujarat-Rajasthan, 11th–15th CEKalpasutra & Kalakacharya-katha; angular faces, the protruding "farther eye", wiry line, gold
  • Support shift: palm-leaf → paper (introduced by the 14th–15th CE) enabled larger, more detailed miniatures and the great courtly schools that followed.
  • The Jain "farther eye": faces in three-quarter profile show the far eye projecting beyond the cheek — the single most recognisable marker of the Western Indian school.
  • These traditions supplied the conventions (flat colour, profile faces, narrative registers) that Mughal naturalism later reacted against.
GS-I hook: The miniature was Indian before it was Mughal; the Mughal achievement was not to invent the form but to fuse indigenous manuscript painting with Persian refinement and European naturalism.

6. Mughal Painting

Mughal painting (16th–18th CE) is the classic Indo-Persian synthesis — Persian technique and refinement married to Indian subjects and, later, European naturalism. It is a court art: secular, historical, portrait-driven, and tied to the personality of each emperor.

EmperorContributionHallmark
Babur / HumayunBrought Persian masters (Mir Sayyid Ali, Abdus Samad)Seeds the atelier
AkbarFounded the imperial atelier; illustrated Hamzanama, AkbarnamaIndianisation; dynamic narrative; large workshops
Jahangir (peak)Naturalism, portraiture, flora & fauna (Ustad Mansur)Refined realism; connoisseur-emperor
Shah JahanFormal, jewel-like, static eleganceGold, precision, court splendour
Aurangzeb / laterDecline of patronage; artists disperseSpread to regional courts
  • Features: fine line and modelling; realistic portraiture and profile faces; muted, naturalistic colour; hierarchical court scenes; nature studies; the use of the halo around the emperor.
  • Peak under Jahangir — a discerning patron whose atelier produced meticulous natural-history studies and psychologically acute portraits.
  • Legacy: when patronage waned under Aurangzeb, trained artists carried Mughal naturalism into Rajput, Pahari and Deccan courts — cross-pollinating regional schools.
Prelims trap: Mughal painting peaks under Jahangir (naturalism, portraiture, Mansur's birds); Akbar Indianised and industrialised it; Shah Jahan made it formal and jewel-like.

7. Rajasthani & Pahari Painting

As Mughal patronage receded, the princely courts of Rajasthan and the Himalayan foothills (Pahari = "of the hills") developed distinctive schools. Where Mughal art was secular and realistic, these were largely devotional (Krishna-bhakti), lyrical and richly coloured.

SchoolRegionSignature
MewarRajasthanBold, vigorous; Ragamala, Ramayana series
KishangarhRajasthanThe Bani Thani — elongated eyes, arched brows; Radha-Krishna
Bundi / KotaRajasthanLush landscape, hunting scenes
BasohliPahari (Jammu hills)Bold, intense colour, beetle-wing green; vigorous
KangraPahari (Himachal)Lyrical, delicate, naturalistic; Gita Govinda, Krishna themes; peak under Sansar Chand
GulerPahariTransition from Basohli's boldness to Kangra's grace
  • Themes: Krishna-lila and the Gita Govinda, Ragamala (visualising musical modes), Baramasa (the twelve months), Nayaka-Nayika (hero-heroine) types, and epics — bhakti made visible.
  • Rajasthani vs Pahari: Rajasthani tends bolder, flatter, more vigorous; Pahari (esp. Kangra) is softer, more lyrical and naturalistic — the Mughal influence filtered through hill sensibility.
  • Bani Thani (Kishangarh, under Sawant Singh/"Nagari Das", artist Nihal Chand) is India's "Mona Lisa" — the exam's favourite single work.
GS-I hook: Rajput and Pahari painting turned the miniature into an instrument of bhakti — the god Krishna, not the emperor, becomes the hero; devotion, season and raga fuse into image.

8. Deccan Painting

The Deccan sultanates (Bijapur, Golconda, Ahmadnagar) developed a parallel court school that blended Persian, Turkish and indigenous Deccani elements — often richer in colour and more opulent than contemporary Mughal work.

  • Centres: Ahmadnagar (earliest), Bijapur (under Ibrahim Adil Shah II, a great patron), and Golconda — each with a distinct palette.
  • Features: lavish use of gold and rich colour, tall willowy figures, lush vegetation, a dreamy, opulent mood; independent of Mughal naturalism until the two later merged.
  • Absorption: after Mughal annexation of the Deccan, the styles interpenetrated, and later still fed into the "Company" idiom.
Prelims trap: Deccani painting = Bijapur, Golconda, Ahmadnagar — independent of, and initially richer than, Mughal painting; Ibrahim Adil Shah II of Bijapur is the classic patron.

9. Conservation & Contemporary Relevance

The murals and manuscripts are fragile and often endangered, making conservation a live governance theme UPSC can link to art history.

  • Ajanta conservation: humidity, past over-restoration and visitor pressure threaten the murals; ASI and international bodies run careful stabilisation — a case study in heritage management.
  • Manuscript heritage: the National Mission for Manuscripts documents and conserves India's vast manuscript corpus, including illustrated Jain and Pala material.
  • Repatriation & GI: traditional painting forms feed into living crafts and GI tags (e.g. Kangra, Madhubani in the folk file), linking art history to livelihoods.
One-line frame: How a society conserves a fading mural is a test of whether it values fragile, non-monetisable heritage over convenience — art as a public trust. check for latest update or data

10. Prelims PYQs

Concept-practice MCQs on Indian painting, built on genuine UPSC themes. Each carries a worked rationale.

Concept practice

Q: The prehistoric rock paintings of Bhimbetka are located in which state, and are a UNESCO World Heritage Site since?

  • (a) Madhya Pradesh; 2003
  • (b) Maharashtra; 1983
  • (c) Chhattisgarh; 2010
  • (d) Rajasthan; 2013

Answer: (a) Madhya Pradesh, inscribed 2003. The most numerous Bhimbetka paintings are Mesolithic; the largest (big-game) figures are the earliest.

Concept practice

Q: The paintings of the Ajanta caves are best described technically as —

  • (a) true (buon) fresco on wet plaster
  • (b) tempera / fresco-secco on a dry plaster ground
  • (c) oil on canvas
  • (d) encaustic (wax) painting

Answer: (b). Ajanta pigments were applied to a dry lime-plaster ground (secco/tempera), not the wet-plaster "true fresco" of Europe — a frequent discriminator.

Concept practice

Q: The Sittanavasal cave paintings belong to which religious tradition?

  • (a) Buddhist
  • (b) Jain
  • (c) Shaiva
  • (d) Vaishnava

Answer: (b) Jain — the Sittanavasal murals (Tamil Nadu, Pandya) depict a lotus-pond (samavasarana) and dancers. Badami, by contrast, holds the earliest Hindu murals.

Concept practice

Q: The famous painting "Bani Thani", India's "Mona Lisa", belongs to which school?

  • (a) Kangra
  • (b) Kishangarh
  • (c) Basohli
  • (d) Bundi

Answer: (b) Kishangarh (Rajasthan) — the Bani Thani, with elongated eyes and arched brows, was painted under Sawant Singh ("Nagari Das") by Nihal Chand.

Concept practice

Q: Mughal painting is generally considered to have reached its peak of naturalism and portraiture under —

  • (a) Akbar
  • (b) Jahangir
  • (c) Shah Jahan
  • (d) Aurangzeb

Answer: (b) Jahangir — a connoisseur-emperor whose atelier (Ustad Mansur) produced meticulous natural-history and portrait studies. Akbar Indianised it; Shah Jahan made it formal.

Concept practice

Q: The "protruding farther eye" in three-quarter profile faces is the diagnostic marker of which painting tradition?

  • (a) Mughal
  • (b) Pala
  • (c) Western Indian / Jain (Apabhramsa)
  • (d) Deccan

Answer: (c). The Western Indian/Jain manuscript school (Kalpasutra) shows the far eye projecting beyond the cheek — its single most recognisable feature.

11. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

Model GS-I questions on Indian painting. Each carries a full skeleton — Introduction → body → Conclusion.

Model question — GS-I 15 marks · 250 words

Q: "The Ajanta murals are the fountainhead of Indian painting." Discuss their themes, technique and enduring influence.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: The Ajanta murals (c. 2nd BCE–6th CE, chiefly Vakataka) are the earliest great body of Indian painting and the model from which later murals and even miniatures draw.
  2. Technique (the base): pigment on a dry lime-plaster ground (fresco-secco/tempera), built up over clay-and-straw layers — not European true fresco.
  3. Themes: Jataka narratives and the Buddha's life woven with a vivid secular world — courts, processions, daily life — sacred and worldly on one wall.
  4. Aesthetic achievement (the core): the compassionate Padmapani and Vajrapani bodhisattvas, expressive tribhanga poses, subtle modelling and eloquent hand-gestures set a canon of grace and feeling (bhava).
  5. Influence (critical edge): its conventions echo at Bagh, Sittanavasal, Badami and across Asia (Sri Lanka's Sigiriya, Central Asian caves) — a pan-Asian fountainhead, not a local school.
  6. Conclusion: Ajanta established the enduring Indian ideal — spiritual serenity expressed through sensuous, graceful form — making it the true source of the tradition.
Model question — GS-I 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Examine Mughal painting as a synthesis of Persian, indigenous Indian and European elements.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Mughal painting (16th–18th CE) is a court art whose distinction lies in fusing three visual worlds into a single refined idiom.
  2. Persian foundation: imported masters (Mir Sayyid Ali, Abdus Samad) brought fine line, flat brilliant colour and manuscript conventions under Humayun.
  3. Indianisation (the core): under Akbar large Indian workshops added dynamic narrative, indigenous faces, landscape and subjects (Hamzanama, Akbarnama, translated epics).
  4. European naturalism: under Jahangir, exposure to European prints brought perspective, modelling, the halo and acute portraiture (Ustad Mansur's natural-history studies).
  5. Critical edge: the synthesis was selective — the Mughals took European realism but never its religious content, keeping a distinctly courtly, secular Indo-Persian character.
  6. Conclusion: Mughal painting is the visual counterpart of Mughal syncretism — a confident, cosmopolitan style that later seeded the Rajput, Pahari and Deccan schools.
Model question — GS-I 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Trace the evolution of the Indian miniature from the Pala and Jain manuscripts to the Rajput courts.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: The miniature evolved over ~800 years from religious palm-leaf manuscripts to secular and devotional courtly painting on paper.
  2. Pala (roots): Buddhist palm-leaf illustration in Bengal-Bihar, linked to Nalanda — sinuous line, small format.
  3. Western Indian/Jain: Kalpasutra manuscripts with angular faces, the protruding "farther eye", wiry line and gold — and the shift from palm-leaf to paper.
  4. Mughal transformation (the core): Persian refinement + Indian narrative + European naturalism produce realistic, portrait-driven court painting.
  5. Rajput/Pahari (the branching): as Mughal patronage waned, trained artists carried the form to princely courts, where it became devotional (Krishna-bhakti), lyrical and richly coloured.
  6. Conclusion: the miniature's arc runs from sacred manuscript, through imperial realism, to regional bhakti — each phase reshaping an inherited form to new patrons and purposes.
Model question — GS-I 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Distinguish the Rajasthani and Pahari schools of painting, and explain how both served the bhakti spirit.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Rajasthani (plains) and Pahari (hill) schools are the two great post-Mughal miniature families, sharing devotional themes but differing in temperament.
  2. Rajasthani: bolder, flatter colour and vigorous line — Mewar's energy, Kishangarh's stylised Bani Thani, Bundi's landscapes.
  3. Pahari: softer, lyrical, naturalistic — Basohli's intense colour maturing into Kangra's delicate grace under Sansar Chand.
  4. Shared bhakti core: both centred on Krishna-lila, the Gita Govinda, Ragamala and Baramasa — the divine, not the emperor, is the hero.
  5. Critical edge: the difference is one of sensibility, not subject — the hill air softened the same devotional themes the plains rendered boldly.
  6. Conclusion: together they turned the courtly miniature into an instrument of devotion, giving Indian bhakti its most tender visual expression.
