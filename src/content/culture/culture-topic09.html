Topic 9: Indian Theatre

Theatre (natya) is the art the Natyashastra was actually written for — a total spectacle fusing drama, dance and music. Indian theatre runs on three tracks: refined Sanskrit classical drama, a teeming world of folk & ritual theatre, and socially engaged modern theatre. The exam tests it as form–region–feature–figure chains. This book-depth file covers the Sanskrit tradition (Bharata, Kalidasa, Bhasa, Shudraka), folk forms across regions (Ramlila, Jatra, Nautanki, Tamasha, Yakshagana, Koodiyattam), and modern theatre (IPTA, Habib Tanvir, Girish Karnad) — with tables, diagrams and PYQs with model answers.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames Indian Theatre

Theatre is tested as form–region matching ("which folk theatre belongs to which state?") plus stray items on Sanskrit playwrights and Koodiyattam (UNESCO). The productive skill is holding accurate form–region–feature chains, not isolated names. Sort your prep:

  • Static/factual — form–region pairs (Jatra/Bengal, Tamasha/Maharashtra, Nautanki/UP, Yakshagana/Karnataka, Bhavai/Gujarat), Sanskrit playwrights & plays (Kalidasa–Shakuntala, Shudraka–Mrichhakatika), Koodiyattam = UNESCO ICH. High recall.
  • Thematic/stylistic — Sanskrit (refined, Natyashastra rules) vs folk (open-air, participatory) vs modern (realist/political); ritual vs entertainment; secular vs devotional. Feature-to-form mapping.
  • Analytical (GS-I) — theatre as social reform and protest (IPTA), as a vehicle of syncretism and mass communication, and as endangered intangible heritage (Koodiyattam). Needs idea + example + judgement.

Note: Sanskrit theatre declined after the classical age, but its spirit survived in ritual forms like Koodiyattam (Kerala) — the oldest continuously performed theatre in the world and India's first UNESCO-listed intangible heritage element (2001, proclaimed).

1. Overview & Natyashastra Roots

Indian theatre is the parent art of the whole Natyashastra system — Bharata wrote his treatise primarily to codify natya (drama), from which dance and music are offshoots. Understanding theatre begins with this foundational, "total-art" conception.

  • Natya as total art: the Natyashastra (c. 2nd BCE–2nd CE) treats theatre as drishya kavya (visual poetry) that fuses text, acting, dance, music and stagecraft into one spectacle aimed at evoking rasa.
  • Divine origin myth: the text calls theatre the "fifth Veda", drawing recitation from the Rigveda, song from the Samaveda, gesture from the Yajurveda and rasa from the Atharvaveda — open to all castes, unlike the four Vedas.
  • Four styles (vrittis): Bharati (verbal), Sattvati (grand), Kaishiki (graceful), Arabhati (energetic) — the modes of dramatic presentation.
  • Three streams: the tradition splits into Sanskrit classical (refined, rule-bound), folk/traditional (regional, oral, participatory) and modern (post-colonial, realist and political) theatre — the organising frame for this file.
One-line frame: Indian theatre is the Natyashastra's original subject — a "total art" of drama+dance+music that later branched into classical Sanskrit, regional folk, and modern socially-engaged theatre.

2. Sanskrit Classical Theatre

Sanskrit drama is the classical, literary summit of Indian theatre — a courtly, rule-bound art that flourished from roughly the 2nd century BCE to the 8th century CE and produced some of the world's great plays before declining in the medieval period.

PlaywrightMajor Play(s)Note
BhasaSwapnavasavadatta, UrubhangaAmong the earliest known Sanskrit dramatists
KalidasaAbhijnanashakuntalam, Vikramorvashiyam, MalavikagnimitramThe greatest; Shakuntala won global fame (Goethe's praise)
ShudrakaMrichhakatika ("The Little Clay Cart")Rare realistic social drama (courtesan & commoner)
VishakhadattaMudrarakshasaPolitical drama (Chanakya–Chandragupta)
Harsha (king)Ratnavali, Nagananda, PriyadarshikaRoyal playwright of the 7th century
BhavabhutiUttararamacharita, Malatimadhava8th-century master of pathos
  • Rules & conventions: Sanskrit plays followed strict Natyashastra norms — a happy/auspicious ending, no on-stage death or violence, use of prose & verse, a Sutradhara (stage-manager/narrator), and characters speaking Sanskrit or Prakrit by rank.
  • Ten forms (Dasarupaka): the Nataka (heroic, e.g. Shakuntala) and Prakarana (invented plot, e.g. Mrichhakatika) were the principal types.
  • Decline: loss of royal patronage, the rigidity of Sanskrit (understood by few), and the rise of regional languages ended the tradition by early-medieval times — but its conventions passed into ritual forms like Koodiyattam.
Prelims trap: Kalidasa wrote Abhijnanashakuntalam; Shudraka wrote Mrichhakatika; Vishakhadatta wrote Mudrarakshasa (Chanakya). Sanskrit convention forbade showing death or violence on stage.

3. Folk Theatre — North & West

As Sanskrit theatre faded, a vibrant folk theatre arose in the regional languages — open-air, musical, improvised and rooted in devotion or entertainment. The North and West gave several of the best-known forms.

FormRegionCharacter
Ramlila / RaslilaNorth India (esp. UP)Devotional enactment of Rama's / Krishna's life
NautankiUttar PradeshOperatic folk theatre; romance & legend; musical
SwangHaryana/Rajasthan/UPSong-and-dialogue folk play
BhavaiGujarat/RajasthanSatirical, comic folk theatre
TamashaMaharashtraLively folk with Lavani song & dance
KhayalRajasthanMusical folk plays
  • Ramlila (enactment of the Ramayana, esp. at Dussehra) is inscribed on the UNESCO ICH list — a mass devotional theatre that turns whole towns into a stage.
  • Nautanki & Tamasha are secular, entertainment-driven forms — Nautanki (UP) is operatic with drum (nagara), Tamasha (Maharashtra) blends Lavani song and dance; both drew huge popular audiences.
  • Bhavai (Gujarat) is sharp and satirical, using humour for social comment — showing how folk theatre could critique society.
GS-I hook: North–West folk theatre split into devotional mass forms (Ramlila, Raslila) and secular entertainment (Nautanki, Tamasha, Bhavai) — both making theatre a people's medium beyond the Sanskrit court.

4. Folk Theatre — East & South

The East and South preserve some of India's richest theatre — from Bengal's rousing Jatra and Assam's Ankia Naat to the elaborate dance-dramas of the peninsula.

FormRegionCharacter
JatraBengal/OdishaRousing operatic folk theatre; music & melodrama
Ankia Naat / BhaonaAssamVaishnava devotional plays by Sankaradeva
YakshaganaKarnatakaElaborate dance-drama on epics; vivid costume/make-up
TherukoothuTamil Nadu"Street play"; epic themes (Mahabharata/Draupadi)
DashavatarKonkan (Goa/Maharashtra)Enacts Vishnu's ten avatars
BhaonaAssamPerformance form of Ankia Naat
  • Jatra (Bengal) is a loud, emotional, music-driven theatre that began as devotional (Chaitanya's Krishna-cult) and became a mass entertainment and even a vehicle of political/social messaging.
  • Ankia Naat (one-act play) and its performance Bhaona were created by the saint-reformer Sankaradeva in Assam to spread Neo-Vaishnava Bhakti — theatre as religious teaching (using a language, Brajavali).
  • Yakshagana (Karnataka) is a spectacular dance-drama with towering head-gear and painted faces, enacting Puranic tales — a cousin of Kathakali in scale.
  • Therukoothu (Tamil Nadu) is a rural "street theatre" centred on the Mahabharata, especially the story of Draupadi.
Prelims trap: Jatra = Bengal; Ankia Naat/Bhaona = Assam (Sankaradeva); Yakshagana = Karnataka; Therukoothu = Tamil Nadu. Ankia Naat is devotional (Vaishnava); Jatra became largely secular.

5. Koodiyattam & Ritual Theatre

Kerala preserves the living remnant of Sanskrit theatre — Koodiyattam — along with a family of ritual temple-theatre forms. This is the single most exam-relevant theatre item for its UNESCO status.

  • Koodiyattam (Kerala): the oldest living Sanskrit theatre tradition (over 2,000 years), performed in temple theatres (Koothambalam) by the Chakyar community; it preserves classical acting (netra abhinaya — eye expression). Proclaimed a UNESCO Masterpiece of Intangible Heritage (2001) — among India's first.
  • Chakyar Koothu: a solo narrative/monologue temple performance (by the Chakyar), a relative of Koodiyattam that allowed satire and social comment.
  • Mudiyettu (Kerala): a ritual theatre-dance depicting the goddess Kali's slaying of Darika — also on the UNESCO ICH list.
  • Ramman (Uttarakhand): a religious festival and ritual theatre of the Garhwal Himalayas — another UNESCO ICH element, showing ritual theatre's spread.
GS-I hook: Koodiyattam is a living time-capsule — it kept Sanskrit theatre alive in Kerala's temples for two millennia after the classical tradition died elsewhere, which is exactly why UNESCO singled it out for safeguarding.

6. Modern Indian Theatre

Modern theatre emerged under colonial contact in the 19th century and became, after Independence, a powerful instrument of social reform, political protest and cultural revival — the "theatre of roots" that reconnected modern stagecraft with folk traditions.

Figure / BodyContribution
IPTA (1943)Indian People's Theatre Association — left-wing theatre for social/political change
Prithviraj KapoorPrithvi Theatres — nationalist, socially-themed drama
Habib TanvirFused folk (Chhattisgarhi Nacha) with modern theatre (Charandas Chor)
Badal Sircar"Third Theatre" — intimate, non-proscenium street theatre (Evam Indrajit)
Girish KarnadUsed myth/history for modern themes (Tughlaq, Hayavadana)
Vijay Tendulkar / Mohan RakeshMarathi/Hindi realist & socially critical drama
  • Colonial beginnings: Western-style proscenium theatre arrived via Calcutta/Bombay/Madras; Parsi theatre popularised commercial, musical, urban drama.
  • IPTA (1943) & protest theatre (the core): the cultural wing of the Left used theatre against colonialism, famine and injustice — theatre as activism; its legacy runs into street theatre (Safdar Hashmi's Jana Natya Manch).
  • Theatre of Roots: post-Independence dramatists (Karnad, Tanvir, Ratan Thiyam of Manipur) deliberately drew on folk forms and myth to create an authentically Indian modern theatre — a decolonising of the stage.
Prelims trap: IPTA (1943) = left-wing people's theatre. Girish Karnad wrote Tughlaq & Hayavadana; Badal Sircar founded the "Third Theatre"; Habib Tanvir fused folk Nacha with modern drama.

8. Themes, Social Role & Syncretism

Beyond names and regions, UPSC's Mains angle is function — what theatre does in Indian society: it teaches, unites, entertains, and protests. This is the analytical spine of the topic.

  • Vehicle of Bhakti & syncretism: Ramlila, Ankia Naat and Jatra spread devotional messages to the illiterate masses; theatre carried the Bhakti movement and blended Hindu–Islamic (Parsi theatre, Urdu drama) idioms.
  • Mass communication & reform: folk theatre reached audiences no book could — used for religious teaching, and later for social reform (against untouchability, dowry) and public health messaging.
  • Theatre as protest (the core): IPTA and later street theatre (Safdar Hashmi) made theatre a tool of political dissent and labour mobilisation — a democratic, public art.
  • Inclusivity: unlike the Vedas, theatre was open to all castes and genders (in principle) — the Natyashastra's "fifth Veda for everyone" ideal.
  • Endangerment: folk theatre faces decline from cinema/TV, loss of patronage and dwindling practitioners — the intangible-heritage safeguarding concern.
GS-I hook: Indian theatre has always been a public art — from Ramlila's devotional crowds to IPTA's protest stage — making it a uniquely democratic medium of teaching, unity and dissent.

9. Institutions & Current Relevance

Modern Indian theatre is sustained by national institutions and academies, even as its folk forms struggle against mass media — a live preservation debate.

  • National School of Drama (NSD, 1959): India's premier theatre-training institution (New Delhi); runs the annual Bharat Rang Mahotsav, Asia's largest theatre festival.
  • Sangeet Natak Akademi (1953): the national academy for music, dance & drama — documents and awards theatre artists and supports folk forms.
  • UNESCO recognition: Koodiyattam, Ramlila, Mudiyettu and Ramman anchor theatre to the global intangible-heritage framework, aiding safeguarding.
  • Challenges: competition from cinema/OTT, shrinking rural audiences, ageing practitioners, and the difficulty of transmitting an oral, live art — the crux of any "safeguarding performing-arts heritage" answer.
  • Revival levers: festivals, documentation, state fellowships, and the "theatre of roots" reintegration of folk forms into modern stagecraft.
One-line frame: Theatre lives only in performance — so safeguarding it means funding the troupe and the festival, not merely archiving the script. check for latest update or data

10. Prelims PYQs

Concept-practice MCQs on Indian theatre, built on genuine UPSC themes. Each carries a worked rationale.

Concept practice

Q: Koodiyattam, the oldest living theatre tradition recognised by UNESCO as a Masterpiece of Intangible Heritage, belongs to which state?

  • (a) Tamil Nadu
  • (b) Karnataka
  • (c) Kerala
  • (d) Andhra Pradesh

Answer: (c) Kerala. Koodiyattam is a Sanskrit temple theatre performed in Koothambalams by the Chakyar community — proclaimed a UNESCO Masterpiece in 2001.

Concept practice

Q: Consider the following pairs of folk theatre and region:
1. Tamasha — Maharashtra
2. Jatra — Bengal
3. Nautanki — Kerala
Which of the pairs are correctly matched?

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Answer: (a). Tamasha–Maharashtra and Jatra–Bengal are correct. Nautanki is a folk theatre of Uttar Pradesh (North India), not Kerala — so pair 3 is wrong.

Concept practice

Q: The Sanskrit play Mrichhakatika ("The Little Clay Cart"), noted for its realistic depiction of ordinary society, was written by —

  • (a) Kalidasa
  • (b) Shudraka
  • (c) Bhasa
  • (d) Vishakhadatta

Answer: (b) Shudraka. Kalidasa wrote Abhijnanashakuntalam; Vishakhadatta wrote Mudrarakshasa (Chanakya); Bhasa wrote Swapnavasavadatta.

Concept practice

Q: Ankia Naat and its performance form Bhaona, devotional one-act plays spreading Neo-Vaishnavism, are associated with which figure and region?

  • (a) Sankaradeva — Assam
  • (b) Chaitanya — Bengal
  • (c) Tulsidas — Uttar Pradesh
  • (d) Namdev — Maharashtra

Answer: (a) Sankaradeva, Assam. He created Ankia Naat (in Brajavali) as part of Assam's Bhakti movement — the same reformer behind Sattriya dance.

Concept practice

Q: The Indian People's Theatre Association (IPTA), founded in 1943, is best described as —

  • (a) A royal patron of Sanskrit drama
  • (b) A left-wing cultural movement using theatre for social & political change
  • (c) A classical dance academy
  • (d) A body regulating film censorship

Answer: (b). IPTA was the cultural wing of the Left, using theatre against colonialism and social injustice — a landmark of modern protest theatre.

Concept practice

Q: Yakshagana, an elaborate dance-drama with vivid costume and make-up enacting Puranic stories, is a traditional theatre of —

  • (a) Odisha
  • (b) Tamil Nadu
  • (c) Karnataka
  • (d) Kerala

Answer: (c) Karnataka. Yakshagana is a spectacular epic dance-drama; Therukoothu is the Tamil Nadu street form, and Koodiyattam is Kerala's Sanskrit theatre.

11. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

Model GS-I questions on Indian theatre. Each carries a full skeleton — Introduction → body → Conclusion.

Model question — GS-I 15 marks · 250 words

Q: "Indian folk theatre has been a powerful medium of devotion, entertainment and social communication." Discuss.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: As Sanskrit drama declined, regional folk theatre became the living theatre of the masses — simultaneously a place of worship, entertainment and public communication.
  2. Devotion: Ramlila, Raslila, Ankia Naat and Yakshagana enacted sacred stories, carrying the Bhakti message to the illiterate and turning towns into devotional stages.
  3. Entertainment (the core): secular forms — Nautanki, Tamasha, Jatra, Bhavai — drew huge popular audiences with music, humour and melodrama, a genuine mass culture.
  4. Social communication: its open, participatory nature made folk theatre a tool for satire (Bhavai), reform, and later public messaging — and it blended Hindu–Islamic idioms (Parsi theatre).
  5. Critical edge: these oral traditions are now endangered by cinema/TV and lost patronage, needing documentation and support.
  6. Conclusion: folk theatre is India's most democratic art — it worshipped, entertained and spoke to the people at once, and its survival is a test of our commitment to living heritage.
Model question — GS-I 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Trace the evolution of Indian theatre from the Sanskrit classical tradition to modern political theatre, highlighting the changing role of theatre in society.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Indian theatre has passed through three great phases — Sanskrit classical, regional folk, and modern — each reflecting the society that produced it.
  2. Sanskrit classical: a courtly, rule-bound literary art (Kalidasa, Shudraka) codified by the Natyashastra, patronised by kings and aimed at rasa; it declined with royal patronage.
  3. Folk transition (the core): theatre moved to the people in regional languages — devotional (Ramlila, Ankia Naat) and secular (Nautanki, Jatra) — becoming a mass, participatory medium.
  4. Modern & political: colonial-era proscenium and Parsi theatre gave way to IPTA's protest theatre and the "theatre of roots" (Karnad, Tanvir), using the stage for reform and dissent.
  5. Changing role: from evoking aesthetic rasa, to spreading devotion, to mobilising political consciousness — theatre's social function widened at every stage.
  6. Conclusion: the arc from Kalidasa to IPTA shows theatre becoming ever more public and democratic — a mirror and mover of Indian society.
Model question — GS-I 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Why is Koodiyattam considered a uniquely important element of India's intangible cultural heritage?

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Koodiyattam, the temple Sanskrit theatre of Kerala, is the oldest continuously performed theatre tradition in the world and was among the first Indian elements proclaimed by UNESCO (2001).
  2. Living antiquity: it preserves, for over two millennia, the classical acting techniques of the Natyashastra — especially netra abhinaya (eye expression) — long after Sanskrit theatre died elsewhere.
  3. Distinct tradition (the core): performed in Koothambalams by the Chakyar community with the Nangiar and Mizhavu drum, it is a complete, codified performance system.
  4. Value: it is a living link to classical India, of immense historical and artistic worth.
  5. Critical edge: its narrow hereditary base and shrinking audiences make it fragile, which is why UNESCO safeguarding matters.
  6. Conclusion: Koodiyattam is a living time-capsule of Sanskrit theatre — preserving it preserves an unbroken 2,000-year artistic memory.
Model question — GS-I 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Examine the contribution of modern Indian theatre to social reform and national consciousness.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: From the 19th century, modern Indian theatre became a conscious instrument of reform and nation-building, not merely entertainment.
  2. Nationalist stage: colonial-era and Prithvi theatre raised nationalist and social themes, sometimes inviting British censorship.
  3. IPTA & protest (the core): the Indian People's Theatre Association (1943) mobilised audiences against colonialism, famine and injustice, making theatre a form of activism.
  4. Post-Independence reform: playwrights (Tendulkar, Karnad, Tanvir) confronted caste, gender and power; street theatre (Safdar Hashmi) took reform to the streets.
  5. Critical edge: such theatre faced censorship and commercial pressure, and its reach was limited against mass cinema.
  6. Conclusion: modern theatre helped forge a critical, reformist national consciousness — a democratic public space for debating Indian society.
