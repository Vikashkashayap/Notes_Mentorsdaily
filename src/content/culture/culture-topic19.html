Topic 19: Indian Fairs & Festivals

India's fairs and festivals are a living map of its diversity — religious festivals across faiths, harvest festivals that change name from state to state, vibrant tribal celebrations, and colossal melas like the Kumbh, the world's largest human gathering and a UNESCO intangible-heritage element. The exam tests this as festival–state–occasion matching (especially harvest festivals) plus GS-I questions on their cultural and integrative role. This book-depth file covers religious, harvest, tribal and seasonal festivals, the great fairs/melas and UNESCO-listed traditions — with grids and PYQs + model answers.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames Fairs & Festivals

Festivals are tested as festival–state–occasion matching ("Bihu is celebrated in which state?" / "Which is a harvest festival?") plus GS-I framing of their integrative and cultural role. The productive skill is a grid keyed by festival, state/region and what it marks (harvest, deity, season). Sort your prep:

  • Static/factual — harvest festivals by state (Pongal-TN, Bihu-Assam, Onam-Kerala, Baisakhi-Punjab, Lohri, Makar Sankranti), tribal festivals, and the big melas & their locations. High recall.
  • Thematic/structural — classification (religious / harvest-seasonal / tribal / trade fairs), and clusters like the many mid-January harvest festivals across states. Structure.
  • Analytical (GS-I) — festivals as engines of social cohesion, economy (tourism), intangible heritage, and unity-in-diversity. Needs idea + example + judgement.

Note: The classic MCQ cluster is the mid-January harvest festival that shares a date but changes name — Makar Sankranti (north/west), Pongal (Tamil Nadu), Lohri (Punjab, eve), Magh Bihu (Assam), Uttarayan (Gujarat). Know the state-to-name map cold.

1. Overview & Classification

Festivals in India arise from religion, the agricultural calendar, tribal tradition and trade. Most are tied to the lunar or solar calendar and to seasons of sowing and harvest, making them a window into the country's ecological, religious and regional diversity.

  • Religious festivals mark deities, saints and sacred events across Hindu, Muslim, Christian, Sikh, Jain, Buddhist and Parsi traditions.
  • Harvest/seasonal festivals celebrate the crop cycle — the same solar event (e.g. mid-January Sankranti) takes different names in different states.
  • Tribal festivals centre on nature, ancestors and the agricultural year in Adivasi communities.
  • Fairs & melas blend pilgrimage, trade and culture — from the vast Kumbh to livestock fairs like Pushkar and Sonepur.
One-line frame: Indian festivals = religious + harvest/seasonal + tribal + fairs/melas — a festival×state×occasion grid, with the mid-January harvest cluster (Pongal/Lohri/Bihu/Sankranti) the highest-yield set.

2. Religious Festivals

Religious festivals are the most widely known and span every faith. UPSC focuses less on the mainstream pan-Indian ones and more on regional temple festivals and inter-faith diversity, so note the state-specific and less-obvious ones.

FestivalFaith / occasionNote
Diwali / HoliHinduLights (Lakshmi/Rama's return); colours (spring)
Navratri / Durga PujaHindu (Shakti)Nine nights; Durga Puja (Bengal), Garba (Gujarat)
Eid-ul-Fitr / Eid-ul-AdhaIslamEnd of Ramzan; sacrifice
Christmas / EasterChristianityBirth & resurrection of Christ
Guru Nanak Jayanti / BaisakhiSikhGurpurab; Khalsa foundation (1699)
Mahavir Jayanti / Buddha PurnimaJain / BuddhistBirth of Mahavira; birth-enlightenment-death of Buddha
  • Regional temple festivals are exam favourites: Rath Yatra (Puri, Odisha — Jagannath), Thrissur Pooram (Kerala), Hemis (Ladakh, Buddhist), Nabakalebara (Puri).
  • Shakti festivals: Navratri is celebrated as Durga Puja in Bengal and Garba/Dandiya in Gujarat — same occasion, distinct regional form.
  • Multi-faith spread: festivals across Islam, Christianity, Sikhism, Jainism, Buddhism and Zoroastrianism (Navroz/Pateti) reflect India's pluralism.
Prelims trap: link temple festivals to states — Rath Yatra = Puri (Odisha); Thrissur Pooram = Kerala; Hemis = Ladakh (Buddhist); Chhath = Bihar/UP (Sun). Distinguish Buddha Purnima (Buddha) from Mahavir Jayanti (Jainism).

3. Harvest Festivals

Harvest festivals are the single highest-yield sub-topic, because the same agricultural season is celebrated under many regional names. Master the state-to-name map, especially the mid-January solar cluster and the spring/new-year harvest festivals.

  • Mid-January cluster: the sun's northward turn (Uttarayan/Makar Sankranti) is Pongal in Tamil Nadu, Lohri in Punjab (eve), Magh Bihu in Assam, and Uttarayan (kite festival) in Gujarat.
  • April cluster: Baisakhi (Punjab, Rabi harvest & Khalsa Day), Rongali Bihu (Assam), Vishu (Kerala), Puthandu (Tamil), Poila Boishakh (Bengal) — spring/new-year harvest festivals.
  • Autumn: Onam (Kerala, King Mahabali, Vamana legend, snake-boat races) and Nuakhai (western Odisha, first-grain) mark the later harvest.
Prelims trap: Pongal (TN) & Bihu (Assam) & Lohri (Punjab) & Makar Sankranti all cluster in mid-January. Onam = Kerala (autumn, Mahabali); Baisakhi = Punjab (April). Assam has three Bihus (Rongali/Kongali/Bhogali). Match state to name precisely.

4. Tribal Festivals

Tribal festivals, tied to nature worship, ancestors and the agricultural year, are an increasingly tested area as UPSC emphasises Adivasi culture. Several have become major tourism and identity events.

FestivalState / tribeNote
Hornbill FestivalNagaland (all Naga tribes)"Festival of festivals"; December; Kisama
Sammakka Saralamma JataraTelangana (Koya)Medaram; India's largest tribal fair (biennial)
SarhulJharkhand (Oraon, Munda)Spring; worship of sal trees

  • Hornbill Festival (Nagaland, December) showcases all Naga tribes' culture at Kisama — a flagship of tribal tourism and identity.
  • Sammakka Saralamma Jatara in Telangana's Medaram is the largest tribal congregation in India, drawing millions of Koya and other devotees biennially.
  • Nature worship: Sarhul (sal trees, Jharkhand) and Wangala (Sun-god, Garo) show the ecological, ancestor-centred character of tribal festivals.
Prelims trap: Hornbill = Nagaland (December, all Naga tribes); Sammakka Saralamma = Telangana (Medaram, largest tribal fair); Sarhul = Jharkhand; Wangala = Meghalaya (Garo). Losar = Buddhist Tibetan new year (Ladakh/Sikkim/Arunachal).

5. Fairs & Melas

Fairs (melas) combine pilgrimage, commerce and culture. The greatest is the Kumbh Mela — the largest peaceful gathering of humans on earth — but livestock and craft fairs across India are equally distinctive.

Fair / melaPlace / stateNote
Kumbh MelaPrayagraj, Haridwar, Ujjain, NashikEvery 12 yrs at each; UNESCO ICH (2017); largest human gathering
Pushkar FairRajasthanCamel/livestock fair; Kartik Purnima
Sonepur MelaBiharAsia's largest cattle fair (Hariharkshetra)
Hemis FestivalLadakhMasked cham dance; Guru Padmasambhava
Surajkund MelaHaryanaInternational crafts fair
Gangasagar MelaWest BengalMakar Sankranti pilgrimage (Sagar Island)
  • Kumbh Mela rotates every 12 years among Prayagraj (Sangam), Haridwar, Ujjain and Nashik; the Ardh (half) Kumbh falls at Prayagraj/Haridwar between them. It is inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list (2017).
  • Livestock/trade fairs: Pushkar (camels, Rajasthan) and Sonepur (cattle, Bihar — Asia's largest) blend commerce with festivity.
  • Craft fairs: the Surajkund International Crafts Mela (Haryana) showcases handicrafts and handlooms nationally and globally.
Prelims trap: Kumbh cities = Prayagraj, Haridwar, Ujjain, Nashik (UNESCO ICH 2017). Pushkar = Rajasthan camel fair; Sonepur = Bihar cattle fair; Surajkund = Haryana crafts. Gangasagar = West Bengal (Sagar Island, Makar Sankranti).

7. UNESCO-listed Traditions

Several Indian festival-linked traditions are inscribed on UNESCO's Representative List of Intangible Cultural Heritage, making them both static facts and current-affairs points worth knowing precisely.

Element (ICH)YearNote
Kumbh Mela2017Largest peaceful congregation of pilgrims

Ramlila2008Traditional performance of the Ramayana
Garba of Gujarat2023Ritual/devotional dance of Navratri

  • Kumbh Mela (2017) and Durga Puja in Kolkata (2021) are the flagship festival inscriptions on the UNESCO ICH list.
  • Garba of Gujarat (2023) is a recent addition, recognising the Navratri devotional dance as intangible heritage.
  • Ramlila (2008) and Chhau (2010) link festival celebration with performing arts on the list.
Prelims trap: Festival-linked UNESCO ICH: Kumbh Mela (2017), Durga Puja Kolkata (2021), Garba Gujarat (2023), Ramlila (2008), Chhau (2010). Note the years — recent additions like Garba are prime current-affairs MCQ material.

8. Cultural Significance

Festivals are far more than holidays — they are engines of social cohesion, economic activity, ecological awareness and cultural transmission, embodying India's "unity in diversity."

  • Social cohesion: festivals bind communities across caste and class, and increasingly cut across religion (Diwali, Eid, Christmas celebrated widely) — a living unity-in-diversity.
  • Economy: festivals drive tourism, handicrafts, retail and the seasonal economy (e.g. Diwali shopping, Pushkar/Surajkund fairs).
  • Ecology: harvest and nature festivals encode agricultural and ecological knowledge; some (Sarhul, Bishnoi practices) reinforce conservation.
  • Heritage transmission: festivals keep music, dance, craft, cuisine and oral traditions alive across generations.
  • Concerns: commercialisation, environmental impact (idol immersion, crackers, crowd management) and safety are contemporary challenges.
Analytical frame: festivals are a powerful integrative and economic force and a repository of intangible heritage — but sustainability (pollution, crowd safety) and commercialisation are live concerns UPSC may pair with the cultural-significance angle.

9. Legacy & Current Affairs

Festivals and fairs are constantly in the news — through UNESCO inscriptions, mega-events like the Maha Kumbh, tribal-festival promotion and debates on environmental sustainability.

  • Maha Kumbh: the Prayagraj Kumbh remains the world's largest gathering; management, crowd safety and the "digital Kumbh" feature in the news.
  • UNESCO ICH: Garba of Gujarat (2023) and Durga Puja in Kolkata (2021) are recent inscriptions widely reported.
  • Tribal tourism: the Hornbill Festival and others are promoted for cultural tourism and Adivasi identity.
  • Sustainability: eco-friendly idols, green crackers and pollution-control measures around festivals are recurrent policy issues.
  • Cultural diplomacy: festivals like Diwali are increasingly recognised abroad, extending India's soft power.

10. Prelims PYQs

Festivals are tested as festival–state–occasion matching. Practice MCQs below mirror the UPSC pattern; blocks without a genuine dated PYQ are honestly labelled "concept practice".

Concept practice

Q: The harvest festival Onam, associated with the legend of King Mahabali and snake-boat races, is celebrated primarily in —

  • (a) Tamil Nadu
  • (b) Kerala
  • (c) Karnataka
  • (d) Andhra Pradesh

Answer: (b) Kerala. Onam marks the Malayalam harvest/new-year season and the return of King Mahabali.

Concept practice

Q: Consider the following pairs (harvest festival — state):
1. Pongal — Tamil Nadu
2. Bhogali Bihu — Assam
3. Lohri — Punjab
Which of the pairs are correctly matched?

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Answer: (d) All correct — all three are mid-January harvest festivals: Pongal (TN), Bhogali/Magh Bihu (Assam), Lohri (Punjab).

Concept practice

Q: The Hornbill Festival, often called the "festival of festivals," is celebrated in —

  • (a) Manipur
  • (b) Nagaland
  • (c) Mizoram
  • (d) Meghalaya

Answer: (b) Nagaland (at Kisama, in December), showcasing all Naga tribes. Wangala is Meghalaya's Garo festival.

Concept practice

Q: The Kumbh Mela, inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list, is held on rotation at which of the following sets of cities?

  • (a) Prayagraj, Varanasi, Mathura, Ayodhya
  • (b) Prayagraj, Haridwar, Ujjain, Nashik
  • (c) Haridwar, Rishikesh, Ujjain, Puri
  • (d) Nashik, Prayagraj, Dwarka, Somnath

Answer: (b) The Kumbh rotates among Prayagraj, Haridwar, Ujjain and Nashik; it was inscribed on the UNESCO ICH list in 2017.

Concept practice

Q: The Sammakka Saralamma Jatara, regarded as one of the largest tribal gatherings in India, is held in —

  • (a) Jharkhand
  • (b) Chhattisgarh
  • (c) Telangana
  • (d) Odisha

Answer: (c) Telangana (Medaram), a biennial Koya tribal fair drawing millions of devotees.

Concept practice

Q: Which of the following festivals was inscribed on the UNESCO Representative List of Intangible Cultural Heritage in 2023?

  • (a) Kumbh Mela
  • (b) Durga Puja in Kolkata
  • (c) Garba of Gujarat
  • (d) Ramlila

Answer: (c) Garba of Gujarat (2023). Kumbh Mela (2017), Ramlila (2008) and Durga Puja Kolkata (2021) were inscribed earlier.

11. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

Model GS-I questions on Indian fairs and festivals. Each carries a full skeleton — Introduction → body → Conclusion.

Model question — GS-I 15 marks · 250 words

Q: "India's fairs and festivals are a mirror of its unity in diversity." Discuss.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: India's festivals, spanning every faith, region and community, express both its vast diversity and its underlying cultural unity.
  2. Diversity: the same season yields Pongal, Bihu, Lohri and Sankranti; each state and tribe has its own signature festivals and forms.
  3. Unity (the core): shared themes — harvest gratitude, victory of good over evil, renewal — and cross-community participation bind these varied festivals together.
  4. Integrative role: festivals like Diwali, Eid and Christmas are celebrated across religious lines, and fairs draw pilgrims and traders from everywhere.
  5. Critical edge: commercialisation, communal tension and environmental strain can undercut the integrative ideal, needing sensitive management.
  6. Conclusion: festivals remain a living embodiment of unity in diversity, provided their inclusive and sustainable character is preserved.
Model question — GS-I 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Examine the cultural and economic significance of fairs and melas in India.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Fairs and melas fuse pilgrimage, commerce and culture, playing a distinctive role in India's social and economic life.
  2. Cultural role: the Kumbh (UNESCO ICH), Pushkar and Hemis sustain religious tradition, performing arts and community identity.
  3. Economic role (the core): livestock fairs (Sonepur, Pushkar), craft melas (Surajkund) and festival tourism generate income, employment and market linkages.
  4. Heritage & skills: melas keep handicrafts, handlooms and folk performance economically viable and visible.
  5. Critical edge: crowd safety, sanitation and environmental impact at mega-melas demand robust governance.
  6. Conclusion: fairs and melas are engines of both cultural continuity and rural/artisanal economy, meriting careful, sustainable promotion.
Model question — GS-I 10 marks · 150 words

Q: How do harvest festivals reflect the ecological and agrarian roots of Indian culture?

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Harvest festivals across India are a calendar of the agricultural year, rooted in ecology and gratitude to nature.
  2. Seasonal cycle: Pongal, Bihu, Baisakhi and Onam mark sowing, harvest and new-year moments tied to specific crops and climates.
  3. Nature worship (the core): the Sun (Makar Sankranti, Chhath), cattle (Pongal, Mattu Pongal), trees (Sarhul) and rivers are venerated as sources of life.
  4. Community & ecology: festivals encode traditional agrarian knowledge and reinforce collective, sustainable practices.
  5. Critical edge: urbanisation and commercialisation risk severing festivals from their ecological meaning.
  6. Conclusion: harvest festivals keep India's agrarian and ecological consciousness culturally alive.
Model question — GS-I 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Assess the challenges of environmental sustainability posed by large festivals and possible solutions.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Mass festivals, while culturally vital, pose real environmental and safety challenges that need balanced management.
  2. Problems: idol immersion pollution, firecracker emissions, plastic waste and water contamination at melas strain ecosystems.
  3. Crowd & safety (the core): mega-gatherings like the Kumbh raise sanitation, stampede and disease-management concerns.
  4. Solutions: eco-friendly idols, green crackers, waste segregation, crowd-management technology and community awareness.
  5. Critical edge: measures must respect religious sentiment and livelihoods, blending tradition with sustainability.
  6. Conclusion: sensitive, participatory governance can reconcile festive celebration with environmental responsibility.
