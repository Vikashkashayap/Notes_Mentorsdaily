Topic 11: Indian Martial Arts

India's martial traditions (Shastra Vidya) are living folk arts that fuse combat, dance, ritual, medicine and spirituality. The exam tests them almost entirely as form–region–weapon matching, with occasional GS-I links to folk culture and heritage revival. This book-depth file covers Kalaripayattu (Kerala, the "mother" form), Silambam & Varma Kalai (Tamil Nadu), Gatka (Punjab), Mardani Khel (Maharashtra), Thoda (Himachal), the North-East cluster Thang-Ta/Huyen Langlon (Manipur), and wrestling forms (Kushti, Malla-yuddha, Lathi Khela, Inbuan) — with region pairs, weapons, philosophy, social role and PYQs with model answers.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames Martial Arts

Martial arts are tested overwhelmingly as form–region–weapon matching ("Thang-Ta belongs to which state?" / "Silambam uses which weapon?"). The productive skill is holding a regional grid with each form's home state and signature weapon locked in. Sort your prep:

  • Static/factual — each form's state and weapon: Kalaripayattu (Kerala), Silambam (Tamil Nadu, staff), Gatka (Punjab, sword-stick), Mardani Khel (Maharashtra), Thang-Ta (Manipur, sword-spear), Thoda (Himachal, archery), Kushti (pan-North). High recall.
  • Thematic/stylistic — armed vs unarmed; the fusion of combat with dance, ritual and healing (Marma/Varma vital-point science); regional clustering (South = weapon-and-body forms, NE = sword-spear, North = wrestling/archery).
  • Analytical (GS-I) — martial arts as composite living heritage, community identity and endangered folk livelihood needing revival (GI, tourism, sport recognition). Needs idea + example + judgement.

Note: A single grid solves most questions — form × state × weapon. Kalaripayattu (Kerala) is routinely called the oldest surviving martial art and the "mother of martial arts"; Silambam is the staff-form of Tamil Nadu; Thang-Ta is the Manipuri sword-and-spear art.

1. Overview & the Martial Map

Indian martial arts (Shastra Vidya / Dhanurveda tradition) are rarely "pure" combat — they braid fighting technique with dance, ritual worship, breath-control and indigenous medicine. Most survive as regional folk arts, often performed at festivals, and cluster geographically. The exam-usable backbone is a region → form → weapon grid.

  • Armed vs unarmed: most forms have both a weapons stage (sword, staff, spear, dagger) and an empty-hand stage; wrestling forms are purely unarmed.
  • Composite character: Kalaripayattu is inseparable from Ayurveda and the Marma vital-point system; Thang-Ta has a ritual "Thengou" sword-dance dimension; Chhau dance itself grew out of martial exercise.
  • Geographic logic: heavy-weapon and vital-point forms in the South, sword/stick/archery in the North-West hills, sword-spear in the North-East, and wrestling everywhere in the Gangetic North.
One-line frame: Indian martial arts = regional living heritage where combat fuses with dance, ritual and medicine — a single form×state×weapon grid answers most questions.

2. Kalaripayattu (Kerala)

Kalaripayattu — from kalari (training arena) and payattu (practice/fight) — is widely regarded as India's oldest surviving martial art and the "mother of martial arts," reputedly influencing East-Asian traditions. Practised in a sunken, temple-like kalari, it is intimately tied to Ayurveda and the science of marma (vital points).

StageNameWhat it covers
1. Body conditioningMeithariFlexibility, jumps, stances, oil massage (uzhichil)
2. Wooden weaponsKolthariLong staff (kettukari), short stick (cheruvadi), otta (curved S-shaped weapon)
3. Metal weaponsAnkathariSword-and-shield, dagger, spear, and the flexible urumi (coiled whip-sword)
4. Bare-hand & vital pointsVerumkai / MarmaEmpty-hand combat targeting marma points; healing knowledge
  • Two main styles: the Northern (Vadakkan) style, more weapon- and flexibility-focused, and the Southern (Thekkan) style, closer to Tamil Varma/Adi Murai and more empty-hand.
  • The urumi — a long flexible-blade "whip sword" — is Kalaripayattu's signature and most difficult weapon.
  • Marma & medicine: the gurukkal (master) is also a healer; the same knowledge of vital points that can injure is used to treat — linking the art to Ayurveda and Kalari chikitsa.
  • Cultural links: the Vadakkan Pattukal (Northern ballads) celebrate legendary Kalari warriors like Thacholi Othenan and Unniyarcha.
Prelims trap: Kalaripayattu = Kerala; often called the "mother of martial arts" and oldest surviving one. Signature weapon = urumi (flexible whip-sword). Training arena = kalari; master = gurukkal; vital points = marma.

3. Silambam & South Indian Forms

Beyond Kalaripayattu, the South — especially Tamil Nadu — has a rich family of stick, empty-hand and vital-point arts, many referenced in ancient Sangam literature and historically exported to South-East Asia by Tamil traders.

FormStateSignature
SilambamTamil NaduBamboo-staff (silambal) fighting; fast spinning & strikes; Sangam-era
Kuttu VarisaiTamil NaduEmpty-hand form (grappling, strikes) paired with Silambam
Varma Kalai / Varma AdiTamil Nadu & Kerala (Thekkan)Vital-point (varma) art; both combat and healing
Adithada / Adi MuraiTamil NaduStriking/kicking empty-hand art, akin to boxing
MallakhambMaharashtra & South (also pan-India)Gymnastic pole/rope training aiding wrestlers
  • Silambam is the flagship Tamil weapon art — a long bamboo staff whirled and struck at high speed; it is recognised as a sport with organised federations and is spreading internationally.
  • Varma Kalai centres on the 108 varmam (vital) points — the same dual "injure and heal" logic as Kerala's marma, reflecting a shared Dravidian body-science.
  • Mallakhamb is strictly a training discipline (aerial yoga-cum-gymnastics on a pole or rope) that builds strength for wrestlers, now a competitive sport in its own right.
Prelims trap: Silambam = Tamil Nadu, bamboo staff. Varma Kalai = vital-point art (Tamil equivalent of marma). Mallakhamb = pole/rope gymnastics, NOT a combat form — a wrestler's training aid.

4. North & West Indian Forms

The North and West preserve martial arts tied to warrior communities and religious tradition — the Sikh Gatka, Maratha Mardani Khel, and hill-archery Thoda — typically weapon-based and displayed at festivals and processions.

FormState / CommunitySignature
GatkaPunjab (Sikhs)Sword and wooden-stick (soti) with shield; displayed at Hola Mohalla / Nihang tradition
Mardani KhelMaharashtra (Marathas)Armed art of sword, vita (rope-dart) & dandpatta (gauntlet-sword); linked to Shivaji-era warfare
ThodaHimachal PradeshArchery-based art-cum-festival (Kullu/Solan), combining combat with dance; Pandava lore
Thang-Ta (see NE)—listed under North-East
Pari-KhandaBihar (Rajputs)Sword (pari) and shield (khanda) play; elements feed into Chhau dance
  • Gatka is closely bound to Sikh martial-religious life — performed by Nihangs, showcased at Hola Mohalla in Anandpur Sahib; uses the wooden soti and shield in training.
  • Mardani Khel (Kolhapur region) is the Maratha battlefield art — famed for the dandpatta (a sword with an integrated gauntlet) that Maratha light cavalry wielded.
  • Thoda blends martial contest with dance and music: two teams of archers shoot blunt arrows at opponents' legs during a spring festival in Himachal, framing the art as heritage more than combat.
  • Pari-Khanda of Bihar/Jharkhand feeds directly into Chhau dance, showing how martial drill became stage art.
Prelims trap: Gatka = Punjab/Sikh (Hola Mohalla). Mardani Khel = Maharashtra/Maratha (dandpatta). Thoda = Himachal (archery + festival). Pari-Khanda = Bihar/Jharkhand, source of Chhau.

5. North-East Forms

The North-East — above all Manipur — has a distinctive martial cluster fusing sword-spear combat with ritual dance, plus tribal wrestling traditions found across the region.

FormStateSignature
Thang-Ta (Huyen Langlon)ManipurThang = sword, Ta = spear; armed art within Huyen Langlon; ritual + combat
Sarit SarakManipurUnarmed (empty-hand) component of Huyen Langlon
Cheibi Gad-gaManipurSword-and-shield sport with a foam-wrapped stick (cheibi)
InbuanMizoramRule-bound wrestling; strict "no stepping outside the circle / no biting" ethic
MuknaManipurFolk wrestling of the Meitei, tied to the Lai Haraoba festival
  • Huyen Langlon ("knowledge of war") is Manipur's overarching martial system with two wings: Thang-Ta (armed — sword & spear) and Sarit Sarak (unarmed).
  • Thang-Ta has three modes: ritual, a spectacular performance/sword-dance, and actual combat — the clearest example of martial art fused with sacred dance.
  • Inbuan wrestling of Mizoram is highly codified — no kicking, biting, hair-pulling or leaving the ring — and dates to pre-migration Lushai society.
Prelims trap: Thang-Ta = Manipur (sword+spear), within Huyen Langlon; Sarit Sarak = its unarmed half. Inbuan = Mizoram wrestling. Mukna = Manipuri wrestling (Lai Haraoba).

6. Wrestling & Unarmed Combat

Wrestling is India's most widespread unarmed martial tradition, rooted in the epic malla-yuddha and sustained in the akhara (traditional gymnasium). Alongside it sit stick-play and boxing-like folk forms.

FormRegionSignature
Kushti / PehlwaniNorth India (Punjab, UP, Haryana, Maharashtra)Mud-pit (akhara) wrestling; Persian-influenced form of malla-yuddha
Malla-yuddhaPan-India (classical)Ancient epic wrestling; four types incl. grappling & striking
Musti YuddhaVaranasi (Kashi)Bare-knuckle boxing using fists, elbows, knees & head
Lathi KhelaWest Bengal & PunjabStick (lathi) fighting; both martial art and folk display
Vajra MushtiGujarat / South (Jyesthimalla)Grappling with a knuckle-duster weapon
  • Kushti (Pehlwani) is practised in the mud akhara under a guru, with a strict diet-and-celibacy regimen; it merges indigenous malla-yuddha with the Persian koshti of the Mughal era.
  • Musti Yuddha of Kashi is one of the oldest bare-knuckle forms — a full-contact art using the four "weapons" of fist, elbow, knee and head.
  • Lathi Khela (Bengal) turns the humble bamboo stick into a fast, whirling combat and festival display — historically a peasant self-defence art.
Prelims trap: Kushti/Pehlwani = akhara mud-wrestling (Persian + malla-yuddha). Musti Yuddha = Varanasi bare-knuckle boxing. Lathi Khela = Bengal stick-fighting. Vajra Mushti = grappling with a knuckle weapon.

8. Weapons, Technique & Philosophy

What distinguishes Indian martial arts is that combat technique is embedded in a wider philosophy of body, breath and spirit — a warrior is also a devotee and a healer. This is the GS-I "composite heritage" angle.

DimensionExpression
Textual rootsDhanurveda (the science of archery/warfare), Agni Purana, Sangam poems
WeaponsStaff, sword-and-shield, spear, dagger, urumi (whip-sword), dandpatta, lathi, bow
Body scienceMarma (Kerala) / Varma (Tamil) vital points — used to injure and heal
Spiritual linkTraining begins with salutation to the deity/guru; kalari has a shrine; Gatka tied to Sikh faith
Dance overlapChhau (from Pari-Khanda/Paika), Thang-Ta sword-dance, Kalari-based Kerala theatre movement
  • Combat ↔ medicine: the marma/varma vital-point knowledge makes the master a healer — the same map of the body serves attack and therapy, a uniquely Indian integration.
  • Combat ↔ dance: several classical/folk dances (Chhau, elements of Kathakali) descend from martial drill, blurring the line between fight and performance.
  • Combat ↔ devotion: practice is ritualised — invocations, festival timing (Hola Mohalla, Thoda's spring fair) and community identity give the arts a sacred frame.
GS-I hook: Indian martial arts are "living composite heritage" — they fuse warfare with medicine, dance and devotion, so preserving them protects a whole ecosystem of body-knowledge, not just a fighting technique.

9. Social Role, Decline & Revival

Martial arts historically served self-defence, community identity, warrior training and festival spectacle. Today most survive precariously and depend on tourism, sport-recognition and state support — the revival story is the current-affairs handle.

  • Decline drivers: loss of princely/feudal patronage; colonial-era arms restrictions (the Indian Arms Act, 1878 curbed practice); modern sport and cinema drawing away youth; ageing gurus and thin livelihoods.
  • Revival levers: tourism performances (Kerala's kalari shows), film exposure, inclusion as competitive sports, GI/heritage tagging, and academy support.
  • Institutional support: the Sangeet Natak Akademi and Ministry of Culture support performance traditions; sport federations (Silambam, Mallakhamb, Kalaripayattu, Gatka) push recognition; martial arts have featured at the Khelo India Youth Games and National Games as demonstration/indigenous-sport events.
  • Community identity: Gatka for Sikhs, Thang-Ta for Meiteis and Mardani Khel for Marathas remain markers of heritage pride, aiding survival.
Current-affairs frame: the policy debate is livelihood-vs-heritage — keeping these arts alive means giving practitioners paying audiences (tourism, sport, film), not just archiving them. check for latest update or data

10. Prelims PYQs

Martial arts appear as region–form–weapon matching. Practice MCQs below mirror the UPSC pattern; where no genuine dated PYQ exists the block is honestly labelled "concept practice".

Concept practice

Q: Often described as the oldest surviving martial art of India and the "mother of martial arts," and closely tied to the Marma vital-point system, is —

  • (a) Silambam
  • (b) Kalaripayattu
  • (c) Thang-Ta
  • (d) Gatka

Answer: (b) Kalaripayattu (Kerala) — practised in a kalari, integrated with Ayurveda and marma; signature weapon the urumi.

Concept practice

Q: Consider the following pairs of martial art and State:
1. Silambam — Tamil Nadu
2. Thang-Ta — Manipur
3. Thoda — Himachal Pradesh
Which of the pairs are correctly matched?

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Answer: (d) All three are correct. Silambam (TN, bamboo staff), Thang-Ta (Manipur, sword & spear), Thoda (Himachal, archery festival).

Concept practice

Q: Gatka, performed with a wooden stick and shield and displayed at the Hola Mohalla festival, is associated with —

  • (a) Maharashtra
  • (b) Punjab
  • (c) Rajasthan
  • (d) Haryana

Answer: (b) Punjab — Gatka is a Sikh martial art, showcased by Nihangs at Hola Mohalla in Anandpur Sahib.

Concept practice

Q: The dandpatta (a gauntlet-sword) is the signature weapon of which martial tradition?

  • (a) Kalaripayattu (Kerala)
  • (b) Mardani Khel (Maharashtra)
  • (c) Lathi Khela (Bengal)
  • (d) Inbuan (Mizoram)

Answer: (b) Mardani Khel — the Maratha armed art, famed for the dandpatta wielded by Maratha cavalry.

Concept practice

Q: Which one of the following is a rule-bound wrestling tradition of Mizoram?

  • (a) Mukna
  • (b) Musti Yuddha
  • (c) Inbuan
  • (d) Vajra Mushti

Answer: (c) Inbuan — Mizo wrestling barring kicking, biting and stepping out of the ring; Mukna is Manipuri and Musti Yuddha is bare-knuckle boxing of Varanasi.

Concept practice

Q: Which of the following statements about Varma Kalai / marma is correct?

  • (a) It is a shadow-puppet form of Kerala
  • (b) It is a vital-point science used both to injure and to heal
  • (c) It is a pole-gymnastics discipline for wrestlers
  • (d) It is a Sikh sword art of Punjab

Answer: (b) Varma (Tamil) / marma (Kerala) is the knowledge of vital points — the same map serving combat and Ayurvedic healing. Option (c) describes Mallakhamb.

11. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

Model GS-I questions on Indian martial arts. Each carries a full skeleton — Introduction → body → Conclusion.

Model question — GS-I 15 marks · 250 words

Q: "Indian martial arts are living composite heritage rather than mere fighting techniques." Discuss with examples.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: India's martial arts braid combat with dance, ritual worship and indigenous medicine, making them living cultural systems rather than isolated fighting skills.
  2. Combat & medicine: Kalaripayattu's marma and Tamil Varma Kalai use one map of vital points to injure and heal, tying the arts to Ayurveda — the master is also a physician.
  3. Combat & dance (the core): Chhau grew from Pari-Khanda/Paika drill, Manipur's Thang-Ta has a ritual sword-dance mode, and Kalari movement shapes Kerala theatre — the fight becomes performance.
  4. Combat & community identity: Gatka anchors Sikh festivals (Hola Mohalla), Mardani Khel carries Maratha memory, Thang-Ta expresses Meitei identity — the arts bind communities.
  5. Critical edge: treating them only as sport or spectacle risks stripping this composite depth; revival must protect the medicine, ritual and lineage, not just the choreography.
  6. Conclusion: preserving Indian martial arts safeguards an entire body-knowledge ecosystem — a synthesis of warfare, healing, dance and devotion unique to India.
Model question — GS-I 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Examine the regional diversity of Indian martial arts and the factors shaping their distribution.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Martial arts cluster regionally, each shaped by geography, warrior communities and available materials — a map of India's fighting heritage.
  2. South: weapon-and-vital-point systems — Kalaripayattu (Kerala), Silambam and Varma Kalai (Tamil Nadu) — rooted in Sangam culture and Dravidian body-science.
  3. North & West (the core): community-defined armed arts — Sikh Gatka (Punjab), Maratha Mardani Khel (Maharashtra), Himachal's archery-festival Thoda, Bihar's Pari-Khanda.
  4. North-East & wrestling belt: Manipur's Thang-Ta/Huyen Langlon and Mizoram's Inbuan; the Gangetic akhara tradition of Kushti and Kashi's Musti Yuddha.
  5. Shaping factors: warrior communities and patronage, local materials (bamboo staff in the South, sword in Maratha country), religion (Sikhism, Meitei ritual) and terrain.
  6. Conclusion: the diversity mirrors India's plural society — one martial impulse expressed through many locally-rooted, community-specific traditions.
Model question — GS-I 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Why are India's traditional martial arts in decline, and what measures can revive them?

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Once tied to warfare and community life, most Indian martial arts now survive precariously as their old functions and patrons disappear.
  2. Causes of decline: loss of princely/feudal patronage, colonial arms restrictions (Arms Act, 1878), competition from modern sport and cinema, and thin livelihoods driving gurus away.
  3. Consequence (the core): rare forms (Ravana-era vital-point lore, small NE traditions) risk extinction along with their attached medicine and lineage knowledge.
  4. Revival measures: tourism showcases, film exposure, sport-federation recognition and Khelo India inclusion, GI/heritage tagging, academy fellowships and documentation.
  5. Critical edge: commodifying the arts as pure spectacle can hollow them out; revival must sustain the guru-lineage and its holistic content.
  6. Conclusion: reviving martial arts means sustaining the practitioner — marrying cultural pride with real economic opportunity.
Model question — GS-I 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Bring out the relationship between Indian martial arts and classical/folk dance.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: In India the boundary between combat and dance is porous — several performance traditions descend directly from martial drill.
  2. Chhau (the core): the Purulia/Seraikella/Mayurbhanj Chhau dance evolved from Pari-Khanda and Paika martial exercises, retaining vigorous mock-combat movement.
  3. Kalari & Kerala arts: Kalaripayattu's flexibility training and stances underpin the body-language of Kathakali and other Kerala performance forms.
  4. Thang-Ta: Manipur's sword-spear art includes a ritual/performance sword-dance mode, blending worship, spectacle and combat.
  5. Significance: this fusion shows Indian aesthetics treating the trained body as a single instrument for war, worship and art.
  6. Conclusion: martial arts are a hidden root of Indian dance — protecting one enriches the other.
