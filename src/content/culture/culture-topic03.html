Topic 3: Artifacts & Excavation

Before the temple and the bronze come the sources that let us date and read them — the inscriptions chiselled by kings, the coins that carried their portraits, the pottery that marks a culture in the soil, and the great monastic universities that preserved knowledge. This book-depth file covers epigraphy (Brahmi, Kharosthi, Prinsep), the Ashokan edicts, numismatics, the pottery sequence (OCP → PGW → NBPW), and the ancient centres of learning — Takshashila, Nalanda, Vikramshila, Valabhi — with labelled diagrams, tables and genuine dated PYQs with model answers.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames Artifacts & Excavation

This topic is tested as source-criticism — "what does this artifact tell us, and how reliable is it?" The examiner wants you to move from an object (a coin, an edict, a potsherd) to the historical inference it supports. Sort your prep:

  • Static/terminology — script–language pairs (Brahmi/Kharosthi, Prakrit/Greek/Aramaic), edict types (major rock, pillar, minor), coin metals and dynasties, the OCP–PGW–NBPW pottery order, university–patron pairs. High recall.
  • Source discrimination — what each source proves: inscriptions (contemporary, official but self-glorifying), coins (economy, chronology, art, extent of empire), pottery (relative dating of cultures), monasteries (state of religion & learning). Object-to-inference mapping.
  • Analytical (GS-I) — why Gupta numismatic art marks a high point; inscriptions as history vs propaganda; ancient universities as a knowledge economy and the causes of their decline. Needs idea + example + judgement.

Note: These sources span the whole subcontinent — from Takshashila (in undivided India's north-west) and Sharada Peeth to Nalanda in the east and Keeladi in the deep south. All recovered antiquities are protected under the Antiquities & Art Treasures Act, 1972, and their repatriation is a live matter of cultural sovereignty.

1. Sources of History & Archaeology

History is reconstructed from two broad streams — literary sources (texts) and archaeological sources (material remains). This file deals mainly with the archaeological stream, which the examiner prizes because it is contemporary and unedited in a way that later copied manuscripts are not.

StreamExamplesStrengthLimitation
Literary — indigenousVedas, epics, Puranas, Sangam texts, ArthashastraRich on ideas, society, religionOften undated, copied later, exaggerated
Literary — foreign accountsMegasthenes' Indica, Fa-Hien, Hiuen Tsang, Al-BiruniOutsider's dated observationHearsay, cultural bias
Archaeological — inscriptionsEdicts, prashastis, land grantsContemporary, precisely locatedRoyal self-glorification
Archaeological — coinsPunch-marked, Kushana, Gupta goldEconomy, chronology, empire extentSurvive selectively
Archaeological — monuments/potteryStupas, viharas, PGW, NBPWRelative dating of culturesInterpretation needed
  • Archaeology studies the past through material remains recovered by excavation and exploration; the Archaeological Survey of India (ASI), founded 1861 under Alexander Cunningham, is the nodal body.
  • Antiquity (legally, an object ≥100 years old) and art treasure are protected categories — a fact that recurs in current-affairs prelims.
GS-I hook: No single source is complete; sound history triangulates — e.g. Samudragupta is known from the Allahabad inscription, his gold coins, and later texts, each checking the others.

2. Inscriptions (Epigraphy)

Epigraphy is the study of inscriptions; palaeography the study of ancient scripts. Inscriptions are the single most reliable source because they are contemporary, publicly displayed and hard to forge — but they are also official documents, so they glorify their patrons and must be read critically.

  • By purpose: commemorative/eulogistic (prashasti — praising a king), donative (recording gifts/land grants to temples or monks), administrative (edicts, laws), votive/dedicatory (at stupas, caves), and memorial (hero-stones, sati-stones).
  • Scripts: Brahmi (left-to-right, the ancestor of most Indian scripts) and Kharosthi (right-to-left, used in the north-west). Ashokan edicts also used Greek and Aramaic in the far north-west.
  • Decipherment: James Prinsep deciphered Brahmi and Kharosthi in 1837, unlocking the entire early corpus — the reason "Devanampiya Piyadasi" could at last be identified as Ashoka.
InscriptionKing / AuthorSignificance
Hathigumpha (Udayagiri, Odisha)Kharavela (Chedi)Kalinga king's deeds; earliest reference to a Jain king

Prelims trap: The first inscription in polished Sanskrit is Rudradaman's Junagadh (c. 150 CE); earlier inscriptions (Ashoka, Satavahana) used Prakrit. Sanskrit as the language of record is a Saka–Gupta development.

3. The Ashokan Edicts

The edicts of Ashoka (c. 268–232 BCE) are the earliest deciphered, precisely dated royal documents in Indian history — and the first time a king speaks directly to his subjects about Dhamma (moral law). They are a source not just for Mauryan administration but for the very idea of a welfare-oriented state.

CategoryNumber / NotesContent
Major Rock Edicts14Dhamma, morality, tolerance, welfare, foreign relations
Pillar Edicts7Later reflections; policy review; polished sandstone pillars
Minor Rock EdictsSeveralPersonal faith; Maski & Gujarra name "Ashoka" directly
Separate (Kalinga) Edicts2 (Dhauli, Jaugada)Remorse after the Kalinga war; care of conquered peoples
  • Languages & scripts: mostly Prakrit in Brahmi; Kharosthi in the north-west (Shahbazgarhi, Mansehra); and Greek & Aramaic in the far north-west (Kandahar) — proof of a multilingual empire reaching Hellenistic borders.
  • Titles: Ashoka calls himself Devanampiya ("Beloved of the Gods") and Piyadasi ("of amiable appearance"); only Maski/Gujarra confirmed the identification.
  • Dhamma: not a sect but a practical ethic — non-violence, respect for elders, tolerance of all sects, welfare works, and dhamma-mahamattas (officers of morality). A tool of imperial integration.
GS-I hook: Ashoka's edicts are simultaneously a historical source (dated, contemporary) and an act of statecraft — using stone as a mass medium to bind a vast, diverse empire through a shared moral vocabulary.

4. Coins (Numismatics)

Numismatics — the study of coins — is a uniquely dense source: a single coin can fix a date (regnal legends), map the extent of an empire (where it is found), reveal the economy (metal, weight, purity), and record art and religion (deities, portraits, symbols). Where inscriptions are few, coins fill the gap.

Gupta gold coin (dinara): king in action on the obverse, a deity on the reverse — art at its numismatic peak.

CoinagePeriod / DynastyFeature
Punch-marked (Karshapana)c. 6th CE BCE onward (Janapadas, Mauryas)Earliest; silver/copper; symbols punched, no legends
Indo-Greek2nd–1st BCEFirst coins with a king's portrait and name; fine die-work
Kushana1st–3rd CEAbundant gold; a pantheon of Greek, Iranian, Indian deities
Gupta gold (dinara)4th–6th CENumismatic high point — king with bow/lyre/horse, Lakshmi reverse
SatavahanaDeccan, 1st BCE–2nd CEMostly lead & potin; ship motifs = overseas trade
  • What coins prove: the debasement (falling gold purity) of later Gupta coins signals economic strain; hoards mark trade routes; Samudragupta's lyre coin reveals a musician-king; Kushana coins document a cosmopolitan, multi-religious realm.
  • Roman coin hoards in South India (Sangam age) confirm a brisk Indo-Roman trade — a favourite prelims fact.
Prelims trap: Punch-marked coins carry symbols, not legends; the first coins to name and portray the issuing king are the Indo-Greek ones — a distinction UPSC loves.

5. Pottery & the Ceramic Sequence

Pottery is the archaeologist's clock. Because styles change slowly and predictably, a ware found in a soil layer dates that layer relative to others. The north-Indian sequence — OCP → PGW → NBPW — is one of the most frequently examined chronologies.

WareAssociated withMarker
OCP (Ochre Coloured Pottery)Copper Hoard culture, late Harappan fringeWorn, ochre-rubbing surface
PGW (Painted Grey Ware)Later Vedic age, upper Ganga–Yamuna doabFine grey ware, black geometric paint; linked to Mahabharata sites
NBPW (Northern Black Polished Ware)Age of the Mahajanapadas / early cities, MauryasGlossy, mirror-like black surface — luxury de-luxe ware
BRW (Black-and-Red Ware)Megalithic South India & elsewhereBlack interior/rim, red body (inverted firing)
  • Harappan pottery is distinct: wheel-made, sturdy red ware with black painted designs (pipal leaves, peacocks) — a mature urban craft.
  • Why it matters: the shift OCP → PGW → NBPW tracks the move from village to city (second urbanisation), the spread of iron, and rising social complexity — pottery is a proxy for the whole society.
GS-I hook: Reading the pottery sequence is reading the arc of early India — from post-Harappan villages (OCP), through the Later Vedic doab (PGW), to the first historical cities and empires (NBPW).

6. Ancient Universities (Centres of Learning)

India's ancient universities were monastic residential universities — endowed, international, and organised around debate. They are tested both as static facts (who founded which, who visited) and as GS-I themes (India as a knowledge economy; the causes of their decline).

UniversityLocation / PatronKnown for
Takshashila (Taxila)NW (Gandhara); pre-MauryanOldest; Panini, Chanakya, Charaka; medicine, grammar, statecraft
NalandaBihar; Kumaragupta I, later Harsha & PalasGreatest Buddhist university; Hiuen Tsang & I-tsing studied here; vast library "Dharmaganja"
VikramshilaBihar; Dharmapala (Pala)Tantric Buddhism; Atisa Dipankara; rival of Nalanda
ValabhiGujarat (Maitrakas)Hinayana centre; secular subjects; visited by I-tsing
Odantapuri & SomapuraBihar / Bengal (Palas)Somapura (Paharpur) = largest monastery, a UNESCO site
Pushpagiri & Sharada PeethOdisha / KashmirRegional Buddhist & Sanskrit learning
  • Features: free residential education, competitive oral entrance debates, international student bodies, huge endowed libraries, and instruction spanning Buddhist philosophy, logic, medicine, grammar, astronomy and the arts.
  • Foreign testimony: the Chinese pilgrims Hiuen Tsang (7th CE) and I-tsing left detailed accounts of Nalanda's scale and rigour — a foreign literary source cross-checking the archaeology.
  • Decline: the withdrawal of royal patronage after the Palas, the rise of a more ritual-bound orthodoxy, and finally the sack of Nalanda and Vikramshila by Bakhtiyar Khalji (c. 1193 CE) ended the tradition.
Prelims trap: Nalanda = Kumaragupta I (Gupta); Vikramshila & Somapura = the Palas (Dharmapala). Mixing up the founding dynasty is the classic error.

7. Monasteries & Excavation Sites

Beyond the famous universities, thousands of viharas (monasteries) and excavation sites document the everyday life of ancient India. How they are dug — layer by layer — is itself an exam theme, because stratigraphy underlies all relative dating.

Law of superposition: in an undisturbed mound, the deeper the layer, the older it is.

  • Types of monastic architecture: the rock-cut chaitya (prayer hall) and vihara (residence), and later the free-standing brick monastery (Nalanda, Somapura).
  • Key sites & what they yielded: Harappan cities (Mohenjo-daro, Dholavira, Rakhigarhi); early-historic Keeladi (Tamil Nadu — urban Sangam-age literacy); Sisupalgarh, Sanchi, Sarnath, Amaravati, Sannati/Kanaganahalli (the Ashoka portrait).
  • Excavation method: horizontal (area) vs vertical (trench) digging; every artifact is recorded by layer, position and association — context is what turns an object into evidence.
GS-I hook: An artifact without its stratigraphic context is almost worthless — which is exactly why looting and unprovenanced antiquities destroy history, not just property.

8. Dating Methods & the Archaeological Survey of India

Archaeology distinguishes relative dating (older/younger, from stratigraphy and typology) from absolute dating (a calendar age, from science). Both feed into how we date every artifact discussed above.

MethodTypeUse
StratigraphyRelativeOrder of layers (superposition)
Typology / seriationRelativeEvolution of tool/pottery styles
Radiocarbon (C-14)AbsoluteOrganic remains up to ~50,000 yrs
DendrochronologyAbsoluteTree-ring counting
ThermoluminescenceAbsoluteDating fired pottery & kilns
  • ASI (1861): founded by Alexander Cunningham; today under the Ministry of Culture, it protects Monuments of National Importance, runs excavations, epigraphy and conservation, and maintains the National Museum lineage of collections.
  • Legal frame: the Ancient Monuments & Archaeological Sites and Remains (AMASR) Act, 1958 (protection & regulated zones) and the Antiquities & Art Treasures Act, 1972 (control of trade & export of antiquities).
Prelims trap: AMASR Act 1958 protects monuments & sites; the Antiquities Act 1972 controls movable antiquities. Keep the two Acts — and their years — separate.

9. Antiquities, Revival & Contemporary Relevance

This static topic is unusually alive in the news, and UPSC increasingly links the two. Keep a few current threads ready to deploy as GS-I examples.

  • Nalanda revival: the modern Nalanda University (established by Act of Parliament, 2010; campus inaugurated at Rajgir, 2024) revives the ancient centre as a pan-Asian institution — a live symbol of civilisational continuity.
  • Keeladi excavations (Tamil Nadu) push back the date of urban, literate Sangam-age culture in the deep south — feeding debates on the antiquity of Tamil-Brahmi.
  • Antiquities repatriation: India has secured the return of many looted idols and artifacts (e.g. via cooperation with the US, Australia, and auction houses) — a matter of cultural sovereignty under the 1972 Act.
  • Digital epigraphy & museums: ASI's digitisation of inscriptions and the "Adopt a Heritage" scheme link conservation with public access.
One-line frame: The way a nation excavates, protects and repatriates its artifacts is a statement about how it values its own past — heritage as both evidence and identity. check for latest update or data

10. Prelims PYQs

Genuine dated UPSC questions plus concept-practice MCQs on artifacts & excavation. Each carries a worked rationale.

UPSC Prelims 2019

Q: Which one of the following is not a Harappan site?

  • (a) Chanhudaro
  • (b) Kot Diji
  • (c) Sohgaura
  • (d) Desalpur

Answer: (c) Sohgaura — a site in Uttar Pradesh famous for a Mauryan copper-plate inscription (a famine-relief record), not a Harappan settlement. The other three are Harappan sites.

UPSC Prelims 2019

Q: In which of the following relief sculpture inscriptions is 'Ranyo Ashoka' (King Ashoka) mentioned along with the stone portrait of Ashoka?

  • (a) Kanganahalli
  • (b) Sanchi
  • (c) Shahbazgarhi
  • (d) Sohgaura

Answer: (a) Kanaganahalli (Sannati, Karnataka) — a relief panel labelled "Ranyo Ashoka" carries the only known contemporary portrait of Ashoka.

Concept practice

Q: The Brahmi and Kharosthi scripts of the Ashokan edicts were first deciphered by —

  • (a) Alexander Cunningham
  • (b) James Prinsep
  • (c) John Marshall
  • (d) Mortimer Wheeler

Answer: (b) James Prinsep (1837). His decipherment made it possible to read the edicts and identify "Devanampiya Piyadasi" as Ashoka.

Concept practice

Q: The first coins in India to bear both the name and the portrait of the issuing king were issued by the —

  • (a) Mauryas (punch-marked)
  • (b) Indo-Greeks
  • (c) Guptas
  • (d) Satavahanas

Answer: (b) Indo-Greeks. Punch-marked coins carry only symbols; the Indo-Greeks introduced legible royal portraits and names in the 2nd–1st century BCE.

Concept practice

Q: Painted Grey Ware (PGW) pottery is most closely associated with which cultural phase?

  • (a) Mature Harappan
  • (b) Later Vedic age
  • (c) Mauryan empire
  • (d) Gupta age

Answer: (b) Later Vedic. PGW (c. 1200–600 BCE) of the upper Ganga–Yamuna doab is the marker ware of the Later Vedic period; the glossy NBPW belongs to the later Mahajanapada–Mauryan cities.

Concept practice

Q: Vikramshila University, a great centre of Tantric Buddhism, was founded by —

  • (a) Kumaragupta I
  • (b) Harsha
  • (c) Dharmapala (Pala)
  • (d) Kanishka

Answer: (c) Dharmapala of the Pala dynasty. (Nalanda, by contrast, was founded by the Gupta king Kumaragupta I.)

11. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

Genuine dated UPSC GS-I questions plus model questions on archaeological sources. Each carries a full skeleton — Introduction → body → Conclusion.

UPSC Mains GS-I 2017 10 marks · 150 words

Q: How do you justify the view that the level of excellence of the Gupta numismatic art is not at all noticeable in later times?

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Gupta gold coinage (4th–6th CE) is regarded as the zenith of Indian numismatic art — a standard later dynasties never recovered.
  2. The peak (what made it excellent): exquisite die-engraving; dynamic king-in-action types (archer, lyrist Samudragupta, horse-sacrifice, tiger-slayer); elegant Sanskrit legends; a graceful deity reverse (Lakshmi/Ganga) — art and statecraft fused in gold.
  3. The decline (the core argument): later Gupta coins show debasement (falling gold content), cruder, stereotyped figures and repetitive legends — economic strain and loss of skilled ateliers.
  4. Why later times fell short: the post-Gupta shift to a land-grant (feudal) economy reduced coin circulation and monetisation; declining long-distance trade removed the demand and the patronage that sustained fine minting.
  5. Critical edge: some regional coinages (e.g. later dynasties) retained utility but never the aesthetic ambition; the Gupta achievement was as much cultural confidence as technique.
  6. Conclusion: the unmatched excellence of Gupta coins reflects a rare convergence of a monetised economy, imperial patronage and classical artistic maturity — conditions that the more agrarian, fragmented later polities could not reproduce.
UPSC Mains GS-I 2020 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Pala period is the most significant phase in the history of Buddhism in India. Enumerate.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Under the Palas of Bengal-Bihar (8th–12th CE), the last great flowering of Indian Buddhism took place before its decline — a phase of institutional, artistic and international significance.
  2. Monastic universities (the core): Pala kings patronised Nalanda and founded Vikramshila, Odantapuri and Somapura (Paharpur) — the greatest concentration of Buddhist learning in the world.
  3. Tantric Vajrayana: the Palas nurtured Vajrayana Buddhism and scholars like Atisa Dipankara, whose mission shaped Tibetan Buddhism — a lasting trans-Himalayan legacy.
  4. Art — the Pala school: refined black-basalt sculpture and illustrated palm-leaf manuscripts spread the "Pala style" to Nepal, Tibet and Southeast Asia.
  5. International reach (critical edge): Pala Buddhism was an export — monks, texts and icons travelled across Asia, making Bengal a hub of a Buddhist commonwealth even as the faith waned elsewhere in India.
  6. Conclusion: the Pala period is "most significant" because it was simultaneously Buddhism's Indian climax and the bridge that carried it abroad — its end (with the sack of Nalanda/Vikramshila) marks the effective close of the tradition in its homeland.
Model question — GS-I 15 marks · 250 words

Q: "Inscriptions are the most reliable, yet not an unbiased, source of ancient Indian history." Examine.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Epigraphy is prized as the most dependable source because inscriptions are contemporary, dated and publicly fixed in stone — but they are official texts, and reliability is not the same as objectivity.
  2. Why reliable: contemporaneity (no later copying errors); precise provenance (a known place); administrative value (land grants reveal economy, society, geography) — e.g. Satavahana donative records.
  3. Where they illuminate (the core): chronology and dynastic succession (Allahabad prashasti), language history (Junagadh's early Sanskrit), religion (Heliodorus pillar), and welfare/administration (Ashokan edicts).
  4. The bias (critical edge): most royal inscriptions are prashastis — eulogies that exaggerate victories and omit defeats; Harishena's account of Samudragupta is glorification, not neutral record.
  5. How historians correct for it: cross-checking with coins, foreign accounts and archaeology; reading donative/private inscriptions (less flattering) alongside royal ones.
  6. Conclusion: inscriptions are indispensable and the firmest chronological anchor we have, but they must be read critically — as evidence to be triangulated, not testimony to be trusted whole.
Model question — GS-I 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Assess the significance of the ancient Indian universities as centres of learning, and analyse the causes of their decline.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Takshashila, Nalanda, Vikramshila and Valabhi made early India one of the world's great knowledge economies — residential, international, and debate-driven.
  2. Significance — scale & method: endowed free education, competitive oral entrance, vast libraries (Nalanda's "Dharmaganja"), and a curriculum from Buddhist logic to medicine, grammar and astronomy.
  3. International standing (the core): the accounts of Hiuen Tsang and I-tsing, and scholars like Atisa who carried Indian learning to Tibet, show these were hubs of a pan-Asian intellectual network.
  4. Causes of decline (critical edge): withdrawal of royal patronage after the Palas; over-dependence on a single monastic-Buddhist base; growing ritual orthodoxy narrowing free enquiry; and the destructive sack of Nalanda & Vikramshila by Bakhtiyar Khalji (c. 1193).
  5. The deeper vulnerability: concentration of knowledge in a few endowed institutions — without a broad, decentralised base, a few blows could extinguish a whole tradition.
  6. Conclusion: their rise reflects the confidence of a plural, well-patronised society; their fall warns that knowledge institutions need diversified support and openness to survive — a lesson the modern Nalanda revival consciously invokes.
