Topic 14: Religions of India

India is the birthplace of four world religions — Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism and Sikhism — and a home to Zoroastrianism, Islam, Christianity and Judaism. The exam tests doctrine–founder–text–council facts plus GS-I themes on syncretism and reform. This book-depth file covers Hinduism (sects & schools), Buddhism (Four Noble Truths, Eightfold Path, councils, the three vehicles), Jainism (Tirthankaras, Digambara/Svetambara, Anekantavada), Sikhism (Ten Gurus, Khalsa), and the Abrahamic & Zoroastrian faiths — with comparison grids, councils, symbols and PYQs with model answers.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames Religion

Religion is tested as doctrine–founder–text–council matching ("Which Buddhist council was held under Kanishka?" / "Anekantavada belongs to which faith?"). The productive skill is a comparative grid across faiths. Sort your prep:

  • Static/factual — founders, core doctrines, sacred texts, councils, sects and symbols of each religion. High recall.
  • Thematic/doctrinal — the shared shramana (Buddhism, Jainism) vs brahmana (Vedic) streams; the "middle path" vs extreme asceticism; monism vs dualism. Structure.
  • Analytical (GS-I) — religion as social reform (heterodox challenge to caste/ritual), as art-patron (stupas, temples), and as a source of syncretism and communal harmony. Needs idea + example + judgement.

Note: Keep the two families apart — the Indic/Dharmic faiths (Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism, Sikhism) born in India, and the Abrahamic + Zoroastrian faiths that arrived and settled. The four Buddhist councils and 24 Jain Tirthankaras are perennial prelims sets.

1. Overview & Religions of India

India's religious landscape has two broad layers — the indigenous Dharmic faiths that arose here, and the Abrahamic and Zoroastrian faiths that came from West Asia and were absorbed. The Constitution makes India a secular republic guaranteeing freedom of religion (Articles 25–28).

  • Dharmic faiths share concepts of karma, rebirth (samsara) and liberation (moksha/nirvana/kaivalya), though they differ sharply on God, soul and ritual.
  • Shramana vs Brahmana: Buddhism and Jainism are shramana (ascetic, anti-Vedic-ritual) movements of the 6th c. BCE; Hinduism grew from the brahmana Vedic tradition.
  • Foreign faiths arrived early and peacefully by trade — Jews and Christians on the Kerala coast, Parsis fleeing Persia (Sanjan, Gujarat), and Islam via Arab traders and later conquest.
One-line frame: India's religions = indigenous Dharmic faiths (Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism, Sikhism) plus absorbed foreign faiths (Zoroastrian, Jewish, Christian, Muslim) — a founder–doctrine–text grid answers most questions.

2. Hinduism

Hinduism (Sanatana Dharma) has no single founder or creed — it is a family of traditions growing from the Vedas through the Upanishads, epics and Puranas, expressed in major sects and six philosophical schools.

AspectDetail
Core textsVedas (shruti), Upanishads, Bhagavad Gita, Ramayana, Mahabharata, Puranas
Key conceptsBrahman, Atman, karma, dharma, samsara, moksha; four purusharthas & ashramas
Major sectsVaishnavism (Vishnu), Shaivism (Shiva), Shaktism (Devi), Smartism
Six schools (Shad-darshana)Nyaya, Vaisheshika, Samkhya, Yoga, Mimamsa, Vedanta
Later movementsBhakti, Vedanta schools (Advaita, Vishishtadvaita, Dvaita)
  • Sects: Vaishnavas worship Vishnu and his avatars (Rama, Krishna); Shaivas worship Shiva; Shaktas worship the Goddess (Durga, Kali); Smartas revere five deities equally.
  • Vedanta schools: Shankara's Advaita (non-dualism), Ramanuja's Vishishtadvaita (qualified non-dualism), Madhva's Dvaita (dualism) — the great theological debate.
  • Philosophical breadth: the six orthodox (astika) schools accept Vedic authority; they are paired traditionally (Nyaya–Vaisheshika, Samkhya–Yoga, Mimamsa–Vedanta).
Prelims trap: Hinduism has no single founder. Astika (orthodox) = 6 schools accepting the Vedas; nastika (heterodox) = Buddhism, Jainism, Charvaka. Advaita = Shankara; Vishishtadvaita = Ramanuja; Dvaita = Madhva.

3. Buddhism

Buddhism was founded by Siddhartha Gautama (the Buddha) in the 6th c. BCE. Its "middle path" rejects both indulgence and extreme asceticism, teaching the Four Noble Truths and the Eightfold Path to end suffering.

ElementContent
Four Noble TruthsSuffering (dukkha); its cause (craving); its cessation; the path (magga)
Eightfold PathRight view, intention, speech, action, livelihood, effort, mindfulness, concentration
Three Jewels (Triratna)Buddha, Dhamma, Sangha
VehiclesHinayana (Theravada), Mahayana, Vajrayana
Sacred sitesLumbini (birth), Bodh Gaya (enlightenment), Sarnath (first sermon), Kushinagar (death)
CouncilPlace / RulerOutcome
1stRajgriha / AjatashatruCompiled Vinaya & Sutta Pitaka
2ndVaishali / KalasokaSchism — Sthaviravadins vs Mahasanghikas
3rdPataliputra / AshokaAbhidhamma compiled; missions abroad
4thKashmir / KanishkaSplit into Hinayana & Mahayana
  • Three vehicles: Hinayana/Theravada (Pali, individual liberation, Sri Lanka/SE Asia), Mahayana (bodhisattva ideal, Sanskrit, East Asia), Vajrayana (tantric, Tibet/Ladakh).
  • Councils: the 4th (Kanishka, Kashmir) formalised the Hinayana–Mahayana split; the 3rd (Ashoka, Pataliputra) sent missionaries as far as Sri Lanka.
  • Ashoka made Buddhism a world religion through his edicts and missions after the Kalinga war.
Prelims trap: 1st council = Rajgriha (Ajatashatru); 3rd = Pataliputra (Ashoka); 4th = Kashmir (Kanishka, Hinayana–Mahayana split). Sarnath = first sermon (Dharmachakra-pravartana); Bodh Gaya = enlightenment.

4. Jainism

Jainism was shaped by 24 Tirthankaras ("ford-makers") — the first being Rishabhanatha (Adinatha) and the 24th and last being Mahavira (Vardhamana), a contemporary of the Buddha. It teaches extreme non-violence and self-conquest.

ElementContent
Tirthankaras24 in all; 1st Rishabhanatha, 23rd Parshvanatha, 24th Mahavira
Three Jewels (Triratna)Right faith, right knowledge, right conduct
Five vowsAhimsa, Satya, Asteya, Aparigraha, Brahmacharya (5th added by Mahavira)
Key doctrineAnekantavada (many-sidedness) & Syadvada (conditioned predication)
SectsDigambara ("sky-clad") & Svetambara ("white-clad")
  • Mahavira (599–527 BCE) added the fifth vow (celibacy) and organised the community; Parshvanatha (23rd) had taught the first four.
  • Anekantavada — the doctrine of many-sidedness (reality has multiple aspects) — is Jainism's distinctive contribution, expressed through Syadvada ("in some respects").
  • Sects: Digambaras hold that monks own nothing (nudity) and that women cannot attain liberation without rebirth; Svetambaras wear white and disagree.
  • Councils: 1st at Pataliputra (Chandragupta–Bhadrabahu era, canon threatened by famine), 2nd at Vallabhi (final compilation of Agamas).
Prelims trap: 24 Tirthankaras; 1st = Rishabhanatha, 23rd = Parshvanatha, 24th = Mahavira (NOT the founder — the reformer/last Tirthankara). Anekantavada & Syadvada = Jain. Digambara (sky-clad) vs Svetambara (white-clad).

5. Sikhism

Sikhism was founded by Guru Nanak (1469–1539) and developed through Ten Gurus, culminating in the Guru Granth Sahib as the eternal Guru. It teaches one formless God, equality and honest living.

GuruContribution
Guru Nanak (1st)Founder; one God, equality, langar
Guru Angad (2nd)Gurmukhi script
Guru Amar Das (3rd)Strengthened langar; opposed sati/purdah
Guru Ram Das (4th)Founded Amritsar
Guru Arjan (5th)Compiled Adi Granth; built Golden Temple; first martyr
Guru Hargobind (6th)Miri-Piri (temporal + spiritual power); Akal Takht
Guru Tegh Bahadur (9th)Martyred defending religious freedom
Guru Gobind Singh (10th)Founded the Khalsa (1699); five Ks; last human Guru
  • Guru Gobind Singh created the Khalsa at Anandpur Sahib (1699) with the five Ks (Kesh, Kangha, Kara, Kachera, Kirpan) and ended the line of human Gurus, vesting authority in the Guru Granth Sahib.
  • Guru Arjan Dev compiled the Adi Granth and built the Harmandir Sahib (Golden Temple) at Amritsar; he was the first Sikh martyr.
  • Miri-Piri (Guru Hargobind) fused spiritual and temporal authority, symbolised by the Akal Takht.
Prelims trap: 10 Gurus; Guru Nanak (1st, founder) to Guru Gobind Singh (10th, Khalsa 1699). Adi Granth compiled by Guru Arjan (5th); Gurmukhi by Guru Angad (2nd); Amritsar by Guru Ram Das (4th).

6. Zoroastrian & Abrahamic Faiths

Beyond the Dharmic faiths, India peacefully absorbed several religions of West-Asian origin, most arriving by trade or as refugees and contributing richly to Indian culture.

ReligionArrival / founderNote
ZoroastrianismZarathustra; Parsis fled Persia (~8th–10th c.)Fire temples (Atash Behram); Avesta; landed at Sanjan, Gujarat
JudaismAncient; Cochin & Bene Israel JewsOne of the oldest diaspora communities; Kerala synagogues
ChristianitySt. Thomas tradition (52 CE), Malabar coastSyrian Christians of Kerala; later Portuguese & missions
IslamArab traders 7th–8th c.; conquest laterFirst mosque: Cheraman Juma Masjid, Kerala; Sufi & Sultanate
  • Zoroastrianism (Parsis): monotheistic, dualist (Ahura Mazda vs Angra Mainyu), venerates fire; the tiny Parsi community has an outsized role in industry and philanthropy (Tatas, Godrej).
  • Christianity is traditionally traced to St. Thomas the Apostle landing at Muziris (Kerala) in 52 CE — making the Syrian/Malabar Christians among the world's oldest.
  • Islam came first through peaceful Arab sea-trade to the Malabar coast (Cheraman Juma Masjid is regarded as India's oldest mosque), long before the Delhi Sultanate.
Prelims trap: Parsis follow Zoroastrianism (Zarathustra, Ahura Mazda, fire temples), landed at Sanjan, Gujarat. St. Thomas tradition (52 CE, Kerala) for Christianity. Cheraman Juma Masjid (Kerala) = reputed oldest mosque in India.

8. Councils, Sects & Symbols

Councils codified scripture and often triggered schisms; sects and symbols carry the identity of each faith. This section consolidates the most-asked sub-facts.

FaithCouncilsMain sects
Buddhism4 councils (Rajgriha, Vaishali, Pataliputra, Kashmir)Theravada (Hinayana), Mahayana, Vajrayana
Jainism2 councils (Pataliputra, Vallabhi)Digambara, Svetambara
Hinduism— (no councils; guru-parampara)Vaishnava, Shaiva, Shakta, Smarta
Islam (in India)—Sunni, Shia; Sufi orders (Chishti, Suhrawardi)
  • Buddhist schism began at the 2nd council (Vaishali) between Sthaviravadins and Mahasanghikas; the 4th (Kashmir) split the faith into Hinayana and Mahayana.
  • Jain schism into Digambara and Svetambara crystallised over monastic nudity and the fate of the canon after the Magadha famine.
  • Symbols: Buddhist Dharmachakra (wheel), Jain open palm with Ahimsa/swastika, Sikh Khanda, Zoroastrian Faravahar & fire.
GS-I hook: the councils show how oral teaching became codified scripture — and how doctrinal disputes produced the great sectarian divisions still visible in living practice.

9. Syncretism, Reform & Current Affairs

India's religions rarely stayed in silos — they borrowed, blended and reformed each other. This syncretic and reformist thread, plus constitutional secularism, is the GS-I/GS-II handle.

  • Heterodox reform: Buddhism and Jainism challenged Vedic ritual, animal sacrifice and caste rigidity in the 6th c. BCE — India's first great social-reform wave.
  • Syncretism: Bhakti-Sufi shared shrines and saints, Sikhism drew from both Hindu and Islamic ideas, and Din-i-Ilahi attempted a synthesis under Akbar.
  • Modern reform: Brahmo Samaj, Arya Samaj, Ramakrishna Mission and Ambedkar's neo-Buddhism (1956 conversion at Nagpur) reshaped religious practice.
  • Constitutional frame: Articles 25–28 guarantee freedom of religion; India's secularism means equal respect (sarva dharma sama bhava), not state atheism.
  • Living heritage: Buddhist-circuit tourism (Bodh Gaya, Sarnath), the Kartarpur Corridor, and inter-faith festivals keep this heritage current.
Current-affairs frame: religion in India is best framed as pluralism and syncretism under a secular constitution — heritage circuits, minority protections and communal-harmony debates are the recurring hooks. check for latest update or data

10. Prelims PYQs

Religion is tested as doctrine–founder–council matching. Practice MCQs below mirror the UPSC pattern; blocks without a genuine dated PYQ are honestly labelled "concept practice".

Concept practice

Q: The Fourth Buddhist Council, associated with the split into Hinayana and Mahayana, was held under the patronage of —

  • (a) Ashoka
  • (b) Ajatashatru
  • (c) Kanishka
  • (d) Kalasoka

Answer: (c) Kanishka (Kashmir). Ashoka hosted the 3rd (Pataliputra); Ajatashatru the 1st (Rajgriha); Kalasoka the 2nd (Vaishali).

Concept practice

Q: The doctrines of Anekantavada and Syadvada are associated with which religion?

  • (a) Buddhism
  • (b) Jainism
  • (c) Sikhism
  • (d) Zoroastrianism

Answer: (b) Jainism — Anekantavada (many-sidedness of reality) and Syadvada ("in some respects") are its distinctive epistemology.

Concept practice

Q: Consider the following pairs (site — event in the Buddha's life):
1. Lumbini — Birth
2. Bodh Gaya — Enlightenment
3. Sarnath — First Sermon
Which of the pairs are correctly matched?

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Answer: (d) All correct. Lumbini (birth), Bodh Gaya (enlightenment), Sarnath (first sermon); Kushinagar was the site of his death (mahaparinirvana).

Concept practice

Q: The Khalsa, with its five Ks, was instituted in 1699 by which Sikh Guru?

  • (a) Guru Nanak
  • (b) Guru Arjan Dev
  • (c) Guru Tegh Bahadur
  • (d) Guru Gobind Singh

Answer: (d) Guru Gobind Singh (10th Guru), at Anandpur Sahib. He also ended the line of human Gurus, vesting authority in the Guru Granth Sahib.

Concept practice

Q: The Parsis of India follow which religion, and where did their forebears first land?

  • (a) Judaism — Cochin
  • (b) Zoroastrianism — Sanjan (Gujarat)
  • (c) Christianity — Malabar
  • (d) Islam — Sindh

Answer: (b) Zoroastrianism; Parsi refugees from Persia landed at Sanjan in Gujarat. They venerate fire (Ahura Mazda) and follow the Avesta.

Concept practice

Q: Which of the following is NOT one of the six orthodox (astika) schools of Hindu philosophy?

  • (a) Nyaya
  • (b) Samkhya
  • (c) Charvaka
  • (d) Vedanta

Answer: (c) Charvaka is a nastika (heterodox, materialist) school, not astika. The six astika schools are Nyaya, Vaisheshika, Samkhya, Yoga, Mimamsa and Vedanta.

11. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

Model GS-I questions on Indian religions. Each carries a full skeleton — Introduction → body → Conclusion.

Model question — GS-I 15 marks · 250 words

Q: "The rise of Buddhism and Jainism in the 6th century BCE was as much a social as a religious movement." Discuss.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: The shramana faiths arose amid social and economic churn, challenging Vedic ritualism and caste as much as offering new paths to salvation.
  2. Religious causes: discontent with costly sacrifices, priestly monopoly and ritual complexity drove a search for simpler, ethical religion.
  3. Social causes (the core): the rigid varna order excluded Vaishyas and Shudras; the new faiths preached equality, opened the sangha to all, and used the people's languages (Pali, Prakrit).
  4. Economic backdrop: a rising merchant class disliked Vedic bans on usury and animal wealth loss; ahimsa and thrift suited trade — they became key patrons.
  5. Critical edge: the equality was spiritual; caste persisted socially, and both faiths later developed their own hierarchies and ritual.
  6. Conclusion: Buddhism and Jainism were reform movements that reshaped Indian society, ethics and art — not merely alternative theologies.
Model question — GS-I 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Examine the syncretic traditions in Indian religion and their contribution to communal harmony.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: India's religions have long borrowed and blended, producing syncretic traditions that remain a resource for pluralism today.
  2. Bhakti-Sufi synthesis: shared saints, shrines (dargahs venerated across faiths) and music (qawwali, kirtan) forged a common devotional idiom.
  3. Institutional syncretism (the core): Sikhism drew on Hindu and Islamic ideas; Akbar's Din-i-Ilahi and sulh-i-kul sought reconciliation; Kabir and Nanak preached one God beyond sect.
  4. Cultural fusion: Indo-Islamic architecture, Urdu, and shared festivals show blending in daily life.
  5. Critical edge: syncretism coexisted with periods of conflict; it must be nurtured, not romanticised, to counter modern communal polarisation.
  6. Conclusion: India's syncretic heritage offers a living model of coexistence — a cultural foundation for constitutional secularism.
Model question — GS-I 10 marks · 150 words

Q: How did royal patronage shape the spread of Buddhism and Jainism?

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Royal patronage transformed two ascetic sects into pan-Indian and even trans-Asian religions.
  2. Buddhism: Ashoka's edicts, missions and stupas spread it across Asia; Kanishka patronised the 4th council and Mahayana.
  3. Jainism (the core): Chandragupta Maurya's association with Bhadrabahu, and Chalukya/Ganga/Kadamba patronage in the south (Shravanabelagola) sustained it.
  4. Art & institutions: patronage funded stupas, chaityas, viharas and universities (Nalanda, Valabhi), embedding the faiths in culture.
  5. Critical edge: dependence on patronage also made the faiths vulnerable when royal support shifted, contributing to Buddhism's decline in India.
  6. Conclusion: state support was decisive — it spread the faiths but also tied their fortunes to political favour.
Model question — GS-I 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Discuss the distinctive contribution of Sikhism to Indian religious and social thought.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Sikhism, born in a Bhakti-Sufi milieu, offered a distinctive monotheistic and egalitarian path.
  2. Theology: one formless God (Ik Onkar), rejection of idolatry, ritualism and caste, and the primacy of honest work and remembrance of God.
  3. Social ideals (the core): langar (community kitchen), sangat and pangat institutionalised equality; opposition to sati and purdah advanced women's status.
  4. Miri-Piri & Khalsa: the fusion of spiritual and temporal power and the Khalsa gave the community resilience and identity.
  5. Critical edge: egalitarian ideals sometimes faced social realities, yet the reforming thrust remained strong.
  6. Conclusion: Sikhism's blend of devotion, equality and courage is a unique contribution to India's religious landscape.
