Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames India–Nepal

Nepal is tested as history (Sugauli, 1950 Treaty), geography (Kalapani, Himalayan rivers), economics (transit, hydropower) and strategy (China buffer & the sovereignty debate). Sort your prep:

  • Static/geography — ~1,850-km open border, the Kali/Mahakali river, Kalapani–Lipulekh–Limpiyadhura trijunction, Susta, Kosi/Gandak/Mahakali rivers, Lipulekh Pass. High-yield map recall.
  • Institutional/legal — 1816 Treaty of Sugauli, 1950 Treaty of Peace & Friendship (Articles 5, 6, 7), Kosi (1954) & Gandak (1959) agreements, Mahakali Treaty (1996), Pancheshwar, the EPG report, BBIN MVA.
  • Analytical (GS-II) — the "special relationship" vs sovereign-equality debate, the 2015 blockade & "big brother" perception, the 2020 map crisis, China's inroads (BRI, Kathmandu–Lhasa rail), hydropower diplomacy, the Agnipath–Gorkha issue. Needs idea + example + judgement.

Territorial note: Kalapani, Lipulekh and Limpiyadhura are integral parts of India (Pithoragarh district, Uttarakhand); India rejects Nepal's 2020 political map depicting them as Nepali territory. The India–Nepal boundary here follows India's reading of the 1816 Treaty of Sugauli.

1. Overview of the Relationship

India and Nepal share a "special relationship" unmatched anywhere in the world — an open border, unrestricted movement & work rights, shared Hindu-Buddhist civilisation, and the deep social bond captured in the phrase "Roti-Beti ka rishta" (ties of bread and marriage). Yet it is a relationship under strain, as Nepal shifts from a sentiment-driven special relationship toward a more pragmatic, sovereignty-conscious engagement, amid territorial disputes and growing Chinese influence.

  • Border: ~1,850-km open border across five Indian states (Uttarakhand, UP, Bihar, West Bengal, Sikkim) — free movement of people & goods.
  • Buffer geography: Nepal is a strategic buffer between India and China; the Himalayas historically shielded the north-Indian plains (as in 1962).
  • Economic dependence: India is Nepal's largest trade partner & source of investment; India acts as Nepal's transit country (Kolkata/Haldia & Vishakhapatnam ports) for third-country trade.
  • The strain: the 2015 unofficial "blockade", the 2020 Kalapani map crisis, and China's outreach have fed a "big brother" perception and pushed Kathmandu toward strategic diversification.
Framing line for essays/GS-II: India must move from a paternalistic "special relationship" to a partnership of "sovereign equality, mutual sensitivity and shared prosperity" — strategic patience over big-brotherism.

3. Sugauli (1816) & the 1950 Treaty

Two treaties define the legal spine of the relationship — the 1816 Treaty of Sugauli (boundary) and the 1950 Treaty of Peace & Friendship (special relationship).

3.1 Treaty of Sugauli, 1816

  • Ended the Anglo-Nepalese War (1814–16); Nepal ceded Sikkim, Kumaon, Garhwal & parts of the Terai.
  • Fixed the Kali (Mahakali) river as the western boundary — but the treaty did not pinpoint the river's source, the ambiguity behind the Kalapani dispute.

3.2 Treaty of Peace & Friendship, 1950

ArticleProvision
Article 1Mutual respect for each other's sovereignty, territorial integrity & independence.
Article 2Duty to inform each other of any serious friction with a neighbour affecting bilateral ties.
Article 5Nepal may import arms & war material through Indian territory, in consultation with India — the most contested clause.
Article 6"National treatment" for each other's citizens in economic & industrial participation.
Article 7Reciprocal rights of residence, property, trade & free movement — the legal basis of the open border.
Article 8Supersedes all prior British-era treaties with Nepal.
Article 10Remains in force indefinitely unless terminated on one year's notice.
Prelims lock: Sugauli (1816) = boundary + Kali river; 1950 Treaty = open border & national treatment (Articles 6, 7), arms-import consultation (Article 5). Nepal seeks revision citing sovereign equality.

6. Trade, Transit & Economy

India is Nepal's overwhelming economic partner — the source of most of its imports and its lifeline to the sea — a dependence that is both the relationship's strength and the source of Nepali unease.

DimensionDetail
Trade volume~Rs 1.09 trillion (2024); India is Nepal's largest trade partner & source of FDI.
DependenceOver 60% of Nepal's imports come from India; large surplus in India's favour.
TransitLandlocked Nepal routes third-country trade through Kolkata/Haldia & Vishakhapatnam ports.
India's exportsPetroleum, vehicles, machinery, cereals, cement.
Nepal's exportsTextiles, jute, agricultural products — and, prospectively, hydropower.
The blockade lessonThe 2015 unofficial blockade showed how dependence can become a grievance — and pushed Nepal to seek Chinese transit alternatives (2016 Transit & Transport Agreement).
Analytical line: the answer to Nepal's "over-dependence" grievance is not less trade but a more equal economic relationship — buy Nepal's hydropower, grant duty-free access, and de-risk transit.

8. Connectivity & Sub-regional Integration

Connectivity binds the open border into a functioning economic space — and is the arena where India competes with China's BRI outreach.

8.1 Flagship connectivity projects

  • Jaynagar–Kurtha (Bijalpura) rail: cross-border passenger & freight rail from Bihar into Nepal; direct rail freight to Biratnagar operationalised (2025 Letter of Exchange).
  • Motihari–Amlekhgunj pipeline: South Asia's first cross-border petroleum pipeline, ensuring fuel supply security for Nepal.
  • Integrated Check Posts (ICPs): Birgunj, Biratnagar etc. to ease trade & movement.
  • Transmission lines: e.g. a 132 kV line linking Tila (Solukhumbu) to Mirchaiya (Siraha) for power exchange.
  • Inland waterways: proposed river routes to give Nepal cheaper cargo access.

8.2 Sub-regional frameworks

  • BBIN (Bangladesh–Bhutan–India–Nepal): the Motor Vehicles Agreement (2015) for seamless vehicle movement — sub-regionalism after SAARC's paralysis.
  • BIMSTEC & SAARC: Nepal hosts the SAARC Secretariat (Kathmandu) & participates in BIMSTEC energy & connectivity.
Why it matters: credible Indian connectivity (rail, pipeline, power) is the most effective counter to China's Kathmandu–Lhasa railway pitch — India must deliver faster & on time.

9. Defence & the Gorkha Question

Defence ties are deep but sensitive — anchored by the unique Gorkha tradition, yet clouded by strategic mistrust.

  • Exercise Surya Kiran: the annual bilateral army exercise — the flagship of defence cooperation.
  • Gorkha recruitment: Nepali Gorkhas serve in the Indian Army under the 1947 Tripartite Agreement (India–Nepal–UK) — a living people-to-people & strategic bond.
  • Agnipath friction: Nepal suspended fresh Gorkha recruitment citing concerns that the Agnipath (short-service) model conflicts with the 1947 agreement — a fresh irritant. check for latest update or data
  • Honorary tradition: the reciprocal conferring of honorary Chief-of-Army-Staff rank — a symbol of the special bond.
  • Equipment & training: India supplies vehicles & equipment (e.g. 2,000 vehicles to Nepal Police) and offers training slots.
GS-II hook: the Agnipath–Gorkha standoff shows how a domestic Indian reform can ripple into a bilateral relationship — a caution on the need for prior consultation with a "special" partner.

10. Strategic Significance of Nepal for India

Nepal is not just a neighbour but a strategic buffer, a spiritual anchor and a security frontier. Its significance is layered:

Security & open border~1,850 km open border with 5 Indian states (Uttarakhand, UP, Bihar, West Bengal, Sikkim) — free movement but also a soft underbelly for smuggling, fake currency & infiltration.

11. The 1950 Treaty Revision Debate & the EPG

The 1950 Treaty of Peace & Friendship is the foundational document — and also the most contested. Nepal sees parts of it as unequal & outdated.

  • Nepal's grievance: Articles restricting Nepal's sovereign right to buy arms freely, and the "national treatment" clauses, are seen as diluting sovereignty in a changed world.
  • India's view: the Treaty gives Nepali citizens huge benefits in India (work, property, movement) — benefits Nepal risks losing if the Treaty is scrapped rather than updated.
  • Eminent Persons Group (EPG): a bilateral group (2016) of experts tasked to review & recommend updates to the entire relationship, including the 1950 Treaty.
  • The EPG deadlock: the EPG finalised a consensus report (2018), but India has not formally received it — a lingering irritant Nepal repeatedly raises. check for latest update or data
GS-II hook: the EPG report limbo is a case study in how process (who receives a report, when) can itself become a diplomatic flashpoint.

12. Key Issues & Irritants

Despite the closeness, several recurring irritants strain the relationship:

  • Boundary disputes: Kalapani–Lipulekh–Limpiyadhura (western) & Susta (southern, on the Gandak) — Nepal's 2020 political map claiming Indian territory was the sharpest recent flare-up.
  • "Big brother" perception: a recurring Nepali sentiment that India is overbearing — weaponised by China & by domestic Nepali politics.
  • The 2015 blockade memory: the months-long obstruction at the border during Nepal's post-earthquake constitution crisis — India was blamed, souring public opinion & pushing Nepal toward China.
  • Madhesi question: the plains-origin Madhesis (ethnically close to India) & their demand for fair representation in Nepal's 2015 Constitution — a sensitive internal issue India is accused of leaning into.
  • Anti-India nationalism: Nepali domestic politics often runs on an anti-India plank, especially before elections.
  • Agnipath–Gorkha standoff: suspended recruitment (see §9).
Pattern: most irritants share one root — asymmetry. A giant neighbour and a smaller state, where every Indian action is magnified in Nepali politics.

13. The China Factor

China's steady push into Nepal is the single biggest strategic variable reshaping India–Nepal ties.

  • BRI entry: Nepal joined the Belt & Road Initiative (2017); the flagship Trans-Himalayan Multi-Dimensional Connectivity Network (rail, road, tunnels) aims to end Nepal's India-only transit dependence.
  • Ports & transit: China offered Nepal access to its sea & land ports (2016 transit treaty) — a direct answer to the 2015 blockade.
  • Political inroads: the Communist parties' ties with the CPC; China's active role in brokering Nepali coalition politics.
  • Boundary encroachment: reports of Chinese encroachment on Nepal's northern border (Humla) — a sign China's own record with Nepal is not clean.
  • India's counter: delivery-focused diplomacy — faster project completion (rail, pipeline, power lines), power-trade access to India's market, & leveraging the unmatched people-to-people bond.

15. Way Forward

  • Deliver, don't lecture: the surest counter to China is timely, visible project completion — rail to Kathmandu, cross-border transmission lines, the Pancheshwar project.
  • Energy partnership: deepen power trade — buy Nepal's hydropower, help build capacity; turn water from an irritant into the anchor of the relationship.
  • Update the 1950 Treaty: receive & act on the EPG report; recast the treaty as a partnership of equals.
  • Boundary mechanism: resolve Kalapani & Susta via a structured, technical, non-public dialogue.
  • People-to-people first: leverage the unmatched "Roti-Beti", pilgrimage & Gorkha bonds — India's one advantage China can never replicate.
  • Consistency & sensitivity: a steady, respectful long-game that de-hyphenates Nepal from domestic anti-India politics.
One-line thesis: India's Nepal policy must shift from "presumed influence" to "earned partnership" — delivery, respect & consistency.

Mains GS-II 2016

"Terrorist activities and mutual distrust have clouded India–Pakistan relations. To what extent the use of soft power like sports and cultural exchanges could help generate goodwill between the two countries?" — Apply the same soft-power lens to India–Nepal: how far can people-to-people & cultural ties offset political distrust?

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: Define soft power (Nye); note India–Nepal share the deepest soft-power reservoir in the region — "Roti-Beti", pilgrimage, language & the Gorkha bond.
  2. Body — where soft power works: open border & kinship; shared Hindu-Buddhist heritage (Lumbini, Janakpur, Pashupatinath); Gorkha tradition; education & health access; disaster relief (2015 earthquake goodwill).
  3. Body — limits: soft power cannot fix the boundary dispute, the "big brother" perception or the China tilt; the 2015 blockade shows one hard-power misstep can erase years of goodwill.
  4. Body — synthesis: soft power must be paired with delivery (projects, energy) & sensitive diplomacy — necessary but not sufficient.
  5. Conclusion: soft power is India's one irreplaceable advantage in Nepal — its value lies in cushioning, not resolving, political friction.
Mains GS-II 2022

"Clean energy is the order of the day." Describe briefly India's changing policy towards climate change in various international fora in the context of geopolitical realities. — Locate India–Nepal hydropower cooperation within this clean-energy & regional-integration story.

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: India's clean-energy pivot (Panchamrit, ISA); regional power grids as a climate + geopolitics instrument.
  2. Body — Nepal's role: ~83,000 MW hydropower potential; power-trade agreements; India importing Nepali hydropower & enabling Nepal–Bangladesh transit — a clean, regional grid.
  3. Body — geopolitics: energy interdependence anchors Nepal to India & counters the China tilt; but the 2015 blockade & distrust show energy ties need trust to hold.
  4. Body — challenges: stalled projects (Pancheshwar), financing, transmission gaps, Nepali sovereignty sensitivities on Indian-built dams.
  5. Conclusion: Himalayan hydropower can make South Asia a clean-energy community — if India delivers projects on time & treats Nepal as an equal partner.
Mains GS-II 2017

"China is using its economic relations and positive trade surplus as tools to develop potential military power status in Asia." In the light of this statement, discuss its impact on India as her neighbour. — Illustrate with China's growing footprint in Nepal.

Full answer skeleton

  2. Body — China in Nepal: BRI & Trans-Himalayan network; port/transit access (2016); investment, aid & party ties; ending Nepal's India-only transit dependence.
  3. Body — impact on India: erosion of transit leverage; weakened Himalayan buffer; anti-India politics fuelled; strategic encirclement ("string" on land).

  • Nepal's 2020 political map claimed the areas — sharpest flare-up
Treaties & water:
  • Treaty of Peace & Friendship 1950 — open border, national treatment (contested)
  • Kosi 1954 · Gandak 1959 · Mahakali 1996
  • Pancheshwar ~6,000 MW on Mahakali; ~83,000 MW potential
Cooperation & connectivity:
  • India = largest trade partner & FDI source; Nepal uses Indian ports for third-country trade
  • Jaynagar–Kurtha rail (Bihar); Motihari–Amlekhgunj pipeline (petroleum, 2019)
  • BBIN · power-trade & Nepal–Bangladesh transit via Indian grid
  • Defence: Surya Kiran; Gorkha under 1947 Tripartite Agreement
Strategic & current:
  • "Roti-Beti" ties; Lumbini, Janakpur, Pashupatinath, Kailash-Mansarovar (Lipulekh)
  • 1950 Treaty / EPG report limbo (2018, not formally received)
  • China factor (BRI, Trans-Himalayan, transit); 2015 blockade memory; Agnipath–Gorkha standoff
