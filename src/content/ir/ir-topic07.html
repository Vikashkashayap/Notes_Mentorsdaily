Topic 7: Phases of India's Foreign Policy

How India's engagement with the world evolved from 1947 to 2026 — the story of a nation moving from optimistic non-alignment to hard-nosed strategic autonomy and today's energetic multi-alignment. Using S. Jaishankar's influential six-era framework (from The India Way), this book-depth file walks through each phase — its context, doctrine, key events and leaders — then draws out the drivers of change and the enduring continuities (autonomy, developmentalism, civilisational identity) that thread them together, with a timeline, comparison tables and real dated PYQs + full model answers.

The one-line arc to remember

India's foreign policy is a single story of protecting strategic autonomy while pursuing development and security, told in changing grammar. The ends have stayed remarkably constant — independence of judgement, developmental priority, a claim to civilisational stature. Only the means shifted with the world: non-alignment when the Cold War forced a binary; Soviet tilt when 1971 demanded a partner; economic diplomacy & multi-alignment once 1991 opened the economy and a rising China reshaped Asia. Jaishankar's six eras are simply chapters in that one arc — do not memorise them as disconnected boxes; read them as an evolving answer to the recurring question: how does a civilisational-sized, developing power stay independent in a world it does not control?

1. Why Study Foreign Policy in Phases

Foreign policy is not a fixed doctrine handed down in 1947; it is a living response to a changing distribution of power, a changing economy and changing leadership. Dividing seven-plus decades into phases lets us see pattern and change together — what endured and what adapted.

Ways scholars periodise Indian FP

  • By leadership — Nehru, Shastri, Indira, Rajiv, Rao, Vajpayee, Manmohan, Modi.
  • By the global system — Cold War (bipolar) → unipolar moment → emerging multipolarity.
  • By doctrine — non-alignment → strategic autonomy → multi-alignment / issue-based coalitions.
  • Jaishankar's six eras — a synthesis of all three, and the most exam-useful framework.
Why UPSC cares: “Trace the evolution…”, “continuity and change…” and “relevance of non-alignment today” are perennial GS-II templates. A clean phase-map lets you write a structured, chronological-yet-analytical answer instead of a jumble of events.

3. Phase I — Optimistic Non-Alignment (1946–1962)

The founding era. A newly independent, resource-poor India chose to stay out of the Cold War blocs and build peace through moral diplomacy.

  • Doctrine: Non-Alignment — refuse permanent membership of either the US-led or Soviet-led bloc; judge each issue on merit.
  • Nehru's premises: military blocs breed war; a poor nation must guard scarce resources for development, not arms; Asia and Africa must have an independent voice.
  • Signature moves: Panchsheel (1954) with China; Bandung Conference (1955); founding pillar of NAM (Belgrade 1961); recognition of PRC and advocacy of its UN seat.
  • Instruments: moral persuasion, mediation (Korea 1953, Indo-China), leadership of the newly decolonised world.
  • Blind spot: under-investment in hard power and a naive read of Chinese intent — "Hindi-Chini Bhai-Bhai" masked the boundary dispute.

Why it ended: the 1962 China war shattered the assumption that goodwill and non-alignment alone could secure India. Idealism met the limits of a weak military.

4. Phase II — Realism & Recovery (1962–1971)

A chastened decade of rebuilding capability and hedging after the 1962 shock.

  • Shift: from pure moralism toward hard-power realism — rapid army modernisation, mountain divisions, nuclear thinking begins.
  • Twin pressures: a hostile China plus a US-armed Pakistan (1965 war) forced India to seek reliable partners.
  • Tilt toward Moscow: the USSR emerged as a dependable supplier of arms and diplomatic cover; the US-Pakistan-China axis pushed India leftward.
  • Domestic backdrop: Nehru's death (1964), Shastri's Tashkent (1966), food crises — foreign policy became tied to survival and self-reliance.

Why it ended: the 1971 Bangladesh crisis pushed India to formalise the Soviet partnership and act decisively, opening a new assertive phase.

5. Phase III — Regional Assertion (1971–1991)

India's most self-confident Cold-War phase — a clear South Asian pre-eminence backed by the Soviet alignment.

  • 1971 war & Bangladesh: decisive military victory; the Indo-Soviet Treaty (Aug 1971) deterred US-China pressure — India as the subcontinent's security manager.
  • Strategic milestones: Pokhran-I "peaceful nuclear explosion" (1974); Sikkim's merger (1975); the "Indira Doctrine" of keeping outside powers out of the neighbourhood.
  • Interventions: IPKF in Sri Lanka (1987–90); Operation Cactus in Maldives (1988) — regional policing role.
  • Costs: over-dependence on a declining USSR; friction with smaller neighbours who resented "big-brother" behaviour.

Why it ended: the Soviet collapse (1991) plus a balance-of-payments crisis destroyed both the strategic anchor and the economic model overnight.

6. Phase IV — Safeguarding Autonomy (1991–1998)

The pivot from a socialist, Soviet-anchored posture to a market-reforming, multi-directional one.

  • Trigger: 1991 twin shock — USSR gone, forex crisis. India liberalised (LPG reforms) and had to earn its security in a US-dominated unipolar world.
  • Look East Policy (1992): Narasimha Rao reoriented India toward ASEAN and East Asia for trade and strategic depth.
  • Mending fences: outreach to Israel (full ties 1992), the US, and the Gulf; economic diplomacy replaced ideological posturing.
  • Core task: preserve strategic autonomy while integrating with a Western-led global economy — hedge, don't bandwagon.

Why it ended: Pokhran-II (1998) transformed India into a declared nuclear-weapon state, changing the terms of its global engagement.

7. Phase V — Rising Balancer (1998–2013)

India converts economic growth and nuclear status into strategic weight, balancing among all major powers.

  • Nuclear mainstreaming: post-Pokhran-II sanctions gave way to the India-US Civil Nuclear Deal (2005–08) and the NSG waiver — India accepted as a responsible nuclear power outside the NPT.
  • Multi-alignment begins: simultaneous deepening with the US, Russia, EU, Japan, and Israel; issue-based coalitions replace fixed blocs.
  • Economic diplomacy: 7–9% growth made India a courted market; rise of BRICS, G20, IBSA.
  • Neighbourhood & East: Look East matures; SAARC and connectivity push; growing India-China trade alongside boundary caution.

Why it ended: a more assertive China, a shifting global balance, and a new government (2014) ushered in a more energetic, front-foot diplomacy.

8. Phase VI — Energetic Engagement (2014–present)

Jaishankar's own era: proactive, interest-driven, multi-aligned diplomacy that seeks to shape outcomes, not merely react.

  • Multi-alignment as strategy: deepen the US partnership (Quad, defence foundational pacts) while retaining Russia (defence, energy) and courting the Gulf — "engage all, ally with none."
  • Neighbourhood First + Act East + SAGAR: connectivity, vaccine diplomacy (Vaccine Maitri), HADR, and Indian Ocean security.
  • Indo-Pacific & minilaterals: Quad revival, I2U2, IMEC, Global South leadership (Voice of Global South Summits, African Union into G20 during India's 2023 presidency).
  • Strategic autonomy tested: continued Russian oil imports post-Ukraine (2022) despite Western pressure — interests over alignment.
  • Self-image: a "leading power," a "Vishwamitra" / Vishwa-Guru shaping the global agenda, not a rule-taker.

Defining logic: convert size and growth into influence; be a decisive, indispensable pole in a multipolar world.

9. What Stays Constant — Enduring Principles

Phases change tactics; a stable core of principles runs through all six eras.

Fig 7.2 — The doctrinal wrapper changes each era, but six principles form an unbroken core across 1946–present.
  • Strategic autonomy: from Non-Alignment to multi-alignment — the label changes, the refusal of bloc-loyalty does not.
  • Development-first: every phase subordinated foreign policy to national economic needs.
  • Territorial integrity & sovereignty: non-negotiable across all governments.
  • Faith in multilateralism: though the target shifted from NAM/G-77 to G20/Quad/reformed-UN.
  • Neighbourhood as priority: the "peaceful periphery" instinct, even when execution faltered.
  • Civilisational self-image: Panchsheel, Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam, moral voice — a constant rhetorical and normative thread.

10. What Drives the Shifts

Transitions between phases are not random — a recurring set of drivers forces each doctrinal reset.

Fig 7.3 — Three drivers — external shock, systemic power shift, and domestic change — converge to force a doctrinal rethink, producing the next phase.
  • External shocks: 1962, 1971, 1991, 1998 — each war or crisis exposed the limits of the prevailing doctrine.
  • Systemic power shifts: bipolar Cold War → unipolar 1990s → emerging multipolarity — India recalibrated to each structure.
  • Domestic transformation: the 1991 reforms, leadership changes (Nehru→Indira→Rao→Vajpayee→Modi), and economic capacity redefined what India could pursue abroad.
  • Rising capability: as GDP, military and technological weight grew, ambition scaled from survival to shaping the order.

11. From Non-Alignment to Strategic Autonomy

The single most examined thread — how the founding idea evolved without abandoning its core.

DimensionNon-Alignment (Cold War)Strategic Autonomy / Multi-Alignment (today)
ContextBipolar, two rigid blocsMultipolar, fluid coalitions
MethodStay out of both blocsEngage all poles, ally with none
PostureReactive, defensive, moralProactive, interest-driven, transactional
InstrumentsMoral suasion, NAM, G-77Quad, BRICS, SCO, I2U2, IMEC, minilaterals
ConstantRefusal to sacrifice decision-making freedom to any external power
  • Continuity: both reject dependence and preserve freedom of choice — the DNA is identical.
  • Change: from abstention (staying away) to engagement (partnering everyone selectively) — a shift from passive to active hedging.
  • Jaishankar's framing: "the willingness to take positions on the merits" — multi-alignment is non-alignment 2.0 for a stronger India.

12. Significance of the Phase Lens

  • Analytical clarity: converts 75+ years of events into a learnable arc — ideal for Mains structuring.
  • Continuity vs change debate: lets you argue that Indian FP is evolutionary, not a series of ruptures.
  • Explains "why now": shows current assertiveness as the product of accumulated capability, not a sudden break.
  • Links to theory (T06): the arc reads as a move from idealism (Nehruvian) toward pragmatic realism — a real-world test of IR paradigms.
  • Policy memory: each phase's failure (1962 naivety, over-dependence on USSR) informs present caution and diversification.

13. Challenges in the Current Phase

  • China's rise: an assertive, powerful neighbour on a live boundary (Galwan 2020, Doklam 2017) — the central strategic challenge.
  • Balancing US and Russia: deepening US ties while retaining Russian defence/energy dependence amid Ukraine-war pressure.
  • Neighbourhood volatility: political churn in Bangladesh, Maldives, Nepal, Sri Lanka, Myanmar; Chinese inroads via BRI.
  • Global economic headwinds: protectionism, technology-decoupling, supply-chain security, energy transition.
  • Reform deficit: stalled UNSC reform; a Western-built order slow to accommodate a rising India.
  • Capacity gaps: a small diplomatic corps relative to India's expanding global footprint.

14. Way Forward

  • Sustain multi-alignment: keep issue-based coalitions flexible; avoid over-commitment to any single pole.
  • Comprehensive national power: grow economic, military, technological and soft-power capacity as the base of influence.
  • Neighbourhood delivery: complete connectivity and development projects on time to counter Chinese competition credibly.
  • Global South leadership: institutionalise the "Voice of the Global South" role to shape norms and reform agendas.
  • Capacity building: expand the foreign service, invest in strategic research, and modernise economic diplomacy.
  • Guard strategic autonomy: diversify defence and energy sources to keep decision-making free.

15. The Sixth Phase in 2025–26

The current era is being written in real time — recent headlines are the live evidence of “Energetic Engagement.” 2025–26

Multi-alignment under strain

Continued Russian-oil purchases alongside deeper US defence ties (iCET, jet-engine co-production) test how far “engage all, ally none” can stretch. Live

Global South leadership

Follow-through from India's G20 presidency — African Union's permanent seat, “Voice of Global South” summits — institutionalising the bridging role. 2025–26

Connectivity contest

IMEC and IORA/SAGAR push against China's BRI in the neighbourhood and Indian Ocean — delivery is the new currency of influence. Live

Neighbourhood churn

Political shifts in Bangladesh, Maldives and Nepal test the “Neighbourhood First” instinct that runs through every phase. Live

China management

Post-Galwan LAC disengagement steps and border-management talks show the Realist caution that has defined every phase since 1962. 2025–26

Diaspora as strategic asset

A record-large, high-profile diaspora leveraged for investment, remittances and soft power — a 21st-century instrument absent in early phases. Live

Exam hook: Read any 2026 foreign-policy headline as a data-point in Phase VI — then ask which enduring principle (autonomy, development, peaceful periphery) it serves and which driver (China's rise, multipolarity) it responds to. That framing lifts a factual answer into an analytical one.

16. Prelims PYQ Practice

A note on sourcing: UPSC Prelims rarely labels a question “phases of foreign policy”; the milestones (Panchsheel, Bandung, NAM, Look East, nuclear deal) instead surface as fact-based MCQs. The items below are UPSC-pattern concept-practice questions built on those exact milestones — honestly labelled as practice, not passed off as dated past papers.

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

Q. The Panchsheel Agreement, embodying the Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence, was first formally articulated in 1954 between India and

  • (a) the Soviet Union
  • (b) China
  • (c) Egypt
  • (d) Yugoslavia

Answer: (b). Panchsheel featured in the 1954 India-China agreement on Tibet; it later became a template for NAM diplomacy.

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

Q. Consider the following events of India's foreign policy and arrange them in chronological order:
1. Bandung Conference  2. First NAM Summit at Belgrade  3. Panchsheel Agreement  4. Pokhran-I nuclear test

  • (a) 3 – 1 – 2 – 4
  • (b) 1 – 3 – 2 – 4
  • (c) 3 – 2 – 1 – 4
  • (d) 1 – 2 – 3 – 4

Answer: (a). Panchsheel (1954) → Bandung (1955) → Belgrade NAM (1961) → Pokhran-I (1974).

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

Q. India's “Look East Policy”, launched in the early 1990s, was primarily a response to

  • (a) the 1962 war with China
  • (b) the end of the Cold War and the 1991 economic crisis, prompting outreach to ASEAN and East Asia
  • (c) the formation of SAARC
  • (d) the Indo-Soviet Treaty of 1971

Answer: (b). Narasimha Rao launched Look East (1992) to integrate India with a booming East/Southeast Asia after the Soviet collapse; it later became “Act East.”

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

Q. The Indo-Soviet Treaty of Peace, Friendship and Cooperation was signed in the context of which event?

  • (a) The 1962 Sino-Indian War
  • (b) The 1965 India-Pakistan War
  • (c) The 1971 Bangladesh Liberation crisis
  • (d) Pokhran-II, 1998

Answer: (c). The August 1971 treaty gave India strategic cover against US-China pressure during the Bangladesh war.

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

Q. In the phrase “strategic autonomy”, as used in India's contemporary foreign policy, the term is best understood as

  • (a) complete isolation from all major powers
  • (b) the freedom to take independent decisions and engage multiple powers without binding alliance commitments
  • (c) permanent military alliance with a single superpower
  • (d) abandonment of multilateral institutions

Answer: (b). Strategic autonomy is non-alignment's successor idea — engagement with all, subordination to none.

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

Q. The India-US Civil Nuclear Deal and the subsequent NSG waiver, which mainstreamed India as a nuclear power, belong to which phase of India's foreign policy?

  • (a) Optimistic Non-Alignment (1946–62)
  • (b) Regional Assertion (1971–91)
  • (c) Rising Balancer (1998–2013)
  • (d) Realism & Recovery (1962–71)

Answer: (c). The 2005–08 nuclear deal and NSG waiver were the signature achievements of the “Rising Balancer” phase.

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

Q. “Act East Policy”, “Neighbourhood First” and “SAGAR” are diplomatic constructs most closely associated with which phase of Indian foreign policy?

  • (a) Energetic Engagement (2014–present)
  • (b) Safeguarding Autonomy (1991–98)
  • (c) Optimistic Non-Alignment (1946–62)
  • (d) Regional Assertion (1971–91)

Answer: (a). All three are hallmarks of the current phase; SAGAR = Security and Growth for All in the Region (2015).

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

Q. Which one of the following correctly pairs a phase of Indian foreign policy with the external shock that ended it?

  • (a) Optimistic Non-Alignment — 1991 forex crisis
  • (b) Regional Assertion — 1991 Soviet collapse and forex crisis
  • (c) Rising Balancer — 1962 China war
  • (d) Safeguarding Autonomy — 1971 Bangladesh war

Answer: (b). Regional Assertion (1971–91) ended with the Soviet collapse and the 1991 balance-of-payments crisis. (Non-Alignment ended with 1962; Safeguarding Autonomy with Pokhran-II 1998.)

Prelims — anticipated themes

Anticipated focus areas: chronological sequencing of milestones (Panchsheel, Bandung, NAM, Look/Act East, nuclear deal); matching a doctrine/construct to its phase; the meaning of “strategic autonomy”, “multi-alignment”, “SAGAR”, “Vaccine Maitri”; and the shock that closed each era (1962, 1971, 1991, 1998).

Prep tip: memorise a one-line-per-phase table with dates, doctrine and closing shock. UPSC favours sequencing and pair-matching over abstract definitions here.

17. Mains PYQ Practice

A note on sourcing: UPSC GS-II sets few questions labelled “phases”, but it repeatedly probes the continuity-vs-change and non-alignment-to-autonomy debates that this chapter frames. Below is one genuine dated GS-II question that turns directly on the phase shift, followed by UPSC-pattern model questions (honestly labelled, not fabricated past papers), each with a full skeleton.

UPSC GS-II 2019

Q. “The long-sustained image of India as a leader of the oppressed and marginalised nations has disappeared on account of its new found role in the emerging global order.” Elaborate. 15 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: This question is a direct test of the phase shift — from the “Optimistic Non-Alignment / Regional Assertion” identity as champion of the decolonised world (NAM, G-77, anti-apartheid) to the “Energetic Engagement” role of a courted major power.
  2. The change: Rise as an emerging economy; strategic convergence with the West (Quad, iCET); pursuit of UNSC/NSG membership; interest-based, selective positions — a more Realist, status-seeking posture than the moral-crusader phases.
  3. Why the image has not disappeared: “Voice of Global South” summits, Vaccine Maitri, disaster relief, African Union into G20, climate-justice and reformed-multilateralism advocacy — the leadership role is re-cast, not abandoned.
  4. Continuity of the core: across every phase, strategic autonomy and a civilisational self-image endure; India now bridges North and South rather than choosing one.
  5. Conclusion: The moral-crusader image has evolved into a pragmatic bridging role — continuity of ends (autonomy, solidarity), change of means (engagement over abstention).
UPSC GS-II — model question

Q. “India's foreign policy since 1947 shows more continuity than change.” Critically examine using the phase framework. 15 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: State the thesis — tactics changed across six phases, but a core of principles endured.
  2. Evidence of change: Non-Alignment → multi-alignment; moral suasion → hard power; socialist economy → market integration; reactive → proactive.
  3. Evidence of continuity: strategic autonomy, development-first, territorial integrity, peaceful periphery, faith in (reformed) multilateralism, civilisational ethos — the Fig 7.2 core.
  4. Synthesis: change is in instruments and posture; continuity is in ends and identity. Each phase is an adaptation of a stable core to a new structure.
  5. Conclusion: Indian FP is evolutionary, not revolutionary — continuity of purpose expressed through changing means.
UPSC GS-II — model question

Q. Trace the evolution from Non-Alignment to Multi-Alignment. Does the shift mark a break with, or a continuation of, Nehruvian foreign policy? 15 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: Define both; state that multi-alignment is “non-alignment 2.0” for a stronger India.
  2. Non-alignment logic: bipolar context, resource-scarce state, moral voice, abstention from blocs.
  3. Multi-alignment logic: multipolar context, capable state, active engagement with all poles (Quad, BRICS, SCO, I2U2) — hedging, not abstention.
  4. Continuity: both refuse binding alliances and guard freedom of choice — the Nehruvian DNA of autonomy persists.
  5. Conclusion: a continuation in ends (autonomy) with a transformation in means (engagement) — adaptation, not repudiation.
UPSC GS-II — model question

Q. How did the 1962 and 1991 crises each reshape India's foreign policy? Compare their long-term impact. 15 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: Both were external shocks that forced a doctrinal rethink (Fig 7.3), ending one phase and opening another.
  2. 1962: shattered naive non-alignment; triggered military modernisation, a Soviet tilt, and hard-power realism (Phase II).
  3. 1991: ended the Soviet-anchored model; forced economic liberalisation, Look East, and multi-directional diplomacy (Phase IV).
  4. Comparison: 1962 was a security shock (rethink of power); 1991 was a systemic-economic shock (rethink of alignment and economy). 1991's impact was more structural and enduring.
  5. Conclusion: both prove the pattern — Indian FP resets when a shock exposes the limits of the prevailing doctrine.
UPSC GS-II — model question

Q. “Strategic autonomy is being tested as never before in a sharply polarised world.” Examine with reference to India's current phase. 15 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: Define strategic autonomy; note that Phase VI operates amid US-China rivalry and the Ukraine war.
  2. Pressures: Western push to align against Russia/China; sanctions and CAATSA risk; technology-decoupling; neighbourhood competition with China.
  3. India's balancing: Russian oil and S-400 alongside Quad and iCET; issue-based coalitions; refusal to join blocs — interests over alignment.
  4. Risks & limits: credibility costs, over-stretch, dependence gaps (defence, tech); the need to diversify.
  5. Conclusion: autonomy survives by resting on comprehensive national power and diversification — the enduring core adapting to a polarised structure.
Mains GS-II — anticipated themes

Anticipated angles: continuity-vs-change across the six phases; non-alignment→strategic-autonomy debate; the role of external shocks in resetting doctrine; India as a “leading power” and Voice of the Global South; and how enduring principles (autonomy, development, peaceful periphery) survive each transition.

Answer skeleton to keep ready: Intro (phase framework in one line) → change (instruments/posture) → continuity (Fig 7.2 core) → driver analysis (Fig 7.3) → conclusion (evolutionary, not revolutionary). Anchor every claim to a dated milestone.

18. Quick Revision

  • The frame: Jaishankar's six eras — a learnable arc for 75+ years of Indian FP.
  • Phase I (1946–62): Optimistic Non-Alignment — Panchsheel, Bandung, NAM; ended by the 1962 China war.
  • Phase II (1962–71): Realism & Recovery — military build-up, Soviet tilt; ended by 1971.
  • Phase III (1971–91): Regional Assertion — 1971 victory, Indo-Soviet Treaty, Pokhran-I, Indira Doctrine; ended by the 1991 Soviet collapse + forex crisis.
  • Phase IV (1991–98): Safeguarding Autonomy — LPG reforms, Look East; ended by Pokhran-II.
  • Phase V (1998–2013): Rising Balancer — nuclear mainstreaming, US nuclear deal, multi-alignment begins.
  • Phase VI (2014–now): Energetic Engagement — Quad, Act East, SAGAR, Global South leadership, multi-alignment as strategy.
  • Constant core (Fig 7.2): strategic autonomy, development-first, territorial integrity, peaceful periphery, multilateralism, civilisational ethos.
  • Drivers of shift (Fig 7.3): external shock + systemic power shift + domestic change → doctrinal rethink → new phase.
  • The thread: Non-Alignment → Strategic Autonomy — continuity of ends, change of means.

Mains Angle

Frame the arc as continuity of ends, change of means: instruments and posture evolve; strategic autonomy and civilisational identity endure.

Use Jaishankar's “six eras” as your organising spine, and anchor every claim to a dated milestone.

Drivers of a Phase Shift

  • External shock (war/crisis/collapse)
  • Systemic power shift (bi→uni→multipolar)
  • Domestic change (economy/leadership)
  • Rising national capability

Converge → doctrinal rethink → new phase.

Common Confusions

  • Non-alignment ≠ neutrality — active, not passive.
  • Non-alignment ≠ isolation — engaged, not withdrawn.
  • Multi-alignment ≠ opportunism — interest-based, principled autonomy.
  • Look East ≠ Act East — 1992 outreach vs 2014 action-oriented upgrade.
  • Phase change ≠ principle change — means shift, core endures.
