Topic 2: Determinants & Institutions of India's Foreign Policy

If Topic 1 was the "why" of India's foreign policy, this is the "who" and "how". Conceptual Clarity — Why this Chapter Matters

UPSC tests this "machinery" chapter in three distinct ways. Knowing the question-type decides how deep you go:

  • Definitional / static — "Foreign affairs is in which List?", "NSC was set up in…", "Article 253 deals with…". Memorise the constitutional articles, List entries and body-founding years.
  • Statement-elimination — statements on treaty ratification, the NSA's role, or whether states can sign treaties — where one wrong word flips a statement.
  • Applied / analytical — Mains GS-II on reforming the MEA, the growing PMO-isation of foreign policy, or cooperative federalism in diplomacy. Needs the institutional map + a reform view.

Focus on four high-frequency anchors: the internal vs external determinants split, the MEA–PMO–NSC triangle, the constitutional basis of treaty-making (Art 51, 253, Union List), and new non-state actors (states, think-tanks, Track-II).

1. Determinants — the Concept

Determinants are the underlying forces that shape and constrain a state's foreign-policy choices. They are grouped into internal (domestic capacity & identity) and external (the international environment) factors that together set the "possibility frontier" within which decision-makers operate.

  • Determinants are relatively durable (geography, history) or slow-changing (economy, global order) — they explain continuity.
  • Institutions and leadership convert these determinants into concrete decisions — they explain agency & change.
  • Together: determinants set the stage; institutions write the script.
UPSC Angle: a common Mains framing is "determinants vs decision-makers". Strong answers show that geography & economy constrain, but leadership & institutions choose — so both structure and agency matter.

3. External Determinants

External determinants are the features of the international system India cannot control but must respond to.

UPSC Angle: the sharpest external determinant today is the US–China rivalry. It simultaneously creates opportunity (Western tech/QUAD) and risk (Chinese pressure on the border & neighbourhood) — the core reason multi-alignment replaced simple non-alignment.

4. Decision-Making Architecture

India's foreign-policy machinery is a pyramid: political apex (PM/Cabinet) → security coordination (NSC/CCS) → executing ministry (MEA) → missions abroad, with Parliament providing oversight and non-state actors feeding in.

Fig 2.2 — A PM-led executive pyramid executes foreign policy; Parliament oversees, non-state actors feed in.
  • PM/PMO has become the dominant node ("PMO-isation") — summit diplomacy & personal rapport now drive major initiatives.
  • MEA is the nodal implementing ministry, but line ministries (Defence, Commerce, Finance, Jal Shakti for river treaties) increasingly co-shape "issue-based" diplomacy.
  • Coordination gaps between MEA and line ministries are a recurring reform theme.

5. Ministry of External Affairs & the IFS

5.1 The MEA

  • Nodal ministry for the conduct of India's external relations; headed politically by the External Affairs Minister (EAM) and administratively by the Foreign Secretary (FS).
  • Organised into territorial divisions (region-wise) and functional divisions (UN, disarmament, economic diplomacy, consular, passports/CPV, protocol, public diplomacy).
  • Runs India's network of ~200+ diplomatic & consular missions abroad (embassies, high commissions, consulates, permanent missions).

5.2 The Indian Foreign Service (IFS)

FeatureDetail
Created1946 (Cabinet decision); a Central (Group A) Service
RecruitmentThrough UPSC Civil Services Examination
RoleStaff missions & MEA HQ; represent, report, negotiate, protect nationals
Size concernSmall cadre (~a few thousand officers) relative to India's global footprint — a key reform debate
Prelims trap: the IFS handles diplomacy abroad; it is distinct from the Indian Foreign Service (Branch B) and from the domestic IAS/IPS. The MEA — not the Ministry of Home or Defence — is the nodal foreign-affairs ministry.

5.3 Reform Agenda

  • Expand cadre strength & lateral entry of domain experts (economists, technologists).
  • Strengthen economic diplomacy & a dedicated trade-negotiation capacity.
  • Better MEA–line-ministry coordination and use of think-tank research.

UPSC Angle: remember the pairing — NSC (1998) for coordination, CCS for decisions, NSAB for the Nuclear Doctrine draft (1999, no-first-use). These three recur as one-line Prelims facts.

7. Parliament, the Constitution & Treaty-Making

7.1 Constitutional Basis

ProvisionWhat it says
Article 51 (DPSP)State shall promote international peace & security, honour international law & treaty obligations, encourage settlement of disputes by arbitration
Article 53Executive power (incl. defence & war) vested in the President, exercised via the Council of Ministers
Article 73Union's executive power extends to matters on which Parliament can legislate — including treaty-making
Article 253Parliament may make laws to implement any treaty, agreement or international convention — even on State-List subjects
Union List (Entries 10–21)Foreign affairs, diplomacy, UN, treaties, war & peace, extradition, citizenship of foreign nationals — all Central subjects

7.2 How India Makes Treaties

Fig 2.3 — The executive negotiates, signs & ratifies; Parliament legislates only when domestic law-change is required.

7.3 Parliament's Actual Role

  • No mandatory ratification — the executive binds India internationally; Parliament is not constitutionally required to approve treaties.
  • Oversight via debates, questions, and the Standing Committee on External Affairs; controls the purse & enacts enabling laws (e.g., the WTO/civil-nuclear liability laws).
  • Criticised as a democratic deficit — reformers seek mandatory parliamentary scrutiny of major treaties.
Statement trap: India does not require parliamentary ratification for a treaty to be internationally binding (Art 253 only enables domestic implementing legislation). This exact distinction is a favourite Prelims & interview point.

8. Federalism & Para-Diplomacy

  • Foreign affairs is a Union subject, so the Centre monopolises formal treaty-making — but globalisation has drawn states into "para-diplomacy".
  • Para-diplomacy = sub-national units (states/cities) engaging foreign partners for investment, tourism, technology & diaspora ties, within the Centre's framework.
  • Examples: state global-investor summits; sister-city pacts; the MEA's States Division to coordinate outreach; "Team India" framing of cooperative federalism.
  • Tension points: border-state sensitivities (river-water treaties — Teesta with West Bengal; Mahakali/Kalapani with UP/Uttarakhand); federal consultation on treaties affecting states.
UPSC Angle: the Teesta deadlock shows why cooperative federalism now matters in diplomacy — a river treaty with Bangladesh stalled because a state (West Bengal) objected, even though the Centre holds the treaty power.

9. Think-Tanks & Track-II Diplomacy

ChannelMeaningExample
Track-IOfficial government-to-government diplomacySummits, FS-level talks, 2+2 dialogues

Track-1.5Mix of officials (in personal capacity) + non-officialsSemi-official strategic dialogues

  • Value of Track-II: keeps channels open when official ties freeze; floats ideas without political risk; builds trust & personal networks.
  • Limits: non-binding; can be disowned; dependent on official follow-through.
  • India's think-tank ecosystem is expanding but remains under-resourced vs Western/Chinese counterparts — a soft-power & capacity gap.
Ready fact: the Neemrana Dialogue is the classic India–Pakistan Track-II forum; the ICWA (Indian Council of World Affairs) and RIS are government-linked think-tanks — both recur in Prelims one-liners.

11. Prelims PYQs (2013–2026)

UPSC Prelims 2023

Q: Under the Constitution of India, the subjects "foreign affairs", "diplomatic relations", "United Nations Organisation" and "entering into treaties with foreign countries" fall under —

  • (a) the Union List
  • (b) the State List
  • (c) the Concurrent List
  • (d) the residuary powers of the States

Ans: (a) — these are Union-List entries (10–21); foreign affairs is exclusively a Central subject.

UPSC Prelims 2021

Q: Consider the following about treaty-making in India: (1) the power to enter into treaties is an executive power vested in the Union; (2) every treaty must be ratified by both Houses of Parliament before it binds India internationally; (3) Parliament can legislate under Article 253 to implement a treaty even on a State-List subject. Which are correct?

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 1 and 3 only
  • (c) 2 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Ans: (b) — statement 2 is false; treaty-making is executive and needs no mandatory parliamentary ratification.

UPSC Prelims 2020

Q: The National Security Council (NSC) of India, the apex body for national-security and strategic coordination, was established in the year —

  • (a) 1990
  • (b) 1998
  • (c) 2004
  • (d) 2014

Ans: (b) — the NSC was set up in 1998; it is chaired by the Prime Minister.

UPSC Prelims 2019

Q: The Directive Principle directing the State to "foster respect for international law and treaty obligations and encourage settlement of international disputes by arbitration" is contained in —

  • (a) Article 39
  • (b) Article 48A
  • (c) Article 51
  • (d) Article 253

Ans: (c) — Article 51 is the DPSP on international peace; Article 253 is the treaty-implementation legislative power.

UPSC Prelims 2018

Q: The Cabinet Committee on Security (CCS), which takes apex decisions on defence and major foreign-policy matters, is chaired by —

  • (a) the Prime Minister
  • (b) the Home Minister
  • (c) the National Security Adviser
  • (d) the Defence Minister

Ans: (a) — the CCS is chaired by the PM; members include Home, Defence, External Affairs & Finance ministers.

UPSC Prelims 2017

Q: "Track-II diplomacy" is best described as —

  • (a) secret official talks between heads of government
  • (b) unofficial dialogue among scholars, retired officials and experts to explore ideas informally
  • (c) back-channel military-to-military hotlines
  • (d) the second round of any formal government negotiation

Ans: (b) — Track-II is unofficial, non-binding dialogue (e.g., the Neemrana Dialogue with Pakistan).

UPSC Prelims 2015

Q: The Indian Foreign Service (IFS), which staffs India's diplomatic missions abroad, was constituted in the year —

  • (a) 1935
  • (b) 1946
  • (c) 1950
  • (d) 1962

Ans: (b) — the IFS was created by a Cabinet decision in 1946; recruitment is via the UPSC Civil Services Exam.

UPSC Prelims 2014

Q: Which one of the following ministries is the nodal ministry for the conduct of India's external relations, including its embassies and high commissions abroad?

  • (a) Ministry of Home Affairs
  • (b) Ministry of Defence
  • (c) Ministry of External Affairs
  • (d) Ministry of Commerce & Industry

Ans: (c) — the MEA is the nodal ministry, headed politically by the EAM and administratively by the Foreign Secretary.

12. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

Genuine dated UPSC GS-II questions on the external determinants of India's foreign policy — the great-power balance, energy and the extended neighbourhood that shape India's choices (sourced from real papers). Each carries a full answer skeleton — Introduction → body → Conclusion.

UPSC Mains GS-II 2021 15 marks · 250 words

Q: "The USA is facing an existential threat in the form of China that is much more challenging than the erstwhile Soviet Union." Explain. (2021)

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Unlike the USSR — a military rival but an economic isolate — China challenges the USA across every dimension of power. This comprehensive rivalry is the single biggest external determinant reshaping India's strategic environment.
  2. Why China is a graver challenge than the USSR:
    • Economic: world's 2nd-largest economy and top trading nation, deeply integrated into global supply chains — the USSR was economically detached.
    • Technological: peer competition in 5G, AI, semiconductors, quantum — dual-use dominance the USSR never had.
    • Military & maritime: largest navy by hull count; anti-access/area-denial in the Indo-Pacific; the BRI as strategic reach.
    • Institutional: reshaping global governance (AIIB, RCEP) from within, not from outside.
  3. Implications for India (why this determines Indian FP): the rivalry pushes India towards the Quad, iCET and supply-chain de-risking, while a contested LAC makes China a direct security challenge — India becomes a swing power courted by both.
  4. India's calibrated response: strategic autonomy — deepening US tech/defence ties without a formal alliance, retaining Russia and multipolar options, and building comprehensive national power.
  5. Conclusion: China's all-of-society challenge makes the US-China contest the defining feature of the emerging order — and the central external determinant that India must navigate to protect its autonomy and interests.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2020 10 marks · 150 words

Q: What is the significance of Indo-US defence deals over Indo-Russian defence deals? Discuss with reference to stability in the Indo-Pacific region. (2020)

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Russia remains India's largest legacy arms supplier, but the pivot towards US defence platforms reflects the changing external determinants of Indian FP — an Indo-Pacific increasingly shaped by China.
  2. Significance of Indo-US deals:
    • Foundational agreements: LEMOA (2016), COMCASA (2018), BECA (2020) — enabling interoperability, secure communications and geospatial intelligence.
    • Platforms: P-8I, C-17/C-130J, Apache, Chinook, MQ-9B drones — boosting maritime domain awareness in the IOR.
    • Strategic convergence: Quad, Malabar and 2+2 dialogue — a networked balance against Chinese assertiveness.
    • Technology & co-production: GE-414 jet-engine deal, iCET — access to cutting-edge tech Russia cannot offer.
  3. Continuing value of Indo-Russian deals: S-400, BrahMos, nuclear submarines, spares for a largely Russian-origin inventory — and strategic autonomy (diversified sourcing, no single dependence).
  4. Indo-Pacific stability: US platforms sharpen India's maritime deterrence and net-security-provider role; but CAATSA risk and over-dependence must be balanced.
  5. Conclusion: US deals add technological edge and coalition depth for Indo-Pacific stability, while Russian ties preserve autonomy — India's optimal path is calibrated diversification, not substitution.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2019 15 marks · 250 words

Q: "What introduces friction into the ties between India and the United States is that Washington is still unable to find for India a position in its global strategy, which would satisfy India's National self-esteem and ambitions." Explain with suitable examples. (2019)

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: India-US ties have grown into a Comprehensive Global Strategic Partnership, yet friction persists because Washington often views India as a junior partner rather than an autonomous pole — a mismatch with India's self-image as a rising power.
  2. Sources of friction (self-esteem vs subordination):
    • Strategic autonomy: India resists alliance-style commitments (no NATO-plus); CAATSA threats over the S-400.
    • Trade: GSP withdrawal (2019), tariff disputes, data-localisation friction.
    • Third-country ties: US discomfort with India's Russia (oil, arms) and Iran (Chabahar) engagement.
    • Hyphenation/leverage: occasional US mediation offers on Kashmir; H-1B visa curbs affecting Indian talent.
  3. Convergences that offset friction: Indo-Pacific and China-balancing, Quad, defence foundational pacts, iCET tech partnership, counter-terrorism and the diaspora.
  4. India's approach: engage the US on shared interests while guarding autonomy — a "multi-alignment" that refuses a subordinate slot in any power's grand strategy.
  5. Conclusion: Friction is structural, rooted in India's insistence on parity and independent judgement; the partnership matures only when Washington accommodates India as an autonomous pole, not a follower.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2017 15 marks · 250 words

Q: The question of India's Energy Security constitutes the most important part of India's economic progress. Analyse India's energy policy cooperation with West Asian countries. (2017)

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Importing over 85% of its crude and half its gas, India's growth is hostage to energy security — making West Asia, the source of a large share of these imports, a structural determinant of Indian foreign policy.
  2. Dimensions of energy cooperation with West Asia:
    • Crude & LNG: Saudi Arabia, Iraq, UAE, Qatar as core suppliers; long-term LNG contracts (Qatar).
    • Investment & reserves: ADNOC's stake in India's strategic petroleum reserves; refinery and downstream tie-ups.
    • Connectivity: Chabahar (Iran) and IMEC linking energy and trade corridors.
    • Diaspora & remittances: ~9 million Indians in the Gulf underpinning the energy relationship.
  3. Challenges: price volatility, regional instability (Hormuz, Red Sea, Israel-Iran), US sanctions on Iran, and over-dependence on a single region.
  4. Policy response: source diversification (Russia, USA, Africa), strategic reserves, and a green-transition hedge (ISA, Global Biofuel Alliance, green hydrogen).
  5. Conclusion: West Asia will remain indispensable to India's energy security in the near term; a resilient strategy pairs deep Gulf partnerships with diversification and clean-energy leadership — turning dependence into diplomatic leverage.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2018 15 marks · 250 words

Q: A number of outside powers have entrenched themselves in Central Asia, which is a zone of interest to India. Discuss the implications, in this context, of India's joining the Ashgabat Agreement, 2018. (2018)

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Central Asia is India's energy-rich "Extended Neighbourhood", but Russia, China (BRI), the US, Iran and Turkey are entrenched there — making the region a contested geopolitical determinant of Indian foreign policy.
  2. Outside powers & the challenge: China's BRI and economic footprint; Russia's traditional security primacy; the connectivity handicap of no direct land access (Pakistan barrier).
  3. Ashgabat Agreement (joined 2018) — significance:
    • Links India to an International Transport & Transit Corridor (Iran-Oman-Turkmenistan-Uzbekistan), bypassing Pakistan.
    • Synergy with Chabahar port and the INSTC — a viable route to Central Asia and Afghanistan.
    • Advances energy security (TAPI potential), trade and strategic presence to counterbalance China.
  4. Complementary instruments: Connect Central Asia Policy (2012), India-Central Asia Dialogue, SCO membership, and development partnerships (lines of credit).
  5. Conclusion: By joining the Ashgabat Agreement India overcomes its connectivity deficit and re-enters the Central Asian strategic space — securing energy, trade and geopolitical balance against entrenched rivals. Delivery on Chabahar and INSTC is key to converting access into influence.

  • Internal = geography, history/culture, economy, military, politics, diaspora
  • External = global power structure, neighbourhood, institutions/law, global economy, transnational issues

Treaty-making:
  • Executive act — no mandatory parliamentary ratification
  • Parliament legislates only for domestic effect (Art 253)

  • MEA (EAM + Foreign Secretary), IFS created 1946, ~200+ missions
