India–Southeast Asia Relations & the Act East Policy

From Look East (1991) to Act East (2014) — how ASEAN became the anchor of India's engagement with the Indo-Pacific.

Core idea: Southeast Asia is India's civilisational bridge to the wider Indo-Pacific. The Act East Policy upgrades the older economic-focused Look East into a strategic, security and connectivity-driven engagement, with ASEAN centrality at its core and the balancing of China as its silent driver.

1. Why Southeast Asia Matters to India

  • Strategic gateway: Southeast Asia sits astride the sea lanes linking the Indian and Pacific Oceans — the heart of the Indo-Pacific.
  • ASEAN as anchor: the 10-member bloc is India's fourth-largest trade partner and the institutional core of the regional architecture.
  • Civilisational depth: two millennia of Hindu-Buddhist cultural links (Angkor Wat, Borobudur, Ramayana traditions) give India unmatched soft-power roots.
  • Counter to China: a strong India presence gives ASEAN states a hedge against Chinese assertiveness in the South China Sea.
  • Economic dynamism: one of the world's fastest-growing regions — markets, supply chains and investment opportunities.
  • Northeast India link: Act East is the external face of developing India's Northeast as a land bridge to the region.

2. The Region & India's Reach

The 10 ASEAN states span mainland and maritime Southeast Asia. India connects to them by land (through Myanmar) and by sea (across the Bay of Bengal and Andaman Sea), with the Andaman & Nicobar Islands as a forward outpost near the Malacca Strait.

Fig 18.1 — Schematic of India's twin (land + sea) reach into Southeast Asia; Myanmar is the land bridge, the Andaman & Nicobar Islands the maritime outpost near the Malacca Strait.
  • Mainland SEA: Myanmar, Thailand, Laos, Cambodia, Vietnam — reachable by land via India's Northeast.
  • Maritime SEA: Indonesia, Malaysia, Singapore, Brunei, Philippines — reached across the Bay of Bengal and Andaman Sea.
  • Chokepoint: the Malacca Strait carries a huge share of world trade and China's energy imports — India's Andaman & Nicobar Command sits astride its western approaches.

3. The Look East Policy (1991)

  • Trigger: the 1991 balance-of-payments crisis and end of the Cold War forced India to look outward for markets and partners; PM Narasimha Rao launched Look East.
  • Economic focus: phase one (1990s) was largely about trade, investment and integrating with ASEAN's booming economies.
  • Institutional entry: India became a Sectoral Dialogue Partner (1992), Full Dialogue Partner (1995), member of the ASEAN Regional Forum (1996) and Summit-level Partner (2002).
  • Phase two (2000s): widened to security and strategic issues — East Asia Summit membership (2005), defence links, connectivity ideas.
  • Limitation: largely economic and reactive; implementation of connectivity projects lagged, prompting the later upgrade.

4. The Act East Policy (2014)

Fig 18.2 — From Look East (economic, 1991) to Act East (strategic, 2014) to the Comprehensive Strategic Partnership (2022).
  • Announced 2014: PM Modi rebranded Look East as Act East at the East Asia Summit — signalling action over rhetoric.
  • The 4 Cs / broadened scope: Culture, Commerce, Connectivity and Capacity-building — later adding a strong security and strategic dimension.
  • 3 Ds: Democracy, Demography, Demand — the shared strengths India brings to the partnership.
  • Wider geography: Act East reaches beyond ASEAN to East Asia (Japan, South Korea) and the Pacific.
  • Northeast at the centre: explicitly links the development of India's Northeast to the region as a physical bridge.
  • Comprehensive Strategic Partnership (2022): India–ASEAN ties elevated to the highest tier on the 30th anniversary of the dialogue.

5. ASEAN & India–ASEAN Relations

  • ASEAN centrality: India explicitly backs ASEAN as the anchor of the regional and Indo-Pacific architecture — a shared principle.
  • Summit partnership: annual India–ASEAN summits since 2002; Comprehensive Strategic Partnership since 2022.
  • ASEAN-led mechanisms India is in: East Asia Summit (EAS), ASEAN Regional Forum (ARF), ADMM-Plus (defence ministers), Expanded Maritime Forum.
  • AITIGA: the ASEAN–India Trade in Goods Agreement (2010) is being reviewed to correct the trade imbalance and non-tariff barriers.
  • Delhi Declaration (2018): at the commemorative summit, all 10 ASEAN leaders were Republic Day chief guests — a diplomatic high point.
  • Not RCEP: India walked out of the Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership (2019) over fears of a China-driven import surge — a key sensitivity in the economic relationship.

6. Indo-Pacific, SAGAR & IPOI

  • Indo-Pacific vision: India sees a free, open, inclusive, rules-based Indo-Pacific with ASEAN at its centre (PM Modi's Shangri-La speech, 2018).
  • SAGAR: "Security and Growth for All in the Region" (2015) — India's maritime doctrine of cooperative security and development for the Indian Ocean.
  • IPOI: the Indo-Pacific Oceans Initiative (2019) — seven pillars (maritime security, ecology, resources, capacity-building, disaster risk, S&T, connectivity); ASEAN states lead pillars.
  • AOIP alignment: India's IPOI dovetails with the ASEAN Outlook on the Indo-Pacific (AOIP) — both stress inclusivity and centrality, not containment.
  • QUAD linkage: the India-US-Japan-Australia Quad reinforces the Indo-Pacific agenda while India insists it is not anti-China or a military alliance.

8. Trade & Economic Ties

  • Big trade partner: ASEAN is India's fourth-largest trading partner; two-way trade is over US$120 billion.
  • Trade deficit: India runs a persistent deficit with ASEAN — a core reason for reviewing the AITIGA goods agreement.
  • Singapore hub: Singapore is India's top ASEAN trade and investment partner and a major source of FDI; CECA since 2005.
  • Supply chains: India courts SEA to diversify supply chains (China+1) in electronics, semiconductors and manufacturing.
  • Digital & fintech: UPI-PayNow linkage with Singapore; growing digital-economy cooperation.
  • Investment corridors: Indian firms in Vietnam, Indonesia and Thailand; ASEAN investment into Indian infrastructure and services.

9. Maritime Security & the South China Sea

  • Freedom of navigation: India consistently backs freedom of navigation and overflight and peaceful settlement of disputes in the South China Sea, per UNCLOS.
  • UNCLOS & the 2016 ruling: India calls for respecting the 2016 arbitral tribunal award against China's expansive claims — without naming China aggressively.
  • Naval diplomacy: MILAN exercise (hosted at Visakhapatnam), coordinated patrols, and port calls across SEA; SIMBEX with Singapore is India's longest-running naval exercise.
  • Vietnam energy stake: ONGC Videsh explores oil/gas in Vietnamese blocks in the South China Sea — a pointed signal to China.
  • Capacity-building: India supplies BrahMos missiles to the Philippines (first export deal, 2022) and coastal-security help — a strategic entry into maritime SEA.
  • Andaman & Nicobar Command: India's only tri-service command watches the western mouth of the Malacca Strait — a natural monitoring post.

11. The China Factor

  • Silent driver: China's rise and South China Sea assertiveness are the unstated logic behind Act East and the Indo-Pacific push.
  • Economic asymmetry: China is ASEAN's #1 trade partner and top infrastructure financier — India cannot match the scale, so it competes on trust and quality.
  • Hedging demand: ASEAN states want India as a balancer so they are not forced to choose between the US and China.
  • ASEAN's caution: ASEAN prizes centrality and avoids overt anti-China blocs — India respects this, keeping Act East inclusive, not containment.
  • RCEP dilemma: India stayed out of RCEP to avoid a China import flood, but this weakens its economic integration with the region — the central trade-off.

12. Challenges

  • Connectivity delays: flagship projects (Kaladan, Trilateral Highway) are chronically behind schedule, worsened by Myanmar's civil conflict.
  • Myanmar instability: the 2021 coup and ongoing conflict block India's only land route to SEA and create refugee and security spillovers into the Northeast.
  • Trade deficit & RCEP absence: a persistent deficit plus staying out of RCEP limit deeper economic integration.
  • China's scale: India's economic offer is dwarfed by Chinese trade and BRI investment in the region.
  • Implementation gap: a recurring "vision-heavy, delivery-light" critique of Act East on the ground.
  • Bureaucratic slowness: project execution and Northeast infrastructure development lag behind political commitments.

13. Way Forward

  • Deliver connectivity: fast-track Kaladan and the Trilateral Highway; develop the Northeast as a genuine land bridge.
  • Rebalance trade: complete the AITIGA review to cut non-tariff barriers and the deficit; deepen supply-chain (China+1) integration.
  • Deepen maritime ties: expand defence exports (BrahMos), joint patrols, and capacity-building across maritime SEA.
  • Leverage soft power: scale the civilisational, Buddhist, diaspora and digital (UPI) links that China cannot replicate.
  • Stabilise Myanmar policy: balance engagement with the junta, humanitarian concerns and border security to protect connectivity.
  • Institutionalise the Indo-Pacific: operationalise IPOI pillars with ASEAN partners and align with AOIP.

14. Current Affairs & Recent Developments

Comprehensive Strategic Partnership

On the 30th anniversary of the dialogue, India–ASEAN ties were elevated to a Comprehensive Strategic Partnership — the highest tier.

BrahMos to the Philippines

India signed its first BrahMos export deal (~US$375 mn) with the Philippines — a landmark defence-export entry into maritime SEA.

AITIGA review launched

India and ASEAN began reviewing the goods agreement to correct the trade imbalance and remove non-tariff barriers by 2025.

The first ASEAN–India Maritime Exercise (AIME) was held — a step up in collective maritime-security engagement.

UPI-PayNow linkage

India's UPI linked with Singapore's PayNow, enabling instant cross-border retail payments — digital connectivity in action.

2024

Myanmar & Kaladan concerns

Continued conflict in Myanmar's Rakhine and Sagaing regions kept the Kaladan project and border security under strain.

Q. India's “Act East Policy” was formally announced in which year?

  • (a) 1991
  • (b) 2005
  • (c) 2014
  • (d) 2019

Answer: (c). Act East was announced by PM Modi in 2014, upgrading the 1991 Look East Policy from an economic focus to a strategic, security and connectivity-driven engagement.

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

Q. The Kaladan Multimodal Transit Transport Project connects India to which Southeast Asian country?

  • (a) Thailand
  • (b) Myanmar
  • (c) Vietnam
  • (d) Malaysia

16. Mains PYQ Practice

A note on sourcing: Act East and the Indo-Pacific are established GS-II themes with genuine dated questions. Below is one authentic UPSC GS-II PYQ, followed by UPSC-pattern model questions (honestly labelled, not fabricated past papers), each with a full skeleton.

UPSC GS-II 2014

Q. With respect to the South China Sea, maritime territorial disputes and rising tension affirm the need for safeguarding maritime security to ensure freedom of navigation and overflight throughout the region. In this context, discuss the bilateral issues between India and China. 12.5 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Frame: the South China Sea is a global commons where India has a stake in freedom of navigation, energy interests (ONGC Videsh in Vietnamese blocks) and the wider Indo-Pacific balance.
  2. India–China friction points: China's objection to India's SCS energy exploration; India's support for UNCLOS and the 2016 award; China's String of Pearls vs India's Act East.
  3. India's stand: freedom of navigation and overflight, peaceful UNCLOS-based settlement, without joining a containment bloc.
  4. Broader canvas: ties to the LAC dispute, trade imbalance and Indo-Pacific competition.
  5. Conclusion: India balances principled support for a rules-based order with pragmatic management of the bilateral with China.
UPSC GS-II — model question

Q. “The Act East Policy is the maritime and strategic maturing of the earlier Look East Policy.” Critically examine. 15 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: Look East (1991) was economic and reactive; Act East (2014) is strategic and proactive.
  2. Maturing: the 4 Cs/3 Ds, ASEAN centrality, security + connectivity + Indo-Pacific dimensions.
  3. Evidence: connectivity projects (Kaladan, Trilateral Highway), defence exports (BrahMos), IPOI.
  4. Gaps: delivery deficit, Myanmar instability, RCEP absence.
  5. Conclusion: a genuine but incomplete maturing — execution must catch up with ambition.
UPSC GS-II — model question

Q. Examine how the instability in Myanmar since 2021 has affected India's Act East connectivity and security interests. 15 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: Myanmar is India's land bridge to Southeast Asia and a security frontier.
  2. Connectivity hit: the 2021 coup stalled Kaladan and the Trilateral Highway.
  3. Security spillover: refugee inflows and insurgency spillovers into the Northeast.
  4. Dilemma: a delicate junta-engagement vs democratic-values balance.
  5. Conclusion: calibrated engagement, border management and connectivity insulation.
UPSC GS-II — model question

Q. “ASEAN centrality is both an opportunity and a constraint for India's Indo-Pacific strategy.” Discuss. 10 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: ASEAN sits at the geographic and institutional core of the Indo-Pacific.
  2. Opportunity: legitimises an inclusive, non-containment Indo-Pacific; platforms (EAS, ARF, ADMM+).
  3. Constraint: ASEAN's caution on China and consensus-based slowness limit hard balancing.
  4. India's approach: aligns IPOI with AOIP to work within centrality.
  5. Conclusion: India gains legitimacy but must hedge with minilaterals like the Quad.
UPSC GS-II — model question

Q. Evaluate India's decision to stay out of RCEP in the context of its economic engagement with Southeast Asia. 10 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: India walked out of RCEP in 2019 despite years of negotiation.
  2. Reasons: China import surge, trade-deficit fears, inadequate safeguards, domestic industry/agriculture concerns.
  3. Cost: weaker regional integration, missed supply-chain gains.
  4. Alternatives: the AITIGA review and bilateral CEPAs as other paths.
  5. Conclusion: a defensible but costly call — India must deepen ties bilaterally to avoid isolation.

Quick Revision — India–Southeast Asia & Act East at a Glance

  • Evolution: Look East (1991, economic) → Act East (2014, strategic) → Comprehensive Strategic Partnership (2022).
  • Act East scope: 4 Cs (Culture, Commerce, Connectivity, Capacity) + 3 Ds (Democracy, Demography, Demand); Northeast at the centre.
  • ASEAN: centrality is the core principle; India in EAS, ARF, ADMM-Plus; stayed out of RCEP (2019).
  • Indo-Pacific tools: SAGAR (2015), IPOI (2019, 7 pillars, launched at EAS), aligns with ASEAN's AOIP; QUAD.
  • Connectivity: Kaladan (Myanmar/Sittwe), Trilateral Highway (Moreh–Mandalay–Mae Sot), MGC, BIMSTEC.
  • Trade: ASEAN = 4th-largest partner, >US$120 bn, persistent deficit; AITIGA under review; Singapore = top partner.
  • Maritime: South China Sea (freedom of navigation, UNCLOS 2016 award), ONGC in Vietnam, BrahMos to Philippines (2022), A&N Command.
  • China factor: silent driver; India = trusted balancer, inclusive not containment.
