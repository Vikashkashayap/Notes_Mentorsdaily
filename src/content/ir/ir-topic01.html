Conceptual Clarity — Why this Chapter Matters

UPSC tests India's foreign policy evolution in three distinct ways. Knowing which question-type you face decides how you study each heading below:

  • Definitional / static — "Panchsheel was signed in…", "Gujral Doctrine principle is…", "SAGAR stands for…". Memorise exact years, full-forms and the one-line meaning of each doctrine.
  • Statement-elimination — two/three statements on NAM origins, non-alignment vs neutrality, or Look East vs Act East, where one wrong word makes a statement false.
  • Applied / analytical — Mains GS-II on continuity & change, strategic autonomy in a multipolar world, or Neighbourhood First vs great-power balancing. Needs the evolutionary arc + current examples.

Focus especially on the four highest-frequency themes since 2013: the determinants of foreign policy, the Non-Alignment → multi-alignment journey, the doctrine set (Panchsheel/Gujral/SAGAR/Act East), and strategic autonomy as today's organising idea. These four anchor almost every question set on this topic.

1. What is Foreign Policy?

1.1 Definition

Foreign policy is the set of principles, goals and strategies a state uses to manage its relations with other states and international actors, to protect and advance its national interest. It is the external projection of a nation's core values, security needs and developmental priorities.

  • It operates at the intersection of national interest (survival, security, prosperity, prestige) and the external environment (balance of power, alliances, global institutions).
  • India's is rooted in the Constitution — Article 51 (DPSP) directs the State to promote international peace, honour treaties, and encourage settlement of disputes by arbitration.

1.2 National Interest — the Compass

TierInterestIndia's expression
Vital / coreSurvival, territorial integrity, sovereigntyKashmir, LAC, nuclear deterrence, one-India principle
StrategicRegional primacy, secure neighbourhood, energy & sea-lane securityNeighbourhood First, SAGAR, Indian Ocean influence
DevelopmentalGrowth, technology, markets, capital, diaspora welfareFTAs, iCET, remittances, evacuations (Vande Bharat, Kaveri)

UPSC Angle: India's foreign policy is best read as the pursuit of strategic autonomy — the freedom to make sovereign choices — not any fixed alliance. That thread runs unbroken from Nehru's non-alignment to today's multi-alignment.

2. Objectives & Determinants of India's Foreign Policy

UPSC rarely asks a bare list — it asks which determinant drove a particular shift, or which objective a policy serves. Learn each determinant with a concrete Indian example.

Fig 1.1 — Internal (geography, history, economy) and external (global balance, neighbourhood, domestic politics) determinants together shape policy.

2.1 Core Objectives

  • Preserve sovereignty & territorial integrity — the non-negotiable core (borders, Kashmir, nuclear deterrence).
  • Secure a peaceful periphery — a stable neighbourhood is the precondition for rise (Neighbourhood First).
  • Accelerate development — harness external capital, technology, energy and markets for domestic growth.
  • Protect Indians abroad — ~32 million-strong diaspora; remittances (~$125 bn, world's highest); evacuations.
  • Advance a rules-based, multipolar order — UNSC reform, reformed multilateralism, Global South voice.

2.2 Determinants

Prelims trap: non-alignment was not neutrality or isolationism — it was active engagement with both blocs while retaining independent judgement on each issue on merit.

3. Phases of Evolution (1947–2020s)

Examiners love the "continuity & change" framing. Learn the arc as five phases, each triggered by a systemic shock, yet all preserving the same core — strategic autonomy.

Fig 1.2 — Each phase is triggered by a shock (1962 war, 1971, 1991 reforms, 2014 rise) yet all preserve strategic autonomy.
PhaseHallmarkKey events / features
I. 1947–62 — Idealist / NehruvianNon-alignment, moral leadership of the newly free worldNAM founding (1961), Panchsheel (1954), Bandung (1955), anti-colonial solidarity; Hindi-Chini Bhai-Bhai
II. 1962–71 — Realist correctionSecurity-first after the China shock1962 war defeat; military modernisation; 1965 Pakistan war; Tashkent (1966); pragmatic aid-seeking
III. 1971–91 — Regional assertionSoviet tilt + South-Asian primacyIndo-Soviet Treaty (1971), Bangladesh liberation, 1974 Pokhran-I, Indira/Rajiv doctrine, IPKF Sri Lanka
IV. 1991–2014 — Economic diplomacyReforms recast foreign policy around growth1991 LPG reforms, Look East (1992), Gujral Doctrine (1996–97), Pokhran-II (1998), Indo-US nuclear deal (2008)
V. 2014–present — Multi-alignmentFrom balancer to "leading power"; issue-based coalitionsNeighbourhood First, Act East, SAGAR, QUAD, G20 presidency (2023), Global South convening (Voice of Global South)
One-line arc to lock: Idealism (1947) → Realism (1962) → Regional power (1971) → Economic engagement (1991) → Multi-alignment / leading power (2014) — core of strategic autonomy unchanged throughout.

4. Non-Alignment & NAM

Non-alignment was India's signature Cold War posture: refusal to join either the US-led or Soviet-led military bloc, while judging each issue independently on its merits. It is a favourite statement-based Prelims theme — and an evergreen "relevance today" Mains question.

4.1 What Non-Alignment Was (and Was Not)

Non-alignment ISNon-alignment is NOT
Active engagement with both blocs, judging each issue on meritNeutrality (which is passive/legal abstention, esp. in war)
Preserving freedom of independent decision (strategic autonomy)Isolationism or equidistance regardless of the issue
Anti-colonialism, anti-racism, disarmament advocacyIndifference to global affairs or moral fence-sitting

4.2 Origins & the Movement

  • Intellectual roots: Nehru's vision, Panchsheel (1954), and the Bandung Afro-Asian Conference (1955) which produced the "Ten Principles".
  • Founders (five): Nehru (India), Tito (Yugoslavia), Nasser (Egypt), Sukarno (Indonesia), Nkrumah (Ghana).
  • First NAM Summit: Belgrade, 1961 — 25 members. NAM is now the largest grouping outside the UN (~120 members).
  • Criteria for membership (Belgrade): independent policy of non-alignment, support for national-liberation movements, no membership of a multilateral military alliance or bilateral alliance with a great power.

4.3 Relevance Today — the Debate

Case for continued relevance

  • Preserves strategic autonomy amid US–China rivalry
  • Platform for Global South solidarity & reformed multilateralism
  • Kept India out of costly bloc entanglements (e.g. Ukraine, Gaza)

Case that it has evolved

  • Cold-War bipolarity (its premise) is gone
  • India skipped NAM summits (2016, 2019) → signalling shift
  • Replaced in practice by multi-alignment / issue-based coalitions (QUAD, BRICS, SCO simultaneously)
UPSC Angle: the crisp exam line is — "non-alignment as a movement has faded, but non-alignment as a principle (strategic autonomy) endures, now expressed as multi-alignment." This nuance wins marks.

5. Core Principles & Doctrines

These named doctrines are the single most factually-tested part of this topic. UPSC asks their year, author-era and one-line meaning. Learn each as a "label → idea" pair.

Fig 1.3 — The through-line is strategic autonomy; doctrines from Panchsheel to SAGAR are era-specific tools serving it.
Doctrine / PrincipleYear / EraCore idea

Non-Alignment1961 (Belgrade)Independent judgement; refusal to join either Cold-War bloc

Prelims trap: the Gujral Doctrine's defining feature is non-reciprocity toward smaller neighbours — not mere "good neighbourliness". And Look East (1992) → Act East (2014) added the strategic/security layer; the upgrade year is 2014.

6. Institutional Framework & Decision-Making

Foreign policy is made and executed by a web of constitutional and executive bodies. UPSC asks "who does what" and the constitutional anchor.

BodyRole in foreign policy
Prime Minister & PMOChief architect; summit diplomacy, strategic direction, National Security Council apex
Ministry of External Affairs (MEA)Nodal ministry; conducts diplomacy through missions & the Indian Foreign Service (IFS)
National Security Council (NSC) / NSAStrategic & security coordination; NSA as key adviser (created 1998)
PresidentFormal head; sends/receives envoys; treaties & war/peace in the President's name (Art. 53, 253)
ParliamentRatifies treaties needing legislation, debates & scrutinises (Standing Committee on External Affairs); Union List entries 10–21
Cabinet Committee on Security (CCS)Apex decision body on defence, security & major foreign-policy calls
  • Constitutional anchors: Art. 51 (DPSP — promote international peace); Art. 246 + Union List (foreign affairs, treaties, war & peace are central subjects); Art. 253 (Parliament may legislate to implement treaties).
  • Emerging inputs: think-tanks, states' "para-diplomacy" (investment MoUs), business chambers, and a large diaspora increasingly shape economic diplomacy.
UPSC Angle: foreign affairs is a Union subject, so the Centre monopolises formal treaty-making — but cooperative federalism now lets states court FDI, making foreign policy more "whole-of-nation".

7. Strategic Autonomy & Multi-Alignment

The defining idea of contemporary Indian foreign policy. Examiners increasingly test the distinction between non-alignment, strategic autonomy and multi-alignment.

7.1 The Three Concepts Distinguished

ConceptEraMeaning
Non-alignmentCold War (bipolar)Stay out of both military blocs; act as a bridge
Strategic autonomyPost-1991 (unipolar → multipolar)Retain sovereign freedom of choice; the enduring principle beneath every label
Multi-alignment2014– (multipolar)Simultaneous, issue-based partnerships with rival poles — QUAD and BRICS and SCO; maximise options, minimise dependence

7.2 Multi-Alignment in Practice

  • With the West: QUAD, iCET, 2+2 dialogues, defence foundational pacts (LEMOA, COMCASA, BECA) — without a formal alliance.
  • With Russia: continued S-400 & discounted-oil purchases despite Western pressure post-Ukraine.
  • With China-adjacent forums: BRICS, SCO, AIIB — engaging even amid the LAC standoff.
  • With the Global South: "Voice of Global South" summits, G20 presidency (2023) inclusion of the African Union.

7.3 From "Balancing Power" to "Leading Power"

  • India now frames itself as a "leading power, not just a balancing power" (a stated shift), and as a Vishwamitra / Vishwaguru and "first responder" in the region (HADR, vaccine Maitri).
  • The organising slogan is issue-based coalitions — "the world is one family" (Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam), used as the theme of the 2023 G20.
Statement trap: multi-alignment is not the abandonment of non-alignment — it is its evolution. Both simultaneously "engage everyone" and "commit to no bloc"; both statements are true at once.

9. Challenges & Way Forward

Key Challenges

  • Two-front pressure — simultaneous China (LAC) & Pakistan hostility
  • China's periphery push — BRI, "string of pearls", debt diplomacy in neighbours
  • US–Russia squeeze — balancing sanctions pressure vs legacy defence ties
  • Neighbourhood volatility — regime churn (Maldives, Bangladesh, Nepal, Sri Lanka, Myanmar)
  • Capacity gap — small IFS cadre relative to India's global ambitions
  • Trade-offs — values (democracy, Global South) vs interests (energy, tech)

Way Forward

  • Deepen multi-alignment while avoiding over-dependence on any pole
  • Scale Neighbourhood First delivery — finish connectivity projects on time
  • Build economic & tech muscle (semiconductors, green energy) as diplomatic leverage
  • Expand the IFS & think-tank ecosystem; harness diaspora & states
  • Lead Global South & reformed multilateralism to convert influence into institutional voice
Mains clincher: India's task is to convert its "swing-state" leverage in a multipolar world into durable institutional power (UNSC seat, standard-setting, supply-chain hub) — moving from rule-taker to rule-shaper.

11. Prelims PYQs (2013–2026)

UPSC Prelims 2022

Q: "SAGAR", associated with India's maritime engagement in the Indian Ocean Region, stands for —

  • (a) Sea and Gulf Access for Regional Development
  • (b) Security and Growth for All in the Region
  • (c) South Asian Groups for Assistance and Relief
  • (d) Strategic Alliance of Gulf and Asian Rim

Ans: (b) — SAGAR = "Security and Growth for All in the Region", articulated in 2015 at Mauritius.

UPSC Prelims 2020

Q: Consider the following about India's policy of Non-Alignment: (1) it meant strict neutrality and equidistance from both Cold-War blocs on every issue; (2) India was a founding member of the Non-Aligned Movement at Belgrade in 1961; (3) it permitted judging each international issue independently on its merits. Which are correct?

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Ans: (b) — statement 1 is false; non-alignment was active engagement, not passive neutrality/equidistance.

UPSC Prelims 2019

Q: The "Panchsheel" — the Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence — was first formally enunciated in the 1954 agreement between India and —

  • (a) the Soviet Union
  • (b) China (on trade with the Tibet region)
  • (c) Yugoslavia
  • (d) Egypt

Ans: (b) — Panchsheel was set out in the 1954 India–China agreement on trade with the Tibet region.

UPSC Prelims 2018

Q: The "Gujral Doctrine" of the mid-1990s is best described as —

  • (a) a policy of strict reciprocity in all dealings with neighbours
  • (b) non-reciprocity toward smaller South-Asian neighbours to build trust
  • (c) a nuclear no-first-use commitment
  • (d) alignment with the Western bloc after the Cold War

Ans: (b) — its defining feature is non-reciprocity: giving more than one takes to smaller neighbours.

UPSC Prelims 2017

Q: India's "Look East Policy", launched in the early 1990s, was formally upgraded to the "Act East Policy" — adding a strategic and security dimension — in the year —

  • (a) 2004
  • (b) 2009
  • (c) 2014
  • (d) 2019

Ans: (c) — "Act East" was announced in 2014, broadening Look East beyond trade to strategy & security.

UPSC Prelims 2016

Q: The 1955 Bandung (Afro-Asian) Conference, an intellectual precursor to the Non-Aligned Movement, was most directly associated with —

  • (a) the creation of the Warsaw Pact
  • (b) Afro-Asian solidarity and the "Ten Principles" of international relations
  • (c) the establishment of the World Trade Organization
  • (d) the founding of the European Economic Community

Ans: (b) — Bandung 1955 produced the "Ten Principles", carrying Panchsheel forward toward NAM (1961).

UPSC Prelims 2015

Q: Which of the following is NOT one of the five principles of Panchsheel?

  • (a) Mutual respect for each other's territorial integrity and sovereignty
  • (b) Mutual non-aggression
  • (c) Collective military defence against external aggression
  • (d) Peaceful coexistence

Ans: (c) — Panchsheel is expressly anti-alliance; collective military defence is not a Panchsheel principle.

UPSC Prelims 2013

Q: The Directive Principle in the Indian Constitution that directs the State to promote international peace and security, foster respect for international law and encourage settlement of disputes by arbitration is —

  • (a) Article 39
  • (b) Article 48
  • (c) Article 51
  • (d) Article 44

Ans: (c) — Article 51 (DPSP) is the constitutional anchor of India's foreign-policy orientation toward peace.

12. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

Genuine dated UPSC GS-II questions on India's foreign-policy evolution, doctrines & instruments (sourced from real papers). Each carries a full answer skeleton — Introduction → body → Conclusion.

UPSC Mains GS-II 2016 12.5 marks · 200 words

Q: Evaluate the economic and strategic dimensions of India's "Look East Policy" in the context of the post-Cold War international scenario. (2016)

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Launched in 1991 by PM P.V. Narasimha Rao amid the USSR's collapse and India's LPG reforms, the Look East Policy (LEP) re-oriented a Cold-War-era continental outlook towards a market-driven engagement with ASEAN and East Asia — centred on the "3Cs": Commerce, Connectivity, Culture.
  2. Economic dimensions:
    • ASEAN as a growth engine — FTA (2010, goods) and later services/investment pacts; ASEAN among India's top trade blocs.
    • Connectivity as economics — India-Myanmar-Thailand Trilateral Highway, Kaladan Multi-Modal project integrating the North-East.
    • Investment & energy linkages with Japan, South Korea, Singapore; access to capital and technology.
  3. Strategic dimensions:
    • Balancing China's rise in the Indo-Pacific; maritime-security cooperation and SLOC protection.
    • Membership of the ASEAN Regional Forum, East Asia Summit; defence ties with Vietnam, Japan (2+2), Singapore.
    • Institution-building — a platform for a rules-based regional order.
  4. Evolution — Look East to Act East (2014): a "4th C" (Capacity-building/Security) added; the policy became the sub-regional pillar of India's Indo-Pacific vision and Quad cooperation — proactive and maritime-centric.
  5. Limitations: project delays, sub-scale trade vs China, RCEP exit (2019), NE insurgency and infrastructure gaps.
  6. Conclusion: LEP converted post-Cold-War compulsion into strategic opportunity — anchoring India in Asia's economic dynamism while building strategic depth against a rising China. Its success now hinges on delivery of connectivity and deeper integration under Act East.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2013 10 marks · 150 words

Q: What is meant by the Gujral Doctrine? Does it have any relevance today? Discuss. (2013)

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Articulated by I.K. Gujral (as EAM/PM, 1996–97), the Gujral Doctrine is a set of five principles governing India's conduct towards its smaller South Asian neighbours, resting on non-reciprocal accommodation.
  2. The five principles:
    • Non-reciprocity — India extends unilateral concessions to smaller neighbours (Nepal, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Bhutan, Maldives).
    • Non-interference in internal affairs.
    • Mutual respect for territorial integrity and sovereignty.
    • Non-use of territory against another's interest.
    • Peaceful, negotiated settlement of disputes.
  3. Contemporary relevance — endures: it is the ideational parent of Neighbourhood First (2014); visible in the Ganga Water Treaty (1996), Land Boundary Agreement with Bangladesh (2015), HADR/first-responder role, Vaccine Maitri, and lines of credit.
  4. Limits & critique: silent on Pakistan; unilateral generosity can be read as weakness; China's cheque-book diplomacy and regime churn (Maldives, Nepal, Sri Lanka) test its soft-power premise; delivery deficits erode goodwill.
  5. Conclusion: As a principle of asymmetric generosity the Gujral Doctrine remains the durable core of India's neighbourhood policy; its relevance today depends on pairing goodwill with timely delivery and a firmer response where security is threatened.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2025 15 marks · 250 words

Q: "Energy security constitutes the dominant kingpin of India's foreign policy, and is linked with India's overarching influence in Middle Eastern countries." How would you integrate energy security with India's foreign policy trajectories in the coming years? (2025)

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: As the world's third-largest energy consumer importing over 85% of its crude and ~50% of its gas, energy security is a structural driver of Indian statecraft — the "kingpin" linking economic growth, strategic autonomy and West Asian diplomacy.
  2. Why West Asia is central: the Gulf supplies a large share of India's oil/LNG; hosts ~9 million diaspora remitting billions; and anchors the Look West Policy (2005), strategic partnerships with UAE/Saudi Arabia, I2U2 and IMEC.
  3. Integrating energy with foreign-policy trajectories:
    • Diversification: beyond the Gulf — Russia (post-2022 discounted crude), USA, Africa, Latin America — to hedge geopolitical shocks (Hormuz, Red Sea).
    • Connectivity corridors: IMEC, INSTC, Chabahar to secure routes and reduce chokepoint risk.
    • Green transition diplomacy: International Solar Alliance, Global Biofuel Alliance, green-hydrogen and critical-minerals partnerships to cut import dependence.
    • Strategic autonomy: balancing US sanctions pressure (Iran, Russia) with national interest; strategic petroleum reserves.
  4. Challenges: price volatility, Gulf instability, the energy-transition trilemma (security-affordability-sustainability), and great-power rivalry over supply chains.
  5. Conclusion: Energy security must be woven into every axis of foreign policy — diversified sourcing, corridor connectivity, clean-energy leadership and calibrated autonomy — converting import dependence into strategic leverage and Global-South leadership.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2023 10 marks · 150 words

Q: Indian diaspora has scaled new heights in the West. Describe its economic and political benefits for India. (2023)

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: With ~35 million people, the Indian diaspora is the world's largest; in the West it has risen to positions of exceptional economic and political influence, becoming a strategic instrument of India's foreign policy.
  2. Economic benefits:
    • Remittances — India is the world's top recipient (over $120 billion), stabilising the current account.
    • Investment, technology & knowledge transfer — Silicon Valley entrepreneurs, venture capital, reverse brain-drain.
    • Trade facilitation and brand-building — Indian-origin CEOs (Google, Microsoft) and professionals boosting India's economic image.
  3. Political benefits:
    • Soft power & lobbying — diaspora advocacy aided the Indo-US civil nuclear deal (2008).
    • Bridge-building — Indian-origin legislators/officials (US, UK, Canada) strengthening bilateral ties.
    • Cultural diplomacy — Yoga, festivals, cuisine amplifying India's global appeal; a mobilisable constituency during crises.
  4. Government engagement: Pravasi Bharatiya Divas, OCI cards, "Know India" programme, and diaspora outreach during PM visits institutionalise the linkage.
  5. Conclusion: The diaspora is a "living bridge" — a source of capital, innovation and influence. Harnessing it through sustained engagement multiplies India's economic strength and diplomatic reach in the West.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2018 15 marks · 250 words

Q: In what ways would the ongoing U.S.-Iran Nuclear Pact Controversy affect the national interest of India? How should India respond to this situation? (2018)

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: The U.S. withdrawal from the JCPOA (2018) and re-imposition of sanctions on Iran placed India — a partner of both Washington and Tehran — in a classic test of strategic autonomy.
  2. Impact on India's national interest:
    • Energy security: Iran was a top crude supplier on favourable terms (rupee trade, credit); sanctions forced sharp import cuts.
    • Connectivity: the Chabahar port and INSTC — India's gateway to Afghanistan and Central Asia bypassing Pakistan — came under sanction risk.
    • Regional stability & diaspora: a West Asian flare-up threatens the ~9 million-strong Gulf diaspora, remittances and oil prices.
    • Strategic autonomy: pressure to align with U.S. policy against India's independent judgement.
  3. How India should respond:
    • Diversify crude sourcing (US, Russia, Gulf) and build strategic petroleum reserves.
    • Seek sanctions waivers/carve-outs (as secured for Chabahar) through quiet diplomacy.
    • Preserve strategic autonomy — engage both partners on the merits of each issue, not as a bloc choice.
    • Champion dialogue and de-escalation multilaterally.
  4. Conclusion: The controversy underscores that India's interests are best served not by choosing sides but by hedging — safeguarding energy and connectivity while preserving the strategic autonomy that is the constant of its foreign policy.
