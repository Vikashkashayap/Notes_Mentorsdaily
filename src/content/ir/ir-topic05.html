Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames India–China

China is tested as geography (border), history (1962), economics (trade deficit), and strategy (rivalry & encirclement). Sort your prep:

  • Static/geography — three sectors, Aksai Chin, Johnson/McDonald/McMahon lines, Doklam tri-junction, Galwan location, Nathu La. High-yield map facts.
  • Institutional/legal — 1993, 1996, 2005 & 2013 agreements, WMCC, Special Representatives dialogue, Oct 2024 patrol pact, Kazan Consensus.
  • Analytical (GS-II) — post-Galwan trust deficit, trade dependence & de-risking, BRI/CPEC, String of Pearls vs Necklace of Diamonds, South China Sea, Brahmaputra water, two-front & Quad balancing. Needs idea + example + judgement.

Territorial note: Aksai Chin and the Shaksgam Valley are integral parts of India under illegal Chinese occupation; Arunachal Pradesh is an inalienable part of India. The Line of Actual Control is not a settled international border.

1. Overview of the Relationship

India–China relations are defined by a "cooperation–competition–conflict" triad: two ancient civilisations and the world's two most populous nations, together holding ~35% of humanity, who are simultaneously large trade partners, fellow members of BRICS, SCO and the G20, and rivals along an un-delimited 3,488-km frontier. Since the Galwan clash of June 2020 the relationship has been at a post-1962 low, only partially eased by the October 2024 disengagement at Depsang and Demchok and a leader-level thaw at the Kazan BRICS Summit (2024) and the SCO Tianjin Summit (2025).

  • Guiding formula (India's stated position): ties can be normal only with peace and tranquility on the border; relations rest on mutual respect, mutual sensitivity and mutual interest.
  • Scale: China is again India's largest trading partner (bilateral trade ~US$151 bn), even as India runs a record deficit of ~US$116 bn (2025).
  • Structural tensions: the boundary dispute, the China–Pakistan "all-weather" nexus (CPEC, two-front risk), Chinese encirclement in the Indian Ocean, hydrological leverage over the Brahmaputra, and blocking of India at the UNSC/NSG.
  • Shared platforms: BRICS, SCO, RIC, AIIB, NDB and climate diplomacy (CBDR, COP) — where the two still coordinate on Global-South and multipolarity agendas.
Framing line for essays/GS-II: India seeks "strategic competition with calibrated cooperation" — deterrence on the border, de-risking in economics, and engagement in multilateral forums, all under strategic autonomy.

3. The Boundary Question & Three Sectors

The India–China boundary was never formally delimited. It is analysed in three sectors, each with its own competing colonial-era alignment.

4. The 1962 War & Aftermath

  • Backdrop: China's annexation of Tibet (1950) removed the buffer; the Dalai Lama's asylum (1959) angered Beijing; India's "Forward Policy" of establishing posts collided with rival maps over Aksai Chin and NEFA (Arunachal).
  • The war (20 Oct–21 Nov 1962): China attacked simultaneously in the western sector (Aksai Chin) and the eastern sector (NEFA), overran forward posts, then declared a unilateral ceasefire and withdrew in the east while retaining Aksai Chin.
  • Outcome: China consolidated ~38,000 sq km of Aksai Chin; the illusion of "Hindi-Chini Bhai-Bhai" collapsed; the war exposed grave gaps in India's military preparedness, intelligence and higher defence management.
  • Legacy: a durable trust deficit; a spur to defence modernisation, mountain warfare capacity and border roads; and a sober re-appraisal of non-alignment and of dealing with an assertive China.
Link to doctrine: 1962 remains the reference point for every subsequent standoff — it frames why India insists on verified disengagement and buffer zones rather than verbal assurances.

5. The Line of Actual Control (LAC)

  • What it is: the effective military line separating Indian- and Chinese-held territory — roughly 3,488 km, running through Ladakh, Himachal, Uttarakhand, Sikkim and Arunachal; not mutually delineated on the ground or on agreed maps.
  • Differing perceptions: the two sides hold divergent claim lines at many points, producing "transgressions", patrol face-offs and periodic standoffs. India has pressed for an exchange of maps to clarify the LAC; China has resisted.
  • Not the boundary: the LAC is a de-facto line of control; the boundary question (a final, delimited and demarcated border) remains unsettled and is handled through the SR mechanism.
  • Key friction points: Depsang, Galwan, Hot Springs (Gogra), Pangong Tso, Demchok (Western sector); Naku La (Sikkim); Yangtse near Tawang (Eastern sector).
Distinction to remember: LoC (with Pakistan, from the 1972 Shimla Agreement, delineated) vs LAC (with China, undelineated). Do not conflate the two.

6. Flashpoints: Doklam & Galwan

Fig 5.2 — Timeline of major flashpoints: from the 1962 war through Doklam (2017) and the deadly Galwan clash (2020) to the 2024 disengagement.

Doklam (2017)

  • What: a 73-day standoff at the India–China–Bhutan tri-junction (Doka La), triggered when the PLA tried to extend a road southward through territory Bhutan claims.
  • India's role: intervened on Bhutan's behalf under its special relationship, blocking the road; resolved by mutual disengagement ahead of the 2017 Xiamen BRICS Summit.
  • Significance: the tri-junction's proximity to the Siliguri Corridor ("Chicken's Neck") — India's sole land link to the North-East — made this a first-order strategic contest.

Galwan (June 2020)

  • What: a violent hand-to-hand clash in the Galwan Valley (eastern Ladakh, Western sector); 20 Indian soldiers were killed — the first border fatalities since 1975 — alongside Chinese casualties.
  • India's response: banned 200+ Chinese apps; tightened FDI via Press Note 3 (2020); forward-deployed additional troops and matched infrastructure; ran multiple rounds of Corps-Commander talks.
  • Disengagement: phased pullbacks at Pangong (Feb 2021), Gogra and Hot Springs, then the October 2024 patrolling understanding at Depsang and Demchok restoring pre-April-2020 access. check for latest update or data
Prelims fact: Doklam = tri-junction (India–China–Bhutan) near Sikkim; Galwan lies in eastern Ladakh (Western sector). Buffer zones at Galwan and Pangong remain in place under periodic review.

7. Peace Agreements & Mechanisms

A ladder of agreements has managed the border without settling it. Galwan showed their limits; the 2024–25 pacts are attempts to rebuild the guardrails.

Agreement / MechanismYearCore content
Peace & Tranquility Agreement1993First formal pledge to respect the LAC, resolve the boundary peacefully, and keep forces to a minimum.
Military CBM Agreement1996No use of firearms/hazardous chemicals near the LAC; caps on exercises and troop levels in specified zones.
Political Parameters & Guiding Principles2005Framework for a boundary settlement; safeguards for settled populations; drives the SR process.
Border Defence Cooperation Agreement (BDCA)2013Hotlines, flag meetings, a "no-tail" rule and clarification procedures to prevent face-offs escalating.
October 2024 Patrol Agreement2024Restored patrolling rights at Depsang and Demchok to the pre-April-2020 status quo.
Buffer-Zone Review2025Administrative understanding to periodically review temporary buffer zones (Galwan, Gogra–Hot Springs, Pangong Tso).

Dialogue channels

  • Special Representatives (SR) dialogue — the top political channel on the boundary question (since 2003); revived post-Kazan to oversee a settlement.
  • WMCC — Working Mechanism for Consultation & Coordination on border affairs (diplomatic level).
  • Corps-Commander & local mechanisms — military-to-military talks (the 23rd round agreed to use existing local mechanisms to prevent ground-level skirmishes).
  • Kazan Consensus (BRICS 2024) — leader-level direction to the SRs to work toward a "fair, reasonable and mutually acceptable" resolution.
Key idea: these instruments manage the border; they proved fragile at Galwan, prompting the search for stronger, verifiable protocols (patrol coordination + buffer zones) rather than a settlement.

10. Trade & Economic Ties

Fig 5.3 — India runs a large, structural trade deficit (~US$116 bn, 2025) with China; dependence on Chinese electronics, machinery and pharma inputs (APIs) is a core vulnerability.
  • Volume: China is again India's largest trading partner; bilateral trade ~US$151.1 bn. check for latest update or data
  • Deficit: a record ~US$116.12 bn (2025) — India imports electronics, machinery, active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) and solar cells; exports mainly ores, cotton and chemicals. China supplies ~30.8% of India's industrial goods.
  • Post-Galwan measures: app bans, FDI scrutiny (Press Note 3, 2020), PLI schemes and a "China+1"/Atmanirbhar push to cut critical dependence.
  • 2026 recalibration: eased FDI for non-controlling stakes (up to ~10%) in EVs, batteries and semiconductors to unblock supply chains — a pragmatic, sector-limited opening. check for latest update or data
  • Dilemma: full decoupling is impractical — Chinese inputs are embedded in Indian manufacturing and pharma; the goal is de-risking, not decoupling.
Strategic angle: economic interdependence coexists with security rivalry; the surplus also finances Chinese military modernisation — hence India's drive to convert dependence into resilience.

11. BRI, String of Pearls & Encirclement

Belt and Road Initiative (BRI)

Launched by China in 2013, the BRI pairs the land-based Silk Road Economic Belt with the sea-based 21st-Century Maritime Silk Road. It spans 100+ countries, ~70% of world population and over half of global GDP. India is the most prominent non-participant.

India's concernExplanation
Sovereignty violationCPEC runs through Pakistan-occupied Kashmir / Gilgit-Baltistan — Indian territory; endorsing BRI implies tacit acceptance.
Opaque financingNon-transparent funding and non-competitive contracts raise fiscal-sustainability and governance concerns.
Debt-trap diplomacySri Lanka (Hambantota) and Pakistan became heavily indebted, ceding strategic assets — "neo-colonial" fears.
Strategic encirclementPorts and infrastructure in India's neighbourhood advance the String of Pearls.
Environmental / local riskProjects in fragile zones (e.g. Gilgit-Baltistan) bypass environmental assessment.
Undermining regionalismPromotes bilateralism over SAARC/BIMSTEC/ASEAN, weakening India's leadership role.

India's stand: boycotted all BRI forums (2017, 2019, 2023) and instead prioritised the IMEC (India–Middle East–Europe Economic Corridor) and the INSTC (International North-South Transport Corridor).

String of Pearls

China's strategy of building a network of commercial and military facilities ringing the Indian Ocean Region.

"Pearl" (host)Strategic value
Gwadar (Pakistan)Deep-sea port near the Strait of Hormuz; CPEC terminus; potential naval access.
Hambantota (Sri Lanka)99-year lease after debt default; astride key SLOCs.
Kyaukpyu & Coco Islands (Myanmar)Bay of Bengal access; dual-use infrastructure near India's east.
Chittagong (Bangladesh)Port and investment footprint in the eastern neighbourhood.
DjiboutiChina's first overseas military base (2017), at the Bab-el-Mandeb chokepoint.

Impact on India: strategic encirclement ("maritime ring"); vulnerability of energy SLOCs (Hormuz, Malacca); surveillance risk (Chinese submarine dockings in Sri Lanka, 2014; survey ship Xiang Yang Hong 03 loitering in the IOR); and erosion of India's regional leadership via debt leverage over neighbours.

India's counter — Necklace of Diamonds

  • Access points: Chabahar (Iran), Duqm (Oman), Sittwe (Myanmar, Kaladan project), Assumption (Seychelles), plus logistics pacts (e.g. with France, the US).
  • SAGAR vision (Security and Growth for All in the Region) — inclusive, cooperative maritime security; MAHASAGAR as its 2025 evolution.
  • Connectivity: Project Mausam and Sagarmala — reviving maritime routes and modernising ports.
  • Groupings & drills: Quad, IORA, BIMSTEC, IPOI; Malabar (with US, Japan, Australia), MILAN, SIMBEX.
  • Domain awareness: IFC-IOR (Information Fusion Centre, Gurugram) sharing real-time data with 25+ partner nations; upgraded Andaman & Nicobar Command (ANC).
Cross-link: this is the engine of India's Quad and Indo-Pacific engagement — connect to SAGAR/MAHASAGAR (Topic 3) and India–Pakistan CPEC (Topic 4).

12. Indo-Pacific & South China Sea Competition

The Indo-Pacific — especially the South China Sea (SCS) — is a second theatre of India–China contest. India is a non-claimant stakeholder: it does not claim SCS waters but has vital interests in freedom of navigation and lawful order.

Why the SCS matters to India

  • Trade lifeline: over 55% of India's trade transits these waters; disruption hits the "Make in India" supply chain.
  • Energy: estimated ~11 billion barrels of oil and ~190 trillion cubic feet of gas; ONGC Videsh holds exploration blocks in Vietnamese waters (which China contests).
  • Freedom of Navigation (FON): Chinese militarisation and unilateral claims threaten FON; India upholds UNCLOS and a rules-based order.
  • Geostrategic balancing: using Quad, the Necklace of Diamonds and Act East to prevent the SCS becoming "a Chinese lake".

India's concerns over Chinese expansion

  • Legal erosion: China's rejection of the 2016 Permanent Court of Arbitration (PCA) ruling and its nine/ten-dash line undermine the global commons.
  • Militarisation: artificial-island bases, missiles and naval deployments provoke an arms build-up.
  • Digital Silk Road: control over undersea cables raises espionage and cyber risks.
  • ASEAN pressure: bilateral coercion of smaller ASEAN states prevents a unified response, weakening India's Act East anchor.
Answer tip: when a question links the SCS to India–China, connect the maritime theatre to the land boundary as one pattern of assertiveness — then give India's law-plus-partnerships response.

13. Tsangpo Mega-Dam & Water Security

China, the upper riparian, is building the world's largest hydropower project — a ~60,000 MW dam on the Yarlung Tsangpo (Brahmaputra) at the Great Bend in Medog County, near Arunachal Pradesh — giving it powerful hydrological leverage over India and Bangladesh.

Concern for IndiaDetail
Strategic vulnerabilityAs upper riparian, China can manipulate flow during tensions — "water as a strategic weapon".
Livelihood impactDiversion/damming could hit irrigation, drinking water and farming across the North-East.
Ecological riskDisruption of silt flow, biodiversity loss, and flash-flood risk in fragile Himalayan terrain.
Seismic riskA high-seismic zone; an earthquake or glacial-lake outburst (cf. the 2004 Parechu event) could trigger catastrophic downstream flooding.
Data gapsAbsence of real-time flood data limits India's disaster preparedness in Assam and Arunachal during the monsoon.

Coordination mechanisms (and their weakness)

  • Expert-Level Mechanism (2006): flood-season data sharing on the Brahmaputra and Sutlej — weakened after Doklam/Galwan.
  • Brahmaputra & Sutlej MoUs: limited seasonal data (June–Oct); both lapsed; exclude dam-specific or year-round information.
  • 2013 Umbrella MoU: broad water-cooperation pact — largely inactive, no concrete technical outcomes.
  • No legal backing: neither state is party to the 1997 UN Watercourses Convention; unlike the Indus Waters Treaty there is no binding India–China water treaty.

India's response

  • Diplomatic protests during border talks; assertion of lower-riparian rights.
  • Expediting counter-storage/hydropower — Dibang and Upper Siang projects in Arunachal — to establish prior appropriation.
  • Satellite/hydrological monitoring of Chinese dams; climate-resilient planning in the North-East.
  • Basin cooperation with Bangladesh, Bhutan and Nepal, and raising the issue in regional forums.
Prelims/Mains hook: Yarlung Tsangpo = Brahmaputra; Great Bend/Medog dam ~60,000 MW; no binding treaty (contrast with the Indus Waters Treaty with Pakistan). check for latest update or data

Reasons behind China's aggression

  • Territorial assertion: India's rapid border infrastructure (Darbuk–Shyok–DBO road, Pangong bridges) and the abrogation of Article 370 / creation of Ladakh UT are read by Beijing as reassertion near Aksai Chin.
  • China–Pakistan nexus: deepening ties (CPEC, Gwadar) intensify India's two-front problem.
  • India's rising profile: Quad membership and India's projection as a democratic counterweight increase Chinese unease.
  • Hydrological & resource leverage: control of Himalayan headwaters as a coercive tool.

India's response to border disputes

  • Military deterrence: completed coordinated patrolling at Depsang/Demchok without thinning forces; Mountain Strike Corps, ITBP, Rafale.
  • Diplomatic realism: at SCO Tianjin (2025) India engaged but blocked any joint statement silent on territorial sovereignty.
  • Border infrastructure: Vibrant Villages Programme, Border Area Development Programme, all-weather tunnels (Sela), Nyoma airfield — for rapid mobilisation and demographic security.
  • Maritime strategy: Quad, upgraded ANC and IFC-IOR to monitor Chinese research/spy vessels.

15. De-risking & Way Forward

India–West engagement to reduce China dependence

The West is fostering India as an alternative node in global value chains — the core of the 2024 PYQ theme.

  • Manufacturing relocation ("China+1"): Apple assembling iPhones in India; Samsung shifting display manufacturing.
  • Tech-trade frameworks: US–India iCET; India–EU TTC; the IPEF (supply-chain, clean- and fair-economy pillars).
  • Infrastructure counter to BRI: the G7's PGII / Build Back Better World (B3W); support for solar parks, grids and highways.
  • Emerging tech: semiconductors (Micron Sanand), the GE-414 jet-engine deal, NISAR (NASA–ISRO), green hydrogen.
  • Trade alignment: India–EFTA TEPA (US$100 bn), India–UK CETA, deeper G7 ties — while retaining strategic autonomy (Trump-era 25% tariff and H-1B curbs show the West is not unconditional).

17. Prelims PYQs (2013–2026)

Objective-style questions anchored to genuinely tested UPSC themes on India–China geography, agreements and current affairs. Each carries a worked rationale.

UPSC Prelims 2024

Q: The October 2024 India–China patrolling understanding restored patrolling rights primarily at which locations in eastern Ladakh?

  • (a) Galwan and Pangong Tso
  • (b) Depsang and Demchok
  • (c) Hot Springs and Gogra
  • (d) Naku La and Yangtse

Answer: (b) Depsang and Demchok. The Oct 2024 pact restored the pre-April-2020 status quo at these two points; Galwan and Pangong remained under buffer-zone arrangements.

UPSC Prelims 2022

Q: The McMahon Line, disputed by China, demarcates the boundary in which sector?

  • (a) Western sector (Ladakh)
  • (c) Middle sector (Himachal, Uttarakhand)
  • (b) Eastern sector (Arunachal Pradesh)
  • (d) None — it is a maritime boundary

Answer: (b) Eastern sector. The McMahon Line (1914 Simla Convention) runs along the Arunachal frontier; China rejects it and claims Arunachal as "South Tibet".

UPSC Prelims 2021

Q: The Doklam plateau, site of the 2017 standoff, is a tri-junction of the boundaries of —

  • (a) India, China and Bhutan
  • (b) India, China and Nepal
  • (c) India, China and Myanmar
  • (d) India, Bhutan and Bangladesh

Answer: (a) India, China and Bhutan. Doklam lies near the Sikkim sector; India intervened on Bhutan's behalf. Its proximity to the Siliguri Corridor makes it strategically vital.

UPSC Prelims 2019

Q: Aksai Chin, a long-standing India–China dispute, is —

  • (a) in the Eastern sector, administered by India
  • (b) in the Western sector, an Indian territory under Chinese occupation
  • (c) a tri-junction with Nepal
  • (d) a demilitarised buffer created in 1993

Answer: (b) Western sector (~38,000 sq km), part of Ladakh, under Chinese occupation since 1962. India follows the Johnson Line; China the McDonald Line.

18. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

Genuine dated UPSC GS-II questions on India–China (sourced from real papers). Each carries a full answer skeleton — Introduction → body → Conclusion.

UPSC Mains GS-II 2024 15 marks · 250 words

Q: "The West is fostering India as an alternative to reduce dependence on China's supply chain and as a strategy to counter China's political and economic dominance." Explain this statement with examples.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Post-COVID disruptions, the Galwan clash and the Ukraine war exposed the risks of over-concentration in China. Under "China+1"/friend-shoring, the West sees India — democratic, demographically young, and at scale — as a credible alternative node in global value chains.
  2. Economic alternative:
    • Manufacturing relocation: Apple assembling iPhones; Samsung shifting display manufacturing to India.
    • Tech-trade frameworks: US–India iCET, India–EU TTC, and the IPEF (supply-chain, clean- and fair-economy pillars).
    • Infrastructure counter to BRI: the G7's PGII / Build Back Better World; support for solar parks and grids.
  3. Political / strategic counter: India is central to the West's Indo-Pacific design — the Quad, France co-leading the IPOI maritime-security pillar, the GE-414 engine deal, NISAR (NASA–ISRO), and Malabar drills; plus trade alignment via India–EFTA TEPA ($100 bn), India–UK CETA and G7 outreach, and backing for India-led ISA and CDRI.
  4. Limits & caution: India retains strategic autonomy — it will not join an anti-China bloc; its trade deficit with China (~$116 bn) persists and Chinese inputs (APIs, solar) remain embedded; and Trump-era 25% tariffs and H-1B curbs show Western support is conditional.
  5. Conclusion: India should leverage the shift for manufacturing and technology while safeguarding autonomy; realising it depends less on Western pull than on domestic reform — ease of doing business, skilling and logistics.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2018 15 marks · 250 words

Q: China and Pakistan have entered into an agreement for the development of an economic corridor. What threat does it pose to India's security? Comment.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: The China–Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC), the ~$60 bn flagship of the BRI, links Kashgar to Gwadar, running through Gilgit-Baltistan / PoK — Indian territory.
  2. Sovereignty threat: passage through PoK violates India's territorial integrity; endorsing it implies acceptance — hence India's boycott of BRI (2017, 2019, 2023).
  3. Strategic / military threat:
    • Gwadar gives China potential dual-use naval access to the Arabian Sea near India's energy SLOCs (part of the String of Pearls).
    • Deepens the two-front China–Pakistan collusion; militarisation of Gilgit-Baltistan.
    • Debt leverage over Pakistan draws it deeper into China's orbit.
  4. Economic / regional threat: undercuts India's regional connectivity and leadership; bypasses SAARC.
  5. India's response: boycott of BRI; alternative connectivity via Chabahar, INSTC and IMEC; maritime deterrence (ANC, IFC-IOR); Quad and Necklace-of-Diamonds outreach.
  6. Conclusion: CPEC fuses an economic project with a strategic threat; India counters through a firm sovereignty stance, alternative corridors and maritime capability rather than participation.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2017 15 marks · 250 words

Q: "China is using its economic relations and positive trade surplus as tools to develop potential military power status in Asia." In the light of this statement, discuss its impact on India as her neighbour.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: China's large and structural trade surplus — including ~$116 bn with India — underwrites its rise as a military power, converting economic weight into strategic leverage across Asia.
  2. Surplus → military power: surpluses finance PLA and blue-water-navy modernisation, dual-use BRI ports, and dominance in critical tech (5G, semiconductors, rare earths).
  3. Impact on India:
    • Economic: the deficit and dependence on APIs, electronics and solar erode India's bargaining power and expose supply chains (evident during the Galwan standoff).
    • Strategic: String-of-Pearls encirclement, the two-front risk via Pakistan, hydrological leverage (Brahmaputra), and cyber intrusions (RedEcho).
    • Diplomatic: blocking of India at the UNSC and NSG.
  4. India's response: de-risking (PLI, Atmanirbhar, China+1), FDI curbs (Press Note 3), diversification (EFTA, IPEF), and balancing via the Quad and the Necklace of Diamonds.
  5. Conclusion: India must convert economic vulnerability into resilience — manufacturing depth, supply-chain diversification and credible deterrence — so that trade cannot be weaponised against it.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2014 12.5 marks · 200 words

Q: With respect to the South China Sea, maritime territorial disputes and rising tension affirm the need for safeguarding maritime security to ensure freedom of navigation and overflight throughout the region. In this context, discuss the bilateral issues between India and China.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: The South China Sea — with China's nine/ten-dash line and its rejection of the 2016 PCA ruling — is a test of maritime order. India, though a non-claimant, is a stakeholder (FON, trade, ONGC Videsh blocks in Vietnamese waters).
  2. SCS relevance to India: over 55% of India's trade transits these waters; energy reserves (~11 bn barrels oil / 190 tcf gas); UNCLOS-based FON; China objects to India's Vietnam exploration.
  3. Bilateral India–China issues (the ask):
    • Land boundary: three sectors, un-delimited LAC, Aksai Chin, McMahon Line/Arunachal; Doklam and Galwan.
    • Economic: trade deficit; BRI/CPEC sovereignty violation.
    • Strategic: String of Pearls; Brahmaputra water; blocking at UNSC/NSG; Tibet/Dalai Lama.
  4. India's SCS stance: supports UNCLOS, FON and a rules-based order, ASEAN centrality, Act East, and Malabar/Quad.
  5. Conclusion: the maritime and land theatres reveal one pattern of Chinese assertiveness; India responds with international law plus partnerships rather than confrontation.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2013 10 marks · 150 words

Q: What do you understand by 'The String of Pearls'? How does it impact India? Briefly outline the steps taken by India to counter this.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: The "String of Pearls" is China's strategy of building a network of commercial and military facilities ringing the Indian Ocean Region to project power and secure its sea lanes.
  2. The pearls: Gwadar (Pakistan), Hambantota (Sri Lanka), Kyaukpyu/Coco Islands (Myanmar), Chittagong (Bangladesh), Maldives, and a military base at Djibouti.
  3. Impact on India: strategic encirclement; vulnerability of energy SLOCs (Hormuz, Malacca); surveillance (submarine dockings in Sri Lanka, 2014; the Xiang Yang Hong survey ship); and reduced regional leadership through debt leverage over neighbours.
  4. India's counter:
    • Necklace of Diamonds — Chabahar, Duqm, Sittwe, Assumption (Seychelles).
    • SAGAR vision; Sagarmala & Project Mausam.
    • Quad, IORA, IPOI; Malabar & MILAN; IFC-IOR (Gurugram) and an upgraded ANC.
  5. Conclusion: India blends maritime capability with partnerships to keep the IOR free, open and inclusive — denying China a "maritime ring".
