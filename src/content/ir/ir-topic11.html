Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames India–Bangladesh

Bangladesh is tested as history (1971), geography (border/enclaves/rivers), economics (trade & connectivity) and strategy (China balancing & the 2024 churn). Sort your prep:

  • Static/geography — 4,096-km border (India's longest), enclaves & the LBA (2015), Siliguri "Chicken's Neck", 54 shared rivers (Ganga, Teesta, Feni, Kushiyara), Chattogram & Mongla ports. High-yield map & fact recall.
  • Institutional/legal — 1972 Treaty of Friendship, 1996 Ganga Waters Treaty, 2015 LBA & the 100th Amendment, Kushiyara pact (2022), BBIN MVA, CORPAT/Bongosagar, CEPA negotiations.
  • Analytical (GS-II) — Neighbourhood First & Act East fulcrum, the Sonali Adhyay "golden chapter", China's growing footprint, the 2024 regime change & strategic equidistance, illegal migration & NRC-CAA, the Rohingya crisis, transboundary water diplomacy. Needs idea + example + judgement.

Framing note: India–Bangladesh is the model for what a working "Neighbourhood First" can look like — yet also the sharpest test of how far a state-to-state, regime-neutral partnership can survive a political transition in Dhaka.

1. Overview of the Relationship

India and Bangladesh share a civilisational bond forged in a common Bengali language, culture and the shared trauma of Partition, and cemented by India's decisive role in the 1971 Liberation War. It is arguably India's most successful bilateral relationship in the neighbourhood — anchored by a settled land & maritime boundary, deep connectivity, and the largest trade of any South Asian neighbour — but one now being recalibrated after the political transition of August 2024.

  • Border: India's longest land border (~4,096 km) touches five states — West Bengal, Assam, Meghalaya, Tripura, Mizoram.
  • Scale: Bangladesh is India's largest trading partner in South Asia and India its second-largest in Asia; bilateral trade ~US$13–14 bn (pre-2024 crisis peak).
  • Strategic value: the gateway to India's landlocked North-East (via transit & the Chattogram/Mongla ports) and the eastern anchor of the Bay of Bengal — central to both Neighbourhood First and Act East.
  • The churn: the fall of the Sheikh Hasina government (Aug 2024) and the interim/elected dispensations after it have pushed Dhaka toward "strategic equidistance" between India and China, testing the durability of the partnership.
Framing line for essays/GS-II: India seeks a "regime-neutral, connectivity-led partnership" with Bangladesh — investing in institutions, rivers, rail and trade that outlast any single government in Dhaka.

3. Border, Enclaves & the Land Boundary Agreement

India and Bangladesh share India's longest land border (~4,096 km). For decades it was complicated by enclaves (chhitmahals), adverse possessions and un-demarcated stretches — a colonial legacy finally resolved by the 2015 LBA.

Fig 6.1 — Schematic. The Siliguri Corridor ("Chicken's Neck") is the sole land bridge to the North-East; Chattogram & Mongla ports and the Akhaura–Agartala rail link give landlocked NE India transit access. Rivers: Ganga/Padma, Brahmaputra/Jamuna, Teesta, Feni.

3.1 The Land Boundary Agreement (LBA), 2015

  • Origin: rooted in the 1974 Indira–Mujib Agreement, finally ratified via the 100th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2015.
  • What it did: exchanged 162 enclaves (111 Indian enclaves in Bangladesh, 51 Bangladeshi enclaves in India, concentrated around Cooch Behar, West Bengal) and settled adverse possessions.
  • People: ~50,000+ stateless enclave residents were given a choice of citizenship — a rare humane, negotiated boundary settlement.
  • Significance: removed the single biggest territorial irritant; a global model of peaceful boundary resolution.
Prelims lock: LBA = 100th Amendment (2015); enclaves clustered around Cooch Behar; maritime boundary settled by a 2014 UNCLOS/PCA award (favourable to Bangladesh, accepted gracefully by India).

5. Connectivity & Sub-regional Integration

Connectivity is the strategic core of the relationship — it reconnects India's landlocked North-East to the sea, revives pre-1965 links severed by Partition, and knits the eastern sub-region together.

5.1 Flagship connectivity projects

  • Akhaura–Agartala rail link (2023): connects Tripura to Bangladesh's rail grid; slashes the distance from Agartala to Kolkata via Dhaka.
  • Maitri Setu (Feni bridge): links Sabroom (Tripura) with Ramgarh (Bangladesh), opening a road corridor to Chattogram port.
  • Port access: transit through Chattogram & Mongla ports for movement of goods to/from the North-East.
  • Inland waterways & coastal shipping: Protocol on Inland Water Transit & Trade (PIWTT) revived river routes.
  • Rail projects (some stalled post-2024): Khulna–Mongla line, Dhaka–Tongi–Joydebpur expansion.

5.2 Sub-regional frameworks

  • BBIN (Bangladesh–Bhutan–India–Nepal): the Motor Vehicles Agreement (2015) for seamless movement of passenger & cargo vehicles — a "coalition of the willing" after Pakistan blocked the SAARC MVA.
  • BIMSTEC: the Bay of Bengal grouping that anchors India's Act East & makes Bangladesh a bridge between South and Southeast Asia.
  • Sagarmala & SAGAR/MAHASAGAR: maritime connectivity vision for the Bay of Bengal littoral.
Why it matters: a stable Bangladesh transit regime can cut North-East logistics costs dramatically and reduce dependence on the vulnerable Siliguri "Chicken's Neck" — a live strategic vulnerability given Chinese presence near the tri-junction.

6. Trade & Economic Ties (CEPA)

Bangladesh is India's largest trade partner in South Asia and one of the biggest markets for Indian exports in Asia; the two are negotiating a Comprehensive Economic Partnership Agreement (CEPA) to deepen a rules-based investment regime.

8. Defence & Security Cooperation

Security cooperation is the quiet backbone of the partnership — from counter-insurgency to maritime patrols in the Bay of Bengal.

  • Counter-insurgency: Bangladesh's crackdown on North-East insurgent groups (ULFA, NDFB, etc.) sheltering on its soil — a decisive security dividend of the Hasina years.
  • Maritime: Exercise Bongosagar (bilateral naval) & CORPAT (Coordinated Patrol) in the Bay of Bengal; growing interoperability.
  • Army/Air: Exercise Sampriti (army) and Air Force exchanges; training slots in Indian academies.
  • Defence LoC: a ~US$500 mn line of credit (2019) for defence procurement — aimed at limiting Chinese hardware dependence.
  • Border management: BSF–BGB coordinated patrols against smuggling, trafficking & illegal crossing; contentious issue of border killings.
Post-2024 concern: a revival of Bangladesh–Pakistan defence/intelligence engagement (including reported interest in JF-17 jets) and reduced counter-terror cooperation are fresh security worries for India.

11. The China Factor

China's deepening presence in Bangladesh is the central strategic variable of the relationship — economic engagement that increasingly carries security & diplomatic weight, and the sharpest edge of the post-2024 recalibration.

Fig 6.3 — The China challenge and India's counter. Chinese economic push → strategic risk for India → India's connectivity, CEPA, soft-power & regime-neutral response.
  • Economic: China is Bangladesh's largest trading partner; ~US$2.1 bn in loans, grants & investments across SEZs, infrastructure, textiles & renewables.
  • Infrastructure: Chinese financing of the Padma Bridge, Payra Port, river projects & economic zones — raising maritime & water-security concerns for India.
  • Military: Bangladesh sources much of its hardware from China; naval visits & defence cooperation give Beijing a Bay of Bengal foothold.
  • India's counter: accelerate its own connectivity & port projects, push CEPA, offer to fund Teesta, deepen BBIN/BIMSTEC, and invest in soft power & a regime-neutral diplomacy that survives Dhaka's politics.
Framing line: India cannot out-spend China in Bangladesh — it must out-connect and out-trust it, leveraging geography, culture and the credibility of a first-responder neighbour.

12. The 2024 Political Crisis & Regime Change

Bangladesh's August 2024 mass protests ended Sheikh Hasina's 15-year rule, brought in an interim administration under Prof. Muhammad Yunus, and opened a new, more uncertain phase for India–Bangladesh ties.

12.1 What changed

  • Strategic equidistance: Dhaka stepped back from its India-centric posture toward a more balanced China engagement.
  • Pakistan thaw: revival of defence & diplomatic contact with Islamabad.
  • Minority safety: attacks on Hindus & on Indian cultural symbols during the unrest.
  • Return to elections: a phased return to elected governance, with implications for domestic stability & regional geopolitics. check for latest update or data

13. The Rohingya Refugee Crisis

The Rohingya crisis is a shared regional challenge that entangles India, Bangladesh & Myanmar — and a delicate test of India's balancing between humanitarian concern and strategic interests.

  • Scale: ~1 million Rohingya refugees shelter in Bangladesh's Cox's Bazar camps after fleeing Myanmar's Rakhine State (2017 exodus).
  • Bangladesh's ask: Dhaka wants India's diplomatic weight to press Myanmar for safe, voluntary repatriation.
  • India's balancing act: India engages both Bangladesh (aid via "Operation Insaniyat") and Myanmar's authorities (for the Kaladan project & border security) — hence a cautious posture.
  • Internal-security angle: concerns over radicalisation & the domestic status of Rohingya in India feed into the NRC-CAA debate.
GS-II hook: the Rohingya issue shows how a third-country humanitarian crisis (Myanmar) shapes a bilateral (India–Bangladesh) relationship — a good example of the interlinked nature of neighbourhood diplomacy.

15. Way Forward

A durable partnership must be future-oriented, trust-based and regime-neutral — built on connectivity, resilience and shared prosperity.

  • Institutionalise CEPA: replace ad-hoc trade with a deep, rules-based investment regime; rationalise NTBs.
  • Operationalise connectivity: make Akhaura–Agartala a permanent cargo route; complete stalled rail & port links.
  • Resolve water diplomatically: carry forward Teesta & renew the Ganga treaty transparently; basin-wide, climate-adaptive river governance.
  • Regime-neutral engagement: a state-to-state model so projects & security pacts survive shifts in Dhaka's politics.
  • Border humaneness: a non-lethal border regime to defuse the emotive "border killings" grievance.
  • Climate & delta resilience: joint Sundarbans conservation, flood forecasting & disaster management across the GBM basin.
  • People-to-people: scale up scholarships, medical visas, cultural exchange to rebuild trust after the 2024 churn.
Closing line for essays: a trust-based India–Bangladesh partnership founded on connectivity, resilience and shared prosperity is the cornerstone of South Asian integration — and the truest test of whether Neighbourhood First can weather political change.

UPSC Mains GS-II 2013 10 marks · 150 words

Q: The protests in Shahbag Square in Dhaka in Bangladesh reveal a fundamental split in society between the nationalists and Islamic forces. What is its significance for India?

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: The 2013 Shahbag movement demanded capital punishment for 1971 war criminals, exposing a deep societal cleavage between secular Bengali nationalists and Islamist forces — a contest over the very identity of the Bangladeshi state.
  2. Why it matters for India:
    • Security: a resurgent Islamist current risks reviving anti-India radicalism & sanctuaries for North-East insurgents; the secular-nationalist strand is more favourable to counter-terror cooperation.
    • Legacy of 1971: the war-crimes trials reaffirm the anti-Pakistan, pro-liberation narrative that underpins India–Bangladesh goodwill.
    • Minority safety: Islamist ascendance threatens Hindu & other minorities, creating migration & humanitarian pressures on India.
    • Strategic orientation: the ideological balance in Dhaka shapes whether Bangladesh leans toward India or toward Pakistan/China.
  3. India's calibrated stance: support democratic, secular stability without overt interference — deepen connectivity, trade & people-to-people ties that anchor Bangladesh to India regardless of the internal contest.
  4. Conclusion: Shahbag was a marker of Bangladesh's unfinished identity debate; its outcome directly conditions India's security, its minorities and the durability of Neighbourhood First — making Dhaka's internal balance a legitimate Indian strategic concern.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2022 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Do you think that BIMSTEC is a parallel organisation like the SAARC? What are the similarities and dissimilarities between the two? How are the Indian foreign policy objectives realized by forming this organisation?

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: BIMSTEC (1997) links five South Asian & two Southeast Asian states around the Bay of Bengal; with SAARC paralysed by India–Pakistan discord, it has become India's preferred regional vehicle — and Bangladesh is a founding, pivotal member.
  2. Similarities: both are regional groupings for economic & functional cooperation; both include India, Bangladesh, Bhutan, Nepal & Sri Lanka; both aim at connectivity & trade.
  3. Dissimilarities:
    • Membership: BIMSTEC excludes Pakistan & adds Myanmar & Thailand — bridging South & Southeast Asia.
    • Geography: BIMSTEC is a Bay-of-Bengal, sector-driven body; SAARC is a purely South Asian, consensus-bound one.
    • Effectiveness: BIMSTEC avoids the India–Pakistan veto that froze SAARC.
  4. How it serves India's objectives: operationalises Neighbourhood First + Act East; isolates Pakistan; anchors Bangladesh, Myanmar & Thailand into Indian-led connectivity (BBIN, coastal shipping, the Bay of Bengal); counters China's regional inroads.
  5. Conclusion: BIMSTEC is not a mere parallel to SAARC but a more functional successor for the eastern sub-region — with Bangladesh as the linchpin connecting the two organisations' overlapping memberships and India's twin doctrines.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2016 12.5 marks · 200 words

Q: Increasing cross-border terrorist attacks in India and growing interference in the internal affairs of member-states by Pakistan are not conducive for the future of SAARC (South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation). In the light of this statement, examine India's approach towards SAARC in future.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: SAARC's consensus rule lets Pakistan hold connectivity & trade hostage — epitomised by the collapse of the 2016 Islamabad summit after Uri. India has since shifted its regional weight toward sub-groupings where Bangladesh is a willing partner.
  2. India's evolving approach:
    • Isolate the spoiler: boycott Pakistan-hosted summits; expose state-sponsored terror.
    • Sub-regionalism: pivot to BBIN & BIMSTEC — where Bangladesh, Bhutan & Nepal advance the MVA & connectivity that Pakistan blocks in SAARC.
    • SAARC-minus-one functional cooperation: the SAARC satellite, COVID fund & disaster response.
    • Keep the door open: retain SAARC as a long-term aspiration contingent on Pakistan ending terror.
  3. Bangladesh's role: Dhaka's endorsement of the 2016 summit boycott and its BBIN leadership show how a cooperative neighbour lets India route around Pakistani obstruction.
  4. Conclusion: India's future SAARC approach is "cooperate where possible, bypass where blocked" — using Bangladesh-anchored sub-regionalism to keep integration alive without rewarding cross-border terror.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2015 12.5 marks · 200 words

Q: Project 'Mausam' is considered a unique foreign policy initiative of the Indian Government to improve relationship with its neighbours. Does the project have a strategic dimension? Discuss.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Project Mausam (2014, Ministry of Culture) seeks to revive India's ancient maritime & monsoon-driven trade & cultural links across the Indian Ocean littoral — a soft-power initiative with a hard strategic edge in the Bay of Bengal, where Bangladesh is a key node.
  2. Cultural / stated aim: reconnect Indian Ocean World civilisations; seek trans-national UNESCO heritage listing; project India as the natural civilisational anchor of the region.
  3. Strategic dimension:
    • Counter to China's MSR: a civilisational counter-narrative to the Maritime Silk Road & String of Pearls.
    • Bay of Bengal & Bangladesh: strengthens maritime & port linkages (Chattogram, Mongla) that tie littoral neighbours to India.
    • SAGAR/Act East synergy: complements maritime connectivity & the Neighbourhood First outreach.
  4. Limitations: under-funded & slow; risks staying rhetorical unless backed by real port, trade & connectivity investment.
  5. Conclusion: Mausam is soft power with strategic intent — a way to embed India's maritime neighbours, Bangladesh foremost, in an Indian-led regional order and blunt China's outreach, provided it is resourced beyond symbolism.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2017 15 marks · 250 words

Q: "China is using its economic relations and positive trade surplus as tools to develop potential military power status in Asia." In the light of this statement, discuss its impact on India as her neighbour.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: China converts economic leverage — trade surpluses, BRI loans, port investments — into strategic reach across India's periphery. Bangladesh is a textbook case, where Chinese finance is steadily acquiring strategic weight.
  2. How economic tools become strategic:
    • Ports & infrastructure: Padma Bridge, Payra Port & SEZs give Beijing a Bay-of-Bengal foothold — the "String of Pearls" logic.
    • Debt & dependence: ~US$2.1 bn in loans/grants & largest-trading-partner status create leverage over Dhaka.
    • Arms: Bangladesh sources much hardware from China, deepening defence dependence.
    • Water: the China-backed Teesta Master Plan turns a river dispute into strategic competition.
  3. Impact on India: encirclement pressure, erosion of strategic space in a key neighbour, added vulnerability around the Siliguri corridor, and competition for Bangladesh's markets.
  4. India's response: fast-track CEPA, accelerate its own connectivity & port projects, offer to fund Teesta, deepen BBIN/BIMSTEC, and leverage civilisational & soft-power ties China cannot match.
  5. Conclusion: China's economics-to-strategy playbook makes India's neighbourhood the frontline of the rivalry; India must out-connect & out-trust rather than out-spend — nowhere more so than in Bangladesh.
