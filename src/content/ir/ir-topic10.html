Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames India–Bhutan

Bhutan is tested as geography (Siliguri Corridor, Doklam trijunction, Himalayan rivers), the hydropower economy, treaty diplomacy (1949/2007) and the China contest (boundary talks, Doklam, Sakteng). Sort your prep:

  • Static/geography — landlocked eastern Himalayan kingdom, the Siliguri Corridor / Chicken's Neck (India's ~22 km lifeline to the Northeast), the Doklam trijunction & Chumbi Valley, hydro rivers (Punatsangchhu, Mangdechhu, Wangchhu). High-yield map recall.
  • Institutional/policy — the 1949 Treaty (revised 2007), the free-trade regime (2016), IMTRAT, the ngultrum's rupee peg, RuPay, BBIN & BIMSTEC, the 10,000 MW hydropower goal, the India-Bhutan SAT.
  • Analytical (GS-II) — the 2017 Doklam standoff, Bhutan's China boundary talks (3-step roadmap, Sakteng, Pangda village), hydro-tariff & trade-diversification irritants, India backing the Gelephu Mindfulness City. Needs idea + example + judgement.

Territorial note: Arunachal Pradesh is an integral part of India — China's "South Tibet" claim is rejected; Aksai Chin is Indian territory under Chinese occupation. India has no territorial dispute with Bhutan; the Doklam plateau is Bhutanese territory, and India supports Bhutan's sovereign position there. Maps in this file reflect India's official boundaries.

1. Overview of the Relationship

India–Bhutan is often called India's most trusted & frictionless partnership — a model of Neighbourhood First built on trust, mutual respect & shared development, anchored by the 1949 Treaty of Friendship (revised 2007).

  • What binds them: an "indestructible security bond", a deep hydropower economy (India buys Bhutan's clean power; Bhutan funds its budget), shared Buddhist heritage & open people-to-people ties.
  • The economic core: India accounts for over 80% of Bhutan's total trade, funds its Five-Year Plans, and the ngultrum is pegged 1:1 to the rupee — an unusually deep integration.
  • The strategic core: Bhutan guards the approaches to the Siliguri Corridor (India's lifeline to the Northeast); the 2017 Doklam standoff showed how India & Bhutan jointly resist Chinese encroachment.
  • The evolving edge: Bhutan's China boundary talks & its Gelephu Mindfulness City diversification test India's ability to stay the indispensable partner without appearing to constrain Bhutan's sovereignty.
One-line frame: Bhutan is India's gold-standard neighbour — a relationship of trust & hydro-interdependence, now quietly recalibrating as China courts Thimphu & Bhutan seeks economic diversification.

3. Geography & Strategic Location

Bhutan's value to India is inseparable from the Siliguri Corridor — the ~22 km "Chicken's Neck" that is India's only land link to its eight Northeastern states. Bhutan sits directly above it.

  • The Chicken's Neck: a narrow strip between Nepal, Bangladesh & Bhutan connecting mainland India to the Northeast — India's single greatest strategic vulnerability.
  • The Doklam trijunction: where Bhutan, China (Chumbi Valley) & India (Sikkim) meet — Chinese control of the plateau would put the Siliguri Corridor within striking range.
  • Chumbi Valley: the Chinese salient pointing at the corridor; Doklam is the buffer that keeps China from dominating it.
  • Himalayan rivers: the Punatsangchhu, Mangdechhu, Wangchhu & Amochhu — the fast-flowing rivers that make Bhutan a hydropower giant & the physical basis of the energy partnership.

5. Hydropower & Energy Partnership

Hydropower is the bedrock of the relationship — a rare win-win where India gets clean power & Bhutan funds its national budget from exports.

  • The model: India finances & builds the dams; Bhutan sells surplus power back to India; the revenue is Bhutan's single largest income source — "the water that lights India, funds Bhutan".
  • Flagship projects: Chukha (336 MW, 1988), Tala (1,020 MW), Mangdechhu (720 MW, 2019), Punatsangchhu-I (1,200 MW, long-delayed) & Punatsangchhu-II (1,020 MW, inaugurated 11 Nov 2025).
  • The 10,000 MW goal: India & Bhutan target ~10,000 MW of installed capacity; Bhutan's untapped potential is ~30,000–40,000 MW, of which only ~2,500–3,000 MW is operational.
  • Trade volume: electricity trade reached ~5,600 GWh (5.6 billion units) in 2021 — the metric of interdependence.
  • The irritants: cost overruns & delays (Punatsangchhu-I stalled ~7 years over geology) & tariff disputes have caused debt-servicing anxiety in Thimphu — the main friction in an otherwise smooth relationship.
GS-II angle: hydropower is both the glue & the grit — it binds the economies together, but delays & debt have fed Bhutanese calls to diversify, opening space for Chinese & third-party interest.

7. Defence & Security

Security cooperation is quiet but foundational — an "indestructible bond" that guards both the Northeast & the Himalayan frontier.

  • IMTRAT: the Indian Military Training Team permanently stationed in Bhutan trains the Royal Bhutan Army — a unique, trusted defence footprint.
  • Operation All Clear (2003): the Royal Bhutan Army's own operation flushing ULFA & NDFB insurgents from southern Bhutan — direct help to India's Northeast security.
  • Siliguri Corridor defence: Bhutan's terrain shields the approaches to the Chicken's Neck; coordinated border surveillance & intelligence-sharing keep the corridor secure.
  • Border-roads & infrastructure: India's Project DANTAK (BRO) built key Bhutanese roads & Paro Airport — dual development & strategic value.
  • Regional forums: both cooperate in BBIN (Bangladesh-Bhutan-India-Nepal) & BIMSTEC for connectivity & security.
The bond: unlike any other neighbour, Bhutan hosts a standing Indian military training presence & coordinates security without friction — the clearest expression of mutual trust.

9. Doklam & the Siliguri Corridor

The 2017 Doklam standoff is the defining strategic episode — it showed how India, Bhutan & China intersect at one small plateau that overlooks India's most vulnerable point.

  • What happened (2017): China began building a road on the Doklam plateau — Bhutanese territory near the trijunction. India intervened on Bhutan's behalf; a ~73-day face-off followed before mutual disengagement.
  • Why India acted: Chinese control of Doklam would extend the Chumbi Valley salient toward the Siliguri Corridor — putting the Chicken's Neck, India's only land link to the Northeast, within artillery range.
  • The trijunction principle: India insists the tri-boundary point be settled by all three parties (2012 understanding); China's unilateral construction violated that — the legal core of India's stand.
  • The lingering risk: since 2017 China has built villages (e.g. Pangda) & infrastructure near Doklam; a Bhutan–China "package deal" that swaps Doklam would be an India red line.
GS-II hook: Doklam is the textbook case of India defending a third country's territory to protect its own vital interest — a demonstration of the "indestructible bond" & of the Siliguri Corridor's centrality to Indian security.

10. The China Factor — The India–Bhutan–China Triangle

China's economic & territorial outreach is reshaping Bhutan's foreign engagements — the central strategic challenge for India in the eastern Himalayas.

Fig 11.3 — The India–Bhutan–China triangle: Beijing's push (boundary talks, Sakteng/Pangda, SOEs, outreach), the risks to India (Doklam land-swap, Siliguri exposure), & India's calibrated response.
  • Territorial assertion: China claims Bhutan's North (Jakarlung, Pasamlung) & West (Doklam), and in 2020 extended a fresh claim to the Sakteng Wildlife Sanctuary (East) & built Pangda village.
  • Economic footprint: Chinese SOEs eye strategic sectors; Chinese firms expand telecom/internet, raising data-sovereignty concerns.
  • India's worry: a Bhutan–China boundary "package deal" involving Doklam would compromise the Siliguri Corridor — India's core red line.

13. Gelephu Mindfulness City & Connectivity

  • Gelephu Mindfulness City (GMC): Bhutan's flagship Special Administrative Region on the India border (Assam side), a "mindfulness economy" hub built on the Gross National Happiness ethos — Bhutan's boldest bid for foreign investment & diversification.
  • India's strategic choice: rather than resist diversification, India backs GMC — ensuring Bhutan's growth story stays anchored to Indian markets, connectivity & capital, not China's.
  • Kokrajhar–Gelephu rail: the cross-border rail link (Assam→Gelephu) is the physical spine connecting GMC to India's rail network — the connectivity that makes anchoring real.
  • GYALSUNG & capacity: India supports Bhutan's national-service & skilling programmes, building human capital for the GMC economy.
  • Multi-modal integration: Project DANTAK roads, waterways & digital links weave Bhutan into India's networks — connectivity as the antidote to strategic drift.
Why it matters: GMC is the test case for a new kind of neighbourhood diplomacy — India shifting from hydropower patron to diversification partner, staying indispensable as Bhutan's economy modernises.

15. Way Forward

  • Strategic autonomy & diplomacy: adopt a non-intrusive engagement model, deepen inter-parliamentary cooperation, & back Bhutan's role in Neighbourhood First — India's support for the Gelephu Mindfulness City keeps diversification anchored to Indian investment.
  • Diversified economic partnership: promote fintech, space-tech, tourism & services aligned with Bhutan's Gross National Happiness model.
  • Treaty revision & sovereignty: periodically review the 2007 Treaty so it reflects Bhutan's status as an equal, sovereign partner while keeping the indestructible security bond.
  • Hydropower & energy: fast-track pending projects toward the 10,000 MW goal; ensure timely execution, fair tariffs & clean-energy partnerships.
  • Countering China's influence: assist Bhutan in managing its border talks (3-step roadmap); consider an India–Bhutan–China trilogue to reduce strategic ambiguity.
  • Defence & border security: enhance joint exercises, real-time intelligence on insurgent threats, air defence & border surveillance.
Closing frame: India–Bhutan is a model of cooperative regionalism — built on trust, mutual respect & shared developmental goals. Guided by sensitivity to each other's strategic interests, it remains South Asia's gold standard of neighbourhood diplomacy.

18. Mains PYQs — model answers

Note on sourcing: UPSC rarely sets a Bhutan-only Mains question, so the papers below are genuine dated GS-II PYQs on India's neighbourhood, China and the Himalayan frontier — each answer is framed explicitly through the India–Bhutan / Doklam / Siliguri lens so the practice stays exam-authentic without inventing a fake "Bhutan PYQ".

UPSC Mains GS-II 2017

"China is using its economic relations and positive trade surplus as tools to develop potential military power status in Asia." In the light of this statement, discuss its impact on India as her neighbour. (250 words)

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: China converts trade surplus → strategic leverage across South Asia; for India the sharpest edge is the Himalayan frontier, where Bhutan — India's closest treaty partner — sits astride the Doklam trijunction guarding the Siliguri Corridor.
  2. Economic-to-military conversion: BRI debt (Sri Lanka Hambantota, Pakistan CPEC, Nepal), SOE-led infrastructure, telecom penetration; in Bhutan China dangles boundary settlement + investment to prise open the last neighbour without diplomatic ties to Beijing.
  3. Impact on India: (i) Doklam 2017 — PLA road toward Jampheri Ridge threatened the ~22 km Chicken's Neck linking mainland to the Northeast; (ii) salami-slicing — Pangda village, Sakteng claim (2020), Jakarlung–Pasamlung in the north; (iii) three-step roadmap + first Chinese FM visit (2023) shrink India's strategic space.
  4. India's counter: IMTRAT & security consultations, fast-tracked hydropower (Punatsangchhu-II inaugurated Nov 2025), backing Gelephu Mindfulness City to keep Bhutan's diversification India-anchored, quiet border management — without appearing to veto Bhutan's sovereign China talks.
  5. Conclusion: Neutralising China's economic-military conversion needs India to remain Bhutan's development partner of first resort while respecting Thimphu's autonomy — security through generosity, not tutelage.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2014

With respect to the diversity of India as a nation, discuss the persisting boundary and territorial issues with India's neighbours and the strategic responses India has adopted. (250 words) [adapted to the Bhutan trijunction]

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: Unlike disputed borders elsewhere, India–Bhutan is fully settled and friendly — the flashpoint is the unsettled China–Bhutan boundary, where the trijunction directly implicates Indian security.
  2. The trijunction problem: Doklam plateau is Bhutanese; China's 2017 road threatened to shift the trijunction south toward Jampheri, overlooking the Siliguri Corridor — India intervened under the 2007 Treaty's "close coordination" clause.
  3. China's tactics: "package deal" (swap western Doklam for northern Jakarlung/Pasamlung), 2020 Sakteng eastern claim as bargaining chip, Pangda border village construction.
  4. India's strategic responses: forward-defence at Siliguri, IMTRAT training presence, treaty consultations, developmental deepening (hydropower, RuPay, 13th FYP support) so Bhutan's calculus stays tilted to Delhi.
  5. Conclusion: India's response blends military readiness with economic partnership — deterring boundary revisionism while honouring Bhutanese sovereignty over its own negotiations.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2016

"The protests in Shahbag Square in Dhaka in Bangladesh reveal a fundamental split in society..." — more broadly, cross-border security threats strain India's neighbourhood diplomacy. Examine with reference to insurgency and India's Himalayan partners. (250 words) [adapted]

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: Cross-border insurgency has repeatedly tested neighbourhood ties; Bhutan stands out as the partner that acted decisively for India's internal security.
  2. Operation All Clear (2003): the Royal Bhutan Army flushed out ULFA, NDFB and KLO camps in southern Bhutan — a sovereign neighbour clearing anti-India insurgents from its soil, rare in the region.
  3. Contrast: vs Pakistan's state-sponsored terror and Myanmar's ungoverned NE-insurgent sanctuaries — Bhutan's cooperation is the gold standard of neighbourly security trust.
  4. Sustaining it: IMTRAT, intelligence sharing, secure Siliguri approaches, and development so southern Bhutan (Gelephu belt) does not become a soft frontier.
  5. Conclusion: Security cooperation flows from political trust; India's Bhutan model shows that development-plus-respect yields harder security dividends than coercion.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2013

The proposed withdrawal of International Security Assistance Force (ISAF) from Afghanistan in 2014 is fraught with major security implications for the countries of the region. Examine in light of the "String of Pearls" and China's regional footprint. (250 words) [String-of-Pearls lens applied to the Himalayan frontier]

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: China's encirclement is not only maritime ("String of Pearls") but continental — a Himalayan pincer via Nepal and Bhutan complements the Indian Ocean pearls.
  2. The continental pearls: BRI in Nepal, Pangda/Sakteng pressure on Bhutan, Aksai Chin roads, CPEC through PoK — a land-side squeeze on India's northern arc.
  3. Bhutan as the last gap: Bhutan alone has no ties with Beijing; the three-step roadmap + Gelephu diversification are China's levers to close the gap and outflank Siliguri.
  4. India's answer: keep Bhutan the exception — hydropower revenue, RuPay/trade integration, security guarantees, and backing Gelephu so growth capital comes from India, not China.
  5. Conclusion: Countering encirclement means denying China its last continental pearl — a secure, prosperous, India-aligned Bhutan is India's best Himalayan insurance.
UPSC Mains GS-II 2022

"If the last few decades were of Asia's growth story, the next few are expected to be of Africa's." In this context, examine India's influence and role among its neighbours. Alternatively — discuss whether India's "Neighbourhood First" policy can coexist with the strategic autonomy of small neighbours. (250 words)

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: Neighbourhood First succeeds only if small neighbours feel it enlarges, not limits, their autonomy — Bhutan is the test case.
  2. The tension: the 2007 Treaty replaced the 1949 "guidance" clause with sovereign-equality; Bhutan now negotiates its own China boundary and courts diversified investment (Gelephu, tourism).
  3. How coexistence works: India funds hydropower and the 13th FYP, integrates payments (RuPay/ngultrum peg), yet does not veto Bhutan's China talks — partnership by consent, not clientelism.
  4. Risks if mishandled: perceived big-brother behaviour (echoes of Nepal 2015 blockade, Maldives "India Out") could push even Bhutan to hedge.
  5. Conclusion: Neighbourhood First and strategic autonomy coexist when India delivers development without conditionality — Bhutan proves generosity plus respect is the durable model.

15-Minute Revision Sprint

Treaties & framework

  • 1949 Indo-Bhutan Treaty (Article 2 "guidance"); revised 2007 — sovereign equality, "close coordination", no guidance clause.
  • Ngultrum pegged 1:1 to rupee; free-trade regime; RuPay live; >80% of Bhutan's trade with India.
  • 13th Five-Year Plan (2024–29) — India the largest development partner; GNH philosophy.

Hydropower spine

  • Chukha 336 MW (1988), Tala 1020 MW, Mangdechhu 720 MW (2019).
  • Punatsangchhu-I 1200 MW (delayed ~7 yrs), Punatsangchhu-II 1020 MW inaugurated 11 Nov 2025.
  • 10,000 MW cooperation goal; potential 30,000–40,000 MW; export revenue = fiscal backbone.

Security & flashpoints

  • IMTRAT training mission; Operation All Clear 2003 (ULFA/NDFB flushed out).
  • Siliguri Corridor / Chicken's Neck ~22 km; Doklam 2017 ~73-day standoff; Chumbi Valley salient.
  • Sakteng claim (2020), Pangda village, Jakarlung–Pasamlung; China 3-step roadmap + first FM visit 2023.

New & connectivity

  • Gelephu Mindfulness City (SAR on Assam border); Kokrajhar–Gelephu rail link.
  • Project DANTAK (BRO), Paro Airport; BBIN & BIMSTEC; TraMCA, India-Bhutan SAT.
  • GYALSUNG national service; Nehru-Wangchuck Scholarship.
