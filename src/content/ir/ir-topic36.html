Topic 36: The Changing Sphere of Diplomacy

Diplomacy is no longer only about war, peace & embassies. It now runs on trade, energy, vaccines, data, climate, culture & the diaspora. This capstone file maps how the instruments, actors & arenas of statecraft have changed — and how India has re-tooled from a non-aligned petitioner into a multi-aligned "leading power" & first responder — covering economic, energy, vaccine (Vaccine Maitri), digital & cyber, climate (ISA, CDRI, LiFE), soft-power, defence, disaster/HADR & summit diplomacy, with tables, dated developments and PYQ practice + model answers.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames the Changing Sphere of Diplomacy

This is a thematic, GS-II favourite tested not as a country but as a lens: how the agenda, instruments and actors of diplomacy have widened beyond traditional political-military statecraft, and how India uses these new tools to project itself as a "leading power" and net security/first provider. Sort your prep:

  • Static/institutional — the named instruments & initiatives: Vaccine Maitri, ISA, CDRI, Mission LiFE, IPOI, IBSA, Voice of Global South Summit, ITEC, IDEAS/Lines of Credit, ICCR, International Day of Yoga (2015), Operations Ganga/Kaveri/Dost. High-yield factual recall.
  • Reform architecture/economic — the shift in what diplomacy delivers: FTAs & commercial diplomacy, digital public infrastructure (DPI) export, critical-mineral & energy sourcing, climate finance & norm-setting.
  • Analytical (GS-II) — the why & the judgement: from non-alignment to multi-alignment/"Vishwamitra", soft power vs hard interests, the rise of non-state actors (MNCs, diaspora, social media, sub-national/paradiplomacy), and the limits (capacity, over-stretch, credibility). Needs idea + example + judgement.

Territorial note: Any map used in this theme shows India with its full official boundaries — Jammu & Kashmir, Ladakh (including PoK & Aksai Chin under illegal Pakistani/Chinese occupation) and Arunachal Pradesh are integral parts of India, reflecting the Government of India's official position.

1. What Is the "Changing Sphere of Diplomacy"?

Diplomacy is the management of a state's external relations by peaceful means. Traditionally it was narrow — conducted by professional diplomats, in capitals, about war, peace, alliances & borders. The "changing sphere" describes how, since the end of the Cold War & the rise of globalisation, the agenda, instruments, actors and arenas of diplomacy have all widened dramatically — so that vaccines, data, climate targets, supply chains, chefs & cricket are now tools of statecraft.

DimensionTraditional diplomacyNew / changing diplomacy
AgendaPeace, war, borders, alliancesTrade, energy, climate, health, tech, migration, culture
ActorsStates & career diplomats only+ MNCs, diaspora, NGOs, cities/states (paradiplomacy), citizens

ArenaBilateral, behind closed doors+ Multilateral, plurilateral, public & digital, real-time

One-line frame: diplomacy has moved from "who governs territory" to "who shapes flows" — of goods, capital, energy, data, people & ideas. India's task is to convert these flows into strategic influence.

2. Drivers of Change: Why Diplomacy Changed

The transformation is not accidental — it flows from structural shifts in the international system after 1991. Understanding the drivers lets you explain any new instrument (vaccine, digital, climate) as a response to the same forces.

  • Globalisation & interdependence: trade, finance & supply chains bind economies — making economic & commercial diplomacy central and turning interdependence into a weapon ("weaponised interdependence": sanctions, chip & energy controls).
  • Technology revolution: the internet, social media & 24/7 news collapse time & secrecy — enabling digital/public diplomacy and real-time signalling.
  • Multipolarity & the rise of the Global South: the end of bipolarity created space for issue-based coalitions & multi-alignment rather than fixed blocs.
  • Transnational challenges: pandemics, climate change, terrorism & cyber threats cross borders — forcing health, climate & cyber diplomacy that no state can handle alone.
  • Rise of non-state actors: MNCs, sovereign funds, the diaspora, NGOs, credit-rating agencies & even influencers now shape outcomes — diluting the state's monopoly.
  • Democratisation of foreign policy: vocal publics & sub-national governments (state CMs courting investment = paradiplomacy) make diplomacy more accountable & participatory.

3. Economic & Commercial Diplomacy

Economic diplomacy — using foreign policy to secure markets, investment, technology & resources, and using economic weight for strategic ends — is now the core of statecraft. For a developing power like India, embassies are increasingly "deal desks" promoting exports, courting FDI and negotiating trade agreements.

  • Trade agreements as diplomacy: the FTA sprint — UAE CEPA (2022), Australia ECTA (2022), EFTA TEPA (2024, ~$100 bn investment pledge), UK CETA (2025), and the India–EU FTA under negotiation — each is a strategic, not merely commercial, act.
  • Investment & "commercial diplomacy": Make in India, PLI schemes & Invest India court semiconductor, EV & supply-chain investors; missions abroad chase capital & technology.
  • Development finance as influence: Lines of Credit (LoCs) under IDEAS, grant projects & capacity-building (ITEC) build goodwill across Africa, South Asia & the Indian Ocean — a softer counter to China's BRI.
  • Weaponised interdependence: sanctions, export controls (chips), and payment systems (SWIFT cut-off of Russia) show economics as coercion — India hedges via rupee-trade, discounted crude & strategic autonomy.
  • Resource & critical-mineral diplomacy: KABIL's lithium hunt in Argentina, the Minerals Security Partnership, and cobalt/copper deals secure the inputs of the green & digital economy.
GS-II hook: for India, economic diplomacy is where development goals meet strategic autonomy — FTAs, LoCs and critical-mineral deals are the new currency of influence, not aid received but partnerships offered.

4. Energy & Resource Diplomacy

As the world's third-largest energy consumer importing ~85% of its crude, India practises energy diplomacy to guarantee affordable, secure & increasingly clean supply — and to shape the global energy transition on its own terms.

  • Diversification of sources: pivoting from West Asia to Russia (discounted crude post-2022), the US & Africa — hedging price & geopolitical risk.
  • Strategic autonomy in sourcing: buying Russian oil despite Western pressure — a textbook multi-alignment choice defended as a developing-country right.
  • Green-energy leadership: the International Solar Alliance (ISA, 2015, co-founded with France, HQ Gurugram) & One Sun One World One Grid (OSOWOG) reposition India as a rule-maker, not just a consumer.
  • Nuclear & gas diplomacy: civil-nuclear deals (post-2008 waiver), the stalled TAPI & the operational Chabahar/INSTC route diversify the energy map.
  • Critical minerals for transition: lithium, cobalt & rare earths sourcing (KABIL, MSP) — energy diplomacy now extends to the materials of clean power.
Prelims pointer: ISA (India + France, 2015, HQ Gurugram) & CDRI (India-led, 2019) & OSOWOG are the three India-founded green-diplomacy platforms most likely to be tested.

5. Vaccine & Health Diplomacy

Health diplomacy uses medicine, vaccines & medical capacity as instruments of goodwill & influence. India — the "pharmacy of the world" (~60% of global vaccine volume) — turned the COVID-19 pandemic into a signature soft-power moment.

  • Vaccine Maitri (2021): India supplied Made-in-India COVID vaccines — grants, commercial sales & COVAX — to ~100 countries, prioritising neighbours & the Global South before wealthy states, in contrast to "vaccine nationalism."
  • Pharmacy of the world: low-cost generics & the Serum Institute (world's largest by volume) underpin India's health-provider identity.
  • Medical missions & capacity-building: field hospitals, medical teams & the export of the Jan Aushadhi generics model build durable goodwill.
  • Traditional medicine diplomacy: the WHO Global Centre for Traditional Medicine (Jamnagar, 2022) & promotion of AYUSH project India's civilisational health heritage.
  • Limits & lessons: the Delta-wave export pause (2021) showed the reputational risk when domestic need collides with pledges abroad — capacity must match commitments.
GS-II hook: Vaccine Maitri fused capability + values — India delivered global public goods while advancing its "first responder / Vishwaguru" narrative, but the pause taught that credibility depends on reliable delivery, not gestures.

6. Digital, Data & Cyber Diplomacy

Digital diplomacy has two faces: (a) diplomacy about the digital — norms for data, cyber & AI; and (b) the digital as an export — India's Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI) as a development & influence tool. India is now a leading voice in shaping the rules of the digital age for the Global South.

  • DPI as a global public good: India offered the India Stack / Aadhaar-UPI model to the world — the G20 2023 endorsed a DPI framework & the Global DPI Repository; UPI links now with UAE, Singapore, France, Sri Lanka & others.
  • Cyber-norm diplomacy: India engages the UN GGE/OEWG on responsible state behaviour in cyberspace, data-flow rules & the fight against ransomware & cross-border cybercrime.
  • Data sovereignty vs free flow: balancing the DPDP Act (2023) & data localisation against Western "free-flow" demands — protecting sovereignty while enabling trade.
  • Tech partnerships: the iCET with the US, semiconductor & AI cooperation, and the Global Partnership on AI (India chaired 2024) place India in critical-tech coalitions.
  • Techno-diplomacy of standards: shaping 5G/6G, AI-governance & quantum norms so that emerging-tech rules are not written only by the US, EU & China.
Prelims pointer: DPI = Digital Public Infrastructure (Aadhaar + UPI + DigiLocker "India Stack"); India's G20 2023 legacy includes the Global DPI Repository & a Social Impact Fund for DPI in the Global South.

7. Climate & Green Diplomacy

Climate diplomacy is where India most visibly shifted from a defensive negotiator (protecting the right to develop) to a proactive coalition-builder (founding institutions & setting norms) — all while defending climate justice & common-but-differentiated responsibilities (CBDR).

  • India-founded platforms: the International Solar Alliance (ISA, 2015), the Coalition for Disaster Resilient Infrastructure (CDRI, 2019) & the LeadIT (with Sweden) — India as institution-builder, not just participant.
  • Panchamrit & Net-Zero 2070: at COP26 (Glasgow 2021) India pledged the five "Panchamrit" targets, including net-zero by 2070 & 500 GW non-fossil capacity by 2030.
  • Mission LiFE (2022): "Lifestyle for Environment" — reframing climate action around demand-side behaviour, an idea India took global (UNEP, G20).
  • Climate justice & finance: India champions the Global South's demand for finance, technology transfer & the Loss & Damage Fund (operationalised at COP28, 2023), insisting the North honour historical responsibility.
  • Green alliances: the Global Biofuels Alliance (launched at G20 2023) & green-hydrogen partnerships extend climate leadership into the energy-transition economy.
GS-II hook: India's climate diplomacy squares a circle — leading on solutions (ISA, LiFE, net-zero 2070) while defending equity (CBDR, climate finance). It converts a vulnerability into a platform for Global-South leadership.

8. Cultural & Soft-Power Diplomacy

Soft power — Joseph Nye's idea of getting others to want what you want through attraction, not coercion — is a natural Indian strength. Cultural diplomacy institutionalises this civilisational appeal into influence.

  • Yoga diplomacy: the UN-declared International Day of Yoga (21 June, since 2015) — India's most successful soft-power campaign, co-sponsored by a record number of states.
  • Buddhist diplomacy: leveraging India as the land of the Buddha to build ties with Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Thailand, Japan & East Asia (Kushinagar airport, IBC).
  • Diaspora & popular culture: Bollywood, cuisine ("gastro-diplomacy"), music & festivals project a modern, plural India; the ICCR runs cultural centres & chairs worldwide.
  • Civilisational branding: "Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam" (the world is one family — the G20 2023 theme), millets (International Year of Millets 2023) & heritage (yoga, Ayurveda) as a distinctive brand.
  • Education & scholarships: ITEC, ICCR scholarships & the "Study in India" programme build long-term networks of goodwill among future foreign elites.
Prelims pointer: International Day of Yoga = 21 June (adopted by UNGA in 2014, first observed 2015); ICCR (Indian Council for Cultural Relations, 1950) is the nodal cultural-diplomacy body.

9. Diaspora Diplomacy

The ~35-million-strong overseas Indian community — the world's largest diaspora and top remittance recipient (~$125 bn) — is a distinctive instrument: a bridge of influence, investment, soft power & advocacy. (For depth on categories & policy, see Topic 29.)

  • Strategic asset: a politically influential diaspora shapes host-country policy — 26 Indian-origin MPs in the UK (2024), US Congress caucuses & leaders across governments.
  • Economic bridge: record remittances, investment, philanthropy & a talent pipeline in tech & finance (Silicon Valley CEOs).
  • Engagement architecture: Pravasi Bharatiya Divas (9 January), OCI cards, the diaspora-outreach of high-profile summit events, and MEA's dedicated diaspora division.
  • Evacuation diplomacy: caring for citizens abroad — Operations Ganga (Ukraine 2022), Kaveri (Sudan 2023) & Vande Bharat — converts consular duty into a "first responder" narrative.
  • Limits: diaspora interests are not monolithic (Khalistan extremism abroad); over-reliance risks host-state backlash & charges of interference.
GS-II hook: the diaspora is a force multiplier that blends soft power, economic weight & political advocacy — but it is an asset to be engaged, not owned, and its diversity means influence is real yet uneven.

10. Summit, Public & Digital Diplomacy

The arena of diplomacy has gone public & personal. Leaders now negotiate directly at summits, and communicate over the heads of governments to foreign publics via media & social platforms.

  • Summit diplomacy: India's G20 Presidency (2023) — consensus New Delhi Declaration, African Union made a permanent member, IMEC & the Global Biofuels Alliance launched — showcased convening power.
  • Voice of the Global South: India hosted Voice of Global South Summits (from 2023), positioning itself as spokesperson & bridge for developing nations.
  • Public diplomacy: the MEA's public-diplomacy division, diaspora mega-events & cultural outreach shape foreign publics' perceptions of India.
  • Digital / "Twitter" diplomacy: real-time signalling, rapid consular help via social media, and rebuttal of disinformation — diplomacy at the speed of the news cycle.
  • Minilateral & plurilateral diplomacy: issue-based coalitions (Quad, I2U2, IBSA, BRICS+, IPEF) — agile "coalitions of the willing" replacing slow, universal bodies.
Prelims pointer: India's G20 2023 deliverables to remember: New Delhi Leaders' Declaration (consensus), AU as permanent member, IMEC, Global Biofuels Alliance, DPI framework & the theme Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam / One Earth·One Family·One Future.

11. Defence & Security Diplomacy

Defence diplomacy uses the military as an instrument of peacetime statecraft — building trust, interoperability & influence without firing a shot. For India it also advances the "Atmanirbhar" arms-export ambition.

  • Joint exercises: a dense web — Malabar (with US/Japan/Australia), Varuna (France), Indra (Russia), Yudh Abhyas (US) & dozens more — signalling partnerships & interoperability.
  • Defence exports: BrahMos to the Philippines (2022), Akash & artillery — India crossing ~₹21,000 cr in exports (FY24), using arms as strategic instruments.
  • Training & capacity-building: foreign officers trained in Indian academies (a durable network of influence), plus defence LoCs & ship/aircraft gifting to Indian Ocean neighbours.
  • Maritime & net-security provider: HADR, anti-piracy escorts, the IFC-IOR (Gurugram, 2018) & SAGAR/MAHASAGAR position India as the Indian Ocean's first responder.
  • 2+2 dialogues: foreign + defence ministerial formats with the US, Japan, Australia, Russia & the UK institutionalise strategic-defence coordination.
GS-II hook: defence diplomacy lets India build strategic weight without alliances — exercises, exports & training create influence & interoperability while preserving strategic autonomy.

12. Disaster, Humanitarian & Development Diplomacy

Increasingly, India projects influence by being the first to help — in disasters, evacuations & development — earning the label "first responder" in its neighbourhood & the Indian Ocean.

  • HADR & disaster relief: rapid aid in crises — Operation Dost (Turkey–Syria earthquake 2023), Nepal (2015), tsunami & cyclone relief across the IOR.
  • Evacuation missions: Operation Ganga (Ukraine 2022), Kaveri (Sudan 2023), Raahat (Yemen 2015), Vande Bharat (COVID) — protecting citizens & often foreign nationals too.
  • Development partnership diplomacy: grants, Lines of Credit (IDEAS), capacity-building (ITEC) & flagship projects (Afghan Parliament, Nepal/Bhutan hydro, Sri Lanka housing) — a demand-driven, "no-strings" model vs BRI.
  • Food & humanitarian aid: wheat & medicine to Afghanistan, Myanmar & the neighbourhood during crises, reinforcing the "Neighbourhood First" & Vishwabandhu image.
  • Global-good provision: vaccines, disaster-resilient-infrastructure (CDRI) & solar access (ISA) — positioning India as a net contributor, not merely a claimant.
Prelims pointer: match the operations — Ganga = Ukraine (2022), Kaveri = Sudan (2023), Dost = Turkey–Syria quake (2023), Raahat = Yemen (2015). IDEAS governs India's concessional Lines of Credit.

13. India's Doctrine: Non-Alignment → Multi-Alignment

The changing sphere is anchored in a changed doctrine. India has evolved from Nehruvian non-alignment (staying out of Cold-War blocs) to multi-alignment / strategic autonomy 2.0 — engaging all major powers on an issue-by-issue basis to maximise gains.

EraDoctrineLogic
1947–1991Non-AlignmentAvoid entanglement in the US–USSR bipolar contest; preserve autonomy
1991–2014Strategic AutonomyPost-Cold-War hedging; economic liberalisation; look East & West
2014–presentMulti-Alignment / "Leading Power"Proactive, issue-based partnerships with all poles; agenda-shaping, not just balancing
  • Multi-alignment: Quad and BRICS/SCO, US defence deals and Russian oil & S-400 — partnerships without alliances.
  • "Vishwamitra / Vishwaguru": India as a friend to the world & a provider of global goods, rather than a swing state.
  • Global-South leadership: Voice of the Global South, "bridge" between North & South — a distinctive positioning.
  • Strategic autonomy retained: the through-line — India resists binding blocs, keeping freedom of manoeuvre in a multipolar order.
GS-II hook: multi-alignment is not a betrayal of non-alignment — it is its update: the same goal (autonomy & a voice) pursued by engaging everyone rather than abstaining from everyone.

14. Challenges to the New Diplomacy

The widened toolkit brings new vulnerabilities. Book-depth answers must weigh the limits, not just celebrate the instruments.

  • Capacity & over-stretch: a small foreign service (~1,000 IFS officers) & limited resources strain against a swelling global agenda — the credibility gap between promise & delivery (recall the vaccine pause).
  • Balancing rival poles: multi-alignment invites pressure & distrust from all sides — US on Russia oil/S-400 (CAATSA), Russia/China on the Quad.
  • Domestic-image spillover: internal politics (minority rights, press freedom) can dent the soft-power brand & invite external criticism.
  • Weaponised interdependence: the same flows that empower can be turned into coercion — sanctions, chip & tech denial, supply-chain chokepoints.
  • Disinformation & digital vulnerability: public/digital diplomacy is a two-way street — hostile narratives & cyber operations target India too.
  • Non-state actors as risk: diaspora extremism, rogue firms & NGOs can complicate ties despite the state's best efforts.
Critical view: the danger of the new diplomacy is style outrunning substance — branding & summitry without matching capacity, delivery & institutional depth risk turning "Vishwaguru" from strategy into slogan.

15. Way Forward

To make the changing sphere a durable strength, India must match ambition with institutional capacity, credibility & consistency.

  • Expand & specialise the foreign service: grow IFS strength & build domain expertise (trade, tech, climate, energy) — a diplomacy for specialised flows needs specialists.
  • Deliver, don't just declare: convert LoCs, FTAs, ISA & DPI pledges into completed, on-time projects — reliability is the real soft power.
  • Whole-of-nation diplomacy: integrate states (paradiplomacy), business, diaspora, think-tanks & academia into a coordinated external effort.
  • Invest in narrative & counter-disinformation: a credible, evidence-based public-diplomacy machine to shape perceptions & rebut hostile narratives.
  • Anchor autonomy in capability: Atmanirbhar defence & tech, critical-mineral security & energy diversification give multi-alignment real leverage.
  • Lead the Global South responsibly: keep offering global goods (vaccines, DPI, solar, disaster relief) to convert goodwill into structural influence & reform of global governance.
One-line frame: the future of Indian diplomacy is capacity + credibility + consistency — turning a wider toolkit into durable strategic weight, and "Vishwaguru" from aspiration into delivered fact.

17. Prelims PYQs

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

"Vaccine Maitri", seen in the news, refers to India's initiative to:

  • (d) Ban the export of vaccines to protect domestic supply

Answer: (b). Vaccine Maitri (2021) supplied Indian-made COVID vaccines to ~100 countries — the flagship of India's health/vaccine diplomacy & "pharmacy of the world" image.

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

The International Solar Alliance (ISA), an instrument of India's green diplomacy, was:

  • (a) Founded by India & the United States, headquartered in Washington
  • (b) Co-founded by India & France (2015), headquartered at Gurugram, India
  • (c) A UN body created under the Kyoto Protocol
  • (d) Founded by India & Germany, headquartered in Berlin

Answer: (b). The ISA was co-launched by India & France at COP21 (2015); its secretariat is at Gurugram — India's signature institution-building in climate/energy diplomacy.

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

18. Mains PYQs — model answers

Note on sourcing: the 2020 GS-II item below is a genuine dated UPSC Mains PYQ (diaspora diplomacy — a core instrument of the changing sphere). The remaining items are yearless model questions on the theme; no fabricated "past-year" tags are used.

UPSC Mains GS-II 2020

"Indian diaspora has a decisive role in the politics and economy of America and European countries." Comment with examples. 250 words

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: the ~35-mn overseas Indian community is a signature instrument of India's changing diplomacy — a bridge of political, economic & soft-power influence in the West.
  2. Political role: 26 Indian-origin MPs in the UK (2024), influential US Congress caucuses & senior leaders across governments shape India-friendly policy & advocacy.
  3. Economic role: record remittances (~$125 bn), Silicon Valley & finance leadership, investment & a high-skilled talent pipeline binding economies.
  4. Soft power: culture, cuisine, yoga & professional success build goodwill & a positive India brand, easing mobility & trade deals.
  5. Caveats: the diaspora is not monolithic — Khalistan extremism, hate crimes & host-state sensitivities mean influence is real but uneven.
  6. Conclusion: a decisive force multiplier to be engaged (PBD, OCI, mobility pacts), not owned — central to India's diaspora diplomacy.
UPSC GS-II — model question

Q. "The instruments of diplomacy have changed more than its objectives." In the light of India's foreign policy, discuss the changing sphere of diplomacy. 15 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: diplomacy's goal — security, prosperity & status — endures; its instruments (trade, vaccines, data, climate, culture) have transformed.
  2. Widened agenda: economic, energy, health, digital & climate diplomacy now sit alongside political-military statecraft.
  3. New instruments & actors: Vaccine Maitri, DPI export, ISA/CDRI/LiFE, FTAs & LoCs; MNCs, diaspora, cities (paradiplomacy) & social media as actors.
  4. New arenas: summit, plurilateral & public/digital diplomacy — India's G20 2023 & Voice of Global South as examples.
  5. Critical view: capacity limits, over-stretch & the risk of style over substance; weaponised interdependence.
  6. Conclusion: India must back a wider toolkit with capacity, credibility & consistency to convert new instruments into durable influence.
UPSC GS-II — model question

Q. Examine how India has used soft power and cultural diplomacy as instruments of foreign policy. What are their limits? 15 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: soft power (Nye) — influence through attraction — is a natural Indian strength rooted in civilisational depth.
  2. Instruments: International Day of Yoga (2015), Buddhist diplomacy, Bollywood & gastro-diplomacy, ICCR chairs & centres, scholarships (ITEC/ICCR).
  3. Strategic pay-off: goodwill eases market access, mobility, coalition-building & the "Vishwaguru/Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam" narrative.
  4. Diaspora & global goods: the diaspora, Vaccine Maitri & disaster relief amplify the soft-power brand.
  5. Limits: soft power cannot substitute for hard capability; domestic-image controversies & delivery gaps can undercut it.
  6. Conclusion: soft power is a force multiplier that must be paired with economic & military capability & consistent delivery.
UPSC GS-II — model question

Q. "Economic and digital diplomacy have become the new frontiers of India's global engagement." Critically analyse. 15 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: as flows of goods, capital & data drive power, economic & digital diplomacy have moved to the core of Indian statecraft.
  2. Economic diplomacy: the FTA sprint (UAE, Australia, EFTA, UK), commercial diplomacy for FDI/tech, LoCs & critical-mineral sourcing.
  3. Digital diplomacy: DPI/India-Stack as a development export, UPI linkages, cyber-norm & data-sovereignty engagement, iCET/AI coalitions.
  4. Strategic pay-off: both advance development and strategic autonomy & Global-South leadership.
  5. Critical view: weaponised interdependence, FTA-imbalance concerns, data-sovereignty vs free-flow tension, capacity limits.
  6. Conclusion: these frontiers can define India's rise if backed by delivery, negotiating depth & digital-security resilience.
UPSC GS-II — model question

Q. "India's transition from non-alignment to multi-alignment reflects the changing sphere of diplomacy." Discuss. 10 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: multi-alignment updates non-alignment for a multipolar age — same goal (autonomy & voice), new method (engage all poles).
  2. The shift: Non-Alignment (1947–91) → Strategic Autonomy (1991–2014) → Multi-Alignment/"Leading Power" (2014–).
  3. Evidence: Quad and BRICS/SCO; US defence deals and Russian oil/S-400; Global-South leadership & "Vishwamitra."
  4. Enabled by new diplomacy: economic, climate, vaccine & digital instruments let India offer value to every partner.
  5. Challenges: pressure from rival poles, credibility & capacity limits.
  6. Conclusion: multi-alignment, anchored in capability, is strategic autonomy fit for a changed sphere of diplomacy.
Mains GS-II — anticipated themes

Likely Mains angles: (i) the changing instruments vs enduring objectives of diplomacy; (ii) soft power & cultural diplomacy & their limits; (iii) economic + digital (DPI) diplomacy as new frontiers; (iv) vaccine/health & climate diplomacy as Global-South leadership; (v) non-alignment → multi-alignment; (vi) the diaspora & "first responder" HADR diplomacy; (vii) the risk of "style over substance" & the capacity gap in Indian diplomacy.

The shift & drivers

  • Diplomacy widened in agenda, actors, instruments & arenas — from borders to flows (goods, energy, data, germs, carbon, people).
  • Drivers: globalisation/interdependence, tech, multipolarity, transnational threats, non-state actors, democratisation (paradiplomacy).
  • Doctrine: Non-Alignment → Strategic Autonomy → Multi-Alignment / "Leading Power" / Vishwamitra.

Economic, energy & digital

  • FTAs as strategy: UAE CEPA 2022, Australia ECTA 2022, EFTA TEPA 2024, UK CETA 2025; LoCs (IDEAS), ITEC, critical minerals (KABIL/MSP).
  • Energy: ~85% crude imported; Russian discounted crude; ISA (2015, India+France, Gurugram), OSOWOG.
  • Digital: DPI / India Stack (Aadhaar+UPI+DigiLocker) as a global good; G20 Global DPI Repository; iCET; cyber norms.

Health, climate & soft power

  • Vaccine Maitri (2021) — ~100 countries; "pharmacy of the world"; WHO Traditional-Medicine Centre, Jamnagar (2022).
  • Climate: ISA, CDRI (2019), Mission LiFE (2022), Panchamrit / net-zero 2070, Loss & Damage Fund, Global Biofuels Alliance.
  • Soft power: Intl Day of Yoga (21 June, 2015), Buddhist & gastro-diplomacy, ICCR, Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam.

Diaspora, summit & first-responder

  • Diaspora ~35 mn, remittances ~$125 bn; 26 UK MPs (2024); PBD (9 Jan); genuine 2020 GS-II PYQ.

  • Watch-outs: capacity/over-stretch, rival-pole pressure, style-over-substance.
