Topic 17: India–Central Asia Relations

Central Asia — the five “Stans” (Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan) — is India's “extended neighbourhood”: a landlocked, resource-rich, strategically pivotal region with deep civilisational ties to India but no direct land access (Pakistan blocks the way). India's engagement rests on the Connect Central Asia Policy (2012), a growing summit architecture (the India–Central Asia Summit and Dialogue), and a fix for the connectivity problem via Chabahar port and the INSTC, bypassing Pakistan through Iran. This book-depth chapter covers geography, history, policy, connectivity, energy & uranium, security & counter-terrorism, the SCO platform, and the ever-present China (BRI) and Russia factor — India's classic “new Great Game” balancing act.

One-line arc: Resource-rich, strategically vital Central Asia is India's extended neighbourhood — but landlocked and blocked by Pakistan, India must reach it through Chabahar and the INSTC, deepen ties via the Connect Central Asia Policy and summit diplomacy, and quietly balance an entrenched China and a dominant Russia.

1. India's Stake in Central Asia

  • Energy: vast hydrocarbons (Kazakh & Turkmen oil/gas) and uranium (Kazakhstan is the world's top producer) for India's nuclear power.
  • Strategic depth: a stable Central Asia buffers India from instability spilling out of Afghanistan — terrorism, radicalisation, narcotics.
  • Markets & connectivity: gateway to Eurasia; a potential corridor to Russia and Europe via the INSTC.
  • Counter-terrorism: shared concern over extremism, drug-trafficking and the Afghanistan spillover.
  • Balancing China & Russia: preventing a Chinese monopoly in India's extended neighbourhood.
  • Civilisational goodwill: centuries of Silk-Road, Buddhist and Mughal-era links create a reservoir of trust.

2. The Five Stans — Geography

A doubly-landlocked heartland: no Stan touches an open ocean, and India shares no direct border — the central strategic problem.

Fig 17.1 — Central Asia is doubly-landlocked and India shares no direct border (Pakistan blocks the way). India's answer: a sea route to Chabahar (Iran) and the INSTC corridor northward — the connectivity spine of the whole relationship. (Schematic, not to scale.)
  • Kazakhstan: the giant — oil, gas and the world's top uranium output; India's largest trade partner in the region.
  • Uzbekistan: the demographic and cultural heart; double-landlocked; the most reform-minded, India-friendly hub.
  • Turkmenistan: huge natural-gas reserves; source of the TAPI pipeline; officially neutral.
  • Kyrgyzstan & Tajikistan: smaller, mountainous; Tajikistan hosts India's Ayni (Farkhor) air-base links — India's only military footprint abroad in the region.
  • The core problem: no ocean access and no land route through a hostile Pakistan — hence Chabahar and the INSTC.

3. Historical & Civilisational Links

  • Ancient Silk Route: Central Asia was the pivot of the trade artery linking India to Europe — ideas, goods, Buddhism flowed both ways.
  • Buddhism: spread from India into the region; sites at Termez (Uzbekistan) and across the steppe carry a shared Buddhist heritage.
  • Mughal roots: Babur came from Fergana Valley (Uzbekistan); deep cultural, linguistic (Persian/Turkic) and culinary imprint on North India.
  • Sufism: shared Sufi traditions — Khwaja Ahmed Yasawi, the Naqshbandi order — still resonate on both sides.
  • Soviet era: the five republics were part of the USSR; India's warm ties with Moscow gave it goodwill and technical links here before 1991.
  • 1991 opening: independence of the five republics opened direct diplomatic ties; India recognised all five immediately.

4. India's Policy: Connect Central Asia

India's structured outreach evolved from ad-hoc post-1991 contact into the formal Connect Central Asia Policy (2012), a broad-based approach spanning politics, security, economy and culture.

Fig 17.2 — The six pillars of the Connect Central Asia Policy (2012).
  • Comprehensive engagement: political, economic, connectivity, security, capacity-building and cultural tracks run in parallel.
  • Extended Neighbourhood: Central Asia is treated as part of India's strategic periphery despite the absence of a shared border.
  • Institutional upgrade: the India–Central Asia Dialogue (foreign-minister level, since 2019) and the 2022 leaders' Summit gave the policy a standing architecture.

5. India–Central Asia Summit & Dialogue

  • First India–Central Asia Summit (Jan 2022): virtual, hosted by PM Modi with all five Presidents — the highest-level India–CA engagement ever, institutionalised as a biennial summit.
  • Delhi Declaration: committed to regular summits, a permanent Secretariat in New Delhi, and cooperation on Afghanistan, connectivity and security.
  • India–Central Asia Dialogue: foreign-minister mechanism since 2019; created working groups on Chabahar, Afghanistan and trade.
  • NSA-level dialogue: National Security Advisers meet on regional security, counter-terrorism and Afghanistan.
  • Afghanistan focus: a recurring shared concern — stability, counter-narcotics, and preventing terror spillover after the 2021 Taliban takeover.

6. Connectivity: The Core Challenge

Landlocked Central Asia plus a hostile Pakistan blocking the direct land route makes connectivity the single biggest bottleneck in the relationship. India's answer is to reach the region through Iran.

7. Energy & Economic Cooperation

  • Uranium: Kazakhstan is the world's largest uranium producer and a major supplier to India's civil-nuclear programme; long-term supply contracts signed.
  • Oil & gas: Kazakhstan (oil), Turkmenistan (world's 4th-largest gas reserves) and Uzbekistan hold vast hydrocarbon wealth India seeks to tap via TAPI and equity stakes.
  • Trade is tiny: India–Central Asia trade is only around US$2 billion — far below potential, choked by the connectivity gap.
  • US$1 billion line of credit: announced for development projects across the five states in priority sectors.
  • Minerals: the region is rich in rare-earths, copper, gold and other critical minerals India needs for its clean-energy transition.
  • Pharma & IT: Indian generics, IT services and higher education are natural growth areas; Indian IT centres set up under ITEC.

8. Security & Defence

  • Ayni (Gissar) air base, Tajikistan: India refurbished this base — its only military footprint abroad in the region; a strategic asset near Afghanistan.
  • Farkhor: the nearby facility India operated during the Afghan war; symbol of India's forward reach.
  • Counter-terrorism: shared threat from radicalisation, ISIS-K and drug trafficking out of Afghanistan; NSA-level and SCO-RATS cooperation.
  • Defence training: Central Asian officers train in Indian military academies under ITEC; joint exercises (e.g. KAZIND with Kazakhstan, Dustlik with Uzbekistan).
  • Afghanistan hedge: Central Asia is India's platform to monitor and influence developments in Taliban-run Afghanistan.

9. SCO & Multilateral Engagement

  • SCO membership (2017): India became a full member of the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation, sharing the platform with all five Central Asian states plus China and Russia.
  • RATS: the SCO Regional Anti-Terrorist Structure gives India a formal counter-terror channel with the region.
  • SCO Presidency (2023): India chaired the SCO and hosted the summit, using it to push connectivity and counter-terror agendas.
  • Balancing forum: SCO lets India engage Central Asia without a China veto on the bilateral track, but also forces it to sit alongside Pakistan and China.
  • CICA & others: India also engages via the Conference on Interaction and Confidence-Building Measures in Asia and the India–CA Dialogue.

10. The New Great Game: China & Russia

India is a latecomer in a region where China and Russia are the dominant external players. India's strategy is to carve a distinct, welcome niche rather than compete head-on.

Fig 17.3 — India balancing China's economic weight and Russia's security primacy in Central Asia.
  • China dominates economically: BRI investment, pipelines and trade make China the top external partner — a debt and dependency concern for the Stans.
  • Russia leads security: via the CSTO, military bases and deep Soviet-era ties, though its weight is dented by the Ukraine war.
  • India's niche: a trusted, non-hegemonic partner offering capacity-building, education, health and connectivity — a welcome hedge against over-dependence.
  • Constraint: India cannot match Chinese money or Russian bases, so it competes on trust, soft power and democratic credentials, not scale.

12. Challenges

  • Connectivity gap: no direct land link; Pakistan blocks transit; Chabahar/INSTC still under-utilised — the binding constraint.
  • China's dominance: India's economic offer is dwarfed by Chinese BRI money and trade volumes.
  • Tiny trade: ~US$2 bn is far below potential and below China's tens of billions.
  • Afghanistan instability: the Taliban takeover, terror and narcotics threaten the whole region and India's routes.
  • US sanctions on Iran: complicate Chabahar and INSTC financing and operations.
  • Implementation deficit: many MoUs and summit pledges move slowly from paper to ground.

13. Way Forward

  • Operationalise Chabahar & INSTC: move from agreements to actual cargo volumes; integrate the Ashgabat corridor.
  • Deepen the summit architecture: hold biennial summits, staff the Secretariat, convert declarations into projects.
  • Leverage soft power: scale ITEC scholarships, IT centres, health and education — India's true comparative advantage.
  • Secure critical minerals & uranium: lock in long-term supply of uranium, rare-earths and copper.
  • Coordinate with like-minded partners: work with the EU, Japan and the US on connectivity to counter BRI dependence.
  • Stay the course on Afghanistan: use Central Asia as the platform for a stable, terror-free Afghan policy.

15. Prelims PYQ Practice

A note on sourcing: Central Asia is a relatively new UPSC theme with few standalone dated PYQs. The MCQs below are UPSC-pattern concept-practice items built strictly on the prescribed syllabus and current affairs, honestly labelled as practice, not passed off as dated past questions.

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

Q. The Ashgabat Agreement, sometimes seen in the news, is an agreement relating to which of the following?

  • (a) A ceasefire in the Nagorno-Karabakh conflict
  • (b) An international transport and transit corridor
  • (c) A regional nuclear-free-zone treaty
  • (d) A free-trade area among CIS states

Answer: (b). The Ashgabat Agreement establishes a transport corridor linking Iran, Oman, Turkmenistan, Uzbekistan and Kazakhstan; India joined in 2018 to connect Chabahar to Central Asia.

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

Q. Which of the following countries share a border with any Central Asian republic?
1. Russia   2. China   3. Afghanistan   4. India

  • (a) 1, 2 and 3 only
  • (b) 1 and 2 only
  • (c) 2, 3 and 4 only
  • (d) 1, 2, 3 and 4

Answer: (a). Russia, China and Afghanistan border Central Asian states; India does not share a border with the region — the core rationale for its connectivity push through Iran.

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

Q. The TAPI pipeline is designed to carry natural gas from which country to India?

  • (a) Kazakhstan
  • (b) Turkmenistan
  • (c) Uzbekistan
  • (d) Iran

Answer: (b). TAPI = Turkmenistan–Afghanistan–Pakistan–India; it would carry Turkmen gas to India but is stalled by Afghan/Pakistan security risk.

16. Mains PYQ Practice

A note on sourcing: Central Asia rarely appears as a standalone dated Mains PYQ; it features within broader “extended neighbourhood” and connectivity questions. Below is one authentic themed UPSC GS-II PYQ, followed by UPSC-pattern model questions (honestly labelled, not fabricated past papers), each with a full skeleton.

UPSC GS-II 2022

Q. “India's relationship with its extended neighbourhood is shaped as much by connectivity as by diplomacy.” Discuss with reference to Central Asia. 15 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Frame: Central Asia is India's “extended neighbourhood” — strategically vital (energy, security, SCO) yet physically unreachable by land due to Pakistan.
  2. Connectivity as the pivot: Chabahar, INSTC and the Ashgabat Agreement are the substance of the relationship; without them, diplomacy stays symbolic.
  3. Diplomacy: Connect Central Asia Policy (2012), India–CA Summit (2022), SCO membership give the political architecture.
  4. Balance: India competes with China's BRI money and Russia's security primacy by offering trust and capacity-building.
  5. Conclusion: operationalising connectivity is the precondition for diplomacy to translate into influence.
UPSC GS-II — model question

Q. Examine the significance of the Chabahar port and the INSTC in advancing India's strategic and economic interests in Central Asia. 15 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: Explain the Pakistan-bypass logic driving both projects.
  2. Chabahar: India-run terminal (2024 deal), gateway to Afghanistan and Central Asia, counter to China's Gwadar.
  3. INSTC: 7,200 km multimodal route, ~30–40% cost/time saving, links Mumbai to Russia via Iran and the Caspian.
  4. Strategic-economic payoff: access for uranium/gas trade, market reach, energy diversification.
  5. Constraints/conclusion: US sanctions on Iran, under-utilisation — way forward is faster operationalisation.
UPSC GS-II — model question

Q. “In the new Great Game in Central Asia, India is a late entrant with limited means but a distinct advantage.” Critically analyse. 15 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: The new Great Game pits China, Russia and India for influence in Central Asia.
  2. China & Russia: economic dominance (BRI) and security primacy (CSTO) respectively.
  3. India's advantage: trust, soft power, capacity-building, non-hegemonic image, democratic values.
  4. Limits: tiny trade, no land access, connectivity gap, late entry.
  5. Conclusion: leverage soft power plus like-minded partnerships to convert goodwill into presence.
UPSC GS-II — model question

Q. Discuss how developments in Afghanistan since 2021 have affected India's engagement with Central Asia. 10 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: The 2021 Taliban takeover reshaped India's regional security calculus.
  2. Risks: terror, narcotics and refugee spillover raised shared concerns.
  3. Response: Central Asia became a shared-concern platform (NSA dialogue, SCO-RATS); value of Chabahar and Ayni rose.
  4. Complications: TAPI and land routes disrupted.
  5. Conclusion: India uses the region to monitor and hedge on Afghanistan.
UPSC GS-II — model question

Q. Evaluate the outcomes of the first India–Central Asia Summit (2022) and the institutional architecture it created. 10 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: The 2022 virtual summit was the apex political milestone of Connect Central Asia.
  2. Outcomes: Delhi Declaration, biennial summit format, permanent Secretariat.
  3. Architecture: working groups on Chabahar/Afghanistan/trade, India–CA Dialogue and NSA mechanism.
  4. Assessment: strong institutional scaffolding but an implementation and delivery gap.
  5. Conclusion: convert the architecture into concrete projects to sustain momentum.

Anticipated angles: India's Central Asia policy constrained by geography not intent; connectivity bottleneck (no land access, hostile Pakistan) as the binding constraint; Chabahar/INSTC/Ashgabat as work-arounds; and China/Russia competition with the implementation deficit.

  • Core problem: no shared border + hostile Pakistan = connectivity is the binding constraint.

  • Security: Ayni/Farkhor base links in Tajikistan; KAZIND & Dustlik exercises; SCO-RATS counter-terror.
  • Multilateral: SCO full member (2017), chaired 2023; CICA.
  • Great Game: China = economic (BRI), Russia = security (CSTO); India = trust, soft power, non-hegemonic third choice.
