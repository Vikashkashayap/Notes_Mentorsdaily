Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames Flashpoints & the Trade Order

This is the most current-affairs-heavy IR chapter, tested where geopolitics and geo-economics fuse. The examiner wants you to see that today's conflicts and today's trade wars are two faces of one shift — the erosion of the post-1945 US-led liberal order. Sort your prep into three buckets:

  • Static/institutional (factual recall): the anatomy of each flashpoint (who, where, since when) and of the trade order — WTO (1995), its three functions, the Appellate Body paralysis (since Dec 2019), MC12 (2022 Geneva) & MC13 (2024 Abu Dhabi). Prelims lives here.
  • Conceptual (the shift): the vocabulary of the new order — multipolarity, "weaponised interdependence", de-globalisation vs slowbalisation, de-risking (not decoupling), friend-shoring/near-shoring, China+1, minilateralism. Know each term and one example.
  • Analytical (GS-II/GS-III): take a position with example + judgement — how does India protect strategic autonomy amid great-power contest? Is India a bridge/"Vishwamitra" or a fence-sitter? Weigh the Russia-oil dilemma, WTO reform stance (food security, e-commerce moratorium), and supply-chain opportunity (PLI, China+1).

Territorial note: India's sovereign extent — including Pakistan-Occupied Kashmir, Gilgit-Baltistan, Aksai Chin, the Shaksgam tract and Siachen — is integral to the Republic of India; any map of flashpoints or trade routes here treats these as Indian territory.

1. Why Flashpoints & Trade Matter Together

Conflict and commerce are no longer separate silos. In an age of "weaponised interdependence", trade routes, energy and technology have become instruments of war, and wars instantly reprice global supply chains. For India — a large importer of energy, a rising exporter and a strategic-autonomy power — the two must be read as one strategic environment.

  • One structural shift: both trends flow from the erosion of the post-1945 US-led liberal order into a contested, multipolar, more transactional world.
  • Weaponised interdependence: sanctions, chip export controls, SWIFT cut-offs, and chokepoint blockades (Suez, Hormuz, Bab-el-Mandeb) turn economic ties into leverage.
  • Direct India impact: a war in Europe spikes India's oil/fertiliser bill; a Red Sea blockade lengthens India–Europe shipping; a chip war shapes India's semiconductor mission.
  • Strategic-autonomy test: India must keep partners on all sides (Russia, US, Gulf, EU) while defending its own growth and energy needs — the core GS-II tension.
GS-II hook: Read every flashpoint through two questions — how does it reshape the global order, and how does it hit India's interests and autonomy?

3. Russia–Ukraine War

The war Russia launched on Ukraine in February 2022 is the largest inter-state conflict in Europe since 1945 and the sharpest test yet of India's strategic autonomy. It fractured the global economy along sanctions lines and forced India to defend a nuanced, interest-driven position.

  • What happened: full-scale Russian invasion (24 Feb 2022); annexation claims over four regions; grinding attritional war; NATO's largest expansion (Finland 2023, Sweden 2024) in response.
  • Economic weaponisation: sweeping Western sanctions, Russia's removal from SWIFT (major banks), the G7 oil price cap (~$60/barrel), and Black Sea grain disruption spiking food/fertiliser prices.
  • India's position: abstained on most UNGA/UNSC votes; called for "dialogue and diplomacy"; PM Modi's "this is not an era of war" (Samarkand SCO, 2022) — principled non-alignment, not endorsement.
  • The oil dilemma: India ramped up discounted Russian crude (from ~2% to a large share of imports) — defended as consumer-interest energy security, criticised by the West.
  • Defence dependence: ~50%+ of India's legacy platforms are Russian-origin — a real constraint on how far India can tilt.
GS-II hook: India's Ukraine stance is the textbook case of strategic autonomy in action — balancing Russia (energy, defence, UNSC), the West (tech, trade, capital) and Global South leadership.

4. West Asia & the Red Sea Crisis

West Asia re-erupted with the Israel–Hamas war from October 2023, which spilled into a wider regional crisis — Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping, Israel–Hezbollah and Israel–Iran exchanges. For India, the region is existential: energy, ~9 million diaspora, remittances and the IMEC corridor all run through it.

  • Israel–Gaza war (from 7 Oct 2023): Hamas attack → Israeli operation in Gaza; huge humanitarian toll; regional escalation risk.
  • Red Sea / Houthi crisis: Yemen's Houthis attacked commercial ships at Bab-el-Mandeb in solidarity with Gaza, forcing traffic to reroute around the Cape of Good Hope — longer transit, higher freight and insurance.
  • Israel–Iran escalation: direct strikes (2024–25) raised the spectre of a Hormuz disruption — a nightmare for India's energy imports.
  • India's exposure: ~9mn diaspora and top remittance source; a big chunk of crude and LNG; the IMEC (India–Middle East–Europe Economic Corridor, G20 2023) directly threatened by instability.
  • India's response: balanced — condemned terrorism & backed Israel's security while reaffirming a two-state solution and Palestinian humanitarian aid; naval deployment (Op Sankalp-style anti-piracy/escort) in the Arabian Sea/Gulf of Aden.
Prelims trap: IMEC (India–Middle East–Europe Economic Corridor) was announced at the G20 New Delhi Summit, 2023 — its viability depends on West Asian stability, which the Gaza war jeopardised.

5. Indo-Pacific & Taiwan

The Indo-Pacific is the century's central theatre of great-power competition, with the Taiwan Strait and South China Sea as its most dangerous fault lines. Here the conflict and trade dimensions fuse most tightly: Taiwan makes the world's advanced semiconductors.

  • US–China strategic competition: the defining rivalry — military, technological (chips, AI), and over the rules of the regional order.
  • Taiwan Strait: China's pressure on Taiwan risks a conflict that would sever the global semiconductor supply (Taiwan/TSMC makes ~90% of the most advanced chips).
  • South China Sea: China's expansive claims vs the 2016 arbitral award (which China rejects); freedom-of-navigation stakes for the world's busiest sea lanes.
  • India's stakes: Quad (India/US/Japan/Australia), a free-open-inclusive Indo-Pacific, SLOC security, and the "China+1" manufacturing opportunity as firms de-risk from China.
GS-II hook: The Indo-Pacific is where India's security frame (Quad, SAGAR) and its economic opportunity (China+1, chips, PLI) converge — a stable Indo-Pacific is both a strategic and an industrial interest.

6. Other Live Flashpoints

Beyond the four big theatres, several regional crises shape India's neighbourhood and Global-South diplomacy.

  • Sahel & Africa: a "coup belt" (Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, Guinea), jihadist insurgency and a scramble for critical minerals — testing multilateral peacekeeping and India's Africa outreach.
  • Sudan civil war (from 2023): army vs RSF; a major humanitarian crisis and Indian evacuation (Operation Kaveri).
  • Afghanistan: Taliban rule since 2021; terrorism, humanitarian and connectivity (Chabahar) implications for India.
  • Myanmar: post-2021 coup and civil conflict — disrupting India's Act East land bridge (Kaladan, Trilateral Highway) and border security.
One-line frame: These "secondary" flashpoints matter to India mainly through evacuations, connectivity and neighbourhood stability — the operational, not the great-power, dimension.

7. The WTO in Crisis

The multilateral trade order built around the World Trade Organization (1995) is in its deepest crisis. Its rule-making has stalled, its dispute-settlement crown jewel is paralysed, and members increasingly bypass it for bilateral and plurilateral deals. Understanding why the WTO is stuck is the analytical core of the trade half of this chapter.

  • Three functions — all strained: (i) a negotiating forum (Doha Round, 2001, effectively collapsed); (ii) a rule-book; (iii) dispute settlement.
  • Appellate Body paralysis: since December 2019 the US has blocked appointment of Appellate Body judges — the AB has no quorum, so appeals go "into the void", gutting binding dispute settlement.
  • MC12 (Geneva, 2022): a "Geneva Package" — a partial fisheries-subsidies agreement, a TRIPS vaccine waiver, and extension of the e-commerce customs-duty moratorium.
  • MC13 (Abu Dhabi, 2024): modest outcomes — moratorium extended again, but no breakthrough on AB reform, agriculture or a permanent food-security solution.
  • North–South divide: developed countries push new issues (e-commerce, investment facilitation); developing countries (led by India) insist on old mandates — food security, a permanent Peace Clause, and Special & Differential Treatment.

8. Protectionism & the New Trade Order

As the WTO froze, the trade order fragmented into a patchwork of regional deals, industrial policy and outright protectionism. The buzzword is not "globalisation" but "slowbalisation" — and geopolitics, not efficiency, increasingly drives where things are made.

  • Return of industrial policy: the US Inflation Reduction Act & CHIPS Act, the EU's Green Deal Industrial Plan, India's PLI — subsidies to reshore strategic manufacturing.
  • Geo-economic tools: tariffs (US–China tariff war), export controls (US chip curbs on China), sanctions, and CBAM (the EU's carbon border tax) reshaping trade flows.
  • FTA turn: with the multilateral track dead, states pursue bilateral/plurilateral FTAs — India–UAE CEPA (2022), India–Australia ECTA (2022), India–EFTA TEPA (2024), India–UK FTA (concluded 2025).
  • Slowbalisation, not de-globalisation: trade is not collapsing but reorganising along trusted, regional and political lines — efficiency yields to resilience and security.

9. De-risking, Friend-shoring & Supply Chains

COVID-19 and the Ukraine war exposed the fragility of hyper-concentrated, China-centric supply chains. The response is a new geo-economic vocabulary — and a genuine opportunity for India to become a trusted manufacturing hub.

  • De-risking, not decoupling: the West's stated aim (EU's von der Leyen framing) — reduce dangerous dependence on China in critical sectors without a full economic break.
  • Friend-shoring / near-shoring: relocating supply chains to trusted allies/partners (US "friend-shoring") or nearer home — trust over pure cost.
  • China+1: firms keep China but add a second base — India, Vietnam, Mexico — to diversify risk.
  • India's plays: PLI schemes (electronics, semiconductors, pharma APIs), the Semiconductor Mission, the Supply Chain Resilience Initiative (SCRI, with Japan & Australia), and IPEF (Indo-Pacific Economic Framework) Pillar-II supply-chain agreement.
  • Critical minerals: the race for lithium, cobalt, rare earths (China dominates processing) — India's KABIL, Critical Minerals Mission and the Minerals Security Partnership.
Prelims trap: SCRI = Supply Chain Resilience Initiative (India–Japan–Australia, 2021). IPEF (2022) has 4 pillars; India joined Trade (Pillar I) only as an observer but signed the Supply Chain (Pillar II), Clean Economy and Fair Economy pillars.

10. India's Response to Flashpoints

India's approach across every flashpoint is remarkably consistent: strategic autonomy, issue-based positioning, and a claim to speak for the Global South. It is best understood not as fence-sitting but as active, interest-driven multi-alignment.

  • Strategic autonomy / multi-alignment: partner with all poles (Russia, US, EU, Gulf, Japan) on merits; avoid binding blocs; keep decision-making sovereign.
  • Issue-based, not bloc-based: abstain on Ukraine votes yet call for diplomacy; back Israel's security yet a two-state solution — positions tailored to interests, not alliances.
  • "Vishwamitra" & Voice of Global South: India as a trusted friend and bridge between North and South (G20 2023 presidency; Voice of Global South Summits).
  • Operational diplomacy: evacuations (Ganga from Ukraine 2022, Kaveri from Sudan 2023), anti-piracy naval escorts in the Red Sea/Gulf of Aden, HADR.
  • Critique & limits: critics call it opportunism/fence-sitting; the Russia-oil stance strains Western ties; India must show its autonomy delivers global public goods, not just self-interest.
GS-II hook: Frame India as a swing power practising strategic autonomy — courted by all sides precisely because it is aligned with none.

11. India & the New Trade Order

On trade, India plays a dual game: defend the multilateral WTO where its development interests lie, while aggressively signing FTAs and building resilient supply chains to capture the China+1 moment.

  • At the WTO: champion of developing-country causes — a permanent solution for public food stockholding (beyond the Bali Peace Clause), Special & Differential Treatment, and caution on new-issue (e-commerce, investment) mandates.
  • The RCEP choice (2019): India walked out of RCEP fearing Chinese import flooding and inadequate safeguards — a signal it will not sign trade deals that hurt farmers/MSMEs.
  • The FTA sprint: UAE CEPA (2022), Australia ECTA (2022), EFTA TEPA (2024, with a $100bn investment pledge), UK FTA (2025); EU FTA under negotiation — deep, "trusted-partner" deals over mega-blocs.
  • Manufacturing push: PLI, Make in India, Semiconductor Mission, Gati Shakti — positioning India as a China+1 base.
  • Digital & data: guarding the e-commerce customs-duty moratorium debate and data-localisation as trade-policy levers.
One-line frame: India defends the multilateral floor (WTO, food security) while building a bilateral ceiling (FTAs, supply chains) — protecting the vulnerable while seizing the opportunity.

12. Energy & Food Security Fallout

Flashpoints hit ordinary Indians through two channels — the price of fuel and the price of food. Managing this fallout is where foreign policy meets kitchen-table economics, and it explains many of India's "controversial" positions.

  • Energy: the Ukraine war spiked crude/LNG; India diversified with discounted Russian oil (energy security for a price-sensitive economy) while accelerating renewables and the International Solar Alliance.
  • Food & fertiliser: the Black Sea disruption raised wheat and fertiliser prices; India briefly restricted wheat/rice exports to protect domestic supply — drawing WTO scrutiny.
  • Chokepoint risk: a Hormuz or Bab-el-Mandeb closure would be a direct energy and trade shock — hence India's naval presence and route diversification (IMEC, Chabahar/INSTC).
  • Strategic petroleum reserves & diversification: India spreads suppliers (Gulf, Russia, US, Africa) and builds reserves to cushion shocks.
GS-III hook: India's "energy security first" logic — buying discounted Russian crude — is a geo-economic decision defended on consumer-welfare grounds, showing how flashpoints translate into domestic policy choices.

13. Challenges

India's balancing act is elegant but fragile. The pressures are mounting on several fronts at once.

  • Balancing fatigue: sustaining ties with rival poles (Russia vs West; Israel vs Arab/Iran) gets harder as crises deepen and demand a choice.
  • Western pressure: secondary-sanctions risk and criticism over Russian oil and defence dependence.
  • China challenge: a two-front economic-and-security contest — border tension plus supply-chain dependence on China.
  • WTO paralysis: the loss of binding dispute settlement removes India's shield against arbitrary trade barriers (e.g., CBAM, deforestation rules).
  • Domestic vulnerability: energy import dependence (~85% crude), farm/MSME sensitivity to FTAs, and the resilience of supply chains still being built.
  • Reputation risk: the "fence-sitter" charge if autonomy is seen as opportunism rather than principle.
GS-II hook: The core challenge is converting strategic autonomy from a defensive hedge into a source of positive global influence.

14. Way Forward

The reform agenda spans diplomacy, trade and capacity — and India's leverage grows as it becomes a bigger market and a more credible bridge.

  • Lead WTO reform: push to restore the Appellate Body, secure a permanent food-security solution, and defend Special & Differential Treatment for developing nations.
  • Deepen strategic autonomy with delivery: keep multi-alignment but back it with global public goods — vaccines, HADR, digital public infrastructure, Global South leadership.
  • Build supply-chain resilience: scale PLI, semiconductors, critical-minerals partnerships (MSP, KABIL) and IPEF/SCRI to become a trusted China+1 hub.
  • Diversify energy: renewables, ISA, green hydrogen, nuclear and supplier spread to cut chokepoint vulnerability.
  • Corridor diplomacy: advance IMEC and INSTC/Chabahar to hedge trade routes against Red Sea/Hormuz risk.
  • Smart FTAs: conclude balanced deals (EU, later others) that protect farmers/MSMEs while opening markets.
One-line frame: India's goal is to be an order-shaper, not an order-taker — defending the multilateral floor while building resilient, diversified, trusted economic ties.

16. Prelims PYQs & Practice

Note on sourcing: the questions below are concept-practice MCQs built on the static facts of these flashpoints and the trade order (chokepoints, WTO structure, key terms, initiatives) in the standard UPSC pattern, plus an anticipated-themes block. Because this is a fast-moving current-affairs area, these are for concept drill and are not attributed to a specific year.

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

Q. The Bab-el-Mandeb strait, frequently in the news due to attacks on shipping, connects the:

  • (a) Persian Gulf and the Gulf of Oman
  • (b) Red Sea and the Gulf of Aden
  • (c) Mediterranean Sea and the Red Sea
  • (d) Arabian Sea and the Bay of Bengal

Answer: (b). Bab-el-Mandeb links the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden (between Yemen and Djibouti/Eritrea) — the Houthi-attack chokepoint. The Suez Canal links the Mediterranean and Red Sea; Hormuz links the Persian Gulf and Gulf of Oman.

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

Q. With reference to the WTO's Appellate Body, consider the following statements:
1. It is the final arbiter in the WTO's dispute settlement mechanism.
2. It has been non-functional since 2019 due to a lack of quorum.
3. It was formally abolished at MC12 in 2022.
Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Answer: (a). The Appellate Body is the final appellate tier of WTO dispute settlement and has lacked a quorum since December 2019 (US blocks appointments). It was not abolished — it exists but is paralysed — so statement 3 is wrong.

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

Q. The India–Middle East–Europe Economic Corridor (IMEC) was announced on the sidelines of which event?

  • (a) BRICS Summit, Johannesburg 2023
  • (b) G20 Summit, New Delhi 2023
  • (c) SCO Summit, Samarkand 2022
  • (d) Quad Summit, Tokyo 2022

Answer: (b). IMEC was launched via an MoU at the G20 New Delhi Summit (2023), linking India–Gulf–Europe by rail and shipping. Its viability depends on West Asian stability.

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

Q. "Friend-shoring" and "de-risking", terms often seen in the context of global supply chains, are best described as strategies to:

  • (a) Fully decouple the Western and Chinese economies
  • (b) Reduce over-dependence on a single country by shifting production to trusted partners
  • (c) Impose uniform global tariffs through the WTO
  • (d) Return all manufacturing to a firm's home country only

Answer: (b). Friend-shoring shifts supply chains to trusted allies/partners; de-risking reduces dangerous dependence (esp. on China) without full decoupling. Returning all production home is "re-shoring", a narrower idea.

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

Q. The Supply Chain Resilience Initiative (SCRI) is a trilateral arrangement involving India and which two other countries?

  • (a) United States and United Kingdom
  • (b) Japan and Australia
  • (c) France and Germany
  • (d) South Korea and Singapore

Answer: (b). SCRI (2021) is an India–Japan–Australia initiative to build resilient, diversified supply chains in the Indo-Pacific.

UPSC Prelims — concept practice

Q. With reference to the WTO "Peace Clause" (Bali, 2013), consider the following statements:
1. It provides interim protection to developing countries' public food-stockholding programmes from being challenged.
2. India has invoked it to shield its Minimum Support Price–based procurement.
Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

  • (a) 1 only
  • (b) 2 only
  • (c) Both 1 and 2
  • (d) Neither 1 nor 2

Answer: (c). The Bali Peace Clause (2013) is an interim shield preventing challenges to developing-country food-security stockholding beyond the subsidy cap; India seeks a permanent solution and has invoked the clause for its MSP-based procurement.

17. Mains PYQs & Model Answers

Note on sourcing: the first two questions are genuine, dated UPSC Mains questions reproduced verbatim; the remaining are model questions in the UPSC pattern with full answer skeletons. Model questions are deliberately yearless.

UPSC GS-II 2016

Q. "The broader aims and objectives of WTO are to manage and promote international trade in the era of globalization. But the Doha round of negotiations seem doomed due to differences between the developed and the developing countries." Discuss in the Indian perspective. 12.5 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: State WTO's aims (liberalise & rule-govern trade) and that the Doha "Development" Round (2001) has stalled on a North–South divide; frame from India's standpoint.
  2. Points of divergence: agriculture & farm subsidies (developed-country domestic support vs developing-country food security), NAMA, services, and new issues pushed by the North.
  3. India's position: defend food security (public stockholding, Peace Clause), Special & Differential Treatment, livelihood of subsistence farmers; resist premature new-issue mandates.
  4. Consequences of the stall: shift to bilateral/plurilateral FTAs, mega-regionals, and now the Appellate Body paralysis — weakening the rules-based system India relies on.
  5. Way forward: revive multilateralism with a development core; permanent food-security solution; restore dispute settlement; India as a bridge for the Global South.
  6. Conclusion: India seeks an equitable, development-friendly WTO, not its abandonment — balancing openness with the protection of its poor.
UPSC GS-II 2020

Q. "'Indian diaspora has a decisive role to play in the politics and economy of America and European Countries.' Comment with examples." — (reproduced as a genuine dated GS-II item; use it to anchor the Global-South/soft-power dimension of India's response to a fracturing order.) 15 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: Link the diaspora to India's leverage in a multipolar order — a soft-power asset amid great-power contest.
  2. Political role: influential Indian-origin leaders/legislators, community lobbying, bridge-building on India-related issues.
  3. Economic role: remittances (world No.1), investment, entrepreneurship and tech leadership (Silicon Valley).
  4. Strategic use: how India mobilises the diaspora for its foreign-policy goals in a contested order.
  5. Limits: divided loyalties debates, host-country politics, over-reliance risks.
  6. Conclusion: the diaspora amplifies India's autonomy and Global-South voice in the new order.
UPSC GS-II — model question

Q. "India's response to the Russia–Ukraine conflict is the clearest expression of its strategic autonomy." Critically examine. 15 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: Define strategic autonomy; state India's posture — abstentions + "not an era of war" + discounted Russian oil.
  2. Evidence of autonomy: balancing Russia (energy, defence, UNSC) with the West (tech, trade, capital) and Global-South leadership; issue-based, not bloc-based.
  3. The interest logic: energy security for a price-sensitive economy; ~50%+ Russian-origin defence platforms; UNSC-reform partner.
  4. Critical/counter-view: the "fence-sitter/opportunist" charge; Western pressure and secondary-sanctions risk; moral vs interest tension.
  5. Broader pattern: the same autonomy shapes India's West Asia and Indo-Pacific positions — a consistent doctrine, not ad-hoc.
  6. Conclusion: autonomy is real and interest-driven; its legitimacy depends on India delivering global public goods, not just self-interest.
UPSC GS-III — model question

Q. "The rules-based multilateral trading order is giving way to a fragmented, security-driven trade regime." Analyse the implications for India and suggest a strategy. 15 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: Frame the shift — WTO paralysis (Appellate Body since 2019, Doha dead) to protectionism, industrial policy and FTAs.
  2. Drivers: US–China rivalry, weaponised interdependence, COVID/Ukraine supply shocks, geopolitics over efficiency.
  3. Implications for India: loss of binding dispute settlement (exposure to CBAM etc.); but a China+1 manufacturing opportunity; FTA leverage.
  4. India's dual strategy: defend the multilateral floor (WTO reform, food security) + build a bilateral ceiling (UAE/Australia/EFTA/UK FTAs, PLI, SCRI, IPEF).
  5. Resilience agenda: critical-minerals partnerships, semiconductors, corridor diplomacy (IMEC, INSTC) to hedge chokepoints.
  6. Conclusion: India must be an order-shaper — protecting the vulnerable while seizing the resilience/diversification opportunity.
UPSC GS-II — model question

Q. Global chokepoints have become instruments of geopolitical leverage. Examine India's vulnerabilities and its diplomatic and strategic responses. 15 marks

Full answer skeleton
  1. Intro: Define chokepoints and "weaponised interdependence"; list the key ones (Hormuz, Bab-el-Mandeb, Malacca, Suez).
  2. India's vulnerabilities: ~85% crude-import dependence; Red Sea rerouting raising freight; SLOC and energy exposure.
  3. Diplomatic responses: supplier diversification, balanced West Asia policy, corridor diplomacy (IMEC, INSTC/Chabahar).
  4. Strategic responses: naval presence (anti-piracy escorts, IFC-IOR, SAGAR), Quad/IONS maritime-domain awareness.
  5. Economic responses: strategic petroleum reserves, renewables/ISA, and resilient supply chains.
  6. Conclusion: chokepoint security is now central to India's energy and trade strategy — requiring diplomacy, hardware and diversification together.
