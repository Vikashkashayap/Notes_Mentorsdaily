Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Tests the S&T Ecosystem

This is an institutions- & policy-heavy area that ties science to governance & the economy. Sort your prep into three question-types:

  • Definitional / static — "which ministry runs CSIR?", "what is GERD?", "what is a patent?". Recall of bodies, schemes & terms.
  • Statement-elimination — matching institutions to their domains, scheme details, IPR facts where one detail decides the answer.
  • Applied / current — a new policy or body in the news (ANRF, STIP, Global Innovation Index rank) linked to a fundamental — the strong GS-III innovation-economy bridge.

Highest-frequency themes: CSIR/DST/DBT/ICMR/ICAR & their ministries · PSA · STIP · ANRF & SERB · INSPIRE & awards · Startup India & AIM · IPR & patents · GERD & Global Innovation Index · scientific temper.

2. S&T Governance & the PSA

  • The Principal Scientific Adviser (PSA) to the Government of India advises the PM & Cabinet on science & technology and coordinates across agencies.
  • The PM-STIAC (Science, Technology & Innovation Advisory Council), chaired by the PSA, drives national science missions.
  • The Ministry of Science & Technology (with DST, DBT, DSIR/CSIR) & sectoral ministries jointly run India's science apparatus.
Prelims facts: the PSA advises the government on S&T & chairs PM-STIAC; the Ministry of Science & Technology houses DST, DBT & DSIR (CSIR). Governance is spread across many ministries — hence coordination is a recurring challenge.

3. STIP & S&T Policy Evolution

  • India's policy journey: Scientific Policy Resolution (1958) → Technology Policy Statement (1983) → S&T Policy (2003) → STI Policy (2013) → the draft Science, Technology & Innovation Policy (STIP).
  • STIP goals: make India a global S&T leader, double the number of researchers & GERD, promote open science, decentralised & inclusive research, self-reliance & a strong innovation culture.
  • Themes: research funding reform (ANRF), inclusivity & diversity, technology self-reliance, science communication & an "One Nation, One Subscription" for journals.
GS-III hook: STIP aims to reposition India from a technology adopter to a technology leader — through more funding, more researchers, open & inclusive science & self-reliance. Knowing the policy lineage (1958→2013→STIP) helps in Mains framing.

4. ANRF & Research Funding

  • The Anusandhan National Research Foundation (ANRF) — set up by the ANRF Act, 2023 — is the apex body to seed, grow & promote research across universities & institutions; it subsumed the earlier SERB.
  • ANRF emphasises private-sector funding (most of its corpus is to come from industry/philanthropy) to raise India's low R&D spend & back research in state/private universities.
  • SERB (Science & Engineering Research Board) earlier funded competitive research grants; ANRF now anchors this role.
Prelims facts: the ANRF (ANRF Act, 2023) is the apex research-funding body; it replaced/subsumed SERB; a key feature is leveraging private & philanthropic funding to boost R&D beyond government labs, especially in universities.

5. Key Schemes, Talent & Awards

  • INSPIRE (Innovation in Science Pursuit for Inspired Research) — scholarships & fellowships to attract youth to science.
  • Awards: the Rashtriya Vigyan Puraskar (RVP) now consolidates top science awards (which replaced the Shanti Swarup Bhatnagar-era scheme structure); other fellowships include Ramanujan & JC Bose.
  • Missions & programmes: National Supercomputing, Quantum & Semiconductor Missions (Topics 12–14), the National Mission on Interdisciplinary Cyber-Physical Systems (NM-ICPS), Deep Ocean Mission & National Geospatial Policy.
  • Talent & skilling: schemes for women in science (e.g. WISE/GATI), returning scientists (VAJRA) & research fellowships.
GS-III hook: India's schemes span the pipeline — INSPIRE (attracting youth) → fellowships (retaining talent) → missions (frontier tech) → awards (recognition). A common gap is translating research into products; hence emphasis on innovation & industry linkage.

6. Innovation & Start-up Ecosystem

  • Startup India & the Startup India Seed Fund support new ventures; India has one of the world's largest start-up & unicorn ecosystems.
  • Atal Innovation Mission (AIM) (under NITI Aayog) runs Atal Tinkering Labs in schools & Atal Incubation Centres to build a grassroots innovation culture.
  • Support structures: incubators & accelerators, technology business incubators (via DST/CSIR), and iDEX for defence (Topic 16).
Prelims facts: Atal Innovation Mission is under NITI Aayog (Atal Tinkering Labs in schools, Atal Incubation Centres); Startup India (with a Seed Fund) drives entrepreneurship. India ranks among the top start-up & unicorn ecosystems globally.

7. Intellectual Property Rights (IPR)

  • IPR protects creations of the mind: patents (inventions), copyright (creative works), trademarks (brands), designs & geographical indications (GI).
  • India's Patents Act, 1970 (amended to meet TRIPS) — Section 3(d) curbs "evergreening" of pharma patents (Novartis case) to protect access to affordable medicines.
  • The National IPR Policy, 2016 ("Creative India; Innovative India") promotes IP awareness, commercialisation & enforcement; the CGPDTM administers IP offices.
  • GI tags (e.g. Darjeeling tea, Banarasi saree) protect region-linked products — a frequent Prelims theme.
Prelims traps: patents = inventions; GI = region-linked goods; Section 3(d) of the Patents Act prevents patent evergreening (Novartis judgment); India is TRIPS-compliant; the National IPR Policy (2016) is administered via the CGPDTM/DPIIT.

8. R&D Spending & Metrics

  • GERD (Gross Expenditure on R&D) = a country's total R&D spend, usually shown as a % of GDP. India's GERD (≈0.6–0.7% of GDP) is low vs peers (2–4%) & is dominated by government (private share is small) — a core weakness STIP/ANRF target.
  • Global Innovation Index (GII) (WIPO) ranks innovation ecosystems; India has climbed steadily into the top ~40, leading among lower-middle-income economies.
  • India is a leader in scientific publications & PhDs by volume but lags in per-capita output, patents & research-to-commercialisation.
Prelims facts: GERD = total R&D spend (% of GDP); India's is low (~0.6–0.7%) & government-dominated; the Global Innovation Index is published by WIPO; raising private R&D & GERD is a stated STIP/ANRF goal.

9. Scientific Temper & Challenges

  • Scientific temper — a rational, questioning, evidence-based outlook — is a Fundamental Duty under Article 51A(h) of the Constitution (the term is associated with Nehru).
  • Challenges: low R&D spend, weak industry–academia linkage, brain drain, poor research-to-product translation, bureaucratic delays & the need for greater scientific literacy.
  • Way forward: boost GERD (esp. private), reform funding (ANRF), strengthen universities, ease of doing research, promote open science & innovation culture.
GS-III hook: developing "scientific temper" (Article 51A(h)) & a strong innovation ecosystem is the human-capital side of self-reliance. India's scale (talent, start-ups) is a strength; its low & skewed R&D funding & weak commercialisation are the gaps STIP/ANRF aim to close.

11. Prelims PYQs

Objective questions anchored to genuinely tested UPSC themes on India's S&T ecosystem. Each carries a worked rationale.

UPSC Prelims — Institutions

Q: The Council of Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) functions under which ministry?

  • (a) Ministry of Science and Technology
  • (b) Ministry of Health
  • (c) Ministry of Agriculture
  • (d) Ministry of Defence

Answer: (a) CSIR (with DST & DBT) is under the Ministry of Science and Technology; ICMR is under Health, ICAR under Agriculture, DRDO under Defence.

UPSC Prelims — Space & atomic energy

Q: ISRO (Department of Space) and the Department of Atomic Energy report to —

  • (a) Ministry of Science and Technology
  • (b) the Prime Minister's Office
  • (c) Ministry of Defence
  • (d) Ministry of Earth Sciences

Answer: (b) The Department of Space (ISRO) and Department of Atomic Energy (DAE) function directly under the PMO, reflecting their strategic importance.

UPSC Prelims — Research funding

Q: The Anusandhan National Research Foundation (ANRF) —

  • (a) replaced ISRO
  • (b) was set up under the ANRF Act, 2023 and subsumed SERB
  • (c) is a private company
  • (d) funds only defence research

Answer: (b) ANRF (ANRF Act, 2023) is the apex research-funding body; it subsumed SERB and emphasises leveraging private/philanthropic funding.

UPSC Prelims — Innovation mission

Q: The Atal Innovation Mission, which runs Atal Tinkering Labs, is under —

  • (a) NITI Aayog
  • (b) DRDO
  • (c) ISRO
  • (d) RBI

Answer: (a) The Atal Innovation Mission is under NITI Aayog; it runs Atal Tinkering Labs in schools and Atal Incubation Centres.

UPSC Prelims — Patents

Q: Section 3(d) of India's Patents Act is significant because it —

  • (a) bans all pharmaceutical patents
  • (b) prevents "evergreening" of patents for minor modifications
  • (c) grants lifetime patents
  • (d) deals only with trademarks

Answer: (b) Section 3(d) blocks evergreening (patents for trivial changes), upheld in the Novartis case — protecting access to affordable generic medicines.

UPSC Prelims — GI tag

Q: A Geographical Indication (GI) tag is granted to —

  • (a) any new invention
  • (b) products with a specific geographical origin and reputation
  • (c) company logos
  • (d) literary works

Answer: (b) A GI identifies goods originating from a place whose quality/reputation is due to that origin (e.g. Darjeeling tea); patents cover inventions, trademarks cover brands.

UPSC Prelims — R&D metric

Q: "GERD", used to compare countries' research effort, refers to —

  • (a) gross energy demand
  • (b) gross expenditure on R&D (often as % of GDP)
  • (c) a defence index
  • (d) a pollution measure

Answer: (b) GERD = Gross Expenditure on R&D; India's (~0.6–0.7% of GDP) is low and government-dominated — a weakness STIP/ANRF target.

UPSC Prelims — Constitution

Q: "To develop the scientific temper, humanism and the spirit of inquiry" is —

  • (a) a Fundamental Right
  • (b) a Directive Principle
  • (c) a Fundamental Duty under Article 51A
  • (d) a preambular ideal

Answer: (c) Developing scientific temper is a Fundamental Duty under Article 51A(h), added by the 42nd Amendment.

12. Mains PYQs + Model Answers

The S&T ecosystem anchors GS-III questions on innovation, funding & self-reliance. The frameworks below show how to deploy it analytically.

Mains GS-III 15 marks · 250 words

Q: India spends too little on R&D and translates too little research into products. Examine the causes and remedies.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: India's GERD (Section 8) is ~0.6–0.7% of GDP — low & government-dominated — and research-to-product translation is weak.
  2. Causes:
    • Low private R&D, weak industry–academia linkage, fragmented funding, poor IP commercialisation, brain drain & bureaucratic delays.
  3. Remedies: ANRF to leverage private funding, STIP reforms, stronger university research, incubators (AIM), IP support & ease of doing research.
  4. Way forward: raise GERD toward 2%, incentivise corporate R&D, mission-mode focus & global collaboration.
  5. Conclusion: boosting & diversifying R&D funding is central to self-reliance & a knowledge economy.
Mains GS-III 15 marks · 250 words

Q: Discuss the objectives of the Anusandhan National Research Foundation (ANRF) and how it seeks to transform India's research landscape.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: ANRF (Section 4), under the ANRF Act 2023, is the apex body to seed & grow research across India.
  2. Objectives:
    • Fund research in universities (not just national labs), leverage private/philanthropic money, promote interdisciplinary & mission research, build a research culture.
  3. Transformation: shifts funding beyond government, broadens the research base, subsumes SERB & aligns with STIP goals.
  4. Challenges: mobilising private funds, autonomy, avoiding bureaucratisation, reaching state universities.
  5. Conclusion: if well-implemented, ANRF can decisively raise India's R&D depth & inclusivity.
Mains GS-III 10 marks · 150 words

Q: How does a strong intellectual property regime balance innovation with public interest? Illustrate with India's approach.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: IPR (Section 7) rewards innovation but must not block access — a balance India tries to strike.
  2. Innovation side: patents/trademarks/GI incentivise R&D, investment & branding; National IPR Policy (2016) promotes commercialisation.
  3. Public-interest side: Section 3(d) curbs evergreening; compulsory licensing & TRIPS flexibilities protect access to medicines.
  4. Conclusion: India's calibrated IPR regime seeks both innovation incentives & affordable access.
Mains GS-III / GS-II 15 marks · 250 words

Q: "Developing scientific temper is essential for national development." Discuss in the context of India's Fundamental Duties.

Model Answer
  1. Introduction: Scientific temper (Section 9) — a rational, evidence-based outlook — is a Fundamental Duty under Article 51A(h).
  2. Why it matters:
    • Drives innovation & problem-solving, counters superstition & misinformation, improves health, governance & economic productivity.
  3. Enablers: quality education, science communication, INSPIRE & grassroots innovation (Atal Tinkering Labs).
  4. Challenges: pseudoscience, weak scientific literacy, misinformation.
  5. Conclusion: a scientifically minded citizenry underpins a modern, self-reliant India.
