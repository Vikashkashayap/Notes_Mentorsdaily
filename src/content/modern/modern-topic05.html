Why this topic matters for UPSC

The eighty-year span from Regulating Act 1773 to Charter Act 1853 built the institutional skeleton that would carry the British Raj into the twentieth century. Even today, the structure of the Indian state — the Collector, the Police, the Judiciary, the Civil Service, the IPC, the CrPC, the CPC, the District Court — is largely inherited from this period. UPSC tests this on four axes:

  • Prelims (factual): Year-by-year provisions of Regulating, Pitt's India, Charter Acts 1793/1813/1833/1853; first Governor-General of Bengal vs first Governor-General of India vs first Governor-General & Viceroy; Cornwallis Code, IPC drafting, ICS competitive exam.
  • Mains GS-I: "Trace the evolution of the office of the Governor-General"; "Discuss the constitutional development of British India till 1858"; "Examine the contribution of Cornwallis and Bentinck to administrative reform".
  • Polity bridge: Almost every chapter of Polity (Indian Constitution & Governance) begins with a survey of these very Acts — this chapter is the historical anchor of constitutional development that Laxmikanth treats in the opening chapter.
  • Cause-and-effect: The drift from a trading company to a sovereign administrative state created the centralised, alien bureaucracy that became the immediate target of the 1857 revolt (Topic 06) and the long-term grievance of the national movement.

1. Three Layers of British Power 1773–1857

Throughout the 1773–1857 period, British rule in India operated through three interlocking layers — each created or modified by a specific parliamentary statute. The history of Indian administration in this era is the history of how power flowed (and was disputed) between these three layers.

1.1 The Dual Government in London

Pitt's India Act 1784 created the famous "double government" in London (distinct from Clive's "double government" in Bengal of 1765–72). After 1784:

  • The Court of Directors (24 men elected by the EIC's Court of Proprietors) handled commercial matters and patronage.
  • The Board of Control (6 Crown ministers, headed by a President — effectively the Secretary of State for India before 1858) handled political, military, and revenue matters.
  • The Board could draft despatches; the Directors signed and dispatched them — an awkward arrangement that nevertheless lasted until 1858.

1.2 Why This Structure Mattered

  • It allowed Parliament to assert sovereignty over India without taking direct administrative responsibility.
  • It blurred accountability — when things went wrong (Bengal famine 1770, Vellore mutiny 1806, Afghan disaster 1842), responsibility was diffused.
  • It became unsustainable after 1857; the GoI Act 1858 (Topic 07) abolished both the Court of Directors and the Board of Control and replaced them with a single Secretary of State for India in Council.

2. The Regulating Act — 10 June / Royal Assent 21 June 1773

Passed by Lord North's ministry in the wake of the Bengal famine of 1770 (one-third of Bengal's population dead) and the EIC's £1.4 million bankruptcy — which forced the Company to ask the Crown for a £1 million loan. Parliament used the loan as leverage to impose its first statute on Indian administration.

2.1 Key Provisions

  • Governor-General of Bengal: The Governor of Bengal was elevated to Governor-General of Bengal, with limited supervisory authority over Madras and Bombay (only in matters of war and peace). Warren Hastings became the first Governor-General of Bengal (effective 20 October 1773; took oath 28 October 1774 under the Act).
  • Executive Council of Four: Four members named in the Act — Lieut.-Gen. John Clavering, Colonel George Monson, Richard Barwell, and Philip Francis. Decisions by majority; the GG had a casting vote only in case of tie. The three "Council members against Hastings" (Clavering, Monson, Francis) blocked nearly every initiative for the first three years.
  • Supreme Court at Calcutta: Established by the Act and constituted under a Royal Charter dated 26 March 1774, with Sir Elijah Impey as Chief Justice. Jurisdiction: British subjects in Calcutta and Indians employed by them; applied English common law and statutes.
  • Court of Directors reform: Tenure of Directors extended to four years, with one-fourth retiring annually.
  • Disclosure to Crown: Court of Directors required to lay all civil, military and revenue despatches before the British government.
  • Ban on private trade: Company servants prohibited from private trade and from accepting "presents" from Indians (loosely enforced).

2.2 Defects

  • Governor-General had only nominal control over Madras and Bombay — both Presidencies frequently went to war without his sanction (e.g. Bombay's First Anglo-Maratha War 1775).
  • Governor-General was paralysed by the Council majority.
  • Supreme Court's jurisdiction overlapped with the Company's Adalat system — produced the Patna case (1777–79) and the Cossijura case (1779–80) conflicts; Justice Impey himself was impeached.
  • The Board of Control was not yet in existence — the Crown lacked a permanent supervisory body.
Significance: The Regulating Act was the first step by the British Parliament to control the affairs of the EIC in India — the first statutory recognition that the Company was a political body, not merely a commercial one. It also recognised, for the first time, parliamentary right to legislate for India. Every subsequent Act in this chapter is a refinement of this principle.

3. Amending Act 1781 & Pitt's India Act — 13 August 1784

3.1 Amending (Declaratory) Act 1781

Passed to settle the Supreme Court vs Adalat jurisdictional disputes:

  • Exempted the Governor-General-in-Council from the Supreme Court's jurisdiction for acts done in official capacity.
  • Exempted Company servants from the Supreme Court for acts done in revenue matters.
  • Excluded revenue cases and matters relating to land from the Supreme Court's purview.
  • Made Supreme Court's law applicable only to British subjects in Calcutta — Hindus and Muslims to be governed by personal laws (Hindu and Muslim law).
  • Appeals from the Sadar Diwani Adalat to lie to the Governor-General-in-Council, not the Supreme Court.

3.2 Pitt's India Act 1784 — The Key Statute

William Pitt (the Younger) became Prime Minister in December 1783 after the rejection of Fox's India Bill 1783 (which had proposed to vest the Company in seven commissioners). Pitt's milder version became law on 13 August 1784 and established the basic structure that would govern Anglo-Indian relations for 74 years.

Key Provisions

  • Board of Control: Established a Board of six members — the Chancellor of the Exchequer, a Secretary of State, and four Privy Councillors nominated by the Crown. The Board could "superintend, direct, and control all acts, operations and concerns" relating to the civil, military, and revenue affairs of the EIC.
  • Court of Directors: Retained, but its decisions on political matters were now subject to the Board's approval; the Board could draft despatches; the Directors signed them.
  • Secret Committee: A 3-member secret committee of the Directors could transmit Board's orders directly to India, bypassing the full Court of Directors — for matters of war, peace, and diplomacy.
  • Governor-General's Council reduced to three members (from four); the GG's casting vote was confirmed — making him effectively master of the Council.
  • Subordination of Madras and Bombay: Explicitly subordinated to the Governor-General of Bengal in matters of war, peace, and diplomacy.
  • "Territorial possessions": The Act used the phrase "British possessions in India" for the first time — an implicit assertion of Crown sovereignty.

3.3 Acts 1786, 1793, 1797

  • Act of 1786: Cornwallis insisted, before accepting the GG appointment, on the power to override the Council in special cases of public safety — granted. Cornwallis also held the post of Commander-in-Chief.
  • Act of 1793 (Charter Act): See §4.
  • Act of 1797: Minor amendments tightening Board's supervision.
Famous verdict: "Pitt's India Act made India the responsibility of the British Cabinet; the Regulating Act had only made it the responsibility of the British Parliament." After 1784, every major policy decision in India required the assent of a Crown minister — the President of the Board of Control — even though the EIC continued to be the formal sovereign.

4. The Charter Acts — 1793, 1813, 1833, 1853

The EIC's commercial charter required renewal every twenty years; each renewal became an occasion for Parliament to legislate fresh administrative reforms. These four Charter Acts are the spine of UPSC's "constitutional development of India" question.

4.1 Charter Act 1793 (Cornwallis's Charter)

  • EIC's commercial monopoly renewed for 20 years.
  • Board of Control members' salaries to be paid from Indian revenue (a controversial precedent — India to pay for its own subjection).
  • Senior Company servants prohibited from leaving India without permission.
  • Governor-General and Governors could now over-ride their Councils in matters of importance.
  • Cornwallis's reforms (Permanent Settlement, separation of revenue and judiciary, Cornwallis Code) given statutory backing.

4.2 Charter Act 1813 (End of Trade Monopoly)

  • End of EIC's commercial monopoly (except for trade with China and the tea trade) — trade with India thrown open to all British subjects. Industrial Revolution lobby in England (Lancashire, Manchester) had demanded markets.
  • Christian missionaries allowed to enter and preach in India for the first time (against Company's earlier policy of religious neutrality).
  • One lakh of rupees annually to be spent on "the revival and improvement of literature, and the encouragement of the learned natives of India, and for the introduction and promotion of a knowledge of the sciences" — the first state grant for education in India.
  • British Crown's sovereignty over the Company's territories in India explicitly asserted.
  • Separate financial accounts to be kept for the EIC's commercial and territorial branches.

4.3 Charter Act 1833 (Centralisation & Indianisation)

The most important Charter Act before 1857 — sometimes called the "Government of India Act 1833".

  • Governor-General of India: The Governor-General of Bengal became Governor-General of India — the first central authority over the whole of British India. William Bentinck became the first Governor-General of India (effective 1833; he had been GG of Bengal since 1828).
  • Legislative power centralised: All legislative powers vested in the Governor-General-in-Council; Madras and Bombay lost their legislative powers (regained partially in 1861).
  • Law Member: A fourth member added to the GG's Council — the Law Member — for legislative business only (no vote in executive matters). Thomas Babington Macaulay became the first Law Member (1834–38); his most famous work was the draft Indian Penal Code (1837).
  • First Indian Law Commission: Constituted under Macaulay as Chairman in 1834; produced the IPC draft, the Lex Loci Report, and laid the foundations for codification.
  • EIC ceased to be a commercial body — deprived of all commercial functions (China trade and tea monopoly also ended). Henceforth it functioned purely as an administrative trust on behalf of the Crown.
  • Anti-discrimination clause: "No native of India... shall by reason only of his religion, place of birth, descent, colour, or any of them be disabled from holding any place, office or employment under the Company." (Section 87) — the famous Indianisation pledge, though largely unimplemented in practice till 1853.
  • Provision for the creation of a new Presidency at Agra (never executed) and for the appointment of a Governor for Bengal distinct from the Governor-General (executed only in 1854 under the North-Western Provinces).

4.4 Charter Act 1853 (Open Competitive ICS & Legislative Council)

The last of the Charter Acts — passed against a backdrop of Crimean War preparations and growing parliamentary impatience with the EIC.

  • EIC's charter renewed indefinitely (no 20-year limit) — but Parliament reserved the right to terminate it at any time. Within four years (1857), Parliament exercised that right.
  • Open competition for the Indian Civil Service: The patronage of the Court of Directors over the Covenanted Civil Service was abolished. Recruitment to the ICS henceforth by open competitive examination — opened to all British subjects, including Indians (in theory). The Macaulay Committee on the Indian Civil Service (Report 1854) designed the examination — first held in London in 1855. Satyendranath Tagore became the first Indian to qualify (1863).
  • Separation of legislative and executive functions: The first Legislative Council of India was constituted — the Governor-General-in-Council was expanded to 12 members for legislative business: GG + Commander-in-Chief + 4 executive councillors + 6 legislative members (Chief Justice and Puisne Judge of Supreme Court + 4 reps from Bengal, Madras, Bombay, North-Western Provinces). This is the constitutional ancestor of today's Indian Parliament.
  • Court of Directors reduced from 24 to 18 (12 elected by Proprietors + 6 nominated by Crown).
  • Local self-government cautiously hinted at (though not implemented till Ripon's resolution of 1882).
UPSC anchor — "Firsts" of the Charter Acts:
  • 1773: First parliamentary statute on Indian affairs; First GG of Bengal (Warren Hastings); First Supreme Court (Calcutta).
  • 1784: First Board of Control; First clear "double government" in London.
  • 1813: First end of EIC monopoly; First state grant for Indian education; First entry of missionaries.
  • 1833: First GG of India (Bentinck); First Law Member (Macaulay); First Law Commission; First anti-discrimination clause; End of EIC as commercial body.
  • 1853: First open competitive ICS; First separate Legislative Council; First constitutional bridge to representative government.

5. The Governor-General & the Executive Council

The office of the Governor-General was the apex of the Indian administration. Its evolution — from "GG of Bengal" (1773) to "GG of India" (1833) to "GG & Viceroy" (1858) — mirrors the journey of British India from a Bengal-centric outpost to a fully-fledged subcontinental state.

5.2 The Executive Council — Composition

  • 1773–84: GG + 4 councillors (named in Regulating Act); majority binding; GG had only casting vote.
  • 1784–1833: GG + 3 councillors; GG could override (after 1786) in cases of public safety.
  • 1833–53: GG + 3 ordinary councillors + 1 Law Member (legislative only, no vote in executive).
  • 1853–58: GG + 3 ordinary councillors + 1 Law Member (now a full member with vote, by Indian Councils Act 1861 onwards — but already in practice); plus the expanded 12-member Legislative Council.
  • The Commander-in-Chief of India was sometimes an additional member (e.g. under Cornwallis).

5.3 Powers and Functions

  • Executive: Highest civil and military authority; appointed and dismissed all officers; conducted diplomacy with princely states; declared war (after 1784, with Board's prior approval).
  • Legislative (after 1833): Enacted Regulations and Acts; from 1853 onwards through the separate Legislative Council.
  • Judicial: Highest court of appeal in Company territory (the Sadar Diwani Adalat appeals reached the GG-in-Council).
  • Revenue: Approved Boards of Revenue settlements; ultimate financial authority.

6. Central Secretariat & Government Departments

Below the Governor-General-in-Council, the daily work of administration was carried out by a Secretariat at Calcutta (and later, in the summer, at Simla — from 1864). The Secretariat evolved from a handful of clerks under Warren Hastings to a multi-departmental bureaucracy by 1857.

6.1 Departments by 1857

  • Home Department (1843): Internal administration, police, prisons, ecclesiastical affairs.
  • Foreign Department (1843): Relations with princely states and foreign powers; ran the Residencies. Earlier known as the "Secret and Political Department".
  • Military Department: Army administration; supplies; cantonments.
  • Finance Department: Revenue, expenditure, accounts; from 1859, separate Financial Member (James Wilson).
  • Public Works Department (PWD, 1854): Roads, bridges, canals, railways, telegraph — separated from the Military Department under Dalhousie.
  • Legislative Department (1853): Drafted Bills for the Legislative Council.

6.2 The Secretary

Each Department was headed by a Secretary (later Chief Secretary in Provinces) — a senior covenanted civilian. Below were Deputy Secretaries, Under-Secretaries, and clerical establishment. The Secretary was the channel between the Governor-General-in-Council and the field administration. The pattern survives in today's GoI Secretariat — Cabinet Secretary, Secretary, Additional Secretary, Joint Secretary, Director, Deputy Secretary, Under Secretary, Section Officer.

Continuity to today: The structure of the Secretariat — departments headed by Secretaries, files moving up through Deputy and Under Secretaries, decisions taken by the political executive (Cabinet) on the Secretary's note — is essentially Cornwallis-Bentinck-Dalhousie's invention. The 1858 transition merely relabelled the centre (Crown for Company); the file system is unchanged.

7. Provincial Administration — The Presidencies

British India in this period was organised into three (later four) "Presidencies" — the original trading factory settlements that had grown into territorial administrations.

7.2 Hierarchy in a Presidency

  • Governor (or Lieutenant-Governor): Madras and Bombay had Governors appointed by Crown on advice of Court of Directors. Bengal was under the Governor-General directly until 1854 (then Lt-Governor). NWP had a Lieutenant-Governor (1836).
  • Council: Madras and Bombay Governors had Councils of three (Governor + 2 members, later 3); the Council had executive and (after 1861) legislative functions. Lieutenant-Governorships had no Council until 1861.
  • Boards of Revenue: Each Presidency had a Board of Revenue (Bengal 1786, Madras 1786, Bombay 1818) to supervise land revenue collection.
  • Sadar Adalats: Each Presidency had its Sadar Diwani Adalat (highest civil court) and Sadar Nizamat / Faujdari Adalat (highest criminal court) for the Company's territory — below the Supreme Courts at the Presidency towns.
  • Commissioners of Divisions: Each Presidency was sub-divided into Divisions (4–6 districts) under a Commissioner (revenue + general supervision; first introduced in Bengal 1829).

7.3 Subordinate Relationship with the Centre

  • Madras and Bombay nominally subordinate to the GG since 1773 (Regulating Act) — formally so since 1784 (Pitt's Act).
  • In legislative matters, fully centralised under the GG from 1833 (Charter Act) until 1861 (when Indian Councils Act restored limited provincial legislative powers).
  • In financial matters, all revenues pooled at the centre — provincial budgets were not introduced till 1870 (Mayo's resolution).

8. District Administration & the Collector

The district was — and remains — the basic unit of Indian administration. The British shape of the district was largely fixed in Bengal between 1772 and 1793 by Warren Hastings and Cornwallis, and then extended (with regional adaptations) to the rest of British India.

8.1 Warren Hastings's Plan of 1772

  • The "Plan of 1772" abolished the Dual Government (1765–72): Company assumed direct revenue collection (the diwani) from Indian intermediaries.
  • European Collectors were appointed in each district to collect land revenue — the first time a British civilian sat in an Indian district as the supervising authority.
  • The Provincial Councils at Calcutta, Patna, Murshidabad and Dacca supervised the Collectors. (The Provincial Councils were abolished by Cornwallis in 1786.)

8.2 Cornwallis — Separation of Functions (1787–93)

  • Cornwallis abolished the Provincial Councils (1786–87) and concentrated authority in the District Collector.
  • By 1787 the Collector was also District Judge and Magistrate — three functions in one office.
  • In 1793 Cornwallis separated the revenue and judicial functions: Collector retained revenue duties; a separate District Judge (Diwani Adalat) was created.
  • The Collector also held magisterial (police) powers — the office of "District Magistrate" emerged.

8.3 Bentinck's Reversal (1829–31)

  • Bentinck (GG 1828–35) found Cornwallis's full separation unwieldy and reunited the offices of Collector and District Magistrate — the famous "Collector-Magistrate" of district administration.
  • He introduced the Commissioners of Divisions (1829) — one Commissioner above 4–6 District Collectors, with combined revenue and general supervision.
  • This Bentinck pattern — District Collector-cum-Magistrate, Divisional Commissioner above, Board of Revenue at the top — is the structure that survives in most Indian states today.

8.4 Subordinate Indian Officials

  • Deputy Collectors / Tahsildars: Indian officials in charge of taluks/tehsils — first regularly appointed in Madras 1817 (Thomas Munro) and extended elsewhere.
  • Munsifs and Sadar Amins: Indian judicial officers at the sub-divisional and district level — introduced by Cornwallis (Munsifs 1793), expanded by Bentinck.
  • Patwaris and Kanungos: Village-level revenue record-keepers — pre-British offices retained by the Company.
  • Daroga, Thanedar: Indian police officers at the thana level — see §13.
Modern continuity: Even after 1947, the District Collector / District Magistrate remains the pivot of Indian administration — revenue head, magisterial authority, returning officer for elections, disaster manager, and head of district-level Centre–State coordination. The office's tri-functional design (revenue + magistracy + general administration) is a direct inheritance from Bentinck's 1831 reform — itself a refinement of Cornwallis's 1787 model.

9. The Civil Service — Cornwallis to Macaulay (1786–1854)

The British Indian civil service began as a body of European Company clerks — the Covenanted Civil Service, so named because each entrant signed a "covenant" with the Company.

9.1 Pre-Cornwallis Period — Corruption Era

  • Company servants in Bengal (1757–86) drew small official salaries but enormous private fortunes — through private trade, "presents", and dastak abuse.
  • Clive's fortune (£234,000 in 1757), Warren Hastings's wealth, the Robber-Baron era documented by Burke at Hastings's impeachment trial (1788–95).

9.2 Cornwallis's Reforms 1786–93

  • Ban on private trade by civil servants; ban on accepting "presents" (1786).
  • Adequate salaries introduced to remove temptation — "make them rich enough that they need not be dishonest, and pay them more if they continue to be honest" (Cornwallis's reasoning).
  • All higher posts (over £500 per annum) reserved for Europeans — the so-called "Cornwallis colour bar" that excluded Indians from senior civil employment for the next 60 years.
  • Promotion strictly by seniority — merit was the official rule, but in practice Directors' patronage and political connection dominated.

9.3 Wellesley & Fort William College 1800

  • Wellesley founded the Fort William College at Calcutta on 10 July 1800 to train newly arrived civilians in Indian languages, law, and culture.
  • Faculty included William Carey (Bengali, Sanskrit) and other Indologists.
  • Disliked by the Court of Directors as too expensive; downgraded after 1806; replaced by Haileybury College in England (1806) for two-year pre-departure training.
  • East India College, Haileybury (1806–58): Trained nominated Company civilians in classics, Indian languages, political economy (Malthus was a professor 1805–34), Sanskrit, Persian, Hindustani.

9.4 Charter Act 1833 — The Indianisation Pledge

  • Section 87: No native of India to be disabled by religion or race from holding any office under the Company.
  • Honoured in the breach — no Indian was actually admitted to the Covenanted Civil Service before 1863 (Satyendranath Tagore).

9.5 Charter Act 1853 & Open Competition

  • Directors' patronage abolished; recruitment to the Covenanted Civil Service henceforth by open competitive examination.
  • Macaulay Committee on the Indian Civil Service — Report 21 November 1854 — designed the examination: open to British subjects between 18 and 23 years (later raised, then lowered repeatedly — a perennial Indian grievance).
  • Examination held in London only — effectively excluding most Indians.
  • Subjects: classics, mathematics, English literature, Sanskrit, Arabic, modern languages — a curriculum biased towards Oxbridge graduates.
  • First examination: July 1855; first batch of competitive ICS officers reached India in 1856.
  • Satyendranath Tagore (elder brother of Rabindranath) became the first Indian to qualify, in June 1863.
  • Renamed "Indian Civil Service" after 1858 (formally from the Government of India Act 1858); the term "Covenanted Civil Service" passed out of use by the 1870s.

9.6 The Uncovenanted Service

  • Below the Covenanted Service lay the Uncovenanted Civil Service — largely Indian (and Eurasian) clerks, deputy collectors, munsifs, tahsildars.
  • Recruited locally; paid much less; no pension.
  • The vast bulk of district and sub-district administration was carried out by these Indian uncovenanted officers under the supervision of a small number of covenanted Europeans — the "thin white line" of British India.
Critique — the "steel frame" was thin: By 1857 the Covenanted ICS in India numbered fewer than 1,000 officers governing 150 million Indians. The Cornwallis colour bar and the London-only examination ensured that the Service remained almost wholly European till well into the 20th century — this exclusion was a central grievance of the moderate nationalist phase (Topic 13). Dadabhai Naoroji's first cause in England was the demand to hold ICS examinations simultaneously in India and London.

10. The Cornwallis Code — 1793

The Cornwallis Code of 1793 was a comprehensive body of 48 Regulations issued by the Bengal Government on 1 May 1793, setting out the basic structure of administration, justice, police, and revenue in Bengal, Bihar and Orissa. It was the first systematic codification of executive law in British India.

10.1 Core Principles

  • Separation of revenue and judicial functions: Collector to collect revenue only; a separate Diwani Adalat Judge for civil cases.
  • Permanent Settlement: Zamindars made permanent landlords on payment of a fixed annual revenue (set 22 March 1793 — the day of the Permanent Settlement Regulation).
  • Rule of Law: Government servants made liable in the Company's courts for official acts — the first attempt at administrative accountability in India.
  • Civil service purification: No private trade, no presents, adequate salaries (see §9).
  • Subordination of the executive to law: "Rule by Regulation" — the executive could no longer issue arbitrary orders; everything had to be done under an enacted Regulation.

10.3 Substantive Law applied

  • Hindu Law applied to Hindus and Muslim Law to Muslims in matters of inheritance, marriage, caste, and religious usages.
  • In matters not covered — "justice, equity and good conscience".
  • Criminal law: Muslim criminal law (the so-called "Mohammedan criminal law" derived from the Hanafi school) initially, with progressive English modifications until the IPC came into force in 1862.

10.4 Defects

  • Delays in civil litigation: by the 1810s Diwani Adalats were choked with backlogs (Bengal had only 23 District Judges for 30 million people).
  • European judges ignorant of local languages and customs — dependent on Indian munshis whose advice was sometimes corrupt.
  • Separation of police from district administration weakened policing.
  • The Permanent Settlement, locked in at 1793 rates, deprived the Company of its share of agricultural growth and impoverished the Bengal peasantry (treated in detail in Topic 09).
Reform after Cornwallis: Subsequent GGs — Wellesley, Hastings (Marquess), Bentinck — reversed or modified parts of the Code: Bentinck reunited Collector and Magistrate (1831), abolished the Provincial Courts of Appeal and Courts of Circuit (1829–33), introduced Commissioners of Divisions (1829), and shifted the criminal courts language from Persian to English/vernaculars (1837). The IPC, CrPC and CPC of the 1860s replaced the substantive and procedural elements of Cornwallis's code.

11. Judicial System — Adalats, Supreme Courts, Privy Council

British India in 1857 had two parallel judicial systems — the Sadar Adalats (Company courts) operating in the mofussil (countryside) and the Supreme Courts (Crown courts) operating in the three Presidency towns. The two would be unified only by the Indian High Courts Act 1861 (post-1857; covered in Topic 07).

  • Madras: Established 1800 under King's Charter (replacing the earlier Mayor's Court of 1727).
  • Bombay: Established 1823 under King's Charter (similarly replacing the Mayor's Court).
  • Applied English common law and statutes; appealed to the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council in London.

11.3 Judicial Committee of the Privy Council

  • The Judicial Committee of the Privy Council (JCPC) was reconstituted by Act of Parliament in 1833 (Brougham's Act) as the apex appellate court for the entire British Empire, including India.
  • From 1833 to 1949, the JCPC heard appeals from the Indian High Courts; its decisions are still cited as binding precedent (when not over-ruled) in Indian courts.
  • JCPC abolished as the final court for India by the Federal Court (Enlargement of Jurisdiction) Act 1947 and Abolition of Privy Council Jurisdiction Act 1949. The Supreme Court of India inherited its appellate jurisdiction on 28 January 1950.

11.4 Personal Laws & Religious Tolerance

  • From 1772 onwards, the Company applied Hindu Law to Hindus and Muslim Law to Muslims in matters of inheritance, marriage, succession, caste, and religious usages.
  • Christians, Parsis, Jews and others governed by general territorial law (or, where applicable, their own community law).
  • Caste Disabilities Removal Act 1850 (the so-called "Lex Loci Act") — protected the property rights of converts who lost caste (e.g. Hindu converts to Christianity could still inherit ancestral property).
  • Pandits and Kazis were attached to the Adalats as expert advisers on Hindu and Muslim law respectively — until codification gradually made their role redundant.
Why three Supreme Courts mattered: The Supreme Courts created the first independent judiciary in India — with judges appointed and removable only by the Crown, applying English common law. The Sadar Adalats, by contrast, were Company courts staffed by Company servants. The tension between the two systems — played out most spectacularly in the Patna case (1777–79) and Cossijura case (1779–80) under Warren Hastings — was resolved only by the unification of 1861.

12. Codification of Laws — Law Commissions & Macaulay's IPC

One of the most enduring contributions of the 1773–1857 administration was the codification of substantive and procedural law — the IPC (1860), CrPC (1861, recast 1872, then 1898, 1973), CPC (1859, recast 1877, 1882, 1908), and Evidence Act (1872) are all direct descendants of the Macaulay-led codification project.

12.1 First Law Commission — 1834

  • Constituted under Section 53 of the Charter Act 1833.
  • Chairman: Thomas Babington Macaulay (the first Law Member of the GG's Council).
  • Other members: J. M. Macleod, G. W. Anderson, F. Millett.
  • Mandate: Codify the substantive criminal law, examine the existing laws of the Presidencies, propose a uniform law for British India.
  • Outputs:
    • Draft Indian Penal Code — submitted 4 October 1837; finally enacted 6 October 1860, in force 1 January 1862 (after Macaulay's death in 1859).
    • Lex Loci Report 1837 — recommended that the lex loci (law of the place) for Hindus be Hindu law, for Muslims Muslim law, and English law as a residuary rule.
    • Report on Limitation — led to Indian Limitation Act 1859.

12.2 Second Law Commission — 1853

  • Set up in England (London-based) under the Charter Act 1853.
  • Chairman: Sir John Romilly; members: Sir John Jervis, Sir Edward Ryan, R. Lowe, J. Macleod, T. F. Ellis, C. H. Cameron.
  • Outputs: Drafts for the Code of Civil Procedure (CPC, enacted 1859), Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC, enacted 1861), Indian Succession Act (1865).

12.3 Subsequent Law Commissions (Post-1857, for completeness)

  • Third Law Commission 1861–70 (London — under Romilly, later Lord Justice James). Output: Indian Contract Act 1872, Indian Evidence Act 1872, Specific Relief Act 1877, Negotiable Instruments Act 1881.
  • Fourth Law Commission 1879 (Whitley Stokes in India). Output: revised CPC 1882, Indian Easements Act 1882, Transfer of Property Act 1882.

12.4 Macaulay's IPC — A Closer Look

  • 23 chapters, 511 sections (in its enacted form of 1860).
  • Drew on the English common law, the Code Napoleon (France), Edward Livingston's Louisiana code, and the Code of Bombay Presidency — but was an original synthesis.
  • Famous for its plain, almost literary, English — Macaulay's prose still flows through Indian criminal law: "Whoever does any act with such knowledge, and under such circumstances, that if he thereby caused death, he would be guilty of culpable homicide not amounting to murder..." (§304).
  • Replaced in 2024 by the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) — the first major substantive overhaul in 164 years. BNS came into force 1 July 2024, alongside BNSS (replacing CrPC) and BSA (replacing Evidence Act).

12.5 Codification — What was its Significance?

  • Replaced the patchwork of Hindu and Muslim criminal law (which had varied by sect, region and judge) with a single, written, uniform law.
  • Made the law accessible — printed in English, then translated into vernaculars; lay-persons could (in principle) know the law.
  • Created the basis of a uniform legal profession — advocates trained in a common body of statutes.
  • Established the principle of rule of law in the substantive sense: the same offence punished by the same procedure across British India.
  • Imposed an alien definition of crime on Indian society — suppressed customary practices (e.g. female infanticide, sati — though some abolitions were welcome; others, like the criminalisation of nomadic communities under the Criminal Tribes Act 1871, were grievously unjust and reversed only in 1949 by independent India).

13. Police Organization — Cornwallis to Bentinck

The British inherited a chaotic policing situation from the Mughals: zamindari police, thanedars, chowkidars, faujdars, kotwals — each pursuing private or political interests. Cornwallis's reforms of 1791–93 created the first modern police structure in India, though it remained inadequate till the Police Act of 1861 (post-1857; Topic 07).

13.1 Pre-Cornwallis Policing

  • Faujdar (Mughal police-judge at sarkar level) — abolished or fell into disuse after 1772.
  • Zamindari police: the zamindar maintained barkandazes (peons) for police duty in his estate — corrupt and abusive.
  • Thanedar (head of a police station) — usually an underpaid Indian whose primary income was bribery.
  • Chowkidar (village watchman) — hereditary; paid in kind by villagers.

13.2 Cornwallis's Police Reform 1791–93

  • 1791: Cornwallis abolished the zamindari police in Bengal; police duties transferred to the District Magistrate.
  • 1792–93: Each district divided into thanas of 20-mile radius (later 10-mile), each under a Daroga (Indian officer of the rank of jamadar — chief of the thana).
  • Daroga assisted by mohurrir (clerk), jamadars, naiks and sepoys.
  • District Magistrate (later Collector-Magistrate, after Bentinck) was the supreme police authority of the district.
  • Chowkidari system retained at village level — paid by villagers, controlled by panchayat.

13.3 Bentinck & Subsequent Reforms

  • Bentinck (1829–31): District Magistrate reunited with Collector; police now firmly under district revenue administration.
  • Madras Police Act 1859: Madras introduced its own police force on a new model — experimental basis for the all-India Act of 1861.
  • Sindh: After Charles Napier's conquest 1843, Sindh got a separate police organised on Irish constabulary lines — an early model for the future all-India force.

13.4 Defects of the Pre-1861 Police

  • Daroga's pay was meagre — encouraged corruption.
  • No separate police hierarchy — the Magistrate was both prosecutor and judge of subordinate offences.
  • Investigation was inefficient; conviction rates low.
  • Police was hated — both by victims (who paid bribes to file FIRs) and accused (who paid larger bribes to escape).
  • These very defects fuelled the 1857 rumours and the popular hostility to all British officials — the police became one of the most despised limbs of the Company state.
Anchor for Topic 07: The 1860 Indian Police Commission under Sir H. M. Stokes investigated the defects of the pre-1857 system; the Police Act 1861 (Act V of 1861) created the modern police force — District Superintendent of Police, Inspector-General at province level, with police separated from the magistracy. The 1861 Act remained in force without major change till the Model Police Act 2006 (recommended by the Soli Sorabjee Committee following the Prakash Singh judgment 2006).

14. The Army — Three Presidency Armies

By 1857 the British Indian Army was the largest standing army in Asia — over 300,000 strong — and was organised in three distinct Presidency armies, each with its own commander-in-chief, its own recruitment base, and its own customs. This federalised structure was both the secret of British military success and the structural weakness that the 1857 mutiny exploited.

14.2 Structure of a Sepoy Regiment

  • European officer cadre: Colonel, Lieutenant-Colonel, Major, Captains, Lieutenants — all British.
  • Indian commissioned officers: Subedar (captain-equivalent), Jemadar (lieutenant-equivalent), Subedar-Major — promoted from the ranks, but with strict ceiling.
  • NCOs & sepoys: Havildar (sergeant), Naik (corporal), Lance Naik, Sepoy — all Indian.
  • A standard infantry regiment: ~1,000 men, divided into 10 companies.
  • A typical brigade: 1 European regiment + 2–3 sepoy regiments + artillery.

14.3 Recruitment Bias and the Brahmin-Rajput Bengal Army

  • The Bengal Army's heavy reliance on high-caste Brahmins and Rajputs from Awadh and Bihar was a deliberate policy — high-caste recruits were considered more imposing in appearance, more disciplined in service, and more useful as a buffer against the local population.
  • This concentration also meant that caste taboos — on food (no beef, no pork, no contact with low-caste cooks), on overseas service (kala pani crossing was a loss of caste), on cartridge grease — could disrupt the army.
  • The annexation of Awadh (Feb 1856) directly alienated the sepoys, many of whom were Awadhi peasants whose families now faced revenue pressure under British rule.
  • Caste-based mutinies were not new: Vellore Mutiny 1806 (over headgear and beard regulations), 19th Native Infantry mutiny at Berhampur February 1857 — all precursors to the 10 May 1857 outbreak at Meerut (treated in Topic 06).

14.4 Pay and Conditions

  • Sepoy pay: Rs 7 per month (basic) + batta (field allowance, when on active service).
  • European private's pay: about Rs 11–12 per month + better rations.
  • The General Service Enlistment Act 1856 (Lord Canning) required all new sepoys of the Bengal Army to serve "anywhere required" — including overseas. This violated long-standing custom that Bengal sepoys served only within India.
  • Allowances and batta cut: 1834–56 saw repeated reductions in foreign-service pay, abolition of free postage, withdrawal of pensions for certain campaigns.

14.5 European Artillery Monopoly

  • From the mid-18th century, the EIC's policy was that all artillery would be manned by Europeans — Indian sepoys could load and serve, but the gun-laying and command was European.
  • This monopoly was the technical reason the 1857 mutineers, despite outnumbering the British 7 to 1, could not stand against British artillery in pitched battle.
  • The exception — Bombay and Madras artillery — had some Indian gunners; this was abolished by 1861 (all artillery henceforth Royal Artillery, European).

14.6 Command Structure

  • Commander-in-Chief, India — senior British officer, usually a member of the GG's Council.
  • Separate C-in-C for each Presidency Army.
  • Below: Major-Generals commanding divisions; Brigadiers commanding brigades.
  • Royal regiments (British army regiments serving in India under Crown) co-existed with Company regiments (under EIC) — both transferred to Crown in 1858–60 and merged into a single Indian Army from 1895 (the Kitchener reforms further unified it in 1903).

  • Lambardar / Mukaddam (village headman; intermediary).

15.4 Effects on Indian Society

  • Permanent Settlement created a class of absentee zamindar — many estates passed to Calcutta merchant-money lenders within a generation.
  • Ryotwari put the peasant directly under the Collector — eliminated intermediary protection.
  • Both systems were over-assessed; periodic resettlements in ryotwari/mahalwari areas typically raised the demand by 30–50%.
  • Defaults led to land alienation, debt bondage, and recurrent agrarian distress — the structural background to peasant rebellions (Topic 11) and the cause of the famines of 1770, 1837–38, 1860s, 1876–78, 1896–97, 1899–1900, 1943.

16. Currency, Banking & Economic Administration

16.1 The Three Presidency Currencies and Unification

  • Until 1835, each Presidency had its own coinage: Calcutta sicca rupee, Madras Arcot rupee, Bombay Bombay rupee — in addition to several hundred local mint coins inherited from princely states.
  • Coinage Act 1835 (Act XVII of 1835): Established a uniform silver rupee for all British India — 11 parts silver, 1 part alloy; weight 180 grains; subdivided into 16 annas / 64 paise / 192 pies.
  • The 1835 rupee bore the head of William IV (and after 1840, Queen Victoria) — the first single coin recognised across British India. It survived (with successive monarchs' busts) till independence; the rupee-anna-pie system was decimalised on 1 April 1957.

16.2 Banking

  • Bank of Hindostan (1770–1832): First modern bank in India, founded by Alexander & Co. at Calcutta. Failed in 1832 during the agency-house collapse.
  • Three Presidency Banks:
    • Bank of Calcutta — established 2 June 1806; renamed Bank of Bengal 2 January 1809.
    • Bank of Bombay — established 15 April 1840.
    • Bank of Madras — established 1 July 1843.
  • These were joint-stock banks — partly owned by the Government, partly by private shareholders — with the right of note-issue limited to their Presidency.
  • Amalgamated by the Imperial Bank of India Act 1920 to form the Imperial Bank of India (effective 27 January 1921) — itself nationalised on 1 July 1955 as the State Bank of India.
  • Reserve Bank of India (RBI Act 1934, established 1 April 1935) became the central bank only after this chapter's period.

16.3 Government Revenue & Expenditure

  • Major revenue heads (c.1856): Land revenue (~55%), Customs & Salt (~15%), Opium (~15%), Excise (~5%), Stamps and Court Fees (~5%), Tributes from Princely States (~5%).
  • Major expenditure heads: Army (~50%), Civil administration (~25%), Public Works (~10%), Tribute to Court of Directors / "Home Charges" (~10%), Interest on debt (~5%).
  • Home Charges: Amounts remitted from India to Britain — the famous "drain of wealth" critiqued by Dadabhai Naoroji and R.C. Dutt. By the 1850s this was about £5 million a year and rising. Detailed treatment in Topic 09.
  • First annual budget for India introduced by James Wilson, Financial Member, in 1860 (post-1857 — Topic 07).

16.4 First Indian Income Tax — 1860

  • Although outside this period (post-1857), worth noting: the Income Tax Act of 24 July 1860 (Act XXXII of 1860), drafted by James Wilson, was introduced to meet the post-Mutiny financial crisis.
  • Suspended 1865; revived in various forms 1869, 1886; comprehensive Income Tax Act of 1922 (replaced 1961, again replaced by the Income Tax Act 2025 effective 1 April 2026).

17. Posts, Telegraph, Railways & the PWD

The 1830s–50s saw a communications revolution that compressed administrative time and space in India. The two crucial figures are Dalhousie (GG 1848–56) and his lieutenants — Sir William O'Shaughnessy (telegraph), R. M. Stephenson (railways), and Henry Falconer (postal).

17.1 Postal System

  • Pre-British: Mughal dak system — horse and runner relays; primarily for official mail.
  • 1766: Lord Clive opened the public postal system in Bengal.
  • 1837 (Post Office Act): Postage paid in cash on delivery; uniform schedule.
  • 1 October 1854: The Post Office Act of 1854 came into effect — introduced the uniform half-anna stamp (Queen Victoria) for letters anywhere in India — the Indian equivalent of Britain's "Penny Black" of 1840. This single reform made postal communication affordable for the middle class and was a foundational step for the press, education, and the national movement.
  • First postage stamp issued: 1 October 1854 (one anna red, half-anna blue, two anna green, four anna red-blue).

17.2 Telegraph

  • 1850: Sir William O'Shaughnessy, a doctor turned engineer (later Director-General of Telegraphs), laid an experimental telegraph line from Calcutta to Diamond Harbour (33 km) in November 1850.
  • 1853: Dalhousie sanctioned a public telegraph network — about 6,400 km were to be laid linking Calcutta, Agra, Bombay, Madras, Peshawar.
  • 18 Feb 1853: Calcutta–Agra line opened to public.
  • 1854: Calcutta–Bombay (via Agra), then Madras connected.
  • By 1856 — on the eve of the Mutiny — 6,400 km of telegraph linked the chief British stations.
  • During the 1857 revolt, the telegraph allowed the British to coordinate troop movements rapidly — Sir Robert Montgomery in Lahore disarmed the Lahore garrison on the news of the Meerut rising telegraphed from Delhi within hours. As one British officer remarked, "the telegraph saved India".
  • India connected to London by submarine cable in 1865 (via Suez).

17.3 Railways

  • Dalhousie's Minute of 1853 (the famous "Railway Minute") laid out a comprehensive plan for an Indian railway network on the "guarantee system" — British investors received a 5% guaranteed return on capital, with the Government bearing the risk.
  • 16 April 1853: First passenger railway in Asia — Great Indian Peninsula Railway (GIPR), Bombay (Boribunder) to Thane, 34 km. 14 carriages, 400 passengers, three locomotives ("Sahib", "Sindh", "Sultan").
  • 15 August 1854: First East Indian Railway service, Howrah to Hooghly, 38 km.
  • 1 July 1856: Madras Railway opened first section, Veyasarpadi to Walajah Road.
  • By 1869, India had over 6,400 km of railway track; by 1900, 40,000 km; by 1947, 53,000 km; by 2025, ~68,500 km of broad-gauge route.
  • Railways were primarily designed for British commercial and strategic purposes — bulk cotton to Bombay port, troops to interior cantonments — not for Indian internal trade. Karl Marx famously praised the railways as the harbinger of capitalism in India in his 1853 articles in the New York Daily Tribune; Indian nationalist economists (Naoroji, R.C. Dutt) later showed the railways drained capital, ruined handicrafts, and concentrated agriculture for export.

17.4 Public Works Department (PWD), 1854

  • Before Dalhousie, public works were managed by the Military Department (using military engineers).
  • Dalhousie separated public works from the army and created a civilian Public Works Department in 1854 in each Presidency.
  • Major projects 1830s–50s: Ganges Canal (opened by Dalhousie 8 April 1854 — 560-km irrigation work in NWP under Colonel Proby Cautley), Eastern and Western Yamuna Canals (extended), Cauvery and Godavari delta systems (under Sir Arthur Cotton in Madras), Grand Trunk Road revival (Calcutta to Peshawar — the British re-paved Sher Shah Suri's road).
  • Roorkee College — established by Cautley in 1847 to train civil engineers for irrigation works; the first engineering college in India and the British Empire. Renamed Thomason College 1854; today IIT Roorkee (since 2001).
Pithy Dalhousie summary: Dalhousie's eight years gave India the "3 Ts" — Telegraph (1853), Trains (1853), Tapal/Post (1854) — plus PWD (1854), Wood's Despatch (1854), Indian Universities Act (drafted; passed January 1857). These reforms made him perhaps the most modernising of all GGs — while his Doctrine of Lapse and Awadh annexation were also among the most provocative causes of 1857.

18. Education Administration till 1857

This section is a brief administrative survey; the substantive debate (Orientalists vs Anglicists, the politics of Macaulay's Minute, vernacular vs English, missionary schools) is treated in detail in Modern Topic 10 — Development of Education and Press under British Rule.

18.1 Pre-1813 Period

  • EIC officially neutral on Indian education — left to missionaries and indigenous institutions (pathshalas, madrasas, tols).
  • Warren Hastings established Calcutta Madrasa (1781) for Islamic studies.
  • Jonathan Duncan, Resident at Banaras, established Banaras Sanskrit College 1791.
  • William Carey, Joshua Marshman, William Ward — the "Serampore Trio" Baptist missionaries — set up vernacular schools and a press from 1800.

18.2 Charter Act 1813 — The Rs 1 Lakh Clause

  • Section 43: One lakh of rupees annually to be spent on "the revival and improvement of literature, and the encouragement of the learned natives of India, and for the introduction and promotion of a knowledge of the sciences among the inhabitants of British territories".
  • The first state grant for education in India — though the money was not actually spent till 1823.
  • General Committee of Public Instruction (1823): Constituted to advise the GG on the use of the Rs 1 lakh; chaired by Lord Amherst's nominee; consisted of Orientalists (Horace Wilson) and Anglicists (Lord Macaulay after 1834).

18.3 Macaulay's Minute — 2 February 1835

  • Macaulay, the Law Member, was asked to settle the Orientalist–Anglicist controversy — whether the Rs 1 lakh should be spent on Sanskrit/Arabic/Persian classical learning (Orientalists) or on English-medium Western education (Anglicists).
  • Macaulay's Minute came down decisively for English — famous (and infamous) phrases:
    • "A single shelf of a good European library was worth the whole native literature of India and Arabia."
    • "We must at present do our best to form a class who may be interpreters between us and the millions whom we govern; a class of persons Indian in blood and colour, but English in tastes, in opinions, in morals, and in intellect."
  • Bentinck's Resolution of 7 March 1835: Adopted Macaulay's recommendation — English would be the medium of higher education; vernacular for primary.

18.4 Wood's Despatch — 19 July 1854

The famous "Magna Carta of English Education in India" by Sir Charles Wood (President of the Board of Control). Its main provisions:

  • Recognition of education as a state responsibility — not purely missionary or private.
  • Departments of Public Instruction in each Presidency (created 1855).
  • Universities to be established on the London model at Calcutta, Bombay, Madras (created January 1857).
  • Hierarchy: village schools (vernacular) → secondary schools (vernacular + English) → colleges (English) → universities.
  • Grants-in-aid system to support privately-managed schools.
  • Training schools for teachers; female education encouraged; vocational schools.
  • Anglo-vernacular education for the middle classes; vernacular for the masses — the "downward filtration" theory of Macaulay survived but with greater attention to mass education.

18.5 Indian Universities Act 1857

  • Three universities established in January 1857 (just months before the Mutiny):
    • University of Calcutta — 24 January 1857 (Act II of 1857).
    • University of Bombay — 18 July 1857.
    • University of Madras — 5 September 1857.
  • Affiliating model — examined and conferred degrees; teaching done by colleges affiliated to the university.
  • By 1857 there were also Government colleges (Hindu College Calcutta — founded 20 January 1817, became Presidency College 1855; Elphinstone Institution Bombay — 1834–35; Madras Christian College — 1837), private missionary colleges, and indigenous institutions.
Critique: The education system created in 1813–57 was elitist (focused on the urban middle class), foreign-language (English), and produced a thin layer of "Macaulay's children" who would become the future Indian National Congress leadership — from Naoroji and Gokhale to Tilak and Gandhi. Mass literacy remained around 4% in 1857 and 12% even at independence in 1947 (NCERT). The unintended consequence was the creation of a class of English-educated Indians who turned the language and ideas of liberal Europe — rule of law, parliamentary government, nationalism — against their British masters.

  • Recruited Residents and Political Agents from the ICS, with a strong cohort from the British Indian Army (Political Officers).
  • The Resident at a major Court (e.g. Hyderabad) was a Crown-appointed personality of high rank; lower-ranking Political Agents served at smaller states or Agencies.
  • Important Residencies and Agencies in this period: Hyderabad, Lucknow (until 1856 annexation), Indore (Central India Agency), Baroda, Jaipur (Rajputana Agency), Sikkim, Kathmandu (Nepal), Kabul (1839–42, disastrously).

19.3 Wellesley's Subsidiary Alliance and Dalhousie's Doctrine of Lapse

  • Subsidiary Alliance (Wellesley, 1798): First applied to Hyderabad; extended to Mysore (1799), Awadh (1801), Peshwa (1802 — Treaty of Bassein), Gaikwad (1802), Scindia and Holkar (1803–04).
  • Doctrine of Lapse (Dalhousie, 1848): Adopted children could not inherit principalities; states without natural heirs would "lapse" to the British. Annexations under the doctrine: Satara 1848, Jaitpur and Sambalpur 1849, Baghat 1850, Udaipur 1852, Nagpur 1854, Jhansi 1854, Awadh 1856 (on grounds of misgovernment, not lapse). All covered in Topic 04.
  • These annexations were a principal cause of the 1857 revolt — especially Awadh, Jhansi, Nagpur. Detailed treatment in Topic 06.

19.4 The Doctrine of Paramountcy

  • The British asserted "paramountcy" over the princely states — not sovereignty in the European sense, but supreme political authority by treaty and convention.
  • Paramountcy was undefined in law — it lay in the discretion of the Crown's representative.
  • The Indian States Committee (Butler Committee, 1929) tried to define it; the Cabinet Mission Statement 1946 declared paramountcy lapsed at independence (15 August 1947), leaving the princely states free to accede to India, Pakistan, or remain independent. The Patel-Menon integration of 1947–48 (Topic 19 of the original plan, future build) gathered the 562 states into India.

20. Critique — Was British Administration "Efficient"?

UPSC routinely asks variants of this question for Mains. The honest answer requires holding two truths together: the British created a uniform, codified, rules-based administration that survived independence; and that same administration was extractive, exclusionary, and produced famines and resentments that ultimately destroyed it.

20.1 What was achieved 1773–1857

  • A uniform legal code (Macaulay's draft IPC, 1837; CPC 1859; CrPC 1861).
  • A trained civil service recruited (after 1853) by open competitive examination.
  • A district administration with a single locus of authority (Collector-Magistrate).
  • A modern judiciary, separated from the executive at the higher levels.
  • A police force, however inadequate, organised under statute.
  • A communications revolution — postal stamp (1854), telegraph (1853), railways (1853).
  • Modern banking (Presidency Banks 1806–43, uniform currency 1835).
  • An education system that, although elitist, produced India's modern political class.
  • An Anglo-Indian official record-keeping tradition (the file, the case-book, the gazette) that survives as the GoI's information infrastructure.

20.2 What was extracted / done badly

  • Drain of wealth: Annual remittance of around £5 million by 1856; Naoroji's later calculation showed it had risen to over £30 million by 1900.
  • Deindustrialisation: The free-trade Charter Act of 1813 destroyed Indian handicraft textiles within a generation; by 1850 India was a net importer of Lancashire cloth.
  • Famines: 1770 Bengal famine (~10 million dead, one-third of Bengal), 1837–38 NWP famine (~800,000 dead). British policy of free-trade in grain even during scarcity, and inflexible revenue demand, were principal causes.
  • Cornwallis colour bar: Indians excluded from all senior positions till 1853 (open competition) and effectively till 1863 (first Indian ICS).
  • Permanent Settlement: Created an absentee zamindar class; impoverished the Bengal peasantry; locked in revenue at 1793 rates — depriving the state of natural revenue growth (and so forcing higher demands elsewhere).
  • Police corruption: The Daroga system was hated and abused.
  • Cultural insensitivity: The 1856 General Service Enlistment Act, the greased cartridge issue (1857), the doctrine of lapse, the annexation of Awadh — all administrative measures that triggered the 1857 revolt.

20.3 The Bridge to 1857

The administration of 1773–1857 was a paradox: the most legalistic, codified, rules-bound state India had ever known — and the most alien, extractive, and inflexible. By 1857, every layer of Indian society had a grievance against it: the sepoy (pay, religion, overseas service), the deposed prince (Doctrine of Lapse), the dispossessed taluqdar (Awadh annexation), the indebted ryot (Permanent Settlement, revenue pressure), the displaced artisan (Lancashire cottons), the alienated religious leader (missionary entry post-1813, Hindu Widow Remarriage Act 1856), and the educated middle class (Cornwallis colour bar). The administrative machine that had been built to govern India became, for a moment in 1857, the principal target of Indian rage.

Final UPSC frame: The British administration till 1857 was simultaneously the most efficient government India had ever had (by 19th-century European standards of orderly, rule-bound, written administration) and the most exploitative (by any reasonable standard of accountability to the governed). It survived 1857 by re-engineering itself (Topic 07) but never solved the contradiction between rules-based efficiency and unaccountable foreignness — that contradiction is what the national movement (Topics 11–17) finally exploited.

15 Must-Know Facts — Quick Revision

  1. Regulating Act 1773 — first parliamentary statute on India; Warren Hastings, first GG of Bengal; Supreme Court at Calcutta (Royal Charter 26 March 1774).
  2. Pitt's India Act 13 August 1784 — created Board of Control (6 Crown ministers) and dual government in London; subordinated Madras and Bombay to GG.
  3. Charter Act 1793 — ratified Cornwallis's reforms; Indian revenue to pay Board's salaries.
  4. Charter Act 1813 — ended EIC's trade monopoly (except China); missionaries allowed; Rs 1 lakh for education.
  5. Charter Act 1833 — created Governor-General of India; first holder William Bentinck; Macaulay as first Law Member; First Indian Law Commission; §87 anti-discrimination clause.
  6. Charter Act 1853 — open competitive ICS exam (first held London July 1855; Satyendranath Tagore first Indian qualifier June 1863); separate Legislative Council of 12 members.
  7. Cornwallis Code 1 May 1793 — 48 Regulations; separated revenue from judiciary; "rule of law"; civil-service purification (no private trade, fixed salaries, Cornwallis colour bar).
  8. Permanent Settlement 22 March 1793 (Cornwallis, drafted by John Shore) — Bengal/Bihar/Orissa; zamindars permanent landlords; revenue fixed forever.
  9. Bentinck reforms (1828–35): Commissioners of Divisions 1829; Collector + Magistrate reunited 1831; Persian replaced by English in courts 1837; Macaulay's Minute on Education 2 February 1835; English as medium of higher education (Bentinck Resolution 7 March 1835).
  10. Macaulay's draft IPC submitted 4 October 1837; enacted 6 October 1860; in force 1 January 1862; replaced by BNS on 1 July 2024.
  11. Wood's Despatch 19 July 1854 — "Magna Carta of English Education"; led to Departments of Public Instruction (1855), Universities of Calcutta (24 Jan 1857), Bombay (18 Jul 1857), Madras (5 Sep 1857).
  12. Dalhousie's "3 Ts" 1853–54: Telegraph (Calcutta–Agra 18 Feb 1853), Trains (Bombay–Thane 16 Apr 1853), Tapal (uniform ½-anna postage stamp 1 Oct 1854); plus PWD (1854) and Ganges Canal (8 Apr 1854).
  13. Three Presidency Banks: Bank of Bengal (renamed 2 Jan 1809 from Bank of Calcutta 2 Jun 1806), Bombay (15 Apr 1840), Madras (1 Jul 1843); merged 1921 into Imperial Bank; nationalised 1 Jul 1955 as SBI.
  14. Three Presidency Armies: Bengal (largest, high-caste, Awadh-Bihar recruited), Madras (mixed castes), Bombay (mixed); ~300,000 sepoys + ~45,000 Europeans by 1857. General Service Enlistment Act 1856 (Canning) triggered Bengal Army discontent — one trigger of the 1857 revolt.
  15. "Firsts" cheat-sheet: First GG of Bengal — Warren Hastings (1773); first GG of India — Bentinck (1833); first GG & Viceroy — Canning (1858); first Law Member — Macaulay (1834); first Indian ICS — Satyendranath Tagore (1863); first engineering college — Roorkee (1847); first passenger railway — Bombay-Thane (16 Apr 1853); first telegraph public line — Calcutta-Agra (18 Feb 1853); first postage stamp — 1 Oct 1854.

Quick Quiz

First Governor-General of Bengal?

Warren Hastings, under the Regulating Act 1773 (took oath 20 October 1773; first formally designated GG of Bengal).

First Governor-General of India?

Lord William Bentinck, under the Charter Act 1833 (which redesignated the GG of Bengal as GG of India). He had been GG of Bengal since 1828; assumed the new title in 1833.

Which Act ended the EIC's trade monopoly with India?

Charter Act 1813 (the China trade and tea monopoly continued until the Charter Act 1833 ended them too).

Who was the first Law Member of the Governor-General's Council?

Thomas Babington Macaulay (1834–38), under the Charter Act 1833.

Year of Macaulay's draft Indian Penal Code?

1837 (submitted 4 October 1837); enacted 6 October 1860, came into force 1 January 1862 — covered in Topic 07.

First Indian to qualify for the ICS?

Satyendranath Tagore, June 1863 (elder brother of Rabindranath Tagore). The open competitive exam began in London in July 1855.

Year of Calcutta Supreme Court's establishment?

By Royal Charter dated 26 March 1774, under the Regulating Act 1773.

Which GG abolished the Provincial Courts of Appeal?

William Bentinck (1829–33) — he also introduced Commissioners of Divisions (1829) and reunited Collector + Magistrate (1831).

When was the first electric telegraph line in India?

Calcutta–Diamond Harbour line, 5 November 1850 (experimental, William O'Shaughnessy); Calcutta–Agra opened to public 18 Feb 1853. Telegraph reached Bombay 1854 — under Dalhousie.

Which document is called the "Magna Carta of English Education" in India?

Wood's Despatch of 19 July 1854 — it laid down the framework for English-medium higher education and established the system of grants-in-aid.
