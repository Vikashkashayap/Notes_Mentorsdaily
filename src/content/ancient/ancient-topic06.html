Ancient History · Topic 06

Buddhism & Jainism

Heterodox movements that challenged Brahminism — Four Noble Truths, Eightfold Path, Pancha Mahavrata, Triratna, and the dawn of philosophical pluralism in Ancient India.

📅 6th century BCE 🔥 Prelims: 9/10 📝 Mains: 5/5 ⏱ 50 min read 📍 Gangetic Plain

⚡ Conceptual Clarity: Why Two New Religions?

The Problem

Vedic Brahminism — expensive sacrifices (yajnas), Sanskrit exclusivity, rigid varna hierarchy, animal slaughter — had become oppressive for the masses and the rising mercantile class.

The Solution

Both Buddhism and Jainism preached in Prakrit/Pali (common languages), rejected caste by birth, emphasised personal ethics over ritual, and offered salvation accessible to all — including women and Shudras.

The Context

6th century BCE: Iron Age urbanisation, rise of 16 Mahajanapadas, growth of trade guilds (shrenis), Kshatriya assertiveness against Brahmin ritual monopoly — eastern India (Bihar, UP) as the epicentre.

The Legacy

Buddhism became a world religion (Asia). Jainism stayed concentrated but shaped Indian ethics (Gandhi's ahimsa, anekantavada in jurisprudence). Both produced rich art, architecture and philosophical traditions.

🌱 1. Reasons for Emergence of New Religions

Social Factors

  • Caste Rigidity: The rigid varna system placed Brahmins at the apex. Kshatriyas and Vaishyas resented Brahmin dominance over religious life.
  • Birth-based discrimination: Jati (sub-caste) distinctions had multiplied, denying social mobility to talented individuals.
  • Status of Women: Women's participation in Vedic rituals had diminished significantly in Later Vedic period.

Religious & Philosophical Factors

  • Ritualism & formalism: Elaborate yajnas (Ashvamedha, Rajasuya) were expensive, priestly monopolised, and had degenerated into mechanical rites.
  • Animal sacrifice: Mass slaughter in Vedic yajnas horrified agricultural communities who depended on cattle.
  • Sanskrit barrier: Religious knowledge locked in Sanskrit (Brahmin monopoly); people could not access scriptures.
  • Upanishadic search: Growing philosophical tradition (Atman–Brahman unity) created receptive audience for metaphysical questioning.

Economic Factors

  • Rise of Vaishya class: Prosperous merchants (sarthavahas — caravan leaders) sought religious sanction and social dignity outside the varna hierarchy.
  • Cattle protection: Farmers needed cattle for agriculture; Vedic sacrifices destroying cattle was economically devastating.
  • Trade routes: New urban centres and trade networks along Gangetic plain demanded a universal, non-exclusive religion.

Political Factors

  • Kshatriya revolt: Both Mahavira and Gautama Buddha were Kshatriyas — symbolising Kshatriya challenge to Brahmin intellectual supremacy.
  • Royal patronage: Rulers of Magadha (Bimbisara, Ajatashatru), Kosala, and later Mauryas were attracted to non-Brahminical faiths as tools for administrative unity.
UPSC Angle: The emergence of Buddhism and Jainism is best understood as a reform movement, not a revolution — they accepted karma and rebirth from Hindu philosophy but rejected caste by birth, Vedic authority, and ritual sacrifice.

☸ 2. Buddhism

2.1 Life of Gautama Buddha

Birth

563 BCE (traditional) / 480 BCE (revised). Lumbini, Nepal. Father: Shuddhodana (Shakya clan chief). Mother: Mayadevi (died 7 days after birth). Raised by aunt Prajapati Gautami.

The Four Sights

Triggered renunciation: (1) Old man, (2) Sick man, (3) Dead body, (4) Wandering ascetic at peace — revealed Dukkha and the monastic path.

Renunciation

Mahabhinishkramana — Great Departure at age 29. Left wife Yashodhara, son Rahula. Studied under Alara Kalama (Vaishali) and Uddaka Ramaputta (Rajagriha).

Enlightenment

Nirvana at Bodh Gaya (under Peepal/Bodhi tree), age 35. After 6 years of austerities and 49 days of meditation. Middle Path — rejected extreme asceticism.

First Sermon

Dhammachakka Pavattana (Turning of the Wheel of Law) at Sarnath (Deer Park, Isipatana) to 5 disciples — the Panchavargiya Bhikshus. This event = Dhamma-Chakra-Pravartana.

Death

Mahaparinirvana at Kushinagar (UP), 483 BCE. Age 80. Under two Sala trees. Last meal: Sukara-maddava (possibly pork or soft mushroom) from Cunda the smith.

2.5 Buddhist Sects / Schools

Hinayana (Theravada)

"Lesser Vehicle" — individual salvation; strict monastic discipline; no idol worship; Buddha as great teacher; Pali language; ideal = Arhat (personal liberation). Survived in Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Thailand.

Mahayana

"Greater Vehicle" — universal salvation via Bodhisattva ideal; elaborate ritual; idol worship; Sanskrit; Buddha as deity; ideal = Bodhisattva. Spread to China, Japan, Korea, Tibet. Nagarjuna (Madhyamaka), Asanga (Yogacara).

Vajrayana

"Thunderbolt/Diamond Vehicle" — tantric Buddhism; use of mantras, mudras, mandalas; esoteric practices; emerged c. 7th century CE; dominant in Tibet/Nepal. Also called Tantrayana or Mantrayana.

2.9 Spread of Buddhism

  • Within India: Magadha → Kosala → Avanti → North-West India under Ashoka.
  • Ceylon/Lanka: Ashoka's son Mahinda and daughter Sanghamitta (who brought the sacred Bodhi tree sapling). Theravada Buddhism still dominant.
  • Central Asia & China: Kushan Empire (Kanishka) facilitated Silk Road spread; Mahayana Buddhism entered China ~1st century CE.
  • South-East Asia: Trade routes carried Buddhism to Burma, Thailand, Cambodia (Angkor Wat), Indonesia (Borobudur).
  • Tibet: 7th century CE — King Songtsen Gampo; Padmasambhava brought Vajrayana ~8th century.

2.10 Decline of Buddhism in India

  • Internal factors: Monastic corruption; accumulation of wealth; split into sects; Brahminisation of Mahayana practices (idol worship, Sanskrit).
  • Brahminical revival: Kumaragupta's support for Gupta Brahminism; Shankaracharya's Advaita Vedanta intellectually challenged Buddhism (8th century).
  • Bhakti movement: Absorbed Buddhist elements into Hindu fold (Buddha as 9th avatar of Vishnu).
  • Turkish invasions: Muhammad Bin Bakhtiyar Khilji destroyed Nalanda (1193 CE) — physical destruction of monastic centres. Monks fled to Tibet and South-East Asia.
  • Loss of royal patronage: Post-Harsha, Buddhist patronage declined.

🕉 3. Jainism

3.2 Life of Mahavira

Birth

599 BCE. Kundagrama (near Vaishali, Bihar). Father: Siddhartha (Lichchhavi chief). Mother: Trishala (sister of Lichchhavi chief Chetak). Born in Jnatrika Kshatriya clan.

Renunciation

Age 30. Left wife Yasoda, daughter Priyadarshana. Practised extreme asceticism (12+ years) — wore no clothes, pulled out hair by roots (Kesh-loch), endured hardships silently.

Enlightenment

Kaivalya (omniscience) under Sala tree at Jrimbhikagrama (near Vaishali), age 42. Also called Jina (conqueror) — those who follow = Jains.

Death

Nirvana at Pavapuri (near Rajgir, Bihar), 527 BCE. Age 72. Death by voluntary starvation (Sallekhana/Santhara) — died simultaneously with Nanda chief Hastipala.

Titles of Mahavira: Vardhamana (grows in virtue), Jina (conqueror of passions), Mahavira (great hero), Nirgantha (free from all ties), Kashyapa (clan name), Shraman/Shramana (ascetic).

Jain Cosmology & Soul (Jiva)

  • Two realities: Jiva (soul/living) and Ajiva (non-living: matter/pudgala, space, time, motion, rest).
  • Karma: Subtle matter that sticks to the soul; all action (good or bad) generates karma.
  • Bondage: Karma accumulation = bondage (bandha).
  • Liberation (Moksha): Stop new karma (samvara) + shed existing karma (nirjara) through asceticism → pure soul rises to Siddhashila (top of universe).
  • Lokakasha: The occupied universe — finite, shaped like a cosmic person; Siddhashila is at the very top.

3.9 Spread and Decline of Jainism

Spread

Strong in Gujarat, Rajasthan, Karnataka, Madhya Pradesh. Merchant communities (Marwaris, Oswals) adopted Jainism — non-violence compatible with trade. Never spread as a world religion unlike Buddhism.

Survival Factors

Strong lay community (shravaka/shravika) supporting monastic order; merchant wealth funding temples; localized communities; not tied to royal patronage as exclusively as Buddhism.

Modern Relevance

~4.5 million Jains in India (0.4% population) but disproportionate economic influence. Anekantavada: pluralism in democratic discourse. Gandhi drew directly from Jain ahimsa via mother Putlibai.

⚖ 4. Buddhism vs Jainism — Comparison

Buddhism vs Jainism — comparative overview of key doctrinal and historical features

4.1 Similarities

  • Both rejected the authority of the Vedas and the supremacy of the Brahmin priestly class.
  • Both rejected animal sacrifice in Vedic yajnas and stressed Ahimsa.
  • Both accepted Karma and Rebirth — but without a permanent Atman (Buddhism) or with eternal individual Jiva (Jainism).
  • Both were founded by Kshatriya princes from the Gangetic plain.
  • Both preached in Prakrit languages (Pali — Buddhism; Ardha-Magadhi — Jainism), not Sanskrit.
  • Both established monastic orders (Sangha/Bhikshu Sangha) and accepted women as nuns.
  • Both preached universal ethics accessible to all castes.
  • Both opposed the caste hierarchy based on birth.
  • Both received patronage from kings, merchants, and trading guilds (shrenis).

4.2 Differences (Key UPSC Distinctions)

🔮 5. Other Heterodox Sects (Shramana Traditions)

The 6th century BCE saw a proliferation of shramana (non-Brahminical) philosophical movements. Buddhist texts mention six major non-Buddhist teachers (Titthiyas) besides Buddha. Several key schools:

Sect / SchoolKey FigureCore DoctrineUPSC Significance
Ajivika Makkhali Gosala (a disciple-turned-rival of Mahavira) Niyativada (Absolute Determinism) — every soul will attain liberation after exactly 8,400,000 rebirths regardless of action; no free will; universe operates on a fixed plan (Niyati = fate) Most important heterodox sect after Buddhism and Jainism; received royal patronage (Bindusara, Ashoka gave Barabar caves). Disappeared by 14th century CE.
Lokayata / Charvaka Charvaka (legendary), Ajita Kesakambali (historical teacher) Materialist philosophy — only the perceivable world exists; no soul, no afterlife, no karma, no God; "eat, drink and be merry" (hedonism); perception (pratyaksha) is the only valid means of knowledge India's only full materialist school; extremely radical — rejected all Vedic and shramana metaphysics. Brihaspatya Sutra attributed to it. Sarvasiddhantasamgraha describes its doctrines.
Ajnana / Agnosticism Sanjaya Belaṭṭhaputta (one of the six teachers) Radical scepticism — it is impossible to know metaphysical truths (soul, afterlife, karma); refused to make any definite philosophical assertions; "eel-wriggling" philosophy (Amaravikkhepika) Two of Buddha's chief disciples (Sariputta and Moggallana) were originally followers of Sanjaya before converting to Buddhism.
Uchchhedavada Ajita Kesakambali Annihilationism — at death, all four elements (earth, water, fire, air) dissolve back into nature; no soul survives death; no rebirth; all consciousness ends permanently at death Directly opposed to both Brahminical soul theory and Buddhist subtle rebirth; rejected by Buddha in Majjhima Nikaya as extreme nihilism
Akriyavada Purana Kassapa Non-action doctrine — actions have no moral consequences (neither merit nor demerit); killing, lying, stealing produce no karma; complete moral nihilism Denied the entire Indian karmic framework; mentioned in Buddhist sources as one of the six heretical teachers
Aniccitvada / Sassatavada Pakudha Kacchayana Eternalism of seven elements — earth, water, fire, air, pleasure, pain, and the soul are eternal, unchanging, and uncreated; no one can actually kill or be killed since these elements simply rearrange Quasi-materialist eternalism; countered by Buddha's dependent origination and impermanence
The Six Heretical Teachers (Titthiyas) in Buddhist texts: (1) Purana Kassapa — Akriyavada; (2) Makkhali Gosala — Niyativada/Ajivika; (3) Ajita Kesakambali — Uchchhedavada/Lokayata; (4) Pakudha Kacchayana — Sassatavada/Seven eternals; (5) Nigantha Nataputta — Mahavira/Jainism; (6) Sanjaya Belaṭṭhaputta — Agnosticism/Eel-wriggling.

🌟 6. Importance and Modern Relevance

Buddhism's Global Legacy

500 million Buddhists worldwide (3rd largest religion). Mindfulness, meditation practices (MBSR) mainstreamed globally. UNESCO World Heritage Sites: Bodh Gaya, Sanchi, Sarnath area.

Jainism & Ahimsa

Gandhi's ahimsa directly derived from Jain influence. Anekantavada cited in Indian Supreme Court judgements on religious pluralism. Jain vegetarianism influenced animal rights movements.

Art & Architecture

Ajanta-Ellora (UNESCO), Sanchi Stupa, Nalanda University (now UNESCO site), Buddhist Circuit tourism. Dilwara, Ranakpur, Gomateshvara — major architectural monuments.

Social Reform

Both movements challenged caste hierarchy, created social space for women and lower castes, established educational institutions (Nalanda, Vikramashila), and spread literacy through vernacular languages.

Anekantavada in Constitutional Debates Several legal scholars and SC judges have cited Jain Anekantavada (many-sidedness of truth) in support of India's pluralistic constitutional framework. Relevance to debates on free speech, religious pluralism, and the limits of absolute claims in democratic society.

✏ Self-Test (Click to Reveal)

Where did Buddha deliver his first sermon? ▼
Sarnath (Deer Park / Isipatana), near Varanasi — the event is called Dhammachakka Pavattana (Turning of the Wheel of Law). To the 5 Panchavargiya Bhikshus.
Which vow did Mahavira add to Parshvanatha's four? ▼
Brahmacharya (celibacy). Parshvanatha's four: Ahimsa, Satya, Asteya, Aparigraha. Mahavira added Brahmacharya as the fourth, making Aparigraha the fifth.
Who founded the Ajivika sect? ▼
Makkhali Gosala — originally a disciple of Mahavira who broke away. Preached Niyativada (absolute determinism): all souls take exactly 8,400,000 rebirths and then automatically attain liberation regardless of effort.
What is Anekantavada? ▼
Jain doctrine of many-sidedness of truth — no single statement fully captures reality. Has two corollaries: Syadvada (each statement true only from a standpoint, prefixed by "syat") and Nayavada (truth has many partial perspectives/nayas).
Name the patron and venue of the Third Buddhist Council. ▼
Patron: Ashoka (Maurya). Venue: Pataliputra. Chairman: Moggaliputta Tissa. Outcome: Compiled Abhidhamma Pitaka, expelled heretics, sent Buddhist missionaries to 9 regions (Mahinda to Lanka, Dharmaraksita to Greece, etc.).
