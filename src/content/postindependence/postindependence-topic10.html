Congress Revival & the Punjab Crisis (1977–84)

How the Janata experiment collapsed under its own contradictions, how Indira Gandhi returned in 1980, and how a demand for autonomy in Punjab spiralled into militancy, Operation Blue Star and a national tragedy.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames this Period

This chapter is tested as a study of Indian federalism and identity politics under strain — how legitimate regional aspirations, mishandled, can slide into secessionist violence. Sort your prep into three buckets:

  • Static / factual recall — the sequence: Janata collapse (1979), Indira’s return (1980), the Anandpur Sahib Resolution (1973/1978), Operation Blue Star (June 1984), the assassination (31 Oct 1984). Names, dates, documents.
  • Federal / institutional — Centre–State tensions, the misuse of Article 356, river-water and Chandigarh disputes, and the Sarkaria Commission response — the structural story beneath the violence.
  • Analytical (GS-I) — why an economic and autonomy grievance became a religious-militant one, the interplay of politics and identity, and how the state eventually restored peace. Idea + example + judgement.

Framing note: Handle Punjab with sensitivity and balance — present it as a case study in federal management and the costs of politicising identity, never as a communal narrative.

1. Setting: after the Emergency

The 1977 verdict had restored democracy but not stability. The Janata Party that swept to power was a coalition of opposites held together only by opposition to Indira Gandhi. Once that glue dissolved, its contradictions surfaced fast — and within three years the electorate turned back to the very leader it had rejected.

Inheritance of 1977Consequence
A coalition of rivalsJana Sangh, socialists, Congress-O, BLD — no shared programme beyond anti-Indira feeling.
Leadership contestMorarji Desai, Charan Singh and Jagjivan Ram all wanted the top job.
Economic stressSecond oil shock (1979), inflation and drought weakened the government.
Unresolved regional demandsPunjab, Assam and other regional aspirations awaited attention.
GS-I hook: The Janata collapse and the Punjab crisis are two faces of one theme — the difficulty of managing a diverse polity once the Congress “system” had broken down. Link them.

2. The collapse of the Janata government

The Janata government (1977–79) unravelled over the “dual membership” issue — the demand that former Jana Sangh members give up their RSS ties. Personal ambition and ideological incompatibility did the rest.

  • Dual membership: socialists insisted Jana Sangh members choose between the party and the RSS; the dispute split the coalition.
  • Charan Singh’s revolt: Morarji Desai lost his majority; Charan Singh became PM in July 1979 with outside Congress support.
  • Stillborn government: Indira Gandhi withdrew support before Charan Singh could face Parliament; he resigned without a floor test — India’s first PM never to face the House.
  • Fresh polls: mid-term elections were called for January 1980.
Prelims trap: Charan Singh was PM (Jul 1979–Jan 1980) but never won a confidence vote. Morarji Desai was the first non-Congress PM (1977); Charan Singh the second.

3. Indira Gandhi’s return, 1980

In the January 1980 elections, Indira Gandhi’s Congress (I) won a decisive victory. The party that had been humiliated in 1977 was back in power in under three years — a testament as much to Janata’s failure as to Congress’s revival.

Feature of the 1980 returnDetail
MandateCongress (I) won a comfortable Lok Sabha majority on the slogan of stability and “a government that works.”
Sanjay GandhiEmerged as heir-apparent and party organiser — but died in an air crash in June 1980.
Rajiv GandhiDrawn into politics after Sanjay’s death; won the Amethi by-election in 1981.
Governance turnA more centralised, personality-driven Congress; growing friction with several states.
One-line frame: 1980 restored Indira Gandhi but not the old Congress “system” — the party was now a top-down organisation increasingly at odds with assertive regional forces.

4. Roots of the Punjab problem

The Punjab crisis was not born of religion alone. It grew from a tangle of economic, federal and political grievances that were left to fester, and which were then given a religious idiom.

GrievanceSubstance
River watersSharing of Ravi–Beas waters with Haryana and Rajasthan; the Sutlej-Yamuna Link (SYL) canal dispute.
ChandigarhThe shared capital of Punjab and Haryana — both claimed it exclusively.
Green Revolution paradoxProsperity for landowning farmers, but rural unemployment among Sikh youth and resentment at central pricing.
AutonomyA demand for greater state powers within the Union — crystallised in the Anandpur Sahib Resolution.
Political competitionCongress’s manoeuvres against the Akali Dal, including the initial cultivation of Bhindranwale, sharpened the conflict.
GS-I hook: Always present the roots as federal-economic first, religious second. The tragedy was that a solvable set of Centre–State disputes was allowed to acquire a militant, communal edge.

5. The Anandpur Sahib Resolution

Adopted by the Akali Dal in 1973 (and reaffirmed in 1978), the Anandpur Sahib Resolution set out the party’s political and religious demands. Its interpretation became the fault-line of the crisis.

  • Core demand: greater autonomy for Punjab — the Centre to retain only defence, foreign affairs, currency and communications, with residual powers to the states.
  • Specific asks: transfer of Chandigarh to Punjab, a fair river-water share, and recognition of Sikh religious concerns.
  • Two readings: the Akalis called it a demand for federal autonomy within India; critics read its ambiguous language as edging towards separatism.
Prelims anchor: The Anandpur Sahib Resolution (1973) is an Akali Dal document on Punjab autonomy — frequently tested by name and year.

6. The rise of militancy & Bhindranwale

Through the early 1980s the movement turned violent. Jarnail Singh Bhindranwale, initially encouraged by sections of the Congress to undercut the Akalis, grew into an independent and militant force, eventually fortifying himself inside the Golden Temple complex in Amritsar.

  • Escalating violence: targeted killings, extortion and communal tension mounted across Punjab from 1981.
  • Sanctuary in the Golden Temple: Bhindranwale and armed followers occupied the Akal Takht within the Harmandir Sahib complex, stockpiling weapons.
  • State paralysis: negotiations between the Centre and the Akalis repeatedly failed; law and order collapsed.
Critical edge: The Frankenstein pattern — a faction cultivated for short-term political advantage that grows beyond its sponsor’s control — is a recurring cautionary theme in Indian political history. Note it without sensationalising.

7. Operation Blue Star (June 1984)

In early June 1984 the government ordered the army to flush out the militants from the Golden Temple complex. Operation Blue Star succeeded militarily but at a heavy human and symbolic cost — the damage to the Akal Takht wounded Sikh sentiment deeply.

Fig 10.1 — The Punjab crisis, from a political resolution to national tragedy.
  • Objective: remove Bhindranwale and armed militants and restore state authority over the shrine.
  • Cost: heavy casualties among militants, soldiers and pilgrims; severe damage to the Akal Takht; deep and lasting alienation among Sikhs.
  • Aftermath: mutinies in some army units; the operation became the immediate provocation for the assassination.
  • Command & toll: led by Lt Gen K.S. Sundarji & Maj Gen K.S. Brar (9 Inf Div); Bhindranwale was killed on 6 Jun 1984. The official White Paper counted 83 army dead and 493 others killed; rural Operation Woodrose followed.
Balance: Present Blue Star as a decision of last resort with grave consequences — acknowledging both the state’s duty to restore order and the profound hurt to a community. Avoid taking sides.

8. Assassination & the 1984 riots

On 31 October 1984 Indira Gandhi was assassinated by two of her own bodyguards, in retaliation for Blue Star. Her death was followed by horrific anti-Sikh violence, especially in Delhi — one of the darkest episodes in independent India’s history.

  • The assassination: Indira Gandhi was shot at her residence; Rajiv Gandhi was sworn in as Prime Minister the same day.
  • Anti-Sikh riots: organised mobs killed thousands of Sikhs in Delhi and elsewhere over the following days; the state’s failure to protect citizens was a grave institutional lapse.
  • Accountability: multiple commissions (Ranganath Misra, later Nanavati) examined the violence; justice was slow and incomplete, a wound that persisted for decades.
  • The toll: the Misra Commission recorded 2,733 Sikhs killed in Delhi (~3,350 nationwide); the Nanavati Commission named Congress figures Sajjan Kumar, Jagdish Tytler & H.K.L. Bhagat — Sajjan Kumar was given a life sentence only in 2018.
GS-I hook: The 1984 riots are a case study in the failure of the state’s protective duty. Treat the loss of life with gravity and use it to discuss accountability, not to assign collective blame.

9. The wider crisis of the early 1980s

Punjab was not the only front. The early 1980s saw a broader wave of regional assertion that tested the federal system — a context that helps explain the period’s instability.

FrontGrievance
AssamThe anti-foreigner agitation over illegal migration (1979–85), culminating in the Assam Accord (1985).
North-EastInsurgencies and statehood demands across the region.
Andhra PradeshThe rise of the Telugu Desam (N.T. Rama Rao, 1983) on regional pride against central “interference.”
Centre–StateGrowing resentment at the misuse of Article 356 to dismiss opposition state governments.
Link it: The common thread is a Centre struggling to accommodate regional aspirations within a highly centralised framework — the structural backdrop to both Punjab and Assam.

10. Federal lessons & the Sarkaria Commission

The crises of the early 1980s forced a rethink of Centre–State relations. In 1983 the government appointed the Sarkaria Commission to review the working of federalism — a direct institutional response to the era’s tensions.

  • Mandate: examine the balance of powers between the Union and the states and recommend reforms.
  • Key recommendations (reported 1988): restraint in using Article 356, appointment of Governors in consultation with states, and a permanent Inter-State Council.
  • Legacy: the Inter-State Council was set up in 1990; the S.R. Bommai case (1994) later put teeth into limits on Article 356.
GS-II crossover: Use the Sarkaria Commission and Bommai as the constructive counterpoint to the era’s conflicts — the system learning to accommodate diversity through institutional reform.

11. Assessment

The period 1977–84 is a study in the costs of centralisation and the mishandling of legitimate regional demands. The Janata collapse and the Punjab tragedy both flowed from the same failure to build durable, consensual institutions.

  • On Janata: its failure showed that anti-incumbency alone cannot sustain a government; a coalition needs a shared programme, which it never had.
  • On Punjab: the tragedy demonstrated the danger of using identity for short-term political gain and of neglecting federal grievances until they turn violent.
  • On the state: the 1984 riots exposed the fragility of the state’s protective duty when politics inflames communal passion.
Assessment frame: The balanced verdict: legitimate autonomy demands deserved a political settlement; the failure to provide one, compounded by cynical politics, converted a solvable dispute into a decade of bloodshed.

12. Towards resolution: the road to peace

The conflict did not end in 1984. The following years saw both continued violence and repeated attempts at a political settlement, culminating in the eventual restoration of peace by the mid-1990s.

StepDetail
Rajiv–Longowal Accord (1985)An agreement with Akali leader Sant Harchand Singh Longowal on Chandigarh and river waters; Longowal was assassinated soon after.
President’s Rule & militancyPunjab remained under central control through much of the late 1980s amid continuing violence.
Restoration of normalcyBy the early-to-mid 1990s, a combination of policing, political engagement and public exhaustion with violence restored peace.
One-line frame: Punjab’s return to normalcy is an important, hopeful coda — proof that even a deep insurgency can be overcome by a mix of security action and political accommodation.

13. Legacy

The events of 1977–84 left a lasting imprint on Indian federalism, party politics and the debate over identity and the state.

  • Federal reform: the Sarkaria Commission, the Inter-State Council and Bommai tightened Centre–State norms.
  • Coalition lesson: Janata’s collapse shaped how later coalitions (1989, 1996) were built and understood.
  • Identity & the state: Punjab became the reference case for the dangers of politicising religion and the importance of timely political settlement.
  • Justice & memory: the long, incomplete pursuit of accountability for 1984 remains a live issue in Indian public life.
GS-I hook: The redemptive thread is federal maturation — India emerged from the crisis with stronger institutional norms for managing diversity, even as the human costs were never fully redressed.

14. Contemporary relevance

The legacies of this period remain visible in current debates on federalism, river-water sharing and accountability.

  • SYL canal: the Sutlej-Yamuna Link dispute between Punjab and Haryana continues to reach the Supreme Court — a living remnant of the 1980s grievances.
  • Federal debates: the roles of the Governor and Article 356 remain contested in Centre–State relations, echoing the Sarkaria concerns.
  • 1984 accountability: convictions in some anti-Sikh riot cases in recent years show the long tail of the pursuit of justice.
Answer value-add: Linking the SYL dispute or the Governor’s-role debate to the 1980s roots demonstrates that you see the continuity between history and current affairs.

15. Prelims practice (PYQ pattern)

The questions below follow the UPSC prelims pattern for this theme. Self-authored practice questions are marked as model questions with no fixed year; only genuinely dated UPSC questions carry a year.

Q1. The Janata government (1977–79) fell primarily over which issue?

  • Select the correct option.
  • (a) River-water sharing
  • (b) The “dual membership” controversy — correct
  • (c) The nationalisation of banks
  • (d) The abolition of privy purses

Answer: (b) The “dual membership” controversy. Socialists demanded that former Jana Sangh members sever RSS ties; the dispute broke the coalition.

Q2. Consider the following statements about the Anandpur Sahib Resolution:

  1. It was adopted by the Akali Dal in 1973.
  2. It demanded greater autonomy for Punjab within the Indian Union.
  3. It was a resolution of the Indian National Congress.
  • Which of the statements given above are correct?
  • (a) 1 and 2 only — correct
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Answer: (a) 1 and 2 only. The Anandpur Sahib Resolution (1973) was an Akali Dal — not Congress — document demanding greater state autonomy.

Q3. Operation Blue Star was conducted in which year and location?

  • Select the correct option.
  • (a) 1983, Chandigarh
  • (b) 1984, the Golden Temple complex, Amritsar — correct
  • (c) 1985, Ludhiana
  • (d) 1982, Jalandhar

Answer: (b) 1984, the Golden Temple complex, Amritsar. The army operation in June 1984 aimed to remove armed militants from the Harmandir Sahib complex.

Q4. Which of the following pairs is/are correctly matched?

  1. Charan Singh — PM who resigned without facing a floor test.
  2. Sarkaria Commission — Centre–State relations.
  3. Rajiv–Longowal Accord — Assam.
  • Select the correct answer using the code below.
  • (a) 1 and 2 only — correct
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Answer: (a) 1 and 2 only. The Rajiv–Longowal Accord (1985) concerned Punjab, not Assam (that was the Assam Accord) — so pair 3 is wrong.

Q5. The Sarkaria Commission (1983) was set up to examine:

  • Select the correct option.
  • (a) Electoral reforms
  • (b) Centre–State relations — correct
  • (c) Administrative corruption
  • (d) Reorganisation of states

Answer: (b) Centre–State relations. It recommended restraint on Article 356, consultation on Governors and a permanent Inter-State Council.

Q6. Arrange the following in correct chronological order:

  1. Indira Gandhi’s return to power
  2. Operation Blue Star
  3. Collapse of the Janata government
  4. Rajiv–Longowal Accord
  • Select the correct chronological sequence.
  • (a) 1–3–2–4
  • (b) 3–1–2–4 — correct
  • (c) 3–2–1–4
  • (d) 1–2–3–4

Answer: (b) 3–1–2–4. Janata collapse (1979) → Indira’s return (1980) → Blue Star (Jun 1984) → Rajiv–Longowal Accord (1985).

16. Mains practice + model answers

Model questions in the UPSC GS-I / GS-II idiom, each with a full answer skeleton. These are practice questions with no fixed year unless a genuine dated UPSC question is cited.

Q1. The Punjab crisis of the 1980s was rooted more in federal and economic grievances than in religion. Critically examine. (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Frame the crisis as a case study in the mismanagement of legitimate regional demands, not as an inherently religious conflict.
  2. The federal-economic roots: river-water sharing (Ravi–Beas, SYL), the Chandigarh dispute, and the Green Revolution’s uneven benefits and rural youth unemployment.
  3. The autonomy demand (the core): the Anandpur Sahib Resolution framed a case for greater state powers within the Union — a federal, not secessionist, demand at its origin.
  4. How it turned religious/militant: the failure of negotiation, cynical political cultivation of Bhindranwale, and the drift to violence gave the movement a communal idiom.
  5. Critical edge: acknowledge the genuine religious dimension too — but insist that treating it as purely religious obscures the solvable federal grievances that were neglected.
  6. Conclusion: the lesson is the primacy of timely political settlement of federal demands; the Sarkaria Commission and later peace show the constructive path not taken early enough.

Q2. Account for the collapse of the Janata government and its significance for coalition politics in India. (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Situate the Janata Party (1977) as India’s first non-Congress government and a coalition of ideological opposites.
  2. Immediate cause: the “dual membership” dispute over Jana Sangh–RSS ties fractured the coalition.
  3. Deeper causes (the core): no shared programme beyond anti-Indira feeling; competing prime-ministerial ambitions (Desai, Charan Singh, Jagjivan Ram); an inability to institutionalise power-sharing.
  4. The collapse: Charan Singh’s brief ministry fell without a floor test; fresh elections restored Indira Gandhi in 1980.
  5. Critical edge: the episode showed that anti-incumbency is a weak foundation for governance — a lesson repeated in 1989–91.
  6. Conclusion: Janata’s failure shaped the design of later, more durable coalitions (common minimum programmes, coordination committees), making it a formative if unhappy experiment.

Q3. “The events of the early 1980s forced India to rethink Centre–State relations.” Discuss with reference to the Sarkaria Commission. (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Link the wave of regional assertion (Punjab, Assam, Andhra) to a crisis of over-centralised federalism.
  2. The tensions: misuse of Article 356 to dismiss opposition governments, the Governor’s partisan role, and unmet regional demands.
  3. The Sarkaria response (the core): the 1983 Commission recommended restraint on Article 356, consultative appointment of Governors and a permanent Inter-State Council — institutionalising cooperative federalism.
  4. Follow-through: the Inter-State Council (1990) and the S.R. Bommai judgment (1994) gave legal shape to the reforms.
  5. Critical edge: many Sarkaria recommendations remain only partly implemented; the Governor’s role is still contested — the rethink is unfinished.
  6. Conclusion: the early-1980s crises catalysed a durable shift toward cooperative federalism, even if the agenda it set is still a work in progress.

Q4. The 1984 anti-Sikh riots represent a failure of the state’s protective duty. Examine the institutional lessons. (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Frame the violence that followed Indira Gandhi’s assassination (31 Oct 1984) as a grave breakdown of the state’s duty to protect its citizens.
  2. What failed: the machinery of law and order in Delhi did not act to stop organised mobs; some in authority were complicit or negligent.
  3. The accountability record (the core): successive commissions (Ranganath Misra, Nanavati) documented the violence, but justice was slow and incomplete for decades.
  4. Institutional lessons: the need for professional, politically insulated policing; swift command responsibility; and independent, time-bound inquiry mechanisms.
  5. Critical edge: stress that accountability is about institutional reform and justice for victims — never collective blame of a community.
  6. Conclusion: 1984 remains a benchmark against which the state’s duty to protect and to deliver justice must be measured.
