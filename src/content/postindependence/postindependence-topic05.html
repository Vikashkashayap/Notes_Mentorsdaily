Chapter 5: Achievements of the Nehruvian Era, Foreign Policy & the Era of War

The first seventeen years of the republic (1947–64) carried the imprint of one man — Jawaharlal Nehru — who built the institutions of a modern state, gave India a distinctive voice in the world, and led it through three wars. This file covers the achievements of the Nehruvian era (Planning & Five-Year Plans, PSUs, IITs, atomic energy, the "temples of modern India"), the philosophy & instruments of foreign policy (non-alignment, Panchsheel, NAM, Bandung 1955, decolonisation), and the era of war (the 1947–48 Kashmir war, the 1962 India–China war, the 1965 India–Pakistan war) — with tables, flowcharts and PYQ-pattern practice + full model answers.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames the Nehruvian Era

This chapter is tested as the foundation-laying phase of the republic — how India built a modern state, economy & foreign policy, and what the early wars taught it. Sort your prep:

  • Static/institutional — the machinery Nehru built: the Planning Commission (1950) & Five-Year Plans, the Mahalanobis (heavy-industry) model, PSUs (Bhilai, Bhakra-Nangal), the IITs, atomic energy (Bhabha), and foreign-policy instruments (Panchsheel 1954, Bandung 1955, NAM 1961). High-yield recall.
  • The wars — the 1947–48 Kashmir war (accession, UN, ceasefire line), the 1962 India–China war (the great reversal), and the 1965 India–Pakistan war (Tashkent). Dates, causes, outcomes.
  • Analytical (GS-I) — balancing Nehru's achievements (democracy, institutions, non-alignment) against failures (1962 defeat, economic under-performance) into a judged verdict. Needs idea + example + judgement.

Territorial note: Maps in this file show India with its full official boundaries — Jammu & Kashmir & Ladakh (including PoK & Aksai Chin) are integral parts of India; Aksai Chin & PoK are shown as Indian territory under illegal occupation.

1. Overview: The Nehruvian Consensus

For its first seventeen years, independent India was shaped by a broad set of ideas historians call the "Nehruvian consensus" — a package of commitments that the political class largely shared, and which set the direction of the new republic.

  • Democracy & a strong Constitution: universal adult franchise, regular elections, civil liberties & an independent judiciary — sustained even amid poverty & illiteracy.
  • Planned, mixed economy: a large public sector & state-led planning alongside a private sector — "the commanding heights" in state hands.
  • Secularism: equal respect for all religions & a state distanced from any one faith — the answer to Partition.
  • Non-alignment: an independent foreign policy refusing to join either Cold-War bloc.
  • Scientific temper & self-reliance: faith in science, heavy industry & institution-building as the route to modernity.
One-line frame: the Nehruvian era laid the institutional foundations of modern India — democratic, planned, secular & non-aligned — whose successes and limits still frame national debate.

2. Planning & the Mixed Economy

At the heart of Nehruvian nation-building was economic planning — the belief that a poor, newly independent country had to direct its scarce resources rather than leave development to the market alone.

InstrumentDetail
Planning Commission (1950)Set up by executive resolution, chaired by the PM, to formulate Five-Year Plans — the engine of state-led development (replaced by NITI Aayog in 2015).
First Plan (1951–56)Based on the Harrod-Domar model; priority to agriculture & irrigation (Bhakra-Nangal), post-Partition rehabilitation. Broadly successful.
Second Plan (1956–61)The Mahalanobis model — priority to heavy industry & capital goods; the Industrial Policy Resolution 1956 reserved key sectors for the state.
Mixed economyA large public sector held the "commanding heights" (steel, power, heavy machinery) while a regulated private sector operated alongside — neither pure capitalism nor Soviet-style command economy.
GS-I hook: the Mahalanobis strategy (heavy industry first) built an industrial base & self-reliance, but its neglect of agriculture, consumer goods & exports sowed the later problems of shortages & the "Hindu rate of growth" — the standard critical edge.

3. "Temples of Modern India": PSUs, Science & Institutions

Nehru called the great public projects the "temples of modern India" — the visible monuments of self-reliant development. This institution-building is his most enduring legacy.

  • Steel & heavy industry: public-sector steel plants at Bhilai, Rourkela & Durgapur (with Soviet, German & British help); heavy engineering (HEC Ranchi, BHEL).
  • Multipurpose river projects: Bhakra-Nangal, Hirakud, Damodar Valley — dams for irrigation, power & flood control.
  • Science & technology: the IITs (from 1951), CSIR labs, and the atomic energy programme under Homi Bhabha (AEC 1948, Apsara reactor 1956); the space programme's seeds (Sarabhai).
  • Higher education & research: UGC, national laboratories, and institutions that produced the scientific & technical manpower of later decades.
  • Financial institutions: nationalisation of the Imperial Bank into the State Bank of India (1955), LIC (1956), and development finance bodies.
  • Science firsts (datable): the Apsara reactor at Trombay went critical on 4 Aug 1956 — Asia's first nuclear reactor; the space programme began with INCOSPAR (1962) under Vikram Sarabhai and the first rocket launch from Thumba (TERLS) on 21 Nov 1963.
One-line frame: the "temples of modern India" gave the country an industrial, scientific & educational base it lacked in 1947 — the platform on which every later economic transformation was built.

4. Social & Democratic Achievements

Beyond steel & dams, the Nehruvian state made foundational political & social gains that are easy to take for granted today.

AreaAchievement
Democracy institutionalisedUniversal adult franchise from the first general election (1951–52); a free press, opposition & independent Election Commission — democracy survived where many new nations lapsed into dictatorship.
Social reformThe Hindu Code Bills (1955–56) reformed marriage, divorce, succession & adoption, advancing women's rights — a landmark despite conservative resistance.
Secular fabricA constitutionally secular state that protected minorities in the shadow of Partition.
Federal accommodationLinguistic reorganisation (1956) accommodated diversity — strengthening, not splitting, the union.
GS-I hook: the survival & deepening of democracy in a poor, illiterate, plural society is arguably Nehru's greatest achievement — a counter-example to the post-colonial slide into authoritarianism elsewhere.

5. Foreign Policy: Philosophy & Determinants

Nehru was his own foreign minister, and India's diplomacy bore his stamp. Understand why India chose the path it did before learning its instruments.

DeterminantHow it shaped policy
Anti-colonial legacyA freedom struggle produced solidarity with decolonising Asia & Africa and a moral, principled diplomatic voice.
Poverty & development needA poor country needed peace & aid from both blocs — making non-alignment practical, not merely idealistic.
Cold-War contextRefusing to be a satellite of either superpower preserved strategic autonomy & bargaining room.
Idealism + realismNehru blended Gandhian moral internationalism (peace, disarmament) with hard national interest — a balance later criticised as tilting too far toward idealism.
One-line frame: Indian foreign policy was born of anti-colonial idealism married to developmental realism — independence in judgement, peace for development, solidarity with the newly free.

6. Non-Alignment & Panchsheel

The two signature ideas of Nehruvian diplomacy were non-alignment (independence from the blocs) and Panchsheel (the code of peaceful coexistence). Learn both precisely.

  • Non-alignment did not mean neutrality or isolation; it meant judging each issue on its merits and refusing permanent military alliance with either the US-led or Soviet-led bloc — preserving strategic autonomy.
  • Panchsheel (1954): the Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence, enunciated in the 1954 India–China (Tibet trade) agreement: (1) mutual respect for territorial integrity & sovereignty; (2) mutual non-aggression; (3) mutual non-interference; (4) equality & mutual benefit; (5) peaceful coexistence.
  • "Hindi-Chini Bhai-Bhai": the slogan of the mid-1950s captured the optimism of Panchsheel — brutally falsified by the 1962 war.
  • Precise anchor: Panchsheel was enunciated in the Sino-Indian Agreement on Trade & Intercourse between the Tibet Region of China and India, signed at Beijing on 29 April 1954 — by which India accepted Tibet as part of China.
  • Indus Waters Treaty (19 Sep 1960): signed by Nehru & Ayub Khan, mediated by the World Bank — eastern rivers (Sutlej, Beas, Ravi) to India, western rivers (Indus, Jhelum, Chenab) largely to Pakistan; the Permanent Indus Commission has survived three wars.
GS-I hook: Panchsheel gave India moral prestige but rested on trust that China did not share — the 1962 betrayal is the classic case of principles unbacked by preparedness (the standard critical edge on Nehruvian diplomacy).

7. Bandung, NAM & Afro-Asian Solidarity

Non-alignment matured into a global movement. Two milestones anchor it in the exam.

Fig 5.1 — Panchsheel (1954) → Bandung (1955) → NAM founded at Belgrade (1961). Schematic timeline.
  • Bandung Conference (1955): the Afro-Asian Conference in Indonesia — 29 newly independent nations affirmed anti-colonialism, sovereignty & the Panchsheel principles; Nehru was a leading voice. The ideological seed of NAM.
  • Non-Aligned Movement (1961): formally founded at the Belgrade Summit by Nehru (India), Nasser (Egypt), Tito (Yugoslavia) & Sukarno (Indonesia) — a bloc of states refusing Cold-War alignment.
  • India's leadership: gave a newly free country outsized diplomatic weight & a voice for decolonisation, disarmament & the Global South.
  • Bandung detail: hosted by Sukarno (Indonesia), 18–24 Apr 1955; attended by Nehru, Nasser, Zhou Enlai, Ho Chi Minh & Tito (observer); it adopted the Ten Principles of Bandung (Dasasila Bandung), incorporating Panchsheel plus respect for human rights and peaceful settlement of disputes.
  • NAM founders (the "five fathers"): Nehru (India), Tito (Yugoslavia), Nasser (Egypt), Sukarno (Indonesia) & Nkrumah (Ghana); first summit at Belgrade, 1–6 Sep 1961, with 25 founding members.
Prelims trap: Panchsheel 1954, Bandung 1955, NAM (Belgrade) 1961; NAM's founding quartet = Nehru, Nasser, Tito, Sukarno. Bandung = Afro-Asian (Indonesia); NAM's first summit = Belgrade.

8. The 1947–48 Kashmir War

India's first war came within weeks of independence — over the accession of Jammu & Kashmir.

  • Trigger: in October 1947 Pakistan-backed tribal raiders invaded J&K; Maharaja Hari Singh sought Indian help.
  • Accession: he signed the Instrument of Accession (26 October 1947), making J&K legally part of India; Indian troops were airlifted to Srinagar.
  • The war & UN: Indian forces pushed the raiders back; Nehru referred the matter to the UN Security Council (1948). A ceasefire (1 January 1949) froze the line — leaving Pakistan in illegal occupation of what India calls Pakistan-occupied Kashmir (PoK).
  • Legacy: the ceasefire line (later Line of Control) & the unfinished status of PoK became a permanent Indo-Pak flashpoint.
GS-I hook: J&K's accession was legally complete via the Instrument of Accession; PoK is Indian territory under illegal Pakistani occupation. The reference to the UN is often criticised as internationalising a domestic legal matter.

9. The 1962 India–China War

The gravest reversal of the Nehruvian era was the 1962 border war with China — a shock that shattered the Panchsheel optimism and dented Nehru's stature.

AspectDetail
RootsDisputed boundaries — the McMahon Line in the east (NEFA) & Aksai Chin in the west; China's occupation of Tibet (1950) & the road through Aksai Chin; asylum to the Dalai Lama (1959).
"Forward Policy"India set up posts up to its claimed line; China responded with a massive assault on 20 October 1962 in both NEFA & Ladakh.
CourseIndian forces, unprepared & ill-equipped for high-altitude war, were overrun in NEFA; China declared a unilateral ceasefire (21 Nov 1962) and withdrew in the east but retained Aksai Chin.
ConsequencesNational humiliation; the fall of Defence Minister Krishna Menon; a shaken Nehru (died 1964); the start of serious military modernisation; a lasting India–China trust deficit.
Rezang La (18 Nov 1962)Major Shaitan Singh and 114 of his men of 'C' Company, 13 Kumaon, died defending the pass at ~17,000 ft in Ladakh; Shaitan Singh was awarded a posthumous Param Vir Chakra — the enduring symbol of Indian valour in the 1962 war.
Costs & inquiryIndia lost ~1,383 killed and de-facto ~38,000 sq km in Aksai Chin; Defence Minister V.K. Krishna Menon resigned (replaced by Y.B. Chavan). The internal Henderson Brooks–Bhagat Report (1963) on the debacle remains officially classified.
One-line frame: 1962 is the classic lesson that idealism must be backed by capability — Panchsheel without military preparedness left India exposed. Aksai Chin remains Indian territory under Chinese occupation.

10. The 1965 India–Pakistan War

Under Nehru's successor Lal Bahadur Shastri, India fought & held its own against Pakistan — restoring confidence after 1962.

  • Trigger: Pakistan's Operation Gibraltar (1965) — infiltrators sent into J&K to spark a revolt — escalated into full war when India crossed the international border toward Lahore.
  • Course: hard fighting across Punjab & the Rann of Kutch; the war ended in a military stalemate but a moral boost for India.
  • Tashkent Agreement (1966): brokered by the Soviet Union; India & Pakistan agreed to withdraw to pre-war positions. Shastri died in Tashkent the next day.
  • "Jai Jawan Jai Kisan": Shastri's slogan linked the soldier & the farmer — national security & food self-sufficiency — the twin priorities after 1962–65.
GS-I hook: 1965 restored military self-belief after 1962 & showed the Soviet Union emerging as India's key partner (Tashkent) — a step toward the strategic tilt formalised in the 1971 treaty.

11. Assessment: Successes & Failures

The mature verdict on the Nehruvian era holds achievements & failures together — the model of a balanced Mains answer.

AchievementsFailures / criticisms
Entrenched democracy, secularism & a strong Constitution.The 1962 China debacle — strategic naivety & military unpreparedness.
Built an industrial, scientific & institutional base ("temples of modern India").Slow growth ("Hindu rate of growth"), neglected agriculture (till the Green Revolution) & consumer goods.
Gave India global stature via non-alignment, Bandung & NAM.Over-idealism in foreign policy; the licence-permit raj bred inefficiency.
Accommodated diversity (linguistic states, federalism).Left the Kashmir question unresolved; social reform incomplete.

Scholars such as Ramachandra Guha and Bipan Chandra stress that, whatever its economic limits, the era's greatest gift was a working democracy & a united nation — achievements too easily discounted in hindsight.

One-line frame: Nehru's balance sheet is positive on nation-building & democracy, mixed on the economy, and negative on 1962 — a founder who got the foundations right even where policies faltered.

12. The Nehruvian Legacy Today

The debates the Nehruvian era began are still live — useful for adding contemporary depth to an answer.

  • Institutions endure: IITs, PSUs, ISRO's & DAE's ancestry, the Election Commission & a planning tradition (now NITI Aayog) — the scaffolding of the modern state.
  • Strategic autonomy: non-alignment has evolved into "multi-alignment" & "strategic autonomy" — the same instinct of independence in a multipolar world.
  • China lesson institutionalised: post-1962 military modernisation & border infrastructure remain central to policy after Galwan-era tensions.
  • Economic re-appraisal: 1991 liberalisation dismantled the licence-raj, but the debate over state vs market that Nehru began continues.
GS-I / GS-II hook: frame today's "strategic autonomy" & self-reliance (Atmanirbhar Bharat) as the descendants of Nehruvian non-alignment & self-reliant planning — continuity beneath the change.

13. Current Affairs Link

Connect the chapter to recent developments to signal an updated preparation.

  • Strategic autonomy & multi-alignment: India's balancing of the Quad, Russia & the Global South echoes non-alignment's core logic.
  • Border infrastructure & the China lesson: post-Galwan focus on Himalayan roads, tunnels & military modernisation traces directly to 1962.
  • NAM's relevance debate: periodic discussion of whether NAM still matters in a multipolar, post-Cold-War world.
  • PSU disinvestment & NITI Aayog: the winding back of the Nehruvian public sector keeps the state-vs-market debate topical.
How to use in the exam: a line linking non-alignment to today's "strategic autonomy", or 1962 to current border policy, shows the examiner the Nehruvian era as living policy DNA, not closed history.

14. Prelims PYQs & Practice

Note on sourcing: the questions below are exam-pattern practice MCQs built strictly on this chapter's high-yield facts, deliberately left yearless (labelled "Model question") and not attributed to any fabricated year. Use them for concept-testing.

Model question — no fixed year

The Second Five-Year Plan was based on the growth model developed by:

  • (a) Harrod and Domar
  • (b) P.C. Mahalanobis
  • (c) Vakil and Brahmananda
  • (d) Gadgil

Answer: (b). The Second Plan (1956–61) used the Mahalanobis model prioritising heavy & capital-goods industry. The First Plan drew on the Harrod-Domar model.

Model question — no fixed year

The Panchsheel principles were first formally enunciated in the agreement of 1954 between India and:

  • (a) The Soviet Union
  • (b) China (on Tibet)
  • (c) Egypt
  • (d) Yugoslavia

Answer: (b). The Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence (Panchsheel) were set out in the 1954 India–China agreement on trade with the Tibet region of China.

Model question — no fixed year

Consider the following about the Non-Aligned Movement:
1. It was formally founded at the Belgrade Summit of 1961.
2. Nehru, Nasser and Tito were among its founders.
3. The Bandung Conference of 1955 preceded it.
Which are correct?

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1, 2 and 3
  • (d) 1 and 3 only

Answer: (c). NAM was founded at Belgrade (1961) by Nehru, Nasser, Tito & Sukarno; the Bandung Afro-Asian Conference (1955) was its ideological precursor.

Model question — no fixed year

The 1962 war with China resulted in China retaining control of which region?

  • (a) NEFA (Arunachal Pradesh)
  • (b) Aksai Chin
  • (c) Sikkim
  • (d) The Chumbi Valley

Answer: (b). China withdrew from NEFA in the east but retained Aksai Chin in the west — Indian territory under Chinese occupation.

Model question — no fixed year

The Tashkent Agreement (1966) that ended the 1965 India–Pakistan war was brokered by:

  • (a) The United States
  • (b) The United Nations
  • (c) The Soviet Union
  • (d) The United Kingdom

Answer: (c). The Tashkent Agreement was brokered by the Soviet Union; Prime Minister Lal Bahadur Shastri died in Tashkent immediately after signing it.

Model question — no fixed year

"Jai Jawan Jai Kisan" was the slogan associated with which Prime Minister?

  • (a) Jawaharlal Nehru
  • (b) Lal Bahadur Shastri
  • (c) Indira Gandhi
  • (d) Morarji Desai

Answer: (b). Lal Bahadur Shastri gave the slogan "Jai Jawan Jai Kisan" during the 1965 war & food crisis, linking the soldier & the farmer.

15. Mains PYQs — Model Answers

Note on sourcing: the questions below are exam-pattern practice questions on this chapter's core analytical themes (left yearless, not attributed to a fabricated year), each with a full answer skeleton.

Model question — no fixed year

"The Nehruvian era laid the institutional foundations of modern India, but its economic and strategic choices carried heavy costs." Critically examine. (250 words) 15 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: The first seventeen years built the scaffolding of a modern, democratic, planned republic — but the same idealism that succeeded in nation-building faltered in economics & the 1962 war.
  2. Institutional achievements: entrenched democracy & secularism; the Planning Commission, PSUs, IITs, atomic energy — the "temples of modern India".
  3. Foreign-policy stature (the core): non-alignment, Panchsheel, Bandung & NAM gave a poor new nation a global voice & strategic autonomy.
  4. The economic cost: the heavy-industry (Mahalanobis) tilt neglected agriculture & consumer goods, yielding shortages & the "Hindu rate of growth".
  5. Critical edge: the 1962 China debacle exposed idealism unbacked by capability — the era's gravest failure.
  6. Conclusion: the foundations were sound & durable; the flaws were of policy, not of purpose — a founder who got the essentials right.
Model question — no fixed year

Examine the philosophy of non-alignment and assess its relevance to India then and now. (250 words) 15 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Non-alignment was not neutrality but the assertion of independent judgement in the Cold War — peace for development & solidarity with the newly free.
  2. Philosophy: rooted in anti-colonialism, developmental need & strategic autonomy; expressed through Panchsheel, Bandung & NAM.
  3. Contemporary gains (the core): gave a poor nation global weight, aid from both blocs & a leadership role in the Global South.
  4. Limits: moralism sometimes outran power (1962); critics called it drift; the Cold-War frame has since dissolved.
  5. Critical edge / today: the instinct survives as "strategic autonomy" & "multi-alignment" — balancing the Quad, Russia & the Global South.
  6. Conclusion: the label has aged but the logic endures — independence of judgement remains India's diplomatic core.
Model question — no fixed year

What lessons did the 1962 India–China war hold for India's security and foreign policy? (150 words) 10 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: The 1962 defeat was a national trauma that shattered Panchsheel optimism and forced a hard-headed reappraisal of security.
  2. Cause of reversal: military unpreparedness, poor intelligence & the "Forward Policy" without capability — idealism outrunning strength.
  3. Lessons (the core): diplomacy must be backed by defence; began serious military modernisation, mountain forces & border infrastructure.
  4. Strategic shift: a more realist, less trusting China policy & eventual tilt toward the Soviet Union.
  5. Critical edge: the lesson recurs — post-Galwan border investment is its direct descendant.
  6. Conclusion: 1962 taught that principles unbacked by preparedness invite aggression — a permanent maxim of Indian security.
Model question — no fixed year

Discuss the significance of planning and the public sector in the making of modern India's economy. (150 words) 10 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: In a poor, capital-scarce economy, Nehruvian India chose state-led planning & a large public sector to build an industrial base the market could not.
  2. The design: the Planning Commission & Five-Year Plans; the Mahalanobis heavy-industry strategy; PSUs holding the "commanding heights".
  3. Achievements (the core): steel, power, heavy engineering, atomic energy & a technical-education base — self-reliance & the platform for later growth.
  4. Costs: neglect of agriculture & consumer goods, the licence-permit raj, inefficiency & slow growth.
  5. Critical edge: 1991 liberalisation corrected the excesses but built on the base planning had created.
  6. Conclusion: planning laid indispensable foundations even as its methods later needed reform — a necessary first stage, not a permanent model.
