The Rajiv Gandhi Years & the Assam Crisis (1984–89)

How a young Prime Minister with a record mandate tried to modernise India through technology and accords — and how scandal and unfulfilled promise brought him down by 1989.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames the Rajiv Years

The Rajiv Gandhi era is tested as a study in modernisation and its limits — a reformist impulse (technology, accords, decentralisation) colliding with old political compulsions (vote-bank management, corruption). Sort your prep into three buckets:

  • Static / factual recall — the 1984 landslide, the 52nd Amendment (anti-defection), the Assam and Mizo accords (both 1985–86), the technology missions, Shah Bano, Bofors. Names, years, instruments.
  • Governance / reform architecture — how Rajiv tried to modernise the state: telecom (Sam Pitroda’s C-DOT), computerisation, the push for panchayati raj that later became the 73rd/74th Amendments.
  • Analytical (GS-I) — the paradox of a reformer undone by the compromises of power: Shah Bano, the Ayodhya shilanyas and Bofors as symbols of drift. Idea + example + judgement.

Framing note: Present Rajiv Gandhi with balance — genuine modernising vision on one side, political missteps and scandal on the other. Avoid hagiography or dismissal.

1. Setting: the 1984 mandate

Rajiv Gandhi became Prime Minister on the day his mother was assassinated (31 October 1984). In the December 1984 elections, riding a sympathy wave and a promise of clean, modern governance, the Congress won its largest-ever Lok Sabha majority — over 400 seats. No Indian government before or since has had so free a hand.

Feature of 1984Detail
MandateCongress won 414 Lok Sabha seats — a record majority.
Leader’s imageA young, non-political “Mr Clean” promising to take India into the 21st century.
Political capitalAn unprecedented opportunity to legislate and reform without obstruction.
ShadowThe mandate rested partly on the tragedy and turmoil of 1984, including the anti-Sikh violence.
GS-I hook: The 1984 mandate is the setup for the chapter’s central irony — the largest majority in Indian history achieved surprisingly little lasting political dominance, undone by drift and scandal.

2. The modernisation agenda

Rajiv Gandhi’s core project was to modernise India — to move it from a slow, licence-bound, inward-looking economy and administration toward technology, efficiency and youth. This impulse, more than any single policy, defines his tenure.

  • Technology first: a belief that computers, telecom and science could leapfrog development bottlenecks.
  • Cautious liberalisation: early steps to ease licensing, cut some import duties and encourage private enterprise — a precursor to 1991.
  • New generation: a cohort of young advisers and technocrats brought in to shake up governance.
  • Decentralisation: a conviction that democracy had to reach the village — the seed of panchayati raj reform.
  • Also under Rajiv: the New Education Policy 1986 (Operation Blackboard, Navodaya Vidyalayas), IGNOU (1985), and the 61st Amendment (1989) lowering the voting age from 21 to 18.
One-line frame: Rajiv Gandhi was the bridge between the licence-permit economy and the 1991 reforms — his instincts pointed forward even when his politics pulled back.

3. Technology & telecom missions

The most enduring legacy of the Rajiv years is the technology push. Working with Sam Pitroda, the government launched a set of “technology missions” and transformed telecommunications — laying groundwork that would pay off for decades.

InitiativeWhat it did
C-DOT (1984)The Centre for Development of Telematics — indigenous digital telecom switching, enabling rural telephony.
Public call offices (PCOs)The yellow STD/PCO booths that spread telephony across small towns and villages.
Technology MissionsFive missions — drinking water, literacy, immunisation, oilseeds and telecom — mission-mode delivery of public goods.
ComputerisationIntroduction of computers in railways (passenger reservation), banks and administration.
MTNL (1986)Set up to modernise telephone services in Delhi and Mumbai.
GS-I hook: When asked for Rajiv Gandhi’s positive legacy, lead with the technology missions and telecom — concrete, lasting achievements that seeded India’s later IT and connectivity revolution.

4. The anti-defection law

Using his massive majority, Rajiv Gandhi enacted the 52nd Amendment (1985), adding the Tenth Schedule to the Constitution — the anti-defection law — to curb the “Aya Ram Gaya Ram” culture of legislators switching sides for office.

  • Core provision: a legislator who defects (voluntarily gives up party membership or defies the whip) can be disqualified.
  • Original exceptions: a “split” of one-third of a party’s members was protected — later removed by the 91st Amendment (2003), which allows only a two-thirds merger.
  • The Speaker’s role: disqualification decided by the presiding officer — a feature later criticised for partisanship.
GS-II crossover: The anti-defection law is a permanent part of the polity syllabus. Note both its aim (stability) and its critique (it can stifle legitimate dissent and concentrate power in party leaderships).

5. Panchayati raj & the reform push

Rajiv Gandhi’s conviction that development must reach the grassroots led to a strong push for local self-government. Though his own constitutional amendments (the 64th/65th) failed in the Rajya Sabha, they laid the direct groundwork for the landmark 73rd and 74th Amendments of 1992–93.

  • The idea: devolve power, funds and functions to elected panchayats and municipalities.
  • The attempt: the 64th and 65th Amendment Bills (1989) sought constitutional status for local bodies but fell short in the Upper House.
  • The payoff: the effort matured into the 73rd/74th Amendments (Narasimha Rao government), which entrenched panchayati raj and urban local bodies.
Link it: Credit Rajiv Gandhi as the political originator of constitutional panchayati raj — a reformist thread that outlived his government.

6. The politics of accords

Rajiv Gandhi’s early tenure was marked by a series of peace accords that sought to defuse the regional insurgencies inherited from the early 1980s. This “politics of accommodation” was one of his most constructive contributions.

AccordSubstance
Punjab Accord (1985)The Rajiv–Longowal Accord on Chandigarh and river waters (implementation later stalled).
Assam Accord (1985)Ended the anti-foreigner agitation; set a cut-off date for detecting illegal migrants.
Mizo Accord (1986)Ended two decades of insurgency; Mizoram became a full state; the MNF entered mainstream politics — one of India’s most successful peace settlements.
OthersEngagement in Darjeeling (Gorkhaland) and Tripura sought to address regional demands.
GS-I hook: The Mizo Accord (1986) is the model of a durable insurgency settlement — cite it as the success story of Rajiv Gandhi’s accord diplomacy.

7. The Assam crisis & the Assam Accord

The Assam Movement (1979–85) was a mass agitation, led by the All Assam Students’ Union (AASU), against the presence of “illegal foreigners” — migrants from Bangladesh — whom Assamese felt were altering the state’s demography and politics. It was largely non-violent but marred by tragedy, notably the Nellie massacre (1983).

Fig 11.1 — The Assam Movement, from agitation to accord to government.
ElementDetail
DemandDetection, deletion (from electoral rolls) and deportation of illegal migrants.
Cut-off dateThe Accord fixed 25 March 1971 as the cut-off for citizenship — those entering after are “foreigners.”
Clause 6Promised safeguards for the cultural, social and linguistic identity of the Assamese people.
Political outcomeThe Asom Gana Parishad (AGP), formed from the movement, won the 1985 state elections — Prafulla Kumar Mahanta became a very young Chief Minister.
Prelims anchor: The Assam Accord’s cut-off date of 25 March 1971 is the constitutional basis for the NRC and is very frequently tested. Distinguish it from the Rajiv–Longowal (Punjab) Accord, also 1985.

8. The Shah Bano controversy

In 1985 the Supreme Court, in Mohd. Ahmed Khan v. Shah Bano Begum, granted maintenance to a divorced Muslim woman under Section 125 CrPC. Facing protests from conservative sections, the Rajiv Gandhi government enacted the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act, 1986, effectively overriding the judgment.

  • The judgment: upheld a secular maintenance right for a divorced woman, regardless of personal law.
  • The reversal: the 1986 Act limited maintenance largely to the iddat period, seen as a concession to conservative opinion.
  • The fallout: criticised as appeasement; it damaged Rajiv Gandhi’s reformist image and became a flashpoint in the debate over a Uniform Civil Code.
GS-I hook: Shah Bano is the classic case study in the tension between secular constitutional rights and religious personal law — and in the politics of “balancing” communities. Present all sides fairly.

9. The Ayodhya turn & communal balance

Seeking to balance the perception of Muslim appeasement after Shah Bano, the government in 1986 allowed the locks of the disputed Babri Masjid–Ram Janmabhoomi site to be opened for Hindu worship. Later, the 1989 shilanyas was permitted near the site. These moves are widely seen as having inflamed communal politics.

  • The balancing act: concessions to conservative opinion on both sides — a strategy that satisfied neither and heightened tensions.
  • Consequence: the Ayodhya issue moved to the centre of national politics, feeding the mobilisation of the late 1980s and early 1990s.
Critical edge: Present this as a cautionary lesson in the risks of managing communal politics through symbolic concessions — short-term calculation with long-term costs. Keep the tone analytical, not partisan.

10. Bofors & the collapse of authority

The defining scandal of the era was Bofors — allegations that kickbacks were paid in a 1986 deal to buy Swedish artillery guns. The charge that the “Mr Clean” government was itself corrupt destroyed Rajiv Gandhi’s central political asset.

  • The allegation: commissions were allegedly paid to middlemen and politicians in the Bofors howitzer contract.
  • V.P. Singh’s exit: Finance-then-Defence Minister V.P. Singh, pursuing corruption cases, left the government and became the face of the anti-corruption opposition.
  • Political effect: Bofors became the rallying cry of the 1989 campaign; the guns themselves later performed well in Kargil (1999), an irony often noted.
  • The specifics: a ₹1,437 crore deal (24 Mar 1986) for 410 FH-77B 155 mm howitzers; kickbacks were exposed by Swedish Radio (16 Apr 1987) and traced by The Hindu to middlemen Ottavio Quattrocchi & Win Chadha.
GS-I hook: Bofors is the pivot from reformist promise to political collapse — the scandal that turned a record majority into a one-term government and launched the coalition era.

11. Foreign policy & the IPKF

Rajiv Gandhi’s foreign policy mixed idealism (nuclear disarmament) with a costly regional entanglement in Sri Lanka.

InitiativeDetail
DisarmamentAn Action Plan for a nuclear-weapons-free and non-violent world order (1988) at the UN.
Indo–Sri Lanka Accord (1987)Sent the Indian Peace Keeping Force (IPKF) to Sri Lanka to disarm the LTTE — a difficult, costly deployment.
SAARCContinued support for the fledgling regional grouping (founded 1985).
Maldives (1988)Operation Cactus — India helped foil a coup attempt, projecting regional influence.
IPKF costThe IPKF peaked at ~80,000 troops with ~1,200 killed (withdrawn Mar 1990); an LTTE bomber assassinated Rajiv Gandhi at Sriperumbudur on 21 May 1991.
Link it: The IPKF misadventure had a tragic personal coda — Rajiv Gandhi was assassinated by the LTTE in 1991. Note the connection when discussing the era’s foreign policy.

12. Assessment

The Rajiv Gandhi years are a study in squandered advantage — the largest mandate in Indian history yielding real modernisation but also political drift and disillusion.

  • Achievements: the technology and telecom revolution, the anti-defection law, durable accords (Mizo, Assam), and the seeding of panchayati raj.
  • Failures: Shah Bano and Ayodhya balancing that inflamed communalism; Bofors that destroyed credibility; unfulfilled economic-reform potential.
  • Net verdict: a genuinely modernising vision undercut by political compromises and scandal — a leader ahead of his party but behind his own mandate.
Assessment frame: The balanced line: Rajiv Gandhi modernised India’s infrastructure and imagination but mismanaged its politics — the technology outlived the government, the missteps outlived the man.

13. Legacy & the road to 1989

By 1989 the sheen of 1984 had gone. V.P. Singh’s Jan Morcha and the broader Janata Dal, allied with the BJP and the Left in a “National Front,” fought the election on corruption. Congress lost its majority, and India entered a sustained era of coalition governments.

Legacy strandWhat endured
TechnologyTelecom, computerisation and the missions seeded India’s IT and connectivity future.
DecentralisationThe panchayati raj push matured into the 73rd/74th Amendments (1992–93).
Anti-defectionThe Tenth Schedule remains a permanent feature of the polity.
Coalition era1989 opened three decades in which coalitions became the norm.
GS-I hook: Frame 1989 as a hinge — the end of one-party majority government and the start of the coalition, Mandal–Mandir era that the next chapter covers.

14. Contemporary relevance

Several threads of the Rajiv years remain sharply relevant today.

  • NRC & the Assam Accord: the 25 March 1971 cut-off and Clause 6 safeguards drive current debates on the NRC and citizenship in Assam.
  • Anti-defection: repeated disputes over disqualification and the Speaker’s role keep the Tenth Schedule in the news.
  • Digital India: the telecom and computerisation foundations of the 1980s are the acknowledged ancestors of today’s digital economy.
Answer value-add: Connecting the Assam Accord’s 1971 cut-off to the contemporary NRC debate shows the direct line from 1985 policy to present controversy.

15. Prelims practice (PYQ pattern)

The questions below follow the UPSC prelims pattern for this theme. Self-authored practice questions are marked as model questions with no fixed year; only genuinely dated UPSC questions carry a year.

Q1. The anti-defection law was added to the Constitution by which amendment, and in which Schedule?

  • Select the correct option.
  • (a) 42nd Amendment — Ninth Schedule
  • (b) 52nd Amendment — Tenth Schedule — correct
  • (c) 44th Amendment — Eleventh Schedule
  • (d) 61st Amendment — Tenth Schedule

Answer: (b) 52nd Amendment (1985), Tenth Schedule. The 91st Amendment (2003) later removed the “split” exception, allowing only a two-thirds merger.

Q2. The Assam Accord (1985) fixed which date as the cut-off for the detection of illegal migrants?

  • Select the correct option.
  • (a) 15 August 1947
  • (b) 26 January 1950
  • (c) 25 March 1971 — correct
  • (d) 1 January 1966

Answer: (c) 25 March 1971. This date — the eve of the Bangladesh war — is the basis for the NRC in Assam.

Q3. Consider the following statements about the technology initiatives of the Rajiv Gandhi era:

  1. C-DOT was set up to develop indigenous telecom switching.
  2. Sam Pitroda was associated with the telecom mission.
  3. Five Technology Missions were launched, including on drinking water and immunisation.
  • Which of the statements given above are correct?
  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3 — correct

Answer: (d) 1, 2 and 3. All three are correct features of the Rajiv-era technology push.

Q4. The Shah Bano case (1985) and the subsequent 1986 Act related to:

  • Select the correct option.
  • (a) Reservation in employment
  • (b) Maintenance rights of a divorced Muslim woman — correct
  • (c) Property rights of daughters
  • (d) Child marriage

Answer: (b) Maintenance rights of a divorced Muslim woman. The 1986 Act effectively overrode the Supreme Court’s maintenance ruling under Section 125 CrPC.

Q5. Which of the following accords ended the Mizo insurgency and led to Mizoram’s full statehood?

  • Select the correct option.
  • (a) Shillong Accord
  • (b) Mizo Accord (1986) — correct
  • (c) Assam Accord
  • (d) Punjab Accord

Answer: (b) Mizo Accord (1986). Widely regarded as one of India’s most successful insurgency settlements; the MNF joined mainstream politics.

Q6. Arrange the following in correct chronological order:

  1. Assam Accord
  2. Bofors allegations surface
  3. Indo–Sri Lanka Accord (IPKF)
  4. Congress’s record 1984 mandate
  • Select the correct chronological sequence.
  • (a) 4–1–2–3 — correct
  • (b) 1–4–2–3
  • (c) 4–2–1–3
  • (d) 4–1–3–2

Answer: (a) 4–1–2–3. 1984 mandate → Assam Accord (1985) → Bofors (1987) → IPKF (1987, later in the year/ongoing).

16. Mains practice + model answers

Model questions in the UPSC GS-I / GS-II idiom, each with a full answer skeleton. These are practice questions with no fixed year unless a genuine dated UPSC question is cited.

Q1. “Rajiv Gandhi modernised India’s infrastructure but mismanaged its politics.” Critically examine. (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Frame the paradox — a record 1984 mandate producing lasting technological modernisation but ending in political collapse by 1989.
  2. The modernisation record: C-DOT and telecom, PCOs, the five Technology Missions, computerisation, cautious liberalisation — a genuine leap in state capacity.
  3. The political mismanagement (the core): the Shah Bano reversal and the Ayodhya balancing inflamed communal politics; Bofors destroyed the “Mr Clean” credibility; the anti-defection law’s partisan use.
  4. The constructive politics too: credit the accords (Mizo, Assam, Punjab) and the panchayati raj push — the picture is mixed, not wholly negative.
  5. Critical edge: the deeper failing was strategic drift — a reformer who could not align his party and coalition-management with his modernising vision.
  6. Conclusion: the technology outlived the government while the missteps outlived the man — a leader ahead of his party yet behind his own mandate.

Q2. Evaluate the Assam Accord (1985) as an instrument of conflict resolution. Why does it remain contentious? (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Situate the Accord as the end of the six-year Assam Movement (1979–85) over illegal migration.
  2. Its achievements: ended a mass agitation peacefully; fixed a citizenship cut-off (25 March 1971); brought the AGP into electoral politics.
  3. Why still contentious (the core): slow and disputed implementation of detection/deportation; the NRC process’s exclusions and errors; unresolved Clause 6 safeguards for Assamese identity.
  4. Wider tension: the clash between humanitarian concerns, citizenship law and demographic anxieties — and the later interaction with the CAA.
  5. Critical edge: an accord can end an agitation without resolving its underlying grievance if implementation lags — Assam is the textbook illustration.
  6. Conclusion: the Accord was a landmark in peaceful conflict resolution, but durable peace requires credible, time-bound implementation — still a work in progress.

Q3. The Shah Bano episode reveals the enduring tension between secular rights and religious personal law. Discuss. (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Frame Shah Bano (1985) as a landmark in the debate over a Uniform Civil Code and gender justice.
  2. The judgment: the Supreme Court upheld a secular maintenance right under Section 125 CrPC, prioritising gender justice over personal law.
  3. The legislative reversal (the core): the 1986 Act limited maintenance to the iddat period — a concession to conservative opinion criticised as appeasement.
  4. The tension: Article 44’s directive (UCC) vs the freedom of religion and community autonomy; and the role of the state in reforming personal law.
  5. Critical edge: note the later trajectory — Danial Latifi (2001) read the 1986 Act to preserve fair maintenance; reform has often come through courts, not legislatures.
  6. Conclusion: the episode shows that balancing pluralism with gender justice requires consensual, rights-centred reform, not electoral calculation.

Q4. How did the Rajiv Gandhi government’s technology initiatives lay the foundations for India’s later digital economy? (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Frame the mid-1980s technology push as the origin point of India’s telecom and IT trajectory.
  2. The building blocks: C-DOT’s indigenous switching, the PCO/STD network, MTNL, and computerisation of railways and banks.
  3. The mission model (the core): the five Technology Missions institutionalised mission-mode delivery of public goods — a template revived in later governance.
  4. The human capital angle: a policy climate that valued science and technology and encouraged a generation of technologists.
  5. Critical edge: the gains were uneven and the wider economy stayed licence-bound until 1991 — the seeds were planted but not yet harvested.
  6. Conclusion: the telecom and computerisation foundations of the 1980s are the acknowledged ancestors of the IT boom and today’s Digital India.
