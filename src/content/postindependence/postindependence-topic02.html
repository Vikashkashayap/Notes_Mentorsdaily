Chapter 2: Integration of the Princely States

On 15 August 1947 India was not yet a single country — ~565 princely states, freed by the lapse of paramountcy, could in theory choose independence. That they were welded into the Union within a few years, largely peacefully, is one of history's great feats of state-building. This file covers the problem of paramountcy & the three options, the Patel–Menon strategy (States Department, Standstill Agreement & Instrument of Accession), the near-total accession by 15 August, the hard cases (Junagadh, Hyderabad & Operation Polo, Jammu & Kashmir), the democratisation & merger of the states, Part B States, and the privy purses & their abolition (1971) — with maps, flowcharts, tables and real dated PYQs + full model answers.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames the Integration of Princely States

This chapter is tested as the completion of Indian unification — how a patchwork of 565 states became one Union, and what it reveals about statecraft, federalism and the use of persuasion vs force. Sort your prep:

  • Static/institutional — the lapse of paramountcy, the States Department (2 July 1947), the Standstill Agreement & Instrument of Accession, the three subjects ceded (defence, external affairs, communications), Part B States, and the key dated events (Junagadh accession & plebiscite Feb 1948; Hyderabad's Operation Polo, 13–17 Sept 1948; J&K's Instrument of Accession, 26 Oct 1947). Factual recall.
  • Method/statecraft — the Patel–Menon combination of persuasion, appeal to patriotism, privy-purse inducement and the credible threat of force ("a basket of apples" strategy) — why it worked where others failed.
  • Analytical (GS-I) — integration as nation-building: the three hard cases as tests of principle (Junagadh = ruler vs people; Hyderabad = force as last resort; Kashmir = accession & its aftermath), and the long tail (privy-purse abolition 1971). Needs idea + example + judgement.

Territorial note: Maps in this file show India with its full official boundaries — Jammu & Kashmir, Ladakh (including PoK & Aksai Chin under illegal Pakistani/Chinese occupation) and Arunachal Pradesh are integral parts of India. J&K acceded to India in full on 26 October 1947; the areas under Pakistani & Chinese occupation are Indian territory.

1. Overview: The Problem of 1947

At Independence, British India was only part of the subcontinent. Alongside the eleven provinces lay ~565 princely states — covering roughly 40% of the territory and 28% of the population — ranging from tiny estates to states as large as France (Hyderabad, Kashmir, Mysore, Travancore).

  • Their legal status: the states were never part of "British India"; they were bound to the Crown by paramountcy, a relationship of subordinate alliance — not sovereignty transferable to India or Pakistan.
  • The danger: the Indian Independence Act 1947 lapsed paramountcy, technically leaving each ruler free to accede to India, accede to Pakistan, or claim independence — a recipe for the "Balkanisation" of India.
  • The stakes: a fragmented subcontinent of hundreds of quasi-sovereign states would have been ungovernable, indefensible and economically unviable — the very outcome the British "divide-and-quit" seemed to invite.
  • The achievement: within about two years, all but three states had acceded and been integrated — a peaceful revolution in territorial unification led by Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel and his secretary V.P. Menon.
One-line frame: the integration of the princely states completed the map of India — converting the legal possibility of 565 sovereignties into a single Union through a mix of persuasion, patriotic appeal, inducement and, only where necessary, force.

2. Lapse of Paramountcy & the Three Options

The crux of the problem was legal: paramountcy lapsed rather than transferred. Britain refused to hand its paramount rights to either successor Dominion, so on 15 August each state legally reverted to its pre-paramountcy independence — then had to choose.

Fig 2.1 — The lapse of paramountcy left each state three options; the Patel–Menon strategy funnelled nearly all into accession to India, with only Junagadh, Hyderabad & Kashmir requiring special handling. Schematic.
  • Accede to India — the option pressed on all states with an Indian majority or geographic contiguity; ultimately the near-universal choice.
  • Accede to Pakistan — realistic only for contiguous Muslim-majority states; attempted anomalously by Junagadh (a Hindu-majority state with a Muslim ruler, surrounded by Indian territory).
  • Independence — claimed by Hyderabad and toyed with by Travancore & Bhopal; treated by India as unacceptable for large, landlocked or strategically vital states.
GS-I hook: the phrase to remember is that paramountcy lapsed, it did not transfer — this legal vacuum, deliberately created by the departing British, is precisely what made integration both a danger and a triumph.

3. The Patel–Menon Strategy

The genius of integration lay in method. Sardar Patel, as head of the new States Department (created 2 July 1947), with V.P. Menon as its secretary, combined generosity, patriotism, inducement and the credible threat of coercion.

  • A limited first ask: states were asked to cede only three subjects — defence, external affairs and communications — a modest, face-saving accession that rulers could accept without appearing to surrender everything.
  • Appeal to patriotism & realism: Patel and Mountbatten jointly urged rulers to join the "natural" nation before 15 August, warning that isolated states could not survive.
  • Inducements: guaranteed privy purses, retention of titles, privileges and personal property — a golden handshake that made accession attractive.
  • The credible threat: behind the velvet glove lay the willingness to use force where a ruler defied the popular will (Hyderabad) — a deterrent that shaped others' calculations.
  • Mountbatten's help: the departing Viceroy's personal appeal at the Chamber of Princes (25 July 1947) lent the process legitimacy and urgency.
Statecraft insight: Patel offered rulers "a basket of apples" — join now on generous terms, or risk losing everything. It was persuasion backed by strength, not conquest — which is why 90%+ acceded voluntarily before Independence.

4. Standstill Agreement & Instrument of Accession

Two legal instruments did the work of integration — understand the difference, a favourite of examiners.

InstrumentWhat it did
Standstill AgreementKept existing administrative arrangements (posts, railways, customs, communications) running unchanged between a state & the Dominion until a permanent settlement — a temporary bridge, not accession.
Instrument of AccessionThe ruler's legal act of acceding to a Dominion, ceding the three subjects (defence, external affairs, communications) — the actual transfer of sovereignty over those matters.
  • Sequence: most states signed the Instrument of Accession before 15 August; Hyderabad signed only a Standstill Agreement (buying time for its bid for independence).
  • The three subjects: restricting accession to defence, external affairs & communications reassured rulers of continued internal autonomy — autonomy later dissolved by the merger process (Section 9).
  • J&K's accession: Maharaja Hari Singh signed the Instrument of Accession on 26 October 1947, legally & unconditionally making J&K part of India — the same instrument used by every other state.
Prelims trap: a Standstill Agreement is NOT accession — it merely freezes the status quo. Hyderabad's use of it to stall, while claiming independence, is the classic contrast with the Instrument of Accession.

5. The Near-Total Accession by 15 August

The strategy worked with remarkable speed. By 15 August 1947, all but a handful of states contiguous to India had signed the Instrument of Accession — a peaceful territorial revolution.

  • The scale: the overwhelming majority of ~565 states acceded to India before or around Independence, leaving only three problem cases — Junagadh, Hyderabad & Kashmir.
  • Early consolidations: clusters of small states were merged into viable units — e.g. the Kathiawar/Saurashtra union, the Patiala & East Punjab States Union (PEPSU), Rajasthan's princely mergers — creating administrable provinces.
  • Travancore & Bhopal: flirted with independence but acceded under pressure — Travancore's Dewan (C.P. Ramaswami Aiyar) abandoned the bid after popular agitation.
  • Jodhpur: the ruler briefly considered Pakistan (tempted by Jinnah) despite a Hindu-majority, Hindu-ruled, geographically Indian state — dissuaded by Patel & Mountbatten.
One-line frame: the near-total accession by 15 August is the quiet triumph — the three famous hard cases (below) are the exceptions that prove how successful the main effort was.

6. The Hard Case: Junagadh

Junagadh, a small state on the Saurashtra coast, was the mirror-image anomaly — a Hindu-majority state with a Muslim Nawab who acceded to Pakistan, though geographically surrounded by Indian territory and with no land link to Pakistan.

  • The problem: the Nawab's accession to Pakistan (August 1947) defied both geography and the wishes of his overwhelmingly Hindu population.
  • India's response: economic pressure, a provisional government (Arzi Hukumat) by the people, and the eventual flight of the Nawab to Pakistan; Indian forces moved in to restore order (November 1947).
  • The plebiscite: a referendum held in February 1948 saw an overwhelming vote to join India — settling the matter by popular will.
  • The principle established: where ruler and people diverged, India invoked the wishes of the people — the same democratic principle it would (contentiously) contrast with Pakistan's stance elsewhere.
GS-I hook: Junagadh is the "ruler-vs-people" test case — India's insistence that popular will, ratified by plebiscite, should decide is the principle examiners want you to extract; it also foreshadows the moral argument India deployed over Kashmir.

7. The Hard Case: Hyderabad & Operation Polo

Hyderabad was the largest and richest princely state — a Hindu-majority population under a Muslim Nizam who sought independence. Landlocked and entirely surrounded by India, an independent Hyderabad was strategically impossible.

StageWhat happened
Standstill Agreement (Nov 1947)The Nizam signed a one-year Standstill Agreement to buy time while pursuing independence — refusing accession.
Razakar violenceThe paramilitary Razakars (under Qasim Razvi) unleashed violence against Hindus & those favouring accession — law & order collapsed.
Communist & peasant revoltThe Telangana peasant uprising against feudal landlordism added to the turmoil, threatening a wider breakdown.
Operation Polo (13–17 Sept 1948)A short Indian "police action" — the army entered Hyderabad; the Nizam surrendered in ~5 days & acceded to India.
Precise detailOperation Polo was led by Maj. Gen. J.N. Chaudhuri; the Nizam acceded on 17 Sep 1948. (For contrast, the Junagadh plebiscite of 20 Feb 1948 returned ~91% for India.)
GS-I hook: Hyderabad is the "force as last resort" case — integration was overwhelmingly peaceful, but where a ruler defied both geography & his people and tolerated Razakar violence, India used calibrated, brief military action. Note the timing: after Independence & after the Kashmir war had begun, and just after Jinnah's death.

8. The Hard Case: Jammu & Kashmir

J&K was the inverse of Junagadh — a Muslim-majority state with a Hindu ruler (Maharaja Hari Singh) who initially sought to stay independent, signing standstill arrangements with both Dominions.

  • The trigger: in October 1947, Pakistan-backed tribal raiders (lashkars) invaded Kashmir, advancing toward Srinagar — forcing the Maharaja's hand.
  • The accession: Hari Singh signed the Instrument of Accession on 26 October 1947, legally & unconditionally making J&K part of India; only then did Indian troops airlift into Srinagar to repel the invaders.
  • The war & ceasefire: the first Indo-Pak war (1947–48) ended with a UN-brokered ceasefire (1 Jan 1949), leaving part of the state under illegal Pakistani occupation (today's "PoK" & Gilgit-Baltistan) — Indian territory.
  • Constitutional footnote: J&K's special transitional arrangement under Article 370 (a temporary provision, since abrogated in August 2019) is covered fully in the Polity & IR files; here the key fact is the lawful accession of the whole state to India.
Territorial clarity: J&K acceded to India in full on 26 October 1947 via the same Instrument of Accession as every other state. The areas under Pakistani (PoK, Gilgit-Baltistan) & Chinese (Aksai Chin, Shaksgam) occupation are integral Indian territory under illegal occupation — India has never surrendered its claim.

9. Democratisation, Merger & Reorganisation

Accession (ceding three subjects) was only the first step. The second — full integration — dissolved the states' internal autonomy and merged them into the democratic, administrative structure of India.

  • Consolidation of small states: hundreds of tiny states were merged into their neighbouring provinces or grouped into new unions (Saurashtra, PEPSU, Rajasthan, Madhya Bharat, Vindhya Pradesh).
  • Part B States: larger states (Hyderabad, Mysore, J&K, Travancore-Cochin & the unions) became Part B States under the 1950 Constitution — governed by a Rajpramukh (usually the former ruler) instead of a Governor.
  • Democratisation: autocratic princely administration gave way to popular, elected government — the states' subjects became citizens of a democratic republic.
  • The 1956 reorganisation: the distinction between Part A, B & C states was abolished by the States Reorganisation Act 1956 (linked to the linguistic reorganisation, Chapter 4) — completing the assimilation of princely territory into a uniform, linguistic-federal structure.
  • The named unions (1948–49): Saurashtra Union (Feb 1948, 217 Kathiawar states); Madhya Bharat (May 1948, Gwalior + Indore); PEPSU (Jul 1948, Maharaja of Patiala as Rajpramukh); Rajasthan Union (Mar 1949, 22 Rajput states); Travancore-Cochin Union (Jul 1949).
One-line frame: accession made the states part of India; the merger & the 1956 reorganisation made them indistinguishable from India — the princely order was not just annexed but democratically dissolved.

10. Privy Purses & Their Abolition

The inducement that had smoothed accession — the privy purse — became politically untenable in a socialist, egalitarian republic, and was abolished within a generation.

  • What it was: a guaranteed, tax-free annual payment to former rulers (plus retained titles & privileges), constitutionally protected under Articles 291 & 362 — the price of peaceful integration.
  • The reversal: in her 1971 "Garibi Hatao" turn, Indira Gandhi moved to abolish privy purses as incompatible with equality; an initial attempt (1970) was struck down by the Supreme Court.
  • The 26th Amendment (1971): Parliament abolished privy purses & princely privileges by constitutional amendment — ending the last vestige of the princely order (covered in the Indira-era chapters).
  • Assessment: the purses were a transitional bargain — generous enough to secure bloodless integration, expendable once the states were fully assimilated.
Forward link: the abolition of privy purses (26th Amendment, 1971) belongs to the Indira-era politics of populist egalitarianism — but its logic begins here, in the transitional deal that made integration possible.

11. Assessment: Patel as the Iron Man

The integration of the princely states is Sardar Patel's defining achievement — earning him the epithets "Iron Man of India" and the "Bismarck of India". How should it be judged?

  • Scale & speed: the peaceful unification of 40% of the subcontinent's territory in ~2 years — a feat with few historical parallels, achieved largely without bloodshed.
  • Method: the blend of persuasion, patriotic appeal, generous terms & the sparing, calibrated use of force (only Hyderabad) — a masterclass in realpolitik tempered by restraint.
  • The Menon partnership: V.P. Menon's administrative brilliance & drafting of the accession instruments were indispensable — integration was a Patel–Menon joint achievement.
  • Critical edge: critics note the coercive undertone (the "basket of apples"), the deferred democratisation, & the unresolved Kashmir tangle — but the counterfactual (a Balkanised subcontinent) makes the achievement decisive.
  • The scale in numbers: ~565 states integrated within about 24 months (Jun 1947–Aug 1949), rewriting the political map without large-scale violence — documented by V.P. Menon in The Story of the Integration of the Indian States (1956), the primary insider account.
GS-I hook: the strongest assessment credits Patel with saving Indian unity while acknowledging the transitional compromises (privy purses, Rajpramukhs) — realpolitik in the service of nation-building, not empire-building.

12. Comparison & Significance

The three hard cases together illustrate the principles India applied — and the wider significance of integration for the Republic.

StateRuler / peoplePath to integration
JunagadhMuslim Nawab / Hindu majorityAcceded to Pakistan → popular agitation → plebiscite (Feb 1948) → India.
HyderabadMuslim Nizam / Hindu majoritySought independence → Razakar violence → Operation Polo (Sept 1948) → India.
Jammu & KashmirHindu Maharaja / Muslim majoritySought independence → tribal invasion → Instrument of Accession (26 Oct 1947) → India.
  • Consistency of principle: India stressed popular will & geography (Junagadh, Hyderabad) and the legality of accession (Kashmir) — a coherent, if contested, framework.
  • Significance: integration completed India's territorial unity, made a strong federal Union viable, & established the precedent that no sub-national unit had a right to secede — a foundation of the Republic.
One-line frame: the three hard cases are not exceptions to a rule but illustrations of it — India integrated through popular legitimacy where possible and force only where a ruler defied both people and geography.

13. Current Affairs Link

Sardar Patel & the integration story remain a live theme in national memory — useful value-addition for Mains & essays.

  • National Unity Day (Rashtriya Ekta Diwas): observed on 31 October (Patel's birth anniversary) to honour his role in unifying India. check for latest update or data
  • Statue of Unity: the world's tallest statue (182 m, Kevadia, Gujarat, inaugurated 2018) commemorates Patel & the integration.
  • Federalism debates: the "strong Centre" design that integration made possible frames contemporary Centre-state debates.
  • Kashmir & Article 370: the 2019 abrogation & reorganisation of J&K revived public discussion of the 1947 accession — a direct link from this chapter.
Essay/value-add: integration is a rich case study of leadership, statecraft & nation-building — pair Patel's realism with Nehru's idealism to show the complementary skills that built modern India.

14. Prelims PYQs & Practice

Note on sourcing: the questions below are exam-pattern practice MCQs built strictly on this chapter's high-yield facts. They are deliberately left yearless (labelled "Model question") and are not attributed to any fabricated year — use them for concept-testing, not for citing a PYQ year.

Model question — no fixed year

With reference to the integration of princely states, the "Instrument of Accession" transferred to the Dominion which of the following subjects?
1. Defence   2. External affairs   3. Communications   4. Finance

  • (a) 1, 2 and 3 only
  • (b) 1, 2 and 4 only
  • (c) 2, 3 and 4 only
  • (d) 1, 2, 3 and 4

Answer: (a). The Instrument of Accession ceded only three subjects — defence, external affairs & communications — leaving internal matters (including finance) with the ruler until the later merger.

Model question — no fixed year

"Operation Polo", a short military action in 1948, was associated with the integration of which princely state?

  • (a) Junagadh
  • (b) Hyderabad
  • (c) Jammu & Kashmir
  • (d) Travancore

Answer: (b). Operation Polo (13–17 September 1948) was the "police action" that ended the Nizam's bid for an independent Hyderabad and secured its accession to India.

Model question — no fixed year

Consider the following statements:
1. A Standstill Agreement amounted to accession to a Dominion.
2. Junagadh's accession to India was ratified by a plebiscite.
3. Jammu & Kashmir acceded to India through an Instrument of Accession.
Which are correct?

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Answer: (b). A Standstill Agreement merely froze existing arrangements (not accession) — statement 1 is wrong. Junagadh's accession was confirmed by the February 1948 plebiscite, and J&K acceded via the Instrument of Accession (26 Oct 1947).

Model question — no fixed year

The States Department, set up in July 1947 to handle the princely states, was headed by:

  • (a) Jawaharlal Nehru
  • (b) Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel
  • (c) C. Rajagopalachari
  • (d) Rajendra Prasad

Answer: (b). Patel headed the States Department (created 2 July 1947), with V.P. Menon as its secretary — the duo that engineered integration.

Model question — no fixed year

Under the Constitution as it stood in 1950, the former larger princely states were classified as:

  • (a) Part A States
  • (b) Part B States
  • (c) Part C States
  • (d) Union Territories

Answer: (b). Larger princely states/unions became Part B States, governed by a Rajpramukh; the A/B/C classification was abolished by the States Reorganisation Act 1956.

Model question — no fixed year

Privy purses to former rulers were abolished by which Constitutional Amendment?

  • (a) 24th Amendment
  • (b) 25th Amendment
  • (c) 26th Amendment
  • (d) 42nd Amendment

Answer: (c). The 26th Constitutional Amendment (1971) abolished privy purses & princely privileges, after the Supreme Court struck down the 1970 executive attempt.

15. Mains PYQs — Model Answers

Note on sourcing: the questions below are exam-pattern practice questions on this chapter's core analytical themes (left yearless, not attributed to a fabricated year), each with a full answer skeleton. Integration of the states most often appears in Mains woven into questions on nation-building & Patel's role.

Model question — no fixed year

"The integration of the princely states was as much a triumph of statecraft as of will." Critically examine the role of Sardar Patel and V.P. Menon. (250 words) 15 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Integrating ~565 states — 40% of the subcontinent — in barely two years, largely peacefully, was a rare feat of nation-building achieved through method as much as resolve.
  2. The problem: the lapse (not transfer) of paramountcy left each ruler free to accede to either Dominion or claim independence — risking Balkanisation.
  3. Statecraft (the core): the limited first ask (three subjects), privy-purse inducements, patriotic appeal & Mountbatten's persuasion secured 90%+ accession before 15 August — a Patel–Menon design.
  4. Will & force: where persuasion failed — Hyderabad's Razakar violence — India used calibrated force (Operation Polo); Junagadh was settled by plebiscite.
  5. Critical edge: the coercive undertone, deferred democratisation (Rajpramukhs, privy purses) & the unresolved Kashmir tangle qualify the triumph — but the counterfactual of a fragmented India makes it decisive.
  6. Conclusion: integration blended realism & restraint — Patel's will, Menon's craft — converting a legal vacuum into national unity.
Model question — no fixed year

Compare the paths by which Junagadh, Hyderabad and Jammu & Kashmir were integrated, and the principles India applied. (250 words) 15 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: The three hard cases — each with a ruler–people mismatch — reveal the consistent principles behind India's integration: geography, popular will & legality of accession.
  2. Junagadh: a Muslim Nawab's accession to Pakistan defied a Hindu majority & geography; settled by popular agitation & the February 1948 plebiscite — the popular-will principle.
  3. Hyderabad (the core contrast): a Nizam's bid for independence, amid Razakar violence & the Telangana revolt, was ended by Operation Polo (Sept 1948) — force as last resort.
  4. Jammu & Kashmir: a Hindu Maharaja's hesitation, broken by the tribal invasion, produced the lawful Instrument of Accession (26 Oct 1947); the ensuing war left part under illegal occupation.
  5. Critical edge: critics allege inconsistency (plebiscite for Junagadh, not Kashmir), but India's case rested on legal accession plus aggression by Pakistan — a defensible, if contested, framework.
  6. Conclusion: across all three, India applied a coherent logic — legitimacy through people, geography or law — using force only where a ruler defied all three.
Model question — no fixed year

"The privy purse was the price of peaceful integration." Discuss the bargain and its later abolition. (150 words) 10 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: The privy purse was a transitional inducement — generous enough to secure bloodless accession, expendable once the states were assimilated.
  2. The bargain: guaranteed tax-free payments, titles & privileges (Articles 291 & 362) persuaded rulers to cede sovereignty voluntarily.
  3. The tension (the core): hereditary privilege sat uneasily with a socialist, egalitarian republic committed to equality before law.
  4. Abolition: the 1970 executive attempt was struck down; the 26th Amendment (1971) abolished purses & privileges amid "Garibi Hatao" populism.
  5. Critical edge: some see a breach of a solemn guarantee; most judge it a justified end to feudal residue once integration was complete.
  6. Conclusion: the purse was a means, not an end — a pragmatic bridge from princely order to democratic republic.
Model question — no fixed year

How did the integration of the princely states shape the federal design of the Indian Republic? (150 words) 10 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Integration did not merely add territory — it shaped a strong-Centre, indissoluble federal design.
  2. No right to secede: the accession-and-merger process established that sub-national units had no sovereign right to leave the Union.
  3. Uniform structure (the core): the merger of states & the 1956 reorganisation dissolved princely distinctions, creating uniform, linguistic states within one Union.
  4. Strong Centre: the fear of fragmentation reinforced the Constituent Assembly's tilt toward a powerful union & single citizenship.
  5. Critical edge: Part B States & Rajpramukhs were transitional compromises soon abolished.
  6. Conclusion: integration underwrote India's cooperative-but-strong-Centre federalism — unity in diversity, without a right of exit.
