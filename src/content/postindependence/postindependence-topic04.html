Chapter 4: Linguistic Crisis, Reorganisation of States & the Growth of Regionalism

No question tested the new republic's unity more sharply than language. Between 1947 and 1966, India redrew its internal map on linguistic lines — a gamble that many feared would splinter the nation but which, in hindsight, strengthened it. This file traces the language question of 1947, the cautious Dhar Commission & JVP Committee, Potti Sriramulu's fast-unto-death and the birth of Andhra (1953), the Fazl Ali States Reorganisation Commission & the States Reorganisation Act, 1956, later reorganisation (Bombay 1960, Punjab 1966), the Hindi-vs-English official-language battle, the 1965 anti-Hindi agitation & three-language formula, and the wider growth of regionalism (sons-of-the-soil, regional parties) — with tables, flowcharts and PYQ-pattern practice + full model answers.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames Linguistic States & Regionalism

This chapter is tested as the great nation-building paradox: how a republic that feared linguistic states would "Balkanise" India ended up using them to consolidate unity. Sort your prep:

  • Static/institutional — the sequence of bodies & laws: Dhar Commission (1948), JVP Committee (1949), Andhra State (1953), Fazl Ali SRC (1953–55), States Reorganisation Act 1956 (14 states + 6 UTs), Bombay split (1960), Punjab split (1966); plus the language provisions (Arts 343–351, Eighth Schedule, Official Languages Act 1963). High-yield recall.
  • The turning point — the Potti Sriramulu martyrdom and Nehru's reluctant concession, which converted an anxious ad-hoc process into a principled national reorganisation. The narrative spine of the chapter.
  • Analytical (GS-I) — the debate on whether linguistic states strengthened or weakened unity, the rise of regionalism (sons-of-the-soil, regional parties, secession vs autonomy) and the Hindi-vs-English question. Needs idea + example + judgement.

Territorial note: Maps in this file show India with its full official boundaries — Jammu & Kashmir & Ladakh (including PoK & Aksai Chin) are integral parts of India, as are Arunachal Pradesh and the entire North-East.

1. Overview: The Language Question in 1947

At independence India inherited administrative provinces drawn for colonial convenience — Madras, Bombay, the Central Provinces — that cut across languages and cultures. Almost immediately, popular movements demanded that states be redrawn so that people who spoke one language would live in one state. To a leadership fresh from Partition, this looked dangerous.

  • The fear: having just seen the country split on religion, Nehru, Patel & the Congress leadership feared that redrawing states on language would unleash fresh "sub-nationalisms" and "Balkanise" India.
  • The promise: yet the Congress itself had, since the Nagpur session (1920), organised its own provincial units on a linguistic basis and had promised linguistic provinces — a commitment now demanded in full.
  • The reality: India is a land of many major languages (today 22 in the Eighth Schedule); governance, education & democracy in a citizen's mother tongue was a genuine popular aspiration, not mere elite politics.
  • The resolution: after initial resistance, India reorganised its states on language between 1953 and 1966 — and, contrary to the doomsayers, the federation held.
One-line frame: linguistic reorganisation was the moment India chose to accommodate diversity rather than suppress it — converting a feared source of fragmentation into a durable basis of unity.

2. Why Language Became the Basis

Before judging the policy, understand why language — not religion, caste or economics — became the organising principle of Indian federalism. The reasons are the analytical backbone of any Mains answer.

DriverExplanation
Congress precedentThe Congress had reorganised its own units linguistically from 1920 (Nagpur) and repeatedly promised linguistic provinces — a pledge the people now cashed in.
Democratic aspirationAdministration, courts, schooling & political participation are meaningful only in a language people understand — a mother-tongue state deepens democracy.
Cultural identityLanguage carries literature, history & pride; a shared language gives a natural unit of collective identity and self-government.
Administrative logicA linguistically coherent state is easier to govern than one where officials and the governed cannot communicate.
Popular mobilisationMass agitations (Andhra, Samyukta Maharashtra, Mahagujarat) made the demand impossible to ignore in a democracy.
The pre-independence pledgeThe Congress had endorsed linguistic provinces since its Nagpur Session (1920) under Gandhi, and drew up linguistic provincial committees in 1921 — so the post-1947 demand invoked the party's own promise.
GS-I hook: language was chosen because it was, paradoxically, the least divisive available basis — unlike religion it did not threaten the secular fabric, and unlike caste it mapped onto a coherent territory that could be democratically governed.

3. Early Caution: Dhar Commission & JVP Committee

The national leadership did not rush. Two bodies in quick succession advised caution, and both effectively counselled putting national unity ahead of linguistic sentiment — a stance the events of 1952 would overturn.

Body (year)Composition & verdict
Dhar Commission (1948)Linguistic Provinces Commission under S.K. Dhar. Held that reorganisation should be based on administrative convenience rather than language alone; warned linguistic states could harm national unity in that fragile hour. Popular disappointment followed.
JVP Committee (1949)Congress committee of Jawaharlal Nehru, Vallabhbhai Patel & Pattabhi Sitaramayya. It too advised against immediate linguistic reorganisation, citing security & unity, but conceded that if popular demand was insistent, Andhra might be considered.

Both reports reflected the leadership's overriding priority in 1948–49: consolidate a shaky nation first, accommodate sentiment later. But this cautious consensus could not survive a determined mass movement.

Prelims trap: remember the order — Dhar (1948) → JVP (1949) → Andhra (1953) → Fazl Ali SRC (1953–55) → SR Act (1956). JVP = Jawaharlal, Vallabhbhai, Pattabhi; both early bodies opposed immediate linguistic states.

4. Andhra: The First Linguistic State

The dam broke in the Telugu-speaking districts of the old Madras Presidency. The demand for a separate Andhra found a martyr whose death forced the government's hand — the single most consequential episode in the chapter.

Fig 4.1 — The Andhra sequence: Potti Sriramulu's fast-unto-death (1952) forced the creation of the first linguistic state (Oct 1953). Schematic timeline.
  • Potti Sriramulu, a Gandhian, began a fast-unto-death in October 1952 demanding a separate Andhra state for Telugu-speakers of Madras Presidency.
  • He died on 15 December 1952 on the 58th day of his fast. His death triggered violent protests, hartals & deaths across the Telugu region.
  • Nehru, moved & alarmed, conceded: Andhra State was created on 1 October 1953 — the first state formed purely on a linguistic basis.
  • The concession made further demands irresistible — if Telugu-speakers had their state, so must Marathis, Gujaratis, Kannadigas & others. A general commission became unavoidable.
One-line frame: Potti Sriramulu's martyrdom is the hinge of the whole chapter — it converted a leadership that opposed linguistic states into one that had to reorganise the entire union along language.

5. The Fazl Ali Commission & the States Reorganisation Act, 1956

To handle the flood of demands systematically, the government appointed a full commission — the decisive institutional response that produced modern India's map.

ElementDetail
States Reorganisation Commission (1953)Chaired by Fazl Ali, with members H.N. Kunzru & K.M. Panikkar. Submitted its report in 1955.
Core recommendationBroadly accepted language as the basis of reorganisation, but balanced it against national unity, security, financial viability & administrative convenience — it rejected the "one language, one state" absolutism (e.g. did not favour splitting bilingual Bombay immediately).
States Reorganisation Act, 1956Implemented the report: reorganised India into 14 states and 6 Union Territories, redrawing boundaries largely on linguistic lines. The Seventh Constitutional Amendment abolished the old Part A/B/C/D classification of states.
Recommendation vs enactmentThe Fazl Ali SRC (report 30 Sep 1955) recommended 16 states + 3 UTs; the States Reorganisation Act, 1956 finally created 14 states + 6 UTs — a favourite Prelims contrast.
Key 1956 creationsKerala (Malabar from Madras + Travancore-Cochin); Mysore/Karnataka (Kannada-speakers consolidated from four entities); Andhra Pradesh (Andhra + Telugu Telangana areas of Hyderabad).

The 1956 settlement was a watershed: for the first time the internal map matched the way people actually lived and spoke. But it left two conspicuous unfinished cases — bilingual Bombay and multi-lingual Punjab — which the next decade had to resolve.

Prelims trap: SRC = Fazl Ali (chair), Kunzru, Panikkar; report 1955; Act 1956 → 14 states + 6 UTs; the 7th Amendment ended the Part A/B/C/D scheme. Do not confuse the SRC (1953–55) with the earlier Dhar Commission (1948).

6. Further Reorganisation: Bombay, Punjab & After

The 1956 Act did not settle everything. Two great agitations and a long tail of later creations completed the linguistic map — showing that reorganisation was a process, not a one-time event.

Case (year)What happened
Bombay split (1960)The Samyukta Maharashtra (Marathi) & Mahagujarat (Gujarati) movements forced the bilingual Bombay State to be divided into Maharashtra & Gujarat on 1 May 1960; Bombay city went to Maharashtra.
Punjab split (1966)After the Punjabi Suba movement (complicated by language-religion overlap), Punjab was reorganised into Punjab (Punjabi), Haryana (Hindi) & the hill areas to Himachal Pradesh; Chandigarh became a shared UT capital.
North-East reorganisation (1960s–70s)Nagaland (1963), and under the North-Eastern Areas (Reorganisation) Act 1971, Meghalaya, Manipur & Tripura and later Mizoram/Arunachal emerged — on ethnic-linguistic lines.
Later linguistic/other creationsGujarat/Maharashtra (1960), Haryana (1966), later Uttarakhand, Jharkhand & Chhattisgarh (2000, more developmental than linguistic), and Telangana (2014, sub-regional).
GS-I hook: Bombay (1960) & Punjab (1966) show the two hard cases — bilingual metropolis & language-religion overlap — that the 1956 Act ducked. Their peaceful resolution proved the system could absorb even the toughest linguistic demands.

7. Language of the Union: Hindi vs English

Reorganising states was only half the language question. The other half — which language would bind the union together — was even more explosive, pitting the Hindi-speaking north against the non-Hindi south.

ProvisionWhat it says
Article 343Hindi in Devanagari is the official language of the Union; English to continue for official purposes for 15 years (i.e. until 1965), extendable by Parliament.
Articles 344 / 351Provide for an Official Language Commission & Committee, and place a duty on the Union to promote the spread & development of Hindi.
Eighth ScheduleLists the recognised languages — originally 14, now 22 after successive additions (e.g. Sindhi, Konkani, Manipuri, Nepali; Bodo, Dogri, Maithili, Santhali in 2003).
Official Languages Act, 1963Allowed English to continue alongside Hindi "as may be" beyond 1965; amended in 1967 to make the continuance of English effectively open-ended, reassuring the south.

The 1965 deadline — when Hindi was to replace English — became a flashpoint, because non-Hindi speakers feared permanent disadvantage in jobs, administration & higher education if Hindi alone prevailed.

One-line frame: the compromise India reached — Hindi plus English as the two link languages, with 22 scheduled languages recognised — is the linguistic equivalent of the federal bargain: no single language could be allowed to dominate.

8. Anti-Hindi Agitation & the Three-Language Formula

As the 1965 deadline approached, the south — above all Tamil Nadu — erupted. The agitation reshaped both language policy and party politics for a generation.

  • 1965 agitation: as Hindi was set to become the sole official language, Tamil Nadu saw violent protests, self-immolations & deaths. The DMK led the opposition, casting Hindi imposition as northern domination.
  • Political fallout: the agitation propelled the DMK to power in Tamil Nadu (1967), ending Congress rule there — a decisive moment in the rise of regional parties.
  • Policy response: Prime Minister Lal Bahadur Shastri, and later the 1967 amendment to the Official Languages Act, assured non-Hindi states that English would continue as long as they wished — a "virtual indefinite" bilingual arrangement.
  • Three-Language Formula: devised (1968 National Policy on Education, based on the Kothari Commission) to balance interests — a student would learn the regional language, Hindi & English in Hindi areas, and the regional language, English & Hindi (or another Indian language) in non-Hindi areas.
RegionThree languages taught
Hindi-speaking statesHindi + English + a modern Indian language (ideally a southern one).
Non-Hindi statesRegional language + Hindi + English.
GS-I hook: the anti-Hindi agitation is the classic case of accommodation defusing secession — by conceding continued English and adopting the three-language formula, the Union turned a potential breakaway into ordinary competitive politics (the DMK worked within the system).

9. Growth of Regionalism: Forms & Causes

Linguistic reorganisation gave regions a political voice — and with it grew regionalism, the assertion of regional identity & interest within (or occasionally against) the nation. UPSC wants you to classify its forms and diagnose its causes.

Form of regionalismMeaning & example
Demand for statehoodSeeking a separate state within the Union — Jharkhand, Uttarakhand, Chhattisgarh, Telangana; the most common & legitimate form.
Demand for autonomySeeking greater powers within an existing state or from the Centre — e.g. autonomy councils, or federal-devolution demands from states.
Inter-state disputesBoundary, river-water & capital disputes — Cauvery, Belgaum (Maharashtra–Karnataka), Chandigarh.
Sons-of-the-soil / nativismPreference for locals in jobs & land against "outsiders" — e.g. Shiv Sena in Bombay; Assam movement.
SecessionismDemand to break away — the extreme, rare & unconstitutional form (early DMK rhetoric, some NE insurgencies) — largely defused over time.

Causes: uneven development & regional economic disparity; a sense of relative deprivation or neglect by the Centre; distinct language/culture; over-centralisation; and competitive electoral politics that rewards regional mobilisation.

One-line frame: regionalism in India is mostly a demand for a fairer share & recognition within the Union, not a rejection of it — which is why the federation has absorbed it through new states, devolution & coalition politics rather than force.

10. Sons-of-the-Soil & Regional Parties

Two by-products of reorganisation deserve their own treatment because they recur in the exam: nativist ("sons-of-the-soil") politics and the rise of regional parties.

  • Sons-of-the-soil doctrine: the claim that a state "belongs" to its linguistic-cultural natives, who therefore have first claim on jobs, education & land — against migrants from other states.
  • Classic example: the Shiv Sena in Bombay/Maharashtra (from 1966) mobilised Marathi "sons of the soil" against South Indian & later North Indian migrants; similar nativist strains appeared in Assam & elsewhere.
  • Tension with the Constitution: nativism collides with Article 19 (freedom to move, reside & work anywhere) & the idea of single Indian citizenship — a recurring GS-I & GS-II theme.
  • Rise of regional parties: reorganisation & the language question nurtured strong regional parties — DMK/AIADMK (Tamil Nadu), Akali Dal (Punjab), TDP (Andhra, 1982), Asom Gana Parishad, National Conference — which by the late 1980s made coalition politics the national norm.
GS-I hook: the paradox — regional parties, feared as centrifugal, actually deepened Indian democracy by giving regional aspirations a constitutional, electoral outlet and, through coalitions, a real stake in governing the Union.

11. Assessment: Did Linguistic Reorganisation Strengthen Unity?

This is the chapter's central Mains question. The mature answer is a qualified yes — hold both the gains and the frictions in view.

Strengthened unityCreated frictions
Defused the most explosive identity demand by accommodating it, not suppressing it.Sharpened linguistic identities and occasional inter-state chauvinism (Belgaum, river-water disputes).
Made administration & democracy work in people's own languages — deepening participation.Encouraged sons-of-the-soil nativism against internal migrants.
Contained secessionism: language demands were met within the Union (DMK entered electoral politics).Fed the growth of regionalism & regional parties, complicating national politics.
Proved the federation was flexible & durable — it could redraw itself peacefully.Left residual disputes (capitals, boundaries, waters) that periodically flare.

The verdict of history — and of scholars like Ramachandra Guha — is that the fears of "Balkanisation" were misplaced: linguistic states gave regional identities a secure home inside the nation, and India, unlike several post-colonial states, did not fragment.

One-line frame: linguistic reorganisation strengthened unity precisely because it accommodated diversity — India held together not despite redrawing itself on language, but because it did.

12. Contemporary Dimensions

The linguistic-reorganisation story is not closed. Its logic and its tensions continue to shape live debates — useful for enriching a Mains answer with current relevance.

  • New states: the creation of Telangana (2014) and continuing demands (Vidarbha, Gorkhaland, Bodoland, Bundelkhand) show reorganisation is an ongoing process — now driven more by development & identity than pure language.
  • Language politics revived: periodic anti-Hindi-imposition flare-ups (over the three-language formula, exam languages, signage) in Tamil Nadu, Karnataka & the North-East.
  • Federal finance & devolution: southern & other states raising fiscal federalism concerns (delimitation, tax-share, cess) — a modern, economic form of regionalism.
  • Eighth Schedule demands: continuing requests to add more languages (e.g. Bhojpuri, Rajasthani, Tulu) to the 22 already listed.
  • Migration & nativism: sons-of-the-soil sentiment resurfaces around internal migration in metros — a live tension with Article 19 mobility rights.
GS-I / GS-II hook: use these to show examiners that the 1950s settlement still frames today's debates on federalism, language & internal migration — the reorganisation question never fully closes in a plural federation.

13. Current Affairs Link

Connect the historical chapter to recent developments to signal an updated preparation.

  • Three-language formula & NEP 2020: renewed debate, with Tamil Nadu resisting what it sees as Hindi imposition — a direct echo of 1965.
  • Delimitation & the "north-south" question: southern states' concern that post-2026 delimitation on population could reduce their parliamentary weight — a fresh federal-regional flashpoint.
  • Statehood & UT demands: continuing debates over statehood for Delhi/J&K, and sub-regional aspirations, keep reorganisation politically live.
  • Classical-language & Eighth-Schedule recognitions: periodic grants of "classical language" status and demands for new Eighth-Schedule entries keep language policy in the news.
How to use in the exam: a single line linking the 1956 settlement to today's three-language / delimitation debates shows the examiner you grasp the chapter as living federalism, not dead history.

14. Prelims PYQs & Practice

Model question — no fixed year

Arrange the following in correct chronological order:
1. Dhar Commission   2. States Reorganisation Act   3. Creation of Andhra State   4. JVP Committee

  • (a) 1 → 4 → 3 → 2
  • (b) 4 → 1 → 2 → 3
  • (c) 1 → 3 → 4 → 2
  • (d) 4 → 1 → 3 → 2

Answer: (a). Dhar Commission (1948) → JVP Committee (1949) → Andhra State (1953) → States Reorganisation Act (1956).

Model question — no fixed year

The States Reorganisation Commission (1953) was chaired by:

  • (a) S.K. Dhar
  • (b) Fazl Ali
  • (c) K.M. Panikkar
  • (d) H.N. Kunzru

Answer: (b). The SRC was chaired by Fazl Ali; Kunzru & Panikkar were the other two members. S.K. Dhar headed the earlier Linguistic Provinces Commission (1948).

Model question — no fixed year

Consider the following statements about the States Reorganisation Act, 1956:
1. It reorganised India into 14 states and 6 Union Territories.
2. It abolished the Part A, B, C and D classification of states.
3. It created the states of Maharashtra and Gujarat.
Which are correct?

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Answer: (a). The 1956 Act created 14 states + 6 UTs and (via the 7th Amendment) ended the Part A/B/C/D scheme. Maharashtra & Gujarat were created later, by splitting Bombay State in 1960.

Model question — no fixed year

Bombay State was bifurcated into Maharashtra and Gujarat in which year?

  • (a) 1956
  • (b) 1960
  • (c) 1966
  • (d) 1953

Answer: (b). Following the Samyukta Maharashtra & Mahagujarat movements, Bombay State was split into Maharashtra & Gujarat on 1 May 1960; Punjab was reorganised later, in 1966.

Model question — no fixed year

Under Article 343, English was to continue for official purposes of the Union for how many years from the commencement of the Constitution?

  • (a) 10 years
  • (b) 15 years
  • (c) 20 years
  • (d) 25 years

Answer: (b). Article 343 provided for English to continue for 15 years (until 1965), extendable by Parliament — the source of the 1965 flashpoint. The Official Languages Act 1963 (amended 1967) allowed English to continue thereafter.

Model question — no fixed year

How many languages are currently listed in the Eighth Schedule of the Constitution?

  • (a) 14
  • (b) 18
  • (c) 22
  • (d) 24

Answer: (c). The Eighth Schedule originally listed 14 languages; it now lists 22 (Bodo, Dogri, Maithili & Santhali were added in 2003).

15. Mains PYQs — Model Answers

Note on sourcing: the first question below is a genuine dated UPSC Mains PYQ; the remainder are exam-pattern practice questions on this chapter's core themes (left yearless), each with a full answer skeleton.

UPSC Mains GS-I 2016

Has the formation of linguistic States strengthened the cause of Indian unity? (200 words) 12.5 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Feared as a recipe for "Balkanisation", linguistic reorganisation (1953–66) instead gave regional identities a secure home within the Union — on balance strengthening, not weakening, Indian unity.
  2. How it strengthened unity: it accommodated the most explosive identity demand democratically; made administration & participation work in people's own languages; and contained secessionism by drawing regional forces into electoral politics.
  3. The evidence (the core): the DMK entered constitutional politics rather than breaking away; feared fragmentation never materialised; India, unlike several post-colonial states, held together and deepened its democracy.
  4. The frictions: it also sharpened linguistic identities, fed sons-of-the-soil nativism, and left inter-state disputes (Belgaum, river waters, capitals) — costs, not fatal flaws.
  5. Critical edge: unity was strengthened because diversity was accommodated, not suppressed — the design's success lay in flexibility, though residual regional grievances persist.
  6. Conclusion: the formation of linguistic states, on the whole, strengthened Indian unity by converting a centrifugal force into a stable, participatory federalism.
Model question — no fixed year

Trace the process by which India moved from opposing linguistic states to reorganising itself on linguistic lines. (250 words) 15 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Fresh from Partition, the leadership feared linguistic states would splinter India — yet within a decade it reorganised the whole union on language. The journey is a study in democratic pressure.
  2. Early caution: the Dhar Commission (1948) favoured administrative convenience; the JVP Committee (1949) advised against immediate reorganisation, prioritising unity & security.
  3. The turning point (the core): Potti Sriramulu's fast-unto-death (1952) and the resulting upheaval forced Nehru to concede Andhra (1953) — making a general commission unavoidable.
  4. Institutionalisation: the Fazl Ali SRC (1953–55) accepted language as the broad basis, and the States Reorganisation Act 1956 created 14 states + 6 UTs; Bombay (1960) & Punjab (1966) completed the map.
  5. Critical edge: the shift was reactive — driven by agitation rather than design — which is why hard cases (bilingual Bombay, Punjab) were left for later.
  6. Conclusion: India moved from fear to acceptance because democracy made accommodation cheaper than resistance — and the gamble paid off in a stable federation.
Model question — no fixed year

Examine the causes and forms of regionalism in India and suggest measures to address it. (250 words) 15 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Regionalism — the assertion of regional identity & interest — is a natural feature of a diverse federation; in India it is mostly a demand for a fairer share within the Union, not against it.
  2. Causes: uneven development & relative deprivation, distinct language/culture, over-centralisation, a sense of neglect by the Centre, and competitive electoral mobilisation.
  3. Forms (the core): demand for statehood (Telangana), autonomy, inter-state disputes (Cauvery, Belgaum), sons-of-the-soil nativism (Shiv Sena), and the rare extreme of secessionism.
  4. Positive role: regional parties & identities deepened democracy & federalism, giving voice through coalitions rather than conflict.
  5. Critical edge / measures: balanced regional development, genuine fiscal federalism & devolution, inter-state dispute mechanisms, and protecting Article 19 mobility against nativism.
  6. Conclusion: managed through accommodation, regionalism is a strength — a channel for diversity within a resilient national framework.
Model question — no fixed year

Discuss the significance of the 1965 anti-Hindi agitation for Indian federalism and language policy. (150 words) 10 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: The 1965 anti-Hindi agitation in Tamil Nadu was the sharpest test of the Union's language bargain — and its peaceful resolution set the template for Indian linguistic federalism.
  2. The trigger: the approaching end of English as an official language (Art 343, 15-year clause) raised fears of permanent northern advantage in jobs & administration.
  3. The response (the core): assurances of continued English, the Official Languages Act amendment (1967) & the three-language formula defused the crisis without coercion.
  4. Federal significance: it entrenched the principle that no single language dominates, and channelled dissent into electoral politics — propelling the DMK to power (1967).
  5. Critical edge: the compromise was durable but not final — imposition fears periodically recur (NEP three-language debate).
  6. Conclusion: the agitation proved accommodation, not imposition, is the way to hold a multilingual union together.
