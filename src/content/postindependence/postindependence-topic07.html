Chapter 7: The End of One-Party Dominance & the Rise of Multi-Party Politics

For two decades the Congress was less a party than a "system" — the umbrella under which all of India's interests bargained. Between 1967 and 1971 that system shattered & remade itself around one leader. This file covers Rajni Kothari's "Congress system" & the causes of its decline, the momentous 1969 Congress split (Indira vs the Syndicate, bank nationalisation & the abolition of privy purses), the Garibi Hatao populism, the 1971 general-election landslide, and the emergence of a competitive multi-party system with strong regional & opposition parties — with tables, flowcharts and PYQ-pattern practice + full model answers.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames the End of Congress Dominance

This chapter is tested as the transformation of the party system — how the all-accommodating Congress gave way both to a personalised Congress and to genuine multi-party competition. Sort your prep:

  • Static/institutional — the concepts & events: the Congress system (Kothari), the 1969 split (Congress-O vs Congress-R), bank nationalisation (July 1969), the abolition of privy purses (26th Amendment, 1971), the 1971 election. High-yield recall.
  • The contest — Indira Gandhi vs the Syndicate (Nijalingappa, Morarji Desai) over the presidential election (V.V. Giri vs Sanjiva Reddy) & economic policy. Who split from whom.
  • Analytical (GS-I) — whether the shift deepened democracy (real competition, pro-poor policy) or weakened it (personality cult, centralisation, decline of the party). Needs idea + example + judgement.

Territorial note: Maps in this file show India with its full official boundaries — Jammu & Kashmir & Ladakh (including PoK & Aksai Chin) are integral parts of India.

1. Overview: From System to Split

The years 1967–71 transformed Indian politics twice over — first breaking the Congress's monopoly, then rebuilding a new, personalised Congress dominance around Indira Gandhi.

  • The old order: a dominant Congress that absorbed every interest & faction — Rajni Kothari's "Congress system".
  • The break (1967–69): electoral setbacks & the 1969 split ended the organisational bosses' control & the old consensual party.
  • The remaking (1969–71): Indira Gandhi rebuilt dominance on populism & personality — bank nationalisation, privy-purse abolition, Garibi Hatao & the 1971 landslide.
  • The lasting change: a genuinely competitive multi-party system emerged alongside a more centralised, plebiscitary Congress — both features of politics ever since.
One-line frame: the Congress system did not simply die — it was replaced by two things at once: a competitive multi-party democracy and a personalised Congress built on direct appeal to the masses.

2. The "Congress System" (Rajni Kothari)

Political scientist Rajni Kothari coined the influential term "the Congress system" to explain how one party could dominate a competitive democracy for two decades. Understand it before you can explain its decline.

  • A one-party-dominant, not one-party, system: elections were free & opposition parties existed, but the Congress won consistently at the Centre & in most states.
  • An umbrella of all interests: the Congress contained factions of every ideology (left, right, caste, region) — internal competition substituted for inter-party competition.
  • Factions as safety valves: disputes were settled within the party; the opposition acted as "parties of pressure" influencing Congress factions rather than replacing the Congress.
  • Consensus & accommodation: the system worked by bargaining & co-opting dissent, giving India stability without dictatorship.
GS-I hook: the Congress system explains the paradox of a competitive democracy dominated by one party — its genius was accommodation; its vulnerability was that once factions could no longer be contained, the whole edifice split.

3. Why the System Declined

Several forces, converging in the late 1960s, eroded the Congress system — the causes examiners want you to list & explain.

CauseExplanation
Loss of the foundersThe deaths of Nehru (1964) & Shastri (1966) removed unifying figures; the party's moral capital from the freedom struggle faded.
Economic distressThe food crisis, 1966 devaluation, inflation & unemployment eroded popular faith & produced the 1967 setback.
Factionalism & the SyndicateThe organisational bosses' grip provoked a leadership struggle with Indira Gandhi that the party could no longer contain.
Rise of the oppositionSocialist, Jana Sangh, Swatantra & regional parties matured, offering real alternatives; 1967 gave them state power.
New social assertionBackward classes, regional & linguistic groups sought direct representation the old umbrella could no longer accommodate.
One-line frame: the system declined because the glue of accommodation failed — economic crisis, the loss of founders & an uncontainable leadership struggle turned intra-party competition into an open split.

4. The 1969 Congress Split

The decisive rupture came in 1969, when the long tension between Indira Gandhi & the Syndicate broke into an open split — the single most important event in the chapter.

Fig 7.1 — The 1969 split: Congress (O) under the Syndicate vs Congress (R) under Indira Gandhi, triggered by the presidential election. Schematic.
  • The trigger — the 1969 Presidential election: the Syndicate's official candidate was N. Sanjiva Reddy; Indira backed V.V. Giri as an independent and issued a "conscience vote" call. Giri won — a direct defeat for the bosses.
  • The split: the party formally divided into Congress (O) "Organisation" (the Syndicate & Morarji Desai) & Congress (R) "Requisitionists" (Indira Gandhi). Indira's faction commanded the majority of MPs.
  • The stakes: Indira cast the fight as progressive pro-poor policy vs conservative bosses, seizing the ideological high ground with bank nationalisation just days before.
GS-I hook: the 1969 split ended the era of the collective party bosses & began the era of a single, dominant leader appealing directly to the people — the decisive shift from organisational to personalised, plebiscitary politics.

5. Bank Nationalisation (1969)

Indira's boldest weapon in the split was a dramatic economic move that positioned her as the champion of the poor.

  • In July 1969, the government nationalised 14 major private banks (those with deposits above a threshold), overriding Finance Minister Morarji Desai (whom she had removed).
  • Stated aims: to direct credit to agriculture, small industry & the neglected priority sectors; to expand rural banking & break the hold of big business over finance.
  • Political effect: a hugely popular, "pro-poor" masterstroke that gave Indira the ideological upper hand in the split & a direct connection with the masses.
  • Legal battle: the Supreme Court initially struck down the manner of nationalisation (R.C. Cooper case, 1970) on compensation grounds; the government re-legislated — part of the wider executive-judiciary conflict of the era.
  • The specifics: the 14 banks each had deposits above ₹50 crore (~85% of all deposits); the ordinance date was 19 July 1969. A second wave on 15 April 1980 nationalised 6 more banks (deposits above ₹200 crore).
One-line frame: bank nationalisation fused economic policy & political strategy — it advanced financial inclusion while making Indira the undisputed leader of a pro-poor Congress against the conservative Syndicate.

6. Abolition of Privy Purses

The second populist thrust targeted the former princes — completing the unfinished business of integration (Chapter 2) & reinforcing the egalitarian image.

  • Background: at integration, the princes had been guaranteed privy purses (annual payments) & certain privileges in exchange for acceding — a promise embedded in the Constitution.
  • The move: Indira's government sought to abolish the privy purses & privileges as anachronistic in a democratic, egalitarian republic.
  • Judicial setback: a first attempt (a presidential order, 1970) was struck down by the Supreme Court (Madhav Rao Scindia case); after the 1971 mandate the government passed the 26th Constitutional Amendment (1971), abolishing them.
  • Significance: another popular, egalitarian measure that consolidated Indira's pro-poor credentials & fed the executive-judiciary tussle over Parliament's amending power.
  • The detail: ~278 ruling families drew purses (Articles 291 & 362); the first bid failed in the Rajya Sabha by one vote (2 Sep 1970), and the 26th Amendment (assent 28 Dec 1971) inserted Article 363A abolishing them.
GS-I hook: bank nationalisation & privy-purse abolition were twin populist strokes that completed the socio-economic side of integration while arming Indira politically — and both fed the constitutional battle over property, compensation & Parliament's power that culminated in Kesavananda Bharati (1973).

7. Garibi Hatao & the New Populism

Indira welded these measures into a single, electrifying slogan that redefined Indian electoral politics.

  • "Garibi Hatao" (Remove Poverty): the 1971 campaign slogan promised direct action against poverty — appealing over the heads of state bosses & intermediaries straight to the poor.
  • A new kind of politics: it shifted the basis of support from local notables & caste networks to a direct, plebiscitary bond between a national leader & the masses.
  • The coalition of the poor: it drew together the poor, minorities, Dalits & sections of the backward classes into a new Congress vote bank.
  • The critique: critics argued the slogan promised more than it delivered & concentrated power in one leader while weakening party & institutions.
One-line frame: "Garibi Hatao" turned elections into a referendum on one leader's promise to the poor — a populist template of direct leader-mass appeal that Indian politics has used ever since.

8. The 1971 Election Landslide

The gamble paid off spectacularly. Indira called early Lok Sabha elections (decoupling them from the state polls) & won a commanding mandate.

  • The result: Congress (R) won a two-thirds majority in the Lok Sabha, crushing the Congress (O) & the "Grand Alliance" of opposition parties (whose slogan was "Indira Hatao").
  • The meaning: Indira's Congress (R) was now indisputably the Congress; the Syndicate & the old guard were finished as a national force.
  • Reinforced by 1971 war: the decisive victory over Pakistan & the creation of Bangladesh (later in 1971) took Indira's prestige to its zenith — and the 1972 state elections completed the sweep.
  • The new dominance: a rebuilt Congress dominance, but now centralised around Indira personally rather than the old federal, factional party.
GS-I hook: 1971 shows dominance reconstituted on a new basis — personality, populism & a direct mass mandate — the "second dominant-party phase" that soon slid toward over-centralisation & the Emergency.

9. The Rise of the Multi-Party System

Even as Indira rebuilt Congress dominance, the deeper structural trend was the maturing of a competitive multi-party system — the enduring transformation of these years.

FeatureDetail
Real opposition1967 gave opposition parties state power; they were now credible governing alternatives, not mere "pressure parties".
Ideological polesA clearer spectrum emerged — Jana Sangh (right), Swatantra (free-market), the socialists & communists (left) & regional parties.
Coalition experienceSVD governments & later the Grand Alliance built the habits of coalition bargaining.
RegionalisationStrong state parties (DMK, Akali Dal, later TDP, AGP) made politics increasingly state-specific.
One-line frame: beneath Indira's personal dominance, India was becoming a genuine multi-party democracy — the two trends coexisted, and the multi-party trend ultimately prevailed after 1989.

11. Assessment: Democracy Deepened or Weakened?

The central Mains debate: did the end of one-party dominance strengthen or damage Indian democracy? The mature answer holds both.

Deepened democracyWeakened institutions
Genuine multi-party competition & credible alternatives emerged.Power centralised around Indira; the Congress organisation withered.
Pro-poor policies (bank nationalisation) advanced inclusion.Personality cult & plebiscitary politics eroded inner-party democracy.
The poor, minorities & backward classes gained political voice.Executive-judiciary conflict & misuse of Article 356 grew.
Opposition governments & coalitions matured federalism.Over-centralisation slid toward the 1975 Emergency.
One-line frame: the transformation was double-edged — it made politics more competitive & inclusive, yet it also concentrated power in one leader, planting the seeds of the Emergency.

12. Legacy & Continuities

The patterns set in 1967–71 still structure Indian politics — useful for contemporary depth.

  • Populism & direct appeal: the "Garibi Hatao" template of a leader appealing over parties to the masses recurs in every major campaign since.
  • Financial inclusion: bank nationalisation's legacy runs to today's Jan Dhan accounts & priority-sector lending.
  • Multi-party & coalition norm: the competitive, regionalised system that matured here became the default after 1989.
  • Personalisation vs institutions: the tension between charismatic leadership & strong institutions — first sharpened here — remains a live concern.
GS-I / GS-II hook: frame today's leader-centred campaigns, financial-inclusion drives & coalition/federal politics as descendants of 1967–71 — the structural DNA of modern Indian democracy.

13. Current Affairs Link

Connect the chapter to recent debates to signal an updated preparation.

  • Financial inclusion: Jan Dhan, priority-sector lending & bank consolidation trace their logic to 1969 nationalisation.
  • Populism & welfare politics: DBT, freebies & welfare-scheme debates echo the "Garibi Hatao" model of direct leader-to-poor appeal.
  • Party organisation vs personality: debates on inner-party democracy & personalised leadership descend from the post-1969 shift.
  • Coalition & regional politics: the multi-party bargaining that structures current national politics matured in this period.
How to use in the exam: a line tying 1969 bank nationalisation to Jan Dhan, or "Garibi Hatao" to today's welfare politics, shows the examiner you grasp these years as the origin of enduring patterns.

14. Prelims PYQs & Practice

Note on sourcing: the questions below are exam-pattern practice MCQs built strictly on this chapter's high-yield facts, deliberately left yearless (labelled "Model question") and not attributed to any fabricated year. Use them for concept-testing.

Model question — no fixed year

The term "the Congress system" to describe one-party dominance in India was coined by:

  • (a) Bipan Chandra
  • (b) Rajni Kothari
  • (c) Granville Austin
  • (d) Paul Brass

Answer: (b). Rajni Kothari coined "the Congress system" to describe a competitive democracy dominated by one accommodative, faction-ridden party.

Model question — no fixed year

The immediate trigger for the 1969 Congress split was the contest over:

  • (a) The rupee devaluation
  • (b) The 1969 Presidential election (Sanjiva Reddy vs V.V. Giri)
  • (c) The abolition of privy purses
  • (d) The choice of Prime Minister

Answer: (b). Indira backed V.V. Giri (who won) against the Syndicate's official candidate N. Sanjiva Reddy, precipitating the split into Congress (O) & Congress (R).

Model question — no fixed year

How many major private banks were nationalised in July 1969?

  • (a) 6
  • (b) 14
  • (c) 20
  • (d) 8

Answer: (b). Fourteen major banks were nationalised in July 1969 (a further six were nationalised in 1980).

Model question — no fixed year

The privy purses of the former princes were abolished by which Constitutional Amendment?

  • (a) 24th Amendment
  • (b) 25th Amendment
  • (c) 26th Amendment (1971)
  • (d) 42nd Amendment

Answer: (c). After an earlier presidential order was struck down, the 26th Amendment (1971) abolished the privy purses & privileges of the former rulers.

Model question — no fixed year

The slogan "Garibi Hatao" is associated with which election?

  • (a) 1967
  • (b) 1971
  • (c) 1977
  • (d) 1980

Answer: (b). "Garibi Hatao" was Indira Gandhi's slogan in the 1971 Lok Sabha election, which her Congress (R) won by a two-thirds majority.

Model question — no fixed year

After the 1969 split, the faction led by Indira Gandhi was known as:

  • (a) Congress (O)
  • (b) Congress (R)
  • (c) Congress (I)
  • (d) Congress (U)

Answer: (b). Indira's faction was Congress (R) "Requisitionists"; the Syndicate's was Congress (O) "Organisation". "Congress (I)" came later, after the 1978 split.

Prelims — anticipated themes

Cooper case); abolition of privy purses (26th Amendment 1971; Madhav Rao Scindia case); Garibi Hatao & the 1971 landslide; Grand Alliance ("Indira Hatao"); Jana Sangh, Swatantra, CPI-M (1964).

15. Mains PYQs — Model Answers

Note on sourcing: the questions below are exam-pattern practice questions on this chapter's core analytical themes (left yearless, not attributed to a fabricated year), each with a full answer skeleton.

Model question — no fixed year

Explain the concept of the "Congress system" and analyse the reasons for its decline by the late 1960s. (250 words) 15 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Rajni Kothari's "Congress system" described a competitive democracy dominated by one accommodative party — a model that held for two decades before collapsing in the late 1960s.
  2. How it worked: an umbrella of all factions & interests, intra-party competition substituting for inter-party contest, dissent co-opted rather than crushed.
  3. Causes of decline (the core): the deaths of Nehru & Shastri, economic distress (food crisis, devaluation), uncontainable factionalism (Indira vs Syndicate) & a maturing opposition that won states in 1967.
  4. The rupture: the 1969 split into Congress (O) & (R) ended the system of bosses & accommodation.
  5. Critical edge: dominance was rebuilt around Indira personally — competitive democracy grew, but so did centralisation.
  6. Conclusion: the system declined because accommodation failed — replaced by both multi-party competition & a personalised Congress.
Model question — no fixed year

"The 1969 Congress split transformed the nature of Indian politics." Critically examine. (250 words) 15 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: The 1969 split was not a routine faction fight but a turning point that replaced organisational politics with personalised, plebiscitary leadership.
  2. The trigger & split: the presidential contest (Giri vs Sanjiva Reddy) divided the party into Congress (O) & (R); Indira's faction held the majority.
  3. The transformation (the core): power shifted from the collective Syndicate to a single leader appealing directly to the masses via bank nationalisation, privy-purse abolition & Garibi Hatao.
  4. Consequences: the 1971 landslide, a rebuilt but centralised Congress, & the decline of the party organisation.
  5. Critical edge: the same centralisation weakened inner-party democracy & institutions, sliding toward the Emergency.
  6. Conclusion: the split remade Indian politics around personality & populism — a double-edged transformation.
Model question — no fixed year

Assess the objectives and impact of bank nationalisation (1969). (150 words) 10 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: The 1969 nationalisation of 14 banks was both an economic-inclusion measure & a decisive political stroke in the Congress split.
  2. Objectives: direct credit to agriculture, small industry & priority sectors; expand rural banking; curb big-business control of finance.
  3. Impact (the core): massive branch expansion, rural & priority-sector lending & deeper financial inclusion — the ancestor of today's Jan Dhan.
  4. Political effect: established Indira as a pro-poor leader & won her the 1969 split & 1971 mandate.
  5. Critical edge: it also bred inefficiency, political lending & NPAs — costs later addressed by reform.
  6. Conclusion: a genuinely inclusive step, though its statist model needed later correction.
Model question — no fixed year

Did the end of one-party dominance strengthen or weaken Indian democracy? Discuss. (150 words) 10 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: The end of Congress dominance was double-edged — it deepened competition even as it concentrated power in one leader.
  2. Strengthened democracy: credible opposition & coalitions, pro-poor policies, & political voice for the poor, minorities & backward classes.
  3. Weakened institutions (the core): personality cult, a withered party organisation, executive-judiciary conflict & over-centralisation.
  4. The tipping point: the same centralisation culminated in the 1975 Emergency.
  5. Critical edge: the multi-party trend ultimately prevailed after 1989, restoring balance.
  6. Conclusion: on balance it deepened democratic competition, but the institutional costs were real & near-fatal.
