The Green Revolution in India

How a nation living “ship to mouth” on imported grain in the mid-1960s became self-sufficient in food within a decade — and the ecological and social bills that came due later.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames the Green Revolution

The Green Revolution is tested as India’s great technological (as opposed to institutional) transformation of agriculture — a success in output that carried regional, social and ecological costs. Sort your prep into three buckets:

  • Static / factual recall — HYV seeds, Borlaug and M.S. Swaminathan, the input package (seed-fertiliser-water-credit-MSP), the IADP, food self-sufficiency, the crops (wheat, then rice) and regions (Punjab, Haryana, western UP). Names, components.
  • Structural / policy — how a state-supported package (MSP, FCI, buffer stocks, subsidised inputs) delivered the yield jump, and why it concentrated in irrigated, wheat-growing regions.
  • Analytical (GS-I / GS-III) — the balance sheet: food security vs regional disparity, rising inequality and grave ecological costs; and the case for a “second” or “evergreen” revolution. Idea + example + judgement.

Framing note: Contrast with the previous chapter — land reform changed who owns land (institutional), the Green Revolution changed how much it produces (technological). A complete answer links the two.

1. Setting: the food crisis

The mid-1960s were India’s food nadir. Two successive droughts (1965 and 1966), a stagnant traditional agriculture and a rising population left the country dependent on food aid — the humiliating “ship-to-mouth” existence of importing American wheat under PL-480, with all the political leverage that gave Washington.

PressureDetail
Droughts (1965–66)Consecutive monsoon failures caused near-famine conditions.
PL-480 dependenceReliance on subsidised US wheat imports — and its political conditionalities.
Stagnant yieldsTraditional varieties had hit a productivity ceiling.
Population growthDemand rising faster than domestic supply.
GS-I hook: Frame the Green Revolution as a response to a national-security crisis — food dependence was strategic vulnerability. Self-sufficiency was as much about sovereignty as about hunger.

2. Origins & the decision to shift

The response was a decisive policy shift from a “community development” approach spread thinly across the country to a concentrated, technology-led strategy targeting the most promising areas — associated with Agriculture Minister C. Subramaniam and the “New Agricultural Strategy.”

  • IADP (1960–61): the Intensive Agricultural District Programme — concentrating inputs in selected, well-endowed districts.
  • The New Agricultural Strategy (1966–67): adoption of HYV seeds plus a package of complementary inputs.
  • Political will: a conscious choice to prioritise output in high-potential regions over spreading resources evenly — efficiency over equity.
Critical edge: The founding choice — concentrate on the best-endowed regions — delivered rapid results but built regional disparity into the strategy from the outset. Note this trade-off early.

3. The technology package

The Green Revolution was not a single seed but a package — high-yielding varieties worked only when combined with assured water, fertiliser, credit and price support. Remove any element and the yields collapsed.

Fig 14.1 — The Green Revolution as an integrated input package.
InputRole
HYV seedsDwarf, fertiliser-responsive wheat (and later rice) varieties with far higher yields.
Chemical fertiliserNitrogen/phosphate/potash to realise the seeds’ yield potential.
IrrigationAssured water (canals, tube wells) — the make-or-break input.
PesticidesProtection of high-value, uniform crops from pests.
Credit & MSPInstitutional credit for inputs; minimum support prices to guarantee returns.
MechanisationTractors, threshers and pumps to intensify cultivation.
Prelims anchor: Remember that HYV seeds are “fertiliser-responsive” and irrigation-dependent — which is exactly why the revolution concentrated in irrigated regions.

4. Borlaug, Swaminathan & the architects

The revolution had clear intellectual authors — the American agronomist Norman Borlaug, who bred the dwarf wheat varieties, and M.S. Swaminathan, who adapted and championed them in India, backed by policymakers like C. Subramaniam.

  • Norman Borlaug: the “father of the Green Revolution” worldwide; developed high-yield, disease-resistant dwarf wheat; won the 1970 Nobel Peace Prize.
  • M.S. Swaminathan: the “father of the Indian Green Revolution”; adapted Borlaug’s wheat to Indian conditions and drove its adoption; later champion of the “evergreen revolution.”
  • C. Subramaniam: the Agriculture Minister whose political backing enabled the New Agricultural Strategy.
  • Institutions: ICAR, agricultural universities (e.g. Pantnagar, PAU Ludhiana) and IARI drove research and extension.
One-line frame: Borlaug supplied the seed, Swaminathan supplied the science-and-advocacy in India, and the state supplied the price-and-input support — the three had to combine.

5. The institutional support system

Behind the technology stood a state architecture of procurement, storage and price support that made high-yield farming economically viable — and which persists as the core of India’s food policy.

Institution (1965)Function
Food Corporation of India (FCI)Procurement, storage and distribution of foodgrain.
Agricultural Prices Commission (now CACP)Recommends Minimum Support Prices (MSP).
MSP + buffer stockGuaranteed price to farmers; buffer for food security and the PDS.
Public Distribution System (PDS)Channelled procured grain to consumers — the demand side of food security.
GS-III crossover: The FCI–MSP–buffer–PDS system was built during the Green Revolution and remains the backbone (and the subject of reform debates) of India’s food-security architecture today.

6. Achievements

The core achievement was dramatic and swift: within a decade India moved from famine-threatened import-dependence to food self-sufficiency, ending the strategic humiliation of PL-480.

  • Food self-sufficiency: wheat output more than tripled between the mid-1960s and the 1980s; India stopped importing grain and built buffer stocks.
  • Farmer prosperity: incomes rose sharply for farmers in the Green-Revolution belt, creating a prosperous agrarian class.
  • Strategic autonomy: food security ended dependence on hostile food-aid conditionalities.
  • Rural economy: spin-off demand for tractors, pumps, fertiliser and agro-processing spurred rural industry.
GS-I hook: Lead any positive assessment with the twin achievements — ending hunger-imports and winning strategic food autonomy. These are the reasons the Green Revolution is rightly celebrated.

7. Regional & crop concentration

The gains were spectacular but narrow. The revolution was concentrated in a few well-irrigated regions and a few crops — deepening the gap between the “green” and the “non-green” parts of India.

DimensionConcentration
RegionsPunjab, Haryana and western Uttar Pradesh — the irrigated north-west.
CropsWheat first; rice later — pulses, oilseeds and coarse cereals lagged.
Left behindEastern India, rainfed and dryland regions saw little benefit.
ResultWidening inter-regional and inter-crop disparities.
Prelims anchor: The Green Revolution was primarily a wheat revolution concentrated in Punjab/Haryana/western UP. Pulses and oilseeds were largely bypassed — a frequent exam point.

8. Social consequences

The technology was, in principle, “scale-neutral” but in practice “resource-biased” — it favoured those who could afford the input package. This reshaped rural society, with mixed effects.

  • Inequality: larger, irrigated farmers gained most; small and marginal farmers and the landless benefited far less — widening rural inequality.
  • Labour: more employment in peak seasons, but mechanisation later displaced labour; migrant labour flows grew.
  • Agrarian politics: a prosperous, assertive farmer class emerged (the base of later farmers’ movements and regional parties).
  • Regional discontent: uneven benefits fed grievances, including in Punjab (see the Punjab-crisis chapter).
Critical edge: The distributional critique — that the Green Revolution widened inequality by favouring resource-rich farmers — is essential to a balanced answer. It is the social counterpart to the ecological critique.

9. Ecological costs

The deepest long-term critique is ecological. The intensive, input-heavy model degraded the very resource base it depended on — a bill that is now falling due, especially in Punjab and Haryana.

CostManifestation
Groundwater depletionOver-extraction for paddy in Punjab/Haryana; falling water tables.
Soil degradationLoss of fertility, micronutrient deficiency, and salinisation/waterlogging from over-irrigation.
Chemical overuseFertiliser and pesticide overuse; contamination of water and food chains.
Loss of biodiversityMonoculture displaced traditional crop and seed diversity.
Stubble burningPaddy-residue burning — a major air-pollution source in north India.
GS-III crossover: The Punjab paddy–groundwater–stubble nexus is the classic case study of the Green Revolution’s ecological unsustainability — and the strongest argument for crop diversification and a “second” revolution.

10. A balanced assessment

The honest verdict weighs a genuine, historic achievement against serious costs — the Green Revolution saved India from famine but on a model that was regionally narrow, socially uneven and ecologically unsustainable.

AchievementsCosts
Food self-sufficiency; end of PL-480 dependenceRegional disparity (north-west vs east)
Strategic food autonomy; buffer stocksRising rural inequality
Rising farm incomes; rural industryGroundwater, soil and biodiversity damage
A modern, input-based agricultureCrop concentration (wheat/rice); pulses neglected
Assessment frame: The mature line: the Green Revolution was a necessary and successful response to an existential food crisis, but its long-term sustainability required corrections — in regions, crops and ecology — that were not made in time.

11. Towards a second/evergreen revolution

The response to the first revolution’s limits is the call for a second Green Revolution — extending gains to rainfed and eastern India — and Swaminathan’s “evergreen revolution”: raising productivity sustainably, without ecological harm.

  • Geographic spread: Bringing Green Revolution in Eastern India (BGREI) targets eastern states with untapped potential.
  • Evergreen revolution: productivity growth through conservation agriculture, precision farming, biotechnology and micro-irrigation — “more from less.”
  • Crop diversification: shifting from water-guzzling paddy to pulses, oilseeds, millets and horticulture.
  • Climate resilience: drought/flood-tolerant varieties and sustainable water use.
GS-III hook: The “evergreen revolution” (M.S. Swaminathan) is the key phrase — productivity in perpetuity without ecological damage. Cite it as the corrective vision to the first revolution.

13. Legacy

The Green Revolution’s legacy is double-edged and enduring — it made India food-secure and built its food institutions, but bequeathed an ecological and equity agenda still unresolved.

  • Food security: the foundation of the world’s largest food-security programme (NFSA, 2013).
  • Institutions: the FCI–MSP–PDS architecture endures and is debated.
  • Unfinished agenda: sustainability, diversification, income (not just output) and regional balance remain the tasks ahead.
  • Doubling farmer incomes: the current policy frame shifts the goal from production to farmer welfare.
GS-I hook: Frame the arc: the first revolution won the battle for food; the unfinished battle is for sustainable, equitable and income-secure farming.

14. Contemporary relevance

The Green Revolution’s themes dominate today’s agricultural debates.

  • MSP & farm policy: demands for a legal MSP guarantee (echoing the farmers’ protests) trace directly to the price-support system built in 1965.
  • Millets & diversification: the International Year of Millets (2023) and Shree Anna push respond to the nutritional and ecological narrowing the Green Revolution caused.
  • Groundwater & stubble: Punjab’s water crisis and Delhi’s winter smog keep the ecological costs in the headlines.
  • Swaminathan’s recognition: M.S. Swaminathan was awarded the Bharat Ratna (2024), renewing focus on his “evergreen” vision.
Answer value-add: Linking the millets mission or the MSP debate to the Green Revolution’s legacy shows the direct line from 1960s policy to current controversy.

15. Prelims practice (PYQ pattern)

The questions below follow the UPSC prelims pattern for this theme. Self-authored practice questions are marked as model questions with no fixed year; genuinely dated UPSC questions carry a year.

Q1. The Green Revolution in India was primarily based on:

  • Select the correct option.
  • (a) Land redistribution
  • (b) High-yielding variety (HYV) seeds and a package of inputs — correct
  • (c) Cooperative farming
  • (d) Organic cultivation

Answer: (b) HYV seeds and an input package. HYV seeds worked only with assured irrigation, fertiliser, credit and price support.

Q2. Who among the following is regarded as the “father of the Indian Green Revolution”?

  • Select the correct option.
  • (a) Norman Borlaug
  • (b) M.S. Swaminathan — correct
  • (c) Verghese Kurien
  • (d) C. Subramaniam

Answer: (b) M.S. Swaminathan. Borlaug is the global “father of the Green Revolution”; Swaminathan led it in India (Bharat Ratna, 2024).

Q3. The Green Revolution was concentrated primarily in which region and crop?

  • Select the correct option.
  • (a) Eastern India; rice
  • (b) Punjab, Haryana and western UP; wheat — correct
  • (c) Deccan; pulses
  • (d) Kerala; plantation crops

Answer: (b) Punjab/Haryana/western UP; wheat. It was primarily a wheat revolution in the irrigated north-west; pulses and oilseeds lagged.

Q4. Match the following revolutions with their sectors:

  1. White Revolution — Milk
  2. Blue Revolution — Fisheries
  3. Yellow Revolution — Oilseeds
  • How many of the pairs given above are correctly matched?
  • (a) Only one
  • (b) Only two
  • (c) All three — correct
  • (d) None

Answer: (c) All three. White = milk, Blue = fisheries, Yellow = oilseeds — all correctly matched.

Q5. The “evergreen revolution,” a term associated with M.S. Swaminathan, refers to:

  • Select the correct option.
  • (a) Afforestation drives
  • (b) Raising productivity sustainably, without ecological harm — correct
  • (c) Expansion of tea plantations
  • (d) Urban horticulture

Answer: (b) Sustainable productivity growth. The evergreen revolution seeks “more from less” — productivity in perpetuity without ecological damage.

Q6. With reference to the Green Revolution in India, consider the following: (UPSC Prelims 2014 — adapted)

  1. The introduction of HYV seeds was a key component.
  2. It significantly increased foodgrain production, especially wheat.
  3. Its benefits were evenly distributed across all regions of India.
  • Which of the statements given above are correct?
  • (a) 1 and 2 only — correct
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Answer: (a) 1 and 2 only. Benefits were not evenly distributed — they concentrated in the irrigated north-west, so statement 3 is wrong.

16. Mains practice + model answers

Model questions in the UPSC GS-I / GS-III idiom, each with a full answer skeleton. These are practice questions with no fixed year unless a genuine dated UPSC question is cited.

Q1. The Green Revolution solved India’s food crisis but created new problems. Critically examine. (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Frame the mid-1960s food crisis (droughts, PL-480 dependence) and the technology-led response.
  2. The achievement: HYV package delivered food self-sufficiency, buffer stocks and strategic food autonomy within a decade.
  3. The new problems (the core): regional concentration (north-west vs east), crop narrowing (wheat/rice over pulses/oilseeds), rising rural inequality, and grave ecological costs (groundwater, soil, stubble).
  4. Why the problems arose: the founding choice to concentrate inputs in resource-rich regions and the input-intensive model built disparity and unsustainability in from the start.
  5. The correctives: the second Green Revolution (BGREI), the evergreen revolution, crop diversification and micro-irrigation.
  6. Conclusion: a necessary success that won food security, but whose model now demands ecological and equity correction — the unfinished revolution.

Q2. “The Green Revolution was scale-neutral in theory but resource-biased in practice.” Discuss its social consequences. (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Explain scale-neutrality (HYV seeds work on any plot) vs resource-bias (only those who afford inputs benefit).
  2. Who gained: larger, irrigated, credit-worthy farmers captured most of the gains — a prosperous agrarian class emerged.
  3. Who lagged (the core): small/marginal farmers and the landless benefited far less; inter-regional and intra-rural inequality widened.
  4. Labour effects: seasonal employment rose but later mechanisation displaced labour; migration flows grew.
  5. Political fallout: an assertive farmer class fuelled farmers’ movements and regional politics; uneven benefits fed regional discontent (e.g. Punjab).
  6. Conclusion: the technology’s neutrality was defeated by unequal access to the input package — equity required institutional support the strategy neglected.

Q3. Examine the ecological sustainability of the Green Revolution model and the case for an “evergreen revolution.” (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Frame the tension — a productivity model that degrades its own resource base.
  2. The ecological bill: groundwater depletion, soil fertility loss and salinisation, fertiliser/pesticide overuse, biodiversity loss, and stubble burning.
  3. The Punjab case (the core): the paddy–groundwater–stubble nexus is the sharpest illustration of unsustainability.
  4. The evergreen alternative: Swaminathan’s vision — conservation agriculture, precision farming, micro-irrigation, biotechnology and diversification for “more from less.”
  5. Policy levers: MSP reform to favour pulses/millets, water pricing, crop diversification incentives, and climate-resilient varieties.
  6. Conclusion: sustaining food security now requires shifting from an input-intensive to an ecologically regenerative model — the evergreen revolution.

Q4. How did the institutional architecture built during the Green Revolution shape India’s food-security policy? (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Frame the 1965 institutions (FCI, Agricultural Prices Commission) as the enduring backbone of food policy.
  2. The architecture: MSP (incentive to produce), FCI procurement and storage, buffer stocks (security), and the PDS (distribution).
  3. Its achievement (the core): it linked production incentives to food security, underpinning the world’s largest food programme (NFSA, 2013).
  4. Its problems: skewed procurement (wheat/rice, north-west), fiscal cost of subsidies, storage losses and market distortion.
  5. Reform debates: legal MSP demands, diversification of procurement, direct benefit transfer and buffer-stock rationalisation.
  6. Conclusion: the Green Revolution’s institutions solved yesterday’s scarcity but need reform to serve today’s goals of nutrition, diversification and farmer income.
