Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames Social Movements

Social movements are tested as the underside of the post-1947 story: while formal politics ran through parties and Parliament, millions pressed claims through mobilisation outside the ballot box. Sort your prep into three buckets:

  • Static / factual (Prelims): who led which movement, its year, place and slogan — Chipko (1973, Reni), Narmada Bachao Andolan (Medha Patkar), Dalit Panthers (1972, Bombay), MKSS & the RTI campaign (Aruna Roy, Rajasthan). Match movement → leader → demand.
  • Conceptual (typology): the distinction between old movements (class-based, redistributive — peasant, worker) and new social movements (identity, rights, ecology, quality-of-life). Know what makes NSMs "new".
  • Analytical (GS-I): what these movements reveal about Indian democracy — deepening participation, the limits of the developmental state, the tension between growth and justice, and how movements feed back into law (FRA 2006, RTI 2005, PESA 1996).

Framing note: Do not memorise movements as a disconnected list. UPSC rewards the ability to connect a movement to the structural gap it exposed and the legislative or institutional change it eventually produced.

1. Overview & Why It Matters

Independence transferred power to elected governments, but it did not automatically deliver land to the tenant, forests to the adivasi, dignity to the Dalit, or a voice to women. Where the state's development model created losers — the displaced, the excluded, the ecologically dispossessed — people organised collective action outside routine electoral politics to demand redress. That is a social movement: sustained, organised, collective mobilisation for or against social change, drawing on shared identity and grievance.

  • Why post-1947 is distinct: the freedom struggle had subordinated sectional demands to the national cause; after 1947 those suppressed demands — caste, gender, ecology, region — surfaced as autonomous movements.
  • Two waves: a first wave of class-based struggles (peasant, worker, land) in the 1950s–60s; a second wave of identity- and rights-based "new" movements from the 1970s, sharpened by the Emergency and the failures of the developmental state.
  • Democratic significance: movements are the "unofficial opposition" — they widen participation, articulate claims parties ignore, and often force legislation the state would not otherwise pass.
GS-I hook: Treat social movements as evidence that Indian democracy deepened from below — not merely through the vote, but through organised claim-making that repeatedly bent policy toward justice.

2. Typology: Old vs New Social Movements

The single most examinable concept here is the distinction between "old" and "new" social movements (NSMs). It is not about chronology alone — it is about the basis of mobilisation and the goal.

DimensionOld social movementsNew social movements (NSMs)
Basis of mobilisationEconomic class (peasant, worker)Identity, values, quality of life (caste, gender, ecology, region)
Core goalRedistribution — land, wages, ownershipRecognition, rights, autonomy, dignity
LeadershipOften party-linked (Left, Kisan Sabha)Autonomous, non-party; NGOs, activists, networks
MethodStrikes, land occupation, agitationsSatyagraha, PILs, media, symbolic action, campaigns
ExamplesTelangana, Tebhaga, Naxalbari, trade unionsChipko, NBA, women's, Dalit, RTI movements
  • What makes NSMs "new": they mobilise around non-class identities, prize autonomy from the party system, use rights-and-law tactics, and target the quality of development, not just its distribution.
  • Caution: the line blurs in India — a farmers' movement is class-based (old) yet uses NSM-style media tactics; an adivasi movement mixes livelihood (old) with identity and ecology (new).
Prelims trap: "New" does not mean "recent" — a 2020 trade-union strike is still an old (class-based) movement, while 1970s Chipko is a new social movement. The label tracks the basis, not the date.

3. Roots in the Nehruvian & Post-Emergency Years

Understanding why the second wave erupted in the 1970s requires seeing what the first three decades did — and failed — to do.

  • Nehruvian developmentalism: a top-down, state-led model prioritising heavy industry, big dams ("temples of modern India") and planning. It delivered growth but displaced adivasis, submerged forests, and bypassed the caste and gender questions.
  • The first wave (1950s–60s): peasant and land movements — the tail of Telangana, Tebhaga's memory, and the Naxalbari uprising (1967) that launched the Naxalite movement — pressed the unfinished agrarian question.
  • The Emergency (1975–77) as a hinge: the suspension of civil liberties, forced sterilisation and slum demolitions discredited the state, energised civil-liberties and grassroots activism, and seeded a generation of non-party political activists (many from the JP movement) who fanned out into environmental, women's and Dalit organising.
  • The "non-party political process": disillusion with both Congress and the organised Left pushed activists toward autonomous, issue-based movements — the hallmark of the NSM wave.
Link: The Emergency did not just reshape party politics (see the JP Movement & Emergency chapter) — it midwifed the new social movements by creating a cadre of activists who distrusted the state and the party alike.

4. The Environmental Movement

India's environmental movement is distinctive: unlike the West's "post-material" ecology, it is a livelihood ecology — the poor defending forests, water and land on which their survival depends. This is Ramachandra Guha's "environmentalism of the poor".

Fig 4.1 — The environmental movement's trajectory, 1973–2006.
MovementYear / placeLeadersCause & method
Chipko1973, Reni & Mandal, UttarakhandSunderlal Bahuguna, Chandi Prasad Bhatt, Gaura DeviVillagers hugged trees to stop commercial felling; Gandhian, women-led
Silent Valley1978–83, KeralaKSSP (Kerala Sastra Sahitya Parishad), SugathakumariSaved rainforest from a hydel project; won 1983—declared national park
Appiko1983, Uttara Kannada, KarnatakaPanduranga HegdeSouthern echo of Chipko; "appiko" = to hug; against forest destruction
Narmada Bachao Andolan1985–, Narmada valleyMedha Patkar, Baba AmteAgainst big-dam displacement; satyagraha, PILs, jal-samarpan
  • Chipko's dual character: Bahuguna stressed ecology ("ecology is permanent economy"); Bhatt stressed local livelihood and village forest-use rights — the movement held both.
  • Women at the centre: as primary gatherers of fuel, fodder and water, women bore forest loss first — making environmental and gender questions inseparable (ecofeminism, Vandana Shiva).
  • Policy feedback: the movements shaped the Forest Conservation Act 1980, the creation of the environment ministry (1985), and eventually the Forest Rights Act 2006.
GS-I hook: India's environmentalism is "of the poor" — frame it as a conflict over who owns and uses natural resources, not a middle-class conservation hobby. That single line separates a top-answer from a factual list.

5. Narmada Bachao Andolan & Big Dams

The Narmada Bachao Andolan (NBA) is the movement most likely to appear in a GS-I or GS-II answer, because it crystallised the central post-1947 dilemma: development for whom, at whose cost?

  • The project: the Sardar Sarovar Dam on the Narmada, part of a mega multi-dam plan, promised irrigation, drinking water and power to Gujarat — while submerging villages and displacing lakhs, disproportionately adivasis, across Madhya Pradesh, Maharashtra and Gujarat.
  • The movement: led by Medha Patkar from 1985, joined by Baba Amte and Arundhati Roy, NBA demanded first full rehabilitation, then a halt, using Gandhian satyagraha, hunger strikes, jal-samarpan (standing in rising waters) and public-interest litigation.
  • The World Bank withdrawal (1993): sustained pressure forced the Bank to pull out after the Morse Committee's damning review — a landmark for movement influence on global development finance.
  • The Supreme Court (2000): allowed construction to resume with height raised in stages conditional on rehabilitation — a partial, contested outcome. Displacement and R&R remained unresolved for many.
What NBA wonWhat it did not
Put displacement & rehabilitation (R&R) at the centre of dam debatesCould not stop the dam; height was raised
Forced the World Bank's exit (1993)Rehabilitation of oustees remained partial & delayed
Shaped the R&R and (later) LARR Act 2013 discourseThe cost-benefit logic of big dams was reasserted by courts
Balance: NBA is not simply "anti-development". Examiners reward candidates who present both the developmental case (water, power for a dry region) and the justice case (displacement without rehabilitation) — and then argue for a rights-based, participatory model of development.

6. Dalit & Anti-Caste Movements

The Dalit movement carries forward the anti-caste struggle of Jotiba Phule and B.R. Ambedkar into independent India, shifting from social reform to political assertion and identity.

  • Ambedkar's legacy: constitutional safeguards (abolition of untouchability, Art. 17; reservations), the 1956 mass conversion to Buddhism at Nagpur, and the ideological arsenal of "annihilation of caste".
  • Dalit Panthers (1972, Bombay): founded by Namdeo Dhasal, J.V. Pawar and Raja Dhale, inspired by the US Black Panthers — a militant, literary-cultural assertion against atrocities and symbolic humiliation. It reshaped Marathi Dalit literature.
  • Political turn: Kanshi Ram's BAMCEF and DS-4 built into the Bahujan Samaj Party (1984), converting social assertion into electoral power in Uttar Pradesh — the movement's most consequential institutional outcome.
  • Legal-institutional gains: the SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act 1989, the debate over sub-categorisation, and continuing mobilisation (e.g., anti-atrocity protests) keep Dalit assertion central to Indian politics.
GS-I hook: The Dalit movement's arc — from social reform (Phule–Ambedkar) to cultural assertion (Panthers) to electoral power (BSP) — is a model answer to "how did an excluded group convert protest into power?"

7. The Women's Movement

The post-1947 women's movement moved from the nationalist-era focus on legal reform toward autonomous feminist mobilisation against violence, for economic rights, and for political representation.

PhaseFocusLandmarks
1950s–60s: reform continuityLegal equalityHindu Code Bills (1955–56); constitutional equality
1970s: the turning pointStatus & violenceTowards Equality report (1974) exposed declining status; Mathura rape case (1979–80) sparked the anti-rape movement
1980s–90s: autonomous feminismDowry, sati, workAnti-dowry & anti-sati (Roop Kanwar, 1987) campaigns; SEWA's organising of informal women workers
2000s–: rights & representationLaw & political voiceDomestic Violence Act 2005; Nirbhaya case & Criminal Law Amendment 2013; Women's Reservation (Nari Shakti Vandan) Act 2023
  • The Mathura moment: the Supreme Court's acquittal in a custodial rape case triggered a nationwide anti-rape movement and a legal-reform campaign led by feminist lawyers — the birth of the autonomous women's movement.
  • Diversity, not one movement: it spans autonomous feminist groups, women in the environmental (Chipko) and Dalit movements, and organisations like SEWA (Ela Bhatt) that fused gender with livelihood.
  • Reservation: 73rd/74th Amendments (1992–93) reserved one-third of panchayat/municipal seats for women — a structural entry into local political power, later extended to Parliament/Assemblies by the 2023 Act (pending delimitation).
Nuance: Avoid treating "the women's movement" as monolithic. Show its plurality — class, caste and region cut across gender, so a Dalit-feminist critique of upper-caste feminism is a mark of a mature answer.

8. Tribal & Adivasi Movements

Tribal movements in independent India defend jal, jangal, zameen (water, forest, land) — and often, statehood and self-rule — against displacement by dams, mines and forest laws.

  • Continuity from colonial revolts: the memory of Birsa Munda's ulgulan and Santhal Hul underpins post-1947 assertion, now channelled into constitutional and movement politics.
  • Statehood movements: the long Jharkhand movement (Jharkhand Party, later JMM) culminated in the 2000 creation of Jharkhand; Chhattisgarh and the Bodoland demand in Assam share this territorial-identity logic.
  • Anti-displacement struggles: adivasis are the largest group of "development refugees" — NBA, anti-mining struggles (e.g., Niyamgiri/Dongria Kondh against bauxite mining, upheld by the 2013 gram-sabha verdict) exemplify resource defence.
  • Legislative gains: the Fifth & Sixth Schedules, PESA 1996 (extending panchayats and gram-sabha powers to Scheduled Areas), and the Forest Rights Act 2006 (recognising individual and community forest rights) are the movement's institutional harvest.
GS-I hook: Frame tribal movements as a triangle — identity (autonomy, statehood), livelihood (jal-jangal-zameen), and ecology — which is why they bridge the "old" and "new" movement categories.

9. Farmers' & Peasant Movements

Farmers' movements evolved from the redistributive peasant struggles of the 1950s–60s to the post-Green-Revolution "new farmers' movements" of prosperous cultivators demanding remunerative prices and lower input costs.

TypeEraDemandExamples
Old peasant movements1950s–60sLand, tenancy, abolition of intermediariesTelangana aftermath, Naxalbari (1967), Kisan Sabha
New farmers' movements1970s–80sRemunerative prices, cheaper inputs, loan waivers ("Bharat vs India")Shetkari Sanghatana (Sharad Joshi), BKU (Mahendra Singh Tikait), Karnataka Rajya Raitha Sangha (Nanjundaswamy)
Contemporary2010s–20sMSP, debt, land acquisition, farm-law repeal2020–21 farm-laws agitation (SKM); Kisan Long March 2018
  • "Bharat vs India": Sharad Joshi framed the divide as exploitation of the rural "Bharat" by the urban-industrial "India" through adverse terms of trade — a powerful, examinable slogan.
  • Non-party but electorally potent: the new farmers' movements were non-partisan yet forced price and subsidy concessions from every government.
  • Class caveat: critics note these movements largely represented surplus-producing landed farmers, not landless labourers — the agrarian question of the poor remained partly unaddressed.
Prelims/Mains link: Connect farmers' movements to the Green Revolution chapter — the very prosperity the GR created generated a new class of market-oriented farmers whose price demands drove the 1980s mobilisations.

10. Backward-Caste & Mandal Assertion

Parallel to the Dalit movement, the mobilisation of Other Backward Classes (OBCs) reshaped politics — peaking with the Mandal moment of 1990.

  • Southern head-start: the non-Brahmin and self-respect movements (Periyar's Dravidian movement in Tamil Nadu, reservations in Mysore) gave the south a decades-long lead in backward-caste empowerment.
  • The Mandal Commission (1980): recommended 27% reservation for OBCs in central jobs; its implementation by V.P. Singh in 1990 triggered upheaval and violent upper-caste protest.
  • Indra Sawhney (1992): the Supreme Court upheld OBC reservation, capped total reservation at 50%, and introduced the "creamy layer" exclusion — the constitutional settlement of the Mandal question.
  • Political consequence: the rise of OBC-based parties (SP, RJD, JD variants) and the "second democratic upsurge" (Yogendra Yadav) — the entry of the lower and backward castes into the heart of electoral power.
Link: The Mandal story overlaps the "Politics after Rajiv Gandhi" chapter (Mandal–Mandir) — here, read it as a social movement outcome: caste identity converted into a permanent axis of Indian democracy.

11. Civil-Liberties & RTI Movement

The Emergency's abuses birthed a civil-liberties movement; two decades later, a grassroots struggle over wages produced the Right to Information — a rare case of a social movement writing a landmark law.

  • Civil-liberties groups: PUCL (People's Union for Civil Liberties, revived 1980) and PUDR emerged from the Emergency experience to document custodial violence, encounter killings and rights violations — using PILs and reports rather than street protest.
  • The RTI story: the Mazdoor Kisan Shakti Sangathan (MKSS), led by Aruna Roy, Nikhil Dey and Shankar Singh in Rajasthan from 1990, demanded access to muster rolls to expose fake entries in famine-relief works — through the innovative jan sunwai (public hearing).
  • From village to statute: "hamara paisa, hamara hisab" (our money, our accounts) scaled into the National Campaign for People's Right to Information (NCPRI) and culminated in the Right to Information Act 2005 — a movement-authored transparency law.
  • Sibling legislations: the same rights-based network shaped NREGA 2005 (right to work) and the food-security and land-acquisition (LARR 2013) debates — the "rights-based" turn of Indian policy.
GS-I/GS-II hook: RTI is the clearest example of a social movement producing a law — use it to argue that Indian democracy is co-authored by citizens' movements, not just legislatures.

12. Assessment & Debates

How successful were India's social movements? The honest answer is: transformative in agenda-setting and law, but uneven in structural outcomes.

Strengths / achievementsLimits / criticisms
Deepened democracy — brought the excluded (Dalit, adivasi, women) into public lifeFragmentation — single-issue movements rarely coalesced into structural change
Produced landmark laws — RTI, FRA, PESA, atrocities & domestic-violence ActsImplementation gaps — laws exist but delivery lags (FRA claims pending, R&R unfinished)
Reframed "development" to include justice, ecology and dignityClass limits — some movements served the relatively better-off within a group
Sustained a vibrant non-party civil societyCo-option & NGO-isation — risk of professionalisation blunting radical edge
  • The autonomy debate: distance from parties preserved moral authority but limited the power to implement demands — the classic movement dilemma.
  • Rajni Kothari's insight: the growth of "non-party political formations" signalled a crisis of representation in the party system — movements filled the gap democratic institutions left open.
Balanced verdict: Movements were more successful at changing the terms of debate and the statute book than at transforming ground realities — a gap between recognition and redistribution that a top answer must name.

13. Legacy & Contemporary Relevance

The movements of the 1970s–90s set templates — tactics, laws and vocabularies — that structure today's mobilisations, from anti-corruption to climate to new farmers' protests.

  • The rights-based state: RTI, NREGA, FRA and the Food Security Act institutionalised the movements' language of entitlements — the enduring legislative legacy.
  • New repertoires: the India Against Corruption movement (2011) and its Lokpal demand, and the 2020–21 farm-laws agitation, borrowed the satyagraha-plus-media playbook of the earlier NSMs.
  • Digital-age mobilisation: social media, online petitions and PILs have widened participation while raising new questions of leaderless, spontaneous protest.
  • Enduring tensions: the growth-vs-justice, displacement-vs-development and identity-vs-class debates the movements opened remain the live fault-lines of Indian politics.
One-line frame: India's social movements turned the promise of the Constitution into a set of claims — and in doing so, made democracy a continuous negotiation rather than a five-yearly event.

14. Current Affairs Linkage

Keep the historical movements alive by connecting them to running debates — UPSC routinely tests the past through the present.

  • Forest Rights Act implementation: continuing litigation over eviction of "encroachers", pending community-forest-rights (CFR) claims, and the tension with conservation and infrastructure — the direct heir of the adivasi and environmental movements.
  • Women's Reservation: the Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam (2023) awaits delimitation-linked implementation — the long arc of the women's movement's representation demand.
  • Farmers' mobilisation: post-repeal demands for a legal MSP guarantee keep the new-farmers'-movement agenda current.
  • RTI under strain: debates over the RTI (Amendment) Act 2019 and vacancies in Information Commissions test the transparency movement's gains.
  • Environmental justice: climate activism, forest-clearance disputes and the "just transition" debate extend the environmentalism-of-the-poor into the climate era.
Answer tip: When a current-affairs question touches FRA, MSP, RTI or women's reservation, anchor it in the movement that produced it — historical depth lifts a routine answer into a top one.

15. Prelims PYQs & Practice

UPSC-pattern multiple-choice practice on social movements in independent India. Attempt before revealing the answer; each includes a one-line explanation.

Q1. The Chipko movement of the 1970s is correctly associated with which of the following?

  • Protecting forests from commercial felling by hugging trees
  • All of the above ✓
  • Originating in the Reni and Mandal areas of present-day Uttarakhand
  • Prominent participation of women such as Gaura Devi

Answer: b. Chipko involved villagers — women prominent — hugging trees to stop commercial felling in the Reni/Mandal region of Garhwal (Uttarakhand); all statements are correct.

Q2. Consider the following pairs of movement and leader:
1. Narmada Bachao Andolan — Medha Patkar
2. Appiko movement — Panduranga Hegde
3. Dalit Panthers — Namdeo Dhasal
Which of the pairs are correctly matched?

  • 1 and 2 only
  • 2 and 3 only
  • 1, 2 and 3 ✓
  • 1 and 3 only

Answer: c. All three are correctly matched — Patkar (NBA), Hegde (Appiko, Karnataka), Dhasal (co-founder, Dalit Panthers, 1972).

Q3. The Mazdoor Kisan Shakti Sangathan (MKSS) is historically significant primarily because it:

  • Launched the Chipko movement in Uttarakhand
  • Pioneered the grassroots campaign that led to the Right to Information Act, 2005 ✓
  • Founded the Bahujan Samaj Party
  • Led the Narmada Bachao Andolan

Answer: b. MKSS (Aruna Roy and others, Rajasthan) used jan sunwais to demand transparency, seeding the national RTI campaign that produced the 2005 Act.

Q4. With reference to "new social movements", consider the following statements:
1. They are primarily organised around economic class.
2. They typically maintain autonomy from established political parties.
Which of the statements is/are correct?

  • 1 only
  • 2 only ✓
  • Both 1 and 2
  • Neither 1 nor 2

Answer: b. NSMs mobilise around identity/values (ecology, gender, caste), not primarily class (statement 1 wrong), and prize autonomy from parties (statement 2 correct).

Q5. The Forest Rights Act, 2006 is best understood as the legislative outcome of:

  • The trade-union movement
  • The anti-dowry campaign
  • Long-running adivasi and environmental (forest-rights) movements ✓
  • The Mandal Commission agitation

Answer: c. The FRA recognises individual and community forest rights — the harvest of sustained tribal and forest-rights mobilisation.

Q6. Which of the following correctly describes the "environmentalism of the poor" thesis associated with Indian environmental movements?

  • Conservation driven mainly by urban middle-class recreation
  • Ecological struggles by the poor to defend the natural resources on which their livelihood depends ✓
  • Government afforestation programmes
  • Corporate green-branding initiatives

Answer: b. Coined in the Indian context (Guha & Martinez-Alier), it frames movements like Chipko and NBA as the poor defending livelihood resources, not post-material recreation.

Anticipated themes — high-probability areas to revise: old-vs-new social movement typology (basis = class vs identity); movement–leader–place matching (Chipko/Bahuguna-Bhatt-Gaura Devi, Silent Valley/KSSP, Appiko/Hegde, NBA/Patkar, Dalit Panthers/Dhasal, MKSS/Roy); movement–law linkages (RTI 2005, FRA 2006, PESA 1996, PoA 1989, DV Act 2005); "environmentalism of the poor"; Mandal–Indra Sawhney (27%, 50% cap, creamy layer); "Bharat vs India" (Sharad Joshi); the Towards Equality report (1974) and Mathura case as triggers of the women's movement.

16. Mains PYQs & Model Answers

Practice GS-I mains questions with full model-answer skeletons. These are model questions in the UPSC pattern; write your own version in 150–250 words.

Q1. "The new social movements of post-independence India reflected the limits of the developmental state." Critically examine. (15 marks, 250 words)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: define new social movements (NSMs) — identity/rights/ecology-based, autonomous from parties — and locate their eruption in the 1970s as a response to the Nehruvian developmental model.
  2. The developmental state's gaps (the core): top-down planning and big dams displaced adivasis (NBA), submerged forests (Chipko), and bypassed caste (Dalit) and gender (women's movement) questions — each NSM marked a specific failure of the state.
  3. How they exposed limits: they targeted the quality and justice of development, not just its pace — "development for whom, at whose cost?"
  4. Their achievements: reframed development to include ecology and dignity; produced laws (FRA, RTI, PESA); deepened participation.
  5. Critical edge: yet fragmentation, implementation gaps and class limits meant recognition often outran redistribution; autonomy limited implementing power.
  6. Conclusion: NSMs were both a critique of, and a corrective to, the developmental state — pushing India toward a rights-based, participatory model without fully realising it.

Q2. Trace the evolution of the environmental movement in India and assess its impact on policy and law. (15 marks, 250 words)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: characterise Indian environmentalism as an "environmentalism of the poor" — livelihood ecology, not post-material conservation.
  2. Evolution (the core): Chipko (1973) → Silent Valley (1978–83) → Appiko (1983) → Narmada Bachao Andolan (1985–) — a shift from forest protection to anti-displacement to rights.
  3. Methods: Gandhian satyagraha, tree-hugging, PILs, jal-samarpan, scientific-literacy (KSSP) — a plural repertoire.
  4. Policy/legal impact: Forest Conservation Act 1980, environment ministry (1985), EPA 1986, and ultimately the Forest Rights Act 2006 and green tribunals.
  5. Critical edge: yet dams were built, displacement continued, and enforcement lags — agenda-setting success outran ground outcomes.
  6. Conclusion: the movement institutionalised environmental justice in law and discourse, leaving an unfinished struggle over implementation in the climate era.

Q3. "The Dalit movement transformed protest into political power." Discuss with reference to post-independence India. (15 marks, 250 words)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: situate the Dalit movement as the continuation of the Phule–Ambedkar anti-caste struggle into independent India.
  2. From reform to assertion (the core): constitutional safeguards and the 1956 Buddhist conversion → the militant cultural assertion of the Dalit Panthers (1972) → electoral mobilisation via Kanshi Ram's BAMCEF and the BSP (1984).
  3. Institutional gains: reservations, the SC/ST (PoA) Act 1989, and Dalit political power in Uttar Pradesh.
  4. Cultural dimension: Dalit literature and identity assertion reshaped the public sphere.
  5. Critical edge: yet atrocities persist, intra-Dalit inequalities and sub-categorisation debates remain, and electoral power did not end social discrimination.
  6. Conclusion: the movement's arc — reform → assertion → power — deepened democracy, but the gap between political voice and social dignity endures.

Q4. Examine the role of non-party civil-society movements in shaping rights-based legislation in India. (15 marks, 250 words)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: define "non-party political process" (Rajni Kothari) — autonomous movements filling the representation gap left by parties.
  2. Case studies (the core): MKSS's jan sunwai campaign → RTI Act 2005; the NREGA and food-security campaigns; the FRA driven by adivasi/forest movements — movements literally authoring statute.
  3. Method: public hearings, PILs, campaigns and networks (NCPRI) rather than electoral contestation.
  4. Significance: the "rights-based turn" of Indian policy — entitlements to information, work, forest and food.
  5. Critical edge: risks of NGO-isation, co-option, dilution (RTI Amendment 2019), and weak implementation blunt the gains.
  6. Conclusion: non-party movements have made Indian democracy co-authored — citizens' movements, not only legislatures, write its most transformative laws.
