Chapter 6: From Shastri to Indira — The Transition Years (1964–1969)

Nehru's death in 1964 opened a period of doubt — could Indian democracy survive its founder? The answer came through two peaceful successions and a decisive test at the polls. This file covers the premiership of Lal Bahadur Shastri (Jai Jawan Jai Kisan, the beginnings of the Green & White Revolutions, the 1965 war & Tashkent), the food crisis & the controversial rupee devaluation of 1966, Shastri's sudden death and the Congress succession struggle (the Kamaraj Plan and the Syndicate), the rise of Indira Gandhi to the premiership, and the watershed 1967 general elections that ended Congress dominance in the states — with tables, flowcharts and PYQ-pattern practice + full model answers.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames the Transition Years

This chapter is tested as the test of democratic maturity — how India managed two successions after Nehru without a coup or crisis, and how one-party dominance began to crack. Sort your prep:

  • Static/institutional — the succession machinery & events: the Kamaraj Plan (1963), the Syndicate, Shastri as PM (1964), the rupee devaluation (1966), Indira as PM (1966), the 1967 general elections (Congress loses many states). High-yield recall.
  • The personalities — Shastri (Jai Jawan Jai Kisan, Tashkent), the Syndicate bosses (Kamaraj, Nijalingappa, S.K. Patil, Atulya Ghosh, Sanjiva Reddy) & Indira Gandhi vs Morarji Desai. Who did what.
  • Analytical (GS-I) — why the peaceful successions mattered for democracy, and why 1967 marked the beginning of the end of Congress "one-party dominance". Needs idea + example + judgement.

Territorial note: Maps in this file show India with its full official boundaries — Jammu & Kashmir & Ladakh (including PoK & Aksai Chin) are integral parts of India.

1. Overview: The Succession Question

When Nehru died on 27 May 1964, foreign observers doubted India could survive without its towering founder — "After Nehru, who?" and even "After Nehru, what?" The years 1964–69 answered both questions.

  • The fear: that Indian democracy was a one-man show that would collapse into instability or army rule after Nehru — the fate of many new nations.
  • The reality: power passed twice — to Shastri (1964) and then to Indira Gandhi (1966) — smoothly, constitutionally & without violence.
  • The deeper shift: beneath the calm, the Congress "system" of one-party dominance was fracturing — a process the 1967 elections made unmistakable.
  • The stakes: these years proved Indian democracy had struck deep roots & could manage leadership change through institutions, not personalities alone.
One-line frame: the transition years showed that Indian democracy had outgrown its founder — institutions, not a single leader, now carried the republic.

2. Shastri Becomes Prime Minister

The first succession set the template of a party-managed, consensual transfer of power.

  • After Nehru's death, the Congress President K. Kamaraj & the party's organisational bosses (the "Syndicate") engineered a consensus choice rather than a divisive contest.
  • Lal Bahadur Shastri — a soft-spoken Gandhian & former Home Minister — was chosen as a unifying, unthreatening figure, defeating the ambition of Morarji Desai.
  • He took office in June 1964, demonstrating that the party could manage succession by consensus — a crucial democratic precedent.
GS-I hook: Shastri's smooth elevation showed the Congress "system" working at its best — the organisational wing (Syndicate) delivering a consensual PM. The same machinery would soon try, and fail, to control Indira Gandhi.

3. The Shastri Record (1964–66)

Though his tenure lasted barely nineteen months, Shastri left a mark far larger than its length — on security, agriculture & national morale.

AreaAchievement
National securityLed India firmly through the 1965 war with Pakistan, restoring military confidence after 1962; the Tashkent Agreement (1966) ended the war.
Food self-sufficiencyAmid a severe food crisis, launched the drive toward the Green Revolution (backing new high-yield seeds & the farmer) — the roots of later self-sufficiency.
White RevolutionBacked the National Dairy Development Board (1965) & the Anand/Amul cooperative model — the seed of Operation Flood.
National moraleHis slogan "Jai Jawan Jai Kisan" linked the soldier & farmer — security & food — and gave the nation a rallying idea in a hard year.
Personal integrityA byword for simplicity & honesty in public life — a moral standard often invoked since.
One-line frame: Shastri's short tenure fused security & self-reliance — standing firm in war while sowing the Green & White Revolutions that would feed India.

4. Food Crisis & the 1966 Devaluation

The mid-1960s were among the republic's hardest years economically — back-to-back droughts, war costs & a dependence on food aid that eroded sovereignty.

  • The food crisis: severe droughts (1965&1966) caused near-famine conditions; India depended humiliatingly on US PL-480 food aid ("ship-to-mouth" existence), used as diplomatic leverage.
  • Rupee devaluation (June 1966): under pressure from the World Bank/IMF & aid donors, Indira Gandhi's government devalued the rupee sharply to boost exports & secure aid.
  • Political backlash: the devaluation was widely seen as a humiliating capitulation to foreign pressure, brought little immediate benefit, and hurt Indira's early standing & the Congress in 1967.
  • The lesson drawn: the crisis hardened the national resolve for food self-sufficiency (Green Revolution) & economic self-reliance — never again "ship-to-mouth".
GS-I hook: the 1966 devaluation & PL-480 dependence are the classic case of economic vulnerability translating into diplomatic weakness — the humiliation that made self-sufficiency a national imperative.

5. Shastri's Death at Tashkent

Shastri's tenure ended as dramatically as it had been consequential.

  • Hours after signing the Tashkent Agreement (10 January 1966) with Pakistan's Ayub Khan, Shastri died of a heart attack in Tashkent — a sudden shock that reopened the succession question.
  • His death, abroad & unexpected, forced the Congress into a second leadership transition within two years — a fresh test of the system.
  • The manner & circumstances of his death have been the subject of much later speculation, but the constitutional succession that followed was orderly.
Prelims trap: Shastri signed the Tashkent Agreement on 10 Jan 1966 & died there the same night; the agreement was brokered by the Soviet Union, ending the 1965 war.

6. The Kamaraj Plan & the Syndicate

To understand both successions, you must know the two organisational devices that dominated Congress politics in the 1960s.

DeviceWhat it was
Kamaraj Plan (1963)K. Kamaraj's proposal that senior Congress ministers resign from office to devote themselves to party organisation. It let Nehru ease out rivals & strengthened the party wing — and elevated Kamaraj himself to Congress President & kingmaker.
The SyndicateA group of powerful regional party bosses — K. Kamaraj (Tamil Nadu), S. Nijalingappa (Karnataka), S.K. Patil (Bombay), Atulya Ghosh (Bengal) & N. Sanjiva Reddy (Andhra) — who controlled the organisation & engineered the choice of both Shastri & Indira, expecting to control the PM.

The Syndicate's fatal miscalculation was choosing Indira Gandhi in 1966 as a supposedly pliable "goongi gudiya" (dumb doll) they could control — a judgement she would soon shatter.

GS-I hook: the Syndicate embodied the organisational wing's dominance over the government wing. Indira's later revolt against it (the 1969 Congress split) is the pivotal turn in Indian political history — the shift from party bosses to a personality-centred, plebiscitary politics.

7. Indira Gandhi Becomes Prime Minister

The second succession brought Nehru's daughter to power — on the Syndicate's terms, or so they thought.

Fig 6.1 — Two peaceful successions after Nehru: Shastri (1964) then Indira Gandhi (1966). Schematic flow.
  • After Shastri's death, the Syndicate backed Indira Gandhi over Morarji Desai; when Desai insisted on a contest, the Congress Parliamentary Party elected Indira (January 1966) by a large margin.
  • The bosses saw her as a tractable figurehead — young, inexperienced & trading on the Nehru name — whom they could guide.
  • Morarji Desai was made Deputy PM & Finance Minister, an uneasy compromise that presaged conflict.
  • The vote was decisive — Indira 355, Morarji Desai 169 (19 Jan 1966); she was sworn in on 24 Jan 1966 as India’s first woman PM.
One-line frame: the Syndicate chose Indira expecting a puppet; within three years she had turned the tables, split the party & made herself the undisputed centre of Indian politics.

8. The 1967 General Elections

The fourth general elections were a political earthquake — the sharpest setback the Congress had ever suffered.

  • Congress weakened at the Centre: it returned to power in the Lok Sabha but with a sharply reduced majority; several stalwarts & Syndicate bosses (Kamaraj, Patil, Atulya Ghosh) lost their seats.
  • Congress routed in the states: it lost power in about half the states — including major ones (Bihar, UP, Bengal, Madras, Punjab, Orissa) — for the first time since independence.
  • Non-Congress governments: opposition coalitions formed Samyukta Vidhayak Dal (SVD) governments in several states — the first significant experiments in coalition & opposition rule.
  • The context: economic distress (food crisis, devaluation, inflation), the Congress's internal splits & anti-incumbency all fed the result.
  • The tally: Congress fell to 283 seats (from 364 in 1962); non-Congress SVD coalitions took power in eight states — the first decisive breach of the "Congress System."
GS-I hook: 1967 ended the myth of Congress invincibility — the "Congress system" of one-party dominance began to crack, and coalition politics & the phenomenon of Aya Ram Gaya Ram defections were born.

9. Why 1967 Was a Watershed

The 1967 elections are a favourite exam theme because they mark a structural turning point in Indian politics.

ChangeSignificance
End of one-party dominanceThe Congress's near-monopoly ended; opposition parties tasted state power for the first time.
Rise of coalitionsSVD governments introduced multi-party coalition rule & bargaining into Indian politics.
Defection politicsAya Ram Gaya Ram — floor-crossing for office — became rampant, eventually prompting the 1985 Anti-Defection Law.
Centre-state frictionNon-Congress state governments vs a Congress Centre sharpened federal tensions & the misuse of Article 356.
Indira's openingThe Congress's weakness & the Syndicate's losses gave Indira the opening to assert herself against the bosses.
One-line frame: 1967 is where the "Congress system" gave way to genuine multi-party competition — the birth of coalition politics, defection & centre-state conflict that define the era after.

10. Indira's Early Challenges

The weakened Congress & a restless Syndicate left Indira Gandhi fighting on several fronts in her first years — the prelude to the dramatic 1969 split (covered in the next chapter).

  • Vs the Syndicate: the bosses tried to control her; she chafed under their tutelage & began building her own popular base.
  • Economic pressure: inflation, the after-effects of devaluation & agrarian distress demanded populist responses.
  • The left turn: she moved sharply leftward — toward bank nationalisation & the abolition of privy purses — to outflank both the Syndicate & the opposition (developed in Chapter 7).
  • Building a personal appeal: the slogan "Garibi Hatao" (Remove Poverty) would soon make her a plebiscitary leader over the heads of the party bosses.
GS-I hook: Indira's early years show a leader converting weakness into strategy — using populism & a direct appeal to the poor to break free of the party machine, setting up the 1969 split.

11. Assessment of the Transition

Weigh the transition years as both a triumph of democratic stability and the start of a deeper transformation.

Democratic strengthsEmerging strains
Two peaceful, constitutional successions after Nehru — democracy passed the test.Economic distress (food crisis, devaluation) exposed vulnerability & foreign dependence.
Shastri's firm war leadership & the seeds of the Green/White Revolutions.The Congress system began breaking down; factionalism & defection rose.
Opposition & coalitions gained ground — a healthier multi-party democracy.Centre-state friction & the misuse of President's Rule intensified.
One-line frame: the transition years were a double success and a warning — democracy survived succession, but the one-party order that had held it together was dissolving, opening a more contested politics.

12. Legacy & Continuities

The themes of these years still resonate — useful for adding depth to an answer.

  • Peaceful succession as a norm: India's record of constitutional leadership change, established in 1964–66, remains a democratic strength rare in the post-colonial world.
  • Food security consciousness: the "ship-to-mouth" trauma continues to shape India's insistence on food self-sufficiency, buffer stocks & the PDS.
  • Coalition & federal politics: the multi-party, centre-state bargaining born in 1967 is the everyday texture of Indian politics today.
  • Anti-defection: the Aya Ram Gaya Ram problem of 1967 led to the Tenth Schedule (1985), still a live constitutional debate.
GS-I / GS-II hook: frame today's coalition bargaining, anti-defection debates & food-security politics as direct descendants of the 1964–67 transition — the structural legacy beneath current affairs.

13. Current Affairs Link

Connect the chapter to recent debates to signal an updated preparation.

  • Anti-defection debates: recurring controversies over defections, the Speaker's role & the Tenth Schedule trace to the 1967 Aya Ram Gaya Ram era.
  • Coalition & federal tension: centre-state disputes over Governors, Article 356 & fiscal share echo the post-1967 dynamic.
  • Food & farmer politics: MSP, procurement & self-sufficiency debates descend from the 1960s food crisis & "Jai Jawan Jai Kisan".
  • Shastri & integrity in public life: periodically invoked as a benchmark of simplicity & honesty in governance.
How to use in the exam: a line linking 1967 to today's coalition/anti-defection politics, or the food crisis to current MSP debates, shows the examiner you read the transition as a structural, still-relevant turning point.

14. Prelims PYQs & Practice

Note on sourcing: the questions below are exam-pattern practice MCQs built strictly on this chapter's high-yield facts, deliberately left yearless (labelled "Model question") and not attributed to any fabricated year. Use them for concept-testing.

Model question — no fixed year

The "Kamaraj Plan" of 1963 proposed that:

  • (a) States be reorganised on a linguistic basis
  • (b) Senior Congress ministers resign office to work for the party organisation
  • (c) The rupee be devalued to boost exports
  • (d) Banks be nationalised

Answer: (b). The Kamaraj Plan asked senior ministers to quit government for party work; it strengthened the organisation & made Kamaraj the Congress President & a kingmaker.

Model question — no fixed year

Who among the following was NOT a member of the Congress "Syndicate"?

  • (a) K. Kamaraj
  • (b) S. Nijalingappa
  • (c) S.K. Patil
  • (d) Jayaprakash Narayan

Answer: (d). The Syndicate comprised organisational bosses such as Kamaraj, Nijalingappa, S.K. Patil, Atulya Ghosh & Sanjiva Reddy. Jayaprakash Narayan was a socialist leader outside the Congress establishment.

Model question — no fixed year

The rupee was devalued sharply in which year, amid a severe food crisis and aid dependence?

  • (a) 1962
  • (b) 1964
  • (c) 1966
  • (d) 1969

Answer: (c). The rupee was devalued in June 1966 under external pressure; it was politically unpopular & hurt the Congress in the 1967 elections.

Model question — no fixed year

Indira Gandhi became Prime Minister in 1966 after defeating which rival in the Congress Parliamentary Party?

  • (a) Jagjivan Ram
  • (b) Morarji Desai
  • (c) Y.B. Chavan
  • (d) Sanjiva Reddy

Answer: (b). Backed by the Syndicate, Indira Gandhi defeated Morarji Desai in the January 1966 contest; Desai became Deputy PM & Finance Minister.

Model question — no fixed year

The 1967 general elections are significant because:
1. The Congress lost power in about half the states.
2. Non-Congress SVD coalition governments were formed.
3. Several Syndicate leaders lost their seats.
Which are correct?

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1, 2 and 3
  • (d) 1 and 3 only

Answer: (c). 1967 saw the Congress lose about half the states, the rise of SVD coalition governments & the defeat of several Syndicate bosses — the beginning of the end of one-party dominance.

Model question — no fixed year

The phenomenon of frequent floor-crossing associated with the post-1967 period was popularly called:

  • (a) Garibi Hatao
  • (b) Jai Jawan Jai Kisan
  • (c) Aya Ram Gaya Ram
  • (d) Total Revolution

Answer: (c). "Aya Ram Gaya Ram" described the rampant defections after 1967; it eventually led to the Anti-Defection Law (52nd Amendment, Tenth Schedule, 1985).

Prelims — anticipated themes

High-probability Prelims hooks: Kamaraj Plan (1963); the Syndicate (members); Shastri & Jai Jawan Jai Kisan; Tashkent Agreement (10 Jan 1966); rupee devaluation (1966); PL-480 food aid; Indira vs Morarji (1966); 1967 elections (states lost, SVD); Aya Ram Gaya Ram; NDDB (1965) & Green/White Revolution beginnings.

15. Mains PYQs — Model Answers

Note on sourcing: the questions below are exam-pattern practice questions on this chapter's core analytical themes (left yearless, not attributed to a fabricated year), each with a full answer skeleton.

Model question — no fixed year

"The peaceful successions after Nehru demonstrated the maturity of Indian democracy." Discuss with reference to 1964–1966. (250 words) 15 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: The anxious question "After Nehru, who?" was answered twice within two years — through orderly, constitutional transfers of power that few new nations managed.
  2. First succession: Kamaraj & the Syndicate engineered a consensual choice of Shastri (1964) over a contest — the party wing delivering stability.
  3. Second succession (the core): after Shastri's sudden death, the Congress Parliamentary Party elected Indira Gandhi (1966) through a contested but constitutional vote — no violence, no army.
  4. Why it mattered: it proved institutions, not one leader, carried the republic — a decisive rebuttal to predictions of collapse.
  5. Critical edge: beneath the calm, the Congress system was fracturing (1967), and personalised politics was emerging — stability with underlying strain.
  6. Conclusion: the successions confirmed Indian democracy's deep roots even as they opened a more contested political era.
Model question — no fixed year

Examine the significance of the 1967 general elections as a turning point in Indian politics. (250 words) 15 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: The 1967 elections were the sharpest reversal the Congress had faced — and the moment India's one-party-dominant system gave way to genuine multi-party competition.
  2. The result: a reduced Congress majority at the Centre, defeat in about half the states, & the fall of Syndicate stalwarts.
  3. Structural change (the core): the rise of SVD coalition governments, opposition rule in the states & the Aya Ram Gaya Ram defection culture.
  4. Consequences: intensified centre-state friction, misuse of Article 356, & the eventual Anti-Defection Law (1985).
  5. Critical edge: it also gave Indira the opening to break from the Syndicate — leading to the 1969 split & personalised politics.
  6. Conclusion: 1967 ended the Congress system & inaugurated the coalition, federal & personality-driven politics that define the era after.
Model question — no fixed year

Assess the contribution of Lal Bahadur Shastri despite his brief tenure as Prime Minister. (150 words) 10 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: In barely nineteen months, Shastri left a mark on security, agriculture & national morale disproportionate to his tenure.
  2. National security: firm leadership in the 1965 war restored confidence after 1962; the Tashkent Agreement ended the conflict.
  3. Self-reliance (the core): amid a food crisis he backed the Green Revolution & the White Revolution (NDDB, 1965) — the seeds of self-sufficiency.
  4. National morale: "Jai Jawan Jai Kisan" fused the soldier & the farmer into a unifying idea.
  5. Critical edge: his tenure was cut short before these initiatives matured; the food crisis persisted into 1966–67.
  6. Conclusion: Shastri's integrity & decisiveness make him a benchmark leader — small in time, large in legacy.
Model question — no fixed year

How did the food crisis and the 1966 devaluation shape India's economic priorities? (150 words) 10 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: The mid-1960s "ship-to-mouth" food dependence & the humiliating 1966 devaluation exposed India's economic vulnerability & reshaped its priorities.
  2. The crisis: back-to-back droughts & reliance on US PL-480 aid, used as diplomatic leverage against India.
  3. The devaluation (the core): forced by aid donors, it was seen as capitulation to foreign pressure & yielded little immediate gain.
  4. The response: a national resolve for food self-sufficiency (Green Revolution) & economic self-reliance — never again dependence.
  5. Critical edge: self-reliance also entrenched the licence-raj, whose inefficiencies needed later reform.
  6. Conclusion: the twin shocks converted vulnerability into the drive for self-sufficiency that defined the next decade.
