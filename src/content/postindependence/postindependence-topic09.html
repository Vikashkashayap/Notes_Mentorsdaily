The JP Movement & the Emergency (1975–77)

How an economic protest in Gujarat grew into a nationwide movement for “Total Revolution,” how a court verdict triggered India’s only internal Emergency, and how the 1977 verdict restored the democratic promise.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames the Emergency

The Emergency is not tested as a single event but as a stress-test of Indian democracy — the one moment the Constitution’s guarantees were suspended from within. Sort your prep into three buckets:

  • Static / factual recall — dates and instruments: Allahabad verdict (12 June 1975), proclamation under Article 352 (25 June 1975), MISA, the 38th/39th/42nd Amendments, the ADM Jabalpur case, the Shah Commission, the 44th Amendment. These are the prelims anchors.
  • Movement & mobilisation — the chain from Nav Nirman → Bihar Movement → JP’s Total Revolution → Railway strike: how economic distress (inflation, unemployment, the 1973 oil shock, corruption) hardened into a demand to overhaul the system itself.
  • Analytical (GS-II / GS-I) — the constitutional questions: how emergency provisions were misused, why judicial review collapsed in ADM Jabalpur, how the 44th Amendment repaired the safeguards, and what the episode teaches about checks and balances. Bring an idea + an example + a judgement.

Framing note: Treat the Emergency as the pivot of the post-1967 story — the point where one-party decline, populist centralisation and institutional erosion collided, and where the electorate ultimately re-asserted itself in 1977.

1. Setting: the crisis of 1973–74

By 1973 the confidence of the 1971 “Garibi Hatao” mandate had drained away. A cluster of shocks — the failure of the monsoon, the 1973 global oil shock, double-digit inflation, food scarcity, unemployment and mounting allegations of corruption — turned popular hope into anger. The very centralisation that had made Indira Gandhi dominant now made her the single address for every grievance.

PressureWhat it looked like on the ground
InflationWholesale prices rose over 20–30% in 1973–74; kerosene, foodgrain and cooking-oil queues.
Oil shock (1973)The OPEC price rise widened the import bill and squeezed an already strained economy.
UnemploymentEducated youth, especially students, faced bleak job prospects — the core of the street protests.
CorruptionCharges against ministers and state governments fed a “the system is rotten” mood.
Institutional strainA “committed” bureaucracy and judiciary debate; the supersession of judges in 1973 raised alarm.
GS-I hook: The Emergency did not fall out of the sky — it grew from a governance failure. Always open an answer by linking the movement to the 1973–74 economic distress, not merely to the court verdict.

2. Nav Nirman Andolan (Gujarat)

The first spark came in Gujarat in December 1973–74. A hostel mess-bill hike at the L.D. College of Engineering in Ahmedabad snowballed into the Nav Nirman (“Reconstruction”) Andolan — a student-led, then broad-based agitation against inflation, corruption and the Chimanbhai Patel state government.

  • Trigger → escalation: mess-bill protest → strikes, bandhs, and street clashes across Gujarat.
  • Outcome: Chief Minister Chimanbhai Patel resigned; President’s Rule was imposed and, crucially, the Assembly was dissolved in early 1974 after a fast by Morarji Desai.
  • Significance: it proved a mass movement could force an elected government out — a template that Bihar would follow and JP would nationalise.
One-line frame: Nav Nirman was the proof-of-concept; the Bihar Movement was the scale-up.

3. The Bihar Movement & JP’s entry

In March 1974 the agitation spread to Bihar, again beginning with students. What changed everything was the decision of Jayaprakash Narayan (JP) — a Gandhian socialist and veteran of the freedom struggle who had long withdrawn from active politics — to lead it. His moral stature gave the protest a national voice and a moral centre.

ElementDetail
LeadershipJP took charge in April 1974; the Chhatra Sangharsh Samiti coordinated students.
DemandDissolution of the Bihar Assembly and a corruption-free, participatory politics.
MethodNon-violent mass mobilisation — bandhs, gheraos, and a call for a “people’s government.”
BreadthOpposition parties (Jana Sangh, socialists, Congress-O, later BLD) rallied around JP.

By 1975 JP was addressing enormous rallies, most famously calling on the police and army to disobey “illegal and immoral” orders — a line the government would later cite as incitement.

Prelims trap: The Bihar Movement (1974–75) and the Nav Nirman Andolan (Gujarat, 1973–74) are distinct but linked; do not merge them. JP led Bihar, not Nav Nirman.

4. “Total Revolution” — JP’s idea

JP framed the movement not as a change of government but as a change of the system. In a June 1974 speech at Patna he gave it a name: Sampoorna Kranti — “Total Revolution.” It was less a programme than a moral vision of remaking society and politics together.

  • Seven dimensions: JP spoke of revolution across the social, economic, political, cultural, ideological/intellectual, educational and spiritual spheres.
  • Core idea: mere elections could not cleanse a corrupt system; citizens must build a “partyless democracy” of engaged, accountable people (drawing on Gandhian and Lohiaite thought).
  • Right of recall & people’s committees: JP pushed for citizen oversight of legislators and grassroots “Janata Sarkars.”
GS-I hook: Total Revolution is the ideological heart of the chapter. Its strength was moral energy; its weakness was vagueness — it never became a concrete governing programme, which is partly why the Janata experiment later floundered.

5. The 1974 Railway Strike

Parallel to the student movements, organised labour struck. In May 1974 the All India Railwaymen’s Federation, led by George Fernandes, launched one of the largest strikes in the world — roughly 1.7 million workers demanding better pay and conditions.

  • Scale: the railways — the lifeline of the economy — were paralysed for about three weeks.
  • State response: a hard crackdown — mass arrests, evictions from railway quarters, and the deployment of paramilitary forces broke the strike.
  • Signal: the government demonstrated it would use coercive power against organised protest — a foretaste of the Emergency’s methods.
Link it: The railway strike shows the crisis was not only student/middle-class but also working-class — the discontent was broad-based, which is why the movement felt so threatening to the ruling establishment.

6. The Allahabad verdict (Raj Narain case)

The immediate trigger for the Emergency was judicial. Raj Narain, Indira Gandhi’s defeated 1971 opponent in Rae Bareli, had challenged her election for corrupt practices. On 12 June 1975, Justice Jagmohan Lal Sinha of the Allahabad High Court found her guilty on two counts (misuse of government machinery), set aside her election and barred her from office for six years.

StepWhat happened
12 June 1975Allahabad HC unseats Indira Gandhi; a 20-day stay allowed her to remain PM temporarily.
24 June 1975The Supreme Court (Justice Krishna Iyer) granted a conditional stay — she could remain PM but not vote in the Lok Sabha.
Opposition demandJP and the opposition demanded her resignation; a massive rally at the Ramlila Maidan (25 June) called for civil disobedience.
Prelims anchor: The case is Indira Nehru Gandhi v. Raj Narain; the trial court was the Allahabad High Court; the date 12 June 1975 is frequently tested.

7. Proclamation of Emergency, 25 June 1975

On the night of 25 June 1975, on the advice of the Prime Minister, President Fakhruddin Ali Ahmed proclaimed a national Emergency under Article 352 on the ground of “internal disturbance.” It was announced to the nation on the morning of 26 June. This was the third emergency (after 1962 and 1971) but the first on internal grounds — and the only one directed against the domestic political opposition.

Fig 9.1 — From court verdict to Emergency in thirteen days.
  • Legal mechanism: the Cabinet was informed after the proclamation was signed — the decision was effectively the PM’s.
  • First act: mass arrests of opposition leaders (JP, Morarji Desai, Charan Singh, Vajpayee, Advani and thousands more) under preventive-detention laws; electricity to newspaper offices was cut.
  • Fundamental rights suspended: the enforcement of Articles 14, 21 and 22 was suspended by presidential order.
  • Legal toolkit: detentions ran on MISA (1971) plus COFEPOSA (1974); organisations including the RSS, Jamaat-e-Islami, Anand Marg & CPI(ML) were banned on 4 July 1975.
GS-II crossover: The proclamation exposed a gap — “internal disturbance” was an undefined, elastic ground. The 44th Amendment later replaced it with the far narrower “armed rebellion.”

8. The Emergency regime: MISA, censorship, 42nd Amendment

The Emergency governed through a combination of preventive detention, a muzzled press, a subordinated judiciary and constitutional amendments that concentrated power. It was legal on paper and authoritarian in practice.

InstrumentEffect
MISA (Maintenance of Internal Security Act)Preventive detention without trial; over 100,000 people detained.
Press censorshipPre-censorship of newspapers; the news agencies were merged into Samachar; dissenting papers harassed.
38th AmendmentMade the proclamation of Emergency non-justiciable (immune from judicial review).
39th AmendmentPlaced the election of the PM/Speaker beyond the reach of the courts — aimed directly at the Raj Narain case.
42nd Amendment (1976)The “mini-Constitution”: added “Socialist, Secular, Integrity” to the Preamble, extended the Lok Sabha term to 6 years, curtailed judicial review, and expanded central power. Later largely reversed by the 44th.
Judicial supersessionFor his lone ADM Jabalpur dissent, Justice H.R. Khanna was passed over for CJI in favour of Justice M.H. Beg (Jan 1977) — and resigned in protest.

The judiciary’s low point came in ADM Jabalpur v. Shivkant Shukla (1976): a 4–1 Supreme Court majority held that during the Emergency a detainee had no right to move a court even against illegal detention. Justice H.R. Khanna’s lone dissent, which cost him the Chief Justiceship, is celebrated as a defence of the rule of law.

Prelims anchor: Remember the amendment map — 38th (proclamation immune), 39th (PM’s election immune), 42nd (mini-Constitution). The ADM Jabalpur majority was later expressly overruled in Puttaswamy (2017).

9. The excesses

What turned public opinion decisively was not the abstract suspension of rights but the visible abuse of power — much of it driven by an extra-constitutional authority, Sanjay Gandhi, and his “five-point programme.”

  • Forced sterilisation: aggressive, quota-driven family-planning drives — men, often poor and rural, were sterilised under coercion; this became the most hated symbol of the Emergency.
  • Slum demolition: “beautification” drives razed settlements; the Turkman Gate demolition and firing in Old Delhi (April 1976) became a byword for state brutality.
  • Detentions & torture: political prisoners, journalists and activists were jailed; custodial abuses were later documented by the Shah Commission.
  • Personality cult: the slogan “India is Indira” captured the collapse of the distinction between party, government and leader.
  • The scale: sterilisations leapt to ~8.3 million in 1976–77 (from ~1.4 million a year earlier) — roughly 75% vasectomies — making coercive family planning the Emergency’s most hated symbol.
GS-I hook: The excesses matter because they explain the 1977 verdict: the sterilisation and demolition drives hit the rural and urban poor — Congress’s own base — and turned the Hindi heartland against the party.

10. Resistance & the underground

Despite censorship and detention, opposition survived. A clandestine network — drawing on socialists, the RSS/Jana Sangh cadre, Sarvodaya workers and Gandhians — kept the movement alive through underground literature, foreign broadcasts and smuggled bulletins.

  • Underground press: cyclostyled bulletins circulated news the censored papers could not print.
  • Symbolic defiance: some newspapers protested subtly — famously, a blank editorial column or an ironic obituary for “D.E.M. O’Cracy.”
  • International attention: exiled activists and a critical foreign press kept the issue visible abroad.
  • Moral resistance: JP’s imprisonment (his health broken in detention) made him a living symbol of the democratic cause.
One-line frame: The Emergency never fully silenced dissent; it drove it underground, where it hardened into the coalition that would contest 1977.

11. The 1977 elections & the Janata government

In a decision that still puzzles historians, Indira Gandhi called fresh elections in January 1977 and released detainees. The opposition parties rapidly merged into the Janata Party, uniting Congress-O, the Jana Sangh, the BLD, the Socialists and, crucially, Jagjivan Ram’s breakaway CFD.

Result (March 1977)Detail
Janata (+ allies) winThe Janata wave swept North India; Congress was routed in the Hindi heartland.
Indira & Sanjay defeatedBoth lost their own seats (Rae Bareli and Amethi).
First non-Congress govtMorarji Desai became India’s first non-Congress Prime Minister.
Regional patternCongress held much of the South, where the Emergency’s excesses were milder — a North–South split.
GS-I hook: 1977 is the electorate’s verdict on the Emergency — proof that even after suspending rights, a government could be dismissed peacefully at the ballot box. It is the single strongest evidence for the resilience of Indian democracy.

12. The Shah Commission & assessment

The Janata government appointed the Shah Commission of Inquiry (Justice J.C. Shah, 1977) to investigate Emergency-era excesses. Its reports documented the arbitrary detentions, the sterilisation drives, the demolitions and the misuse of authority — providing the first official record of the abuses.

  • Findings: catalogued illegal detentions, custodial deaths, coercive sterilisation and the extra-constitutional role of Sanjay Gandhi’s coterie.
  • Limits: the reports had little legal follow-through; when Indira Gandhi returned to power in 1980, copies were withdrawn and prosecutions lapsed.
  • Balance-sheet: defenders pointed to a brief dip in inflation and improved “discipline”; critics answered that these gains were coerced and temporary, bought at the price of liberty.
Assessment frame: The honest verdict is that the Emergency’s “order” was real but hollow — a peace of fear, not of consent — and its costs to institutions and to the poor far outweighed any administrative gain.

13. Legacy & the 44th Amendment

The Emergency’s deepest legacy is the set of safeguards built to prevent its repetition. The Janata government’s 44th Amendment (1978) systematically undid the 42nd’s authoritarian core and hard-wired protections into the Constitution.

Safeguard (44th Amendment, 1978)What it fixed
“Armed rebellion” replaces “internal disturbance”Narrowed the ground for a national Emergency under Article 352.
Written Cabinet adviceThe President can proclaim Emergency only on the written advice of the Cabinet, not the PM alone.
Parliamentary checkProclamation must be approved within one month by a special majority and re-approved every six months.
Articles 20 & 21 protectedThe right to life and protection against ex-post-facto/double-jeopardy can never be suspended, even in Emergency.
Right to propertyRemoved from Fundamental Rights (Art 31) and made a legal right (Art 300A).
GS-I/GS-II hook: The arc of the chapter is redemptive — abuse (42nd) → electoral rejection (1977) → institutional repair (44th). The Emergency is the reason India’s emergency provisions are now among the hardest in the world to misuse.

14. Contemporary relevance

The Emergency remains a living reference point in Indian public life — invoked in debates on civil liberties, press freedom, preventive detention and the independence of institutions.

  • Sanvidhaan Hatya Diwas: in 2024 the government designated 25 June as a day to remember the Emergency’s victims — a sign of its continuing political salience.
  • Judicial memory: the Supreme Court’s Puttaswamy (2017) privacy judgment expressly overruled the ADM Jabalpur majority, closing the book on the Emergency’s worst legal precedent.
  • Recurring themes: debates over preventive-detention laws, sedition, internet shutdowns and press freedom routinely reach back to 1975–77 for their moral yardstick.
Answer value-add: Citing the Puttaswamy overruling of ADM Jabalpur shows the examiner you understand the long constitutional arc, not just the 1970s narrative.

15. Prelims practice (PYQ pattern)

The questions below follow the UPSC prelims pattern for this theme. Where a question is self-authored for practice it is marked as a model question with no fixed year; only genuinely dated UPSC questions carry a year.

Q1. With reference to the Nav Nirman Andolan, consider the following statements:

  1. It began in Gujarat over a hostel mess-bill hike.
  2. It led to the dissolution of the Gujarat Legislative Assembly.
  3. It was led at the national level by Jayaprakash Narayan.
  • Which of the statements given above are correct?
  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 1 and 2 only — correct
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Answer: (a) 1 and 2 only. Nav Nirman began over a mess-bill hike and forced the dissolution of the Gujarat Assembly. JP led the Bihar Movement, not Nav Nirman — so statement 3 is wrong.

Q2. The term “Total Revolution” (Sampoorna Kranti) is associated with:

  • Select the correct option.
  • (a) Ram Manohar Lohia
  • (b) Jayaprakash Narayan — correct
  • (c) Acharya Narendra Dev
  • (d) Morarji Desai

Answer: (b) Jayaprakash Narayan. JP gave the call for Total Revolution in a Patna speech in June 1974, envisioning revolution across seven spheres of life.

Q3. The Emergency of 1975 was proclaimed under which Article of the Constitution, and on what ground?

  • Select the correct option.
  • (a) Article 356 — failure of constitutional machinery in states
  • (b) Article 352 — internal disturbance — correct
  • (c) Article 360 — financial emergency
  • (d) Article 355 — duty of the Union to protect states

Answer: (b) Article 352 on the ground of “internal disturbance.” The 44th Amendment (1978) later replaced “internal disturbance” with the narrower “armed rebellion.”

Q4. Consider the following pairs of constitutional amendments and their effect during the Emergency era:

  1. 38th Amendment — made the proclamation of Emergency immune from judicial review.
  2. 39th Amendment — placed the election of the Prime Minister beyond the courts.
  3. 42nd Amendment — added “Socialist” and “Secular” to the Preamble.
  • How many of the pairs given above are correctly matched?
  • (a) Only one
  • (b) Only two
  • (c) All three — correct
  • (d) None

Answer: (c) All three. All three pairings are correct; the 42nd is often called the “mini-Constitution.”

Q5. In which case did the Supreme Court hold, during the Emergency, that a detainee could not move a court against illegal detention — a ruling later overruled?

  • Select the correct option.
  • (a) Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala
  • (b) ADM Jabalpur v. Shivkant Shukla — correct
  • (c) Minerva Mills v. Union of India
  • (d) Golaknath v. State of Punjab

Answer: (b) ADM Jabalpur v. Shivkant Shukla (1976). The 4–1 majority (Justice H.R. Khanna dissenting) was expressly overruled in Puttaswamy (2017).

Q6. Consider the following provisions introduced by the 44th Amendment (1978):

  1. “Armed rebellion” replaced “internal disturbance” as a ground for national Emergency.
  2. Articles 20 and 21 cannot be suspended even during an Emergency.
  3. The President can proclaim Emergency only on the written advice of the Cabinet.
  • Which of the statements given above are correct?
  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3 — correct

Answer: (d) 1, 2 and 3. All three are core safeguards of the 44th Amendment, enacted precisely to prevent a repeat of 1975.

  • Chronology matching: Nav Nirman (1973–74) → Bihar Movement (1974) → Railway strike (May 1974) → Allahabad verdict (12 Jun 1975) → proclamation (25 Jun 1975) → 1977 elections.
  • The Shah Commission — who headed it and what it investigated.
  • MISA vs the National Security Act — preventive-detention laws and their history.
  • The 42nd vs the 44th Amendment — which added and which repaired.

16. Mains practice + model answers

Model questions in the UPSC GS-I / GS-II idiom, each with a full answer skeleton. These are practice questions with no fixed year unless a genuine dated UPSC question is cited.

Q1. The Emergency of 1975–77 was as much a failure of institutions as an act of one leader. Critically examine. (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Frame the Emergency (25 Jun 1975–21 Mar 1977) as India’s only internal Emergency — and pose the question of agency: personal ambition vs institutional weakness.
  2. The leader’s role: the trigger was personal — the Allahabad verdict threatening Indira Gandhi’s office; the proclamation was effectively her decision; the personality cult (“India is Indira”) and Sanjay Gandhi’s extra-constitutional power drove the excesses.
  3. The institutional failure (the core): Parliament passed the 38th/39th/42nd Amendments; the President signed without demur; the Supreme Court capitulated in ADM Jabalpur; the bureaucracy and police executed detentions and sterilisations — every check folded.
  4. Underlying causes: the erosion of intra-party democracy after the 1969 split, the “committed” institutions debate, and centralisation had already hollowed out the guard-rails before 1975.
  5. Critical edge: reducing it to one leader lets institutions off the hook; but reducing it to structures ignores the specific choices made — both are needed for an honest verdict.
  6. Conclusion: the 44th Amendment’s design — written Cabinet advice, parliamentary re-approval, inviolable Articles 20–21 — shows India learned that safeguards must not depend on the virtue of any single office-holder.

Q2. Evaluate Jayaprakash Narayan’s idea of “Total Revolution.” Why did the movement it inspired succeed in 1977 yet fail to transform Indian politics? (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Define Total Revolution (Sampoorna Kranti, Patna 1974) as a Gandhian-socialist vision of remaking society across seven spheres, not merely changing a government.
  2. Its strengths: it supplied moral energy, a national leader above party, and a unifying banner that welded a fractured opposition — enough to defeat the Emergency at the polls.
  3. Its achievement (the core): the 1977 verdict — the first non-Congress government — proved the electorate could dismiss even an authoritarian regime, vindicating JP’s faith in the people.
  4. Why it failed to transform: Total Revolution was a moral vision, not a programme; the Janata Party was a coalition of incompatible parties (Jana Sangh, socialists, Congress-O) held together only by anti-Indira feeling; it collapsed within three years over the “dual membership” issue.
  5. Critical edge: the movement excelled at negation (removing the Emergency) but had no shared positive project — a recurring pattern in Indian opposition politics.
  6. Conclusion: JP’s legacy is democratic, not programmatic — he restored the system he could not remake, and his real monument is the resilience of the ballot itself.

Q3. “The 44th Amendment is best understood as the Constitution’s memory of the Emergency.” Discuss. (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Situate the 44th Amendment (1978) as the Janata government’s deliberate reversal of the 42nd and the abuses of 1975–77.
  2. What it repaired: narrowed the Emergency ground to “armed rebellion”; required written Cabinet advice; mandated parliamentary re-approval every six months; protected Articles 20 and 21 absolutely.
  3. Why each fix maps to an abuse (the core): “internal disturbance” → the elastic 1975 ground; PM’s sole decision → written Cabinet advice; indefinite duration → six-month review; ADM Jabalpur → inviolable Art 21.
  4. Balance: note what it left intact — the basic Emergency architecture survives; it was tightened, not abolished, preserving a genuine national-security tool.
  5. Critical edge: the amendment shows constitutional learning, but safeguards on paper still depend on institutions willing to enforce them — law is necessary, not sufficient.
  6. Conclusion: the 44th Amendment encodes a hard-won lesson into text — the truest institutional memory a democracy can keep.

Q4. Account for the Congress rout in the 1977 general election. What does it reveal about the resilience of Indian democracy? (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Frame March 1977 as the electorate’s verdict on the Emergency and the first defeat of the Congress at the Centre.
  2. Immediate causes: the excesses — forced sterilisation and slum demolitions — alienated the rural and urban poor; detentions and censorship angered the middle class; a united Janata Party removed vote-splitting.
  3. The heartland collapse (the core): the sterilisation drive hit North India hardest, so the Hindi belt swung decisively against Congress, while the South — less affected — stayed loyal, producing a stark North–South split.
  4. What it reveals: even after suspending rights, a regime could be removed peacefully at the ballot box — the deepest proof of democratic resilience.
  5. Critical edge: qualify the triumphalism — the Janata government’s swift collapse showed the electorate’s verdict was a rejection of the Emergency, not an endorsement of a coherent alternative.
  6. Conclusion: 1977 demonstrated that in India ultimate sovereignty rests with the voter — a lesson every subsequent government has had to respect.
