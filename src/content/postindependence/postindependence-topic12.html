Politics after Rajiv Gandhi (1989–99)

How India entered its coalition age — the decade of Mandal and Mandir, of the 1991 reforms that remade the economy, and of the churn that produced a new, more federal and more plural politics.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames the 1990s

The 1990s are tested as the decade of three simultaneous transformations — Mandal, Mandir and Market — that together remade Indian politics, society and the economy. Sort your prep into three buckets:

  • Static / factual recall — the governments (V.P. Singh, Chandra Shekhar, Rao, Deve Gowda, Gujral, Vajpayee), the Mandal Commission and Indra Sawhney, the Babri demolition (6 Dec 1992), the 1991 reforms, the 73rd/74th Amendments.
  • Structural transformation — the rise of OBC and regional parties (social justice), the growth of Hindutva mobilisation (identity), and the shift from a licence-permit to a liberalised economy (market).
  • Analytical (GS-I) — how these three currents interacted to end Congress dominance and produce a durable coalition, more federal, more plural order. Idea + example + judgement.

Framing note: Treat Mandir and communal events with strict balance and sensitivity — analyse them as political-historical developments, never take a partisan or communal position.

1. Setting: the coalition turn

The 1989 election ended the era of single-party majority government that had defined India since 1952 (with the brief Janata exception). For the next quarter-century, no party won a Lok Sabha majority. Power now depended on alliances — making the 1990s the true birth of India’s coalition age.

GS-I hook: Open any answer on this decade by naming its defining feature — the coalition turn — and its three engines: Mandal (caste), Mandir (religion) and Market (economy).

2. The National Front & V.P. Singh

The 1989 election, fought on the Bofors corruption issue, brought the National Front to power with V.P. Singh as Prime Minister, supported from outside by the ideologically opposed BJP and Left. It was a fragile arrangement from the start.

  • Anti-corruption plank: the government came to office promising clean governance after Bofors.
  • The balancing act: dependence on both the BJP (right) and the Left made the coalition inherently unstable.
  • The gamble: facing pressure, V.P. Singh played the Mandal card in 1990 — the decision that defined and ultimately felled his government.
One-line frame: The V.P. Singh government is remembered for one act above all — the implementation of the Mandal Commission report.

3. Mandal: OBC reservation

In August 1990 V.P. Singh announced the implementation of the Mandal Commission report, reserving 27% of central government jobs for Other Backward Classes (OBCs). The decision transformed Indian politics by placing caste-based social justice at its centre.

ElementDetail
Mandal CommissionSet up in 1979 (B.P. Mandal); reported in 1980; recommended 27% OBC reservation.
ImplementationAnnounced August 1990; triggered nationwide protests, including tragic self-immolations.
Indra Sawhney (1992)The Supreme Court upheld 27% OBC reservation but introduced the “creamy layer” exclusion and a 50% ceiling on total reservation.
Political effectEmpowered OBC and regional parties (SP, RJD, etc.); reshaped the party system around social justice.
Prelims anchor: Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (1992) is the landmark — 27% OBC quota upheld, “creamy layer” introduced, 50% ceiling affirmed. Very frequently tested.

4. Mandir: the Ayodhya movement

Parallel to Mandal ran “Mandir” — the movement for a Ram temple at the disputed site in Ayodhya. The BJP’s support surged through mass mobilisation, most dramatically L.K. Advani’s 1990 Rath Yatra, which reshaped the ideological map of Indian politics.

  • The mobilisation: the VHP-led temple movement and the Rath Yatra (1990) galvanised support for the BJP.
  • Advani’s arrest: the yatra’s halt in Bihar led the BJP to withdraw support from V.P. Singh, bringing down his government.
  • The rise of the BJP: from 2 seats in 1984 to a major national force by 1991 — the fastest ascent in Indian party history.
Critical edge: Analyse Mandal and Mandir as competing mobilisations — one around caste-based social justice, the other around religious identity — that between them reorganised the entire party system. Keep the treatment strictly analytical.

5. The demolition of the Babri Masjid

On 6 December 1992, the Babri Masjid was demolished by a large gathering of kar sevaks, triggering widespread communal violence across India. It was one of the gravest challenges to the secular fabric of the Republic and a defining event of the decade.

  • The event: the sixteenth-century mosque was pulled down despite assurances to the courts; riots followed in several cities.
  • Institutional response: the Liberhan Commission was appointed to inquire; the state governments concerned were dismissed.
  • Legal coda: the Supreme Court’s 2019 Ayodhya verdict awarded the disputed site for a temple and allotted alternative land for a mosque, while terming the 1992 demolition an illegal act.
  • Constitutional coda — S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994): upholding the dismissal of the BJP state governments, the Supreme Court held secularism to be part of the basic structure, made Article 356 justiciable, and made the floor test the sole test of majority.
  • The violent aftermath: the Bombay riots (Dec 1992–Jan 1993, ~900 dead, later probed by the Srikrishna Commission) and the 12 March 1993 Bombay serial blasts (257 killed) — the demolition’s bloodiest sequel.
GS-I hook: Treat 6 December 1992 as a constitutional and secular turning point — discuss its impact on communal relations, the party system and the debate over secularism, with balance and without partisanship.

6. The crisis of 1991

Even as politics churned, the economy reached breaking point. By mid-1991 India faced a severe balance-of-payments crisis — foreign reserves fell to barely two weeks of imports, and the country had to pledge gold to meet obligations.

CauseEffect
Fiscal & current-account deficitsYears of high borrowing and import dependence.
Gulf War (1990–91)Oil prices spiked; remittances from the Gulf fell.
Political instabilityLoss of investor confidence; capital flight.
Reserves collapseIndia pledged gold to the Bank of England / UBS to avoid default.
Link it: The 1991 crisis is the trigger for the reforms — always present liberalisation as a response to an existential emergency, not merely an ideological choice.

7. The 1991 economic reforms

Responding to the crisis, the Narasimha Rao government, with Finance Minister Manmohan Singh, launched the reforms of Liberalisation, Privatisation and Globalisation (LPG) — dismantling the licence-permit raj and opening India to the world.

Fig 12.1 — The LPG reforms of 1991.
MeasureSubstance
Industrial delicensingAbolished industrial licensing for most sectors; ended the “permit raj.”
Trade & rupeeDevalued the rupee; slashed import tariffs; moved toward convertibility.
Public sectorOpened sectors to private and foreign investment; began disinvestment.
Financial reformReforms of banking, capital markets (SEBI) and foreign investment norms.
GS-I/GS-III hook: The 1991 reforms are the single most consequential economic event of post-independence India after the Green Revolution — they doubled growth rates over the following decades and created a new middle class.

8. The Narasimha Rao government

P.V. Narasimha Rao (1991–96) led a minority government that nonetheless completed a full term and presided over epochal change — earning him the label “the father of Indian economic reforms” and a reputation as a shrewd political survivor.

  • Economic: steered the LPG reforms through a fractious Parliament.
  • Foreign policy: launched the “Look East” policy and normalised ties with Israel and the US after the Cold War.
  • Challenges: the Babri demolition (1992) on his watch and later corruption cases clouded his record.
One-line frame: Rao converted a weak political hand into a transformative tenure — proof that minority governments can deliver historic change.

9. The 73rd & 74th Amendments

The Rao government enacted the landmark 73rd and 74th Constitutional Amendments (1992–93), giving constitutional status to panchayats (rural) and municipalities (urban) — the culmination of the decentralisation push begun under Rajiv Gandhi.

Feature73rd (Panchayats)74th (Municipalities)
StructureThree-tier: village, block, districtNagar panchayat, municipal council, municipal corporation
ElectionsEvery 5 years; State Election CommissionEvery 5 years; State Election Commission
ReservationSC/ST and one-third for womenSC/ST and one-third for women
SchedulesEleventh Schedule (29 subjects)Twelfth Schedule (18 subjects)
GS-II crossover: The 73rd/74th Amendments are core polity material — deepening democracy to the grassroots and mandating women’s reservation in local bodies, a quiet social revolution.

10. The United Front governments

The 1996 election produced a hung Parliament. After a 13-day Vajpayee government failed to secure a majority, the United Front — a coalition of regional and Left parties supported from outside by Congress — formed two short-lived governments.

  • H.D. Deve Gowda (1996–97): a Janata Dal leader from Karnataka; presided over a coalition of thirteen parties.
  • I.K. Gujral (1997–98): known for the Gujral Doctrine — non-reciprocal accommodation of smaller South Asian neighbours in foreign policy.
  • Instability: both governments depended on Congress’s external support and fell when it was withdrawn.
Prelims anchor: The Gujral Doctrine (five principles of good-neighbourliness, non-reciprocity with smaller neighbours) is associated with I.K. Gujral — a commonly tested link.

11. The rise of the NDA & Vajpayee

The instability of the United Front paved the way for the National Democratic Alliance (NDA). After a 13-month Vajpayee government (1998–99) fell by one vote, the NDA won a stable majority in 1999 — the first non-Congress coalition to complete a full term.

MilestoneDetail
Pokhran-II (May 1998)India conducted nuclear tests and declared itself a nuclear-weapon state.
Kargil War (1999)India repelled Pakistani intrusion in Kargil — a decisive military and diplomatic success.
Coalition managementThe NDA institutionalised coalition governance with a common agenda across ~two dozen parties.
1999 mandateA stable NDA majority under Vajpayee, completing a full term (1999–2004).
GS-I hook: Vajpayee’s NDA demonstrated that a stable, full-term coalition was possible — the maturing of the coalition experiment that had begun in disorder in 1989.

12. Assessment of the decade

The 1990s were a decade of creative destruction — the old Congress-dominant, statist, upper-caste-led order gave way to a more federal, more plural, market-oriented India.

  • Democratic deepening: Mandal empowered OBCs; the 73rd/74th Amendments took democracy to the village — representation widened.
  • Economic transformation: the 1991 reforms unleashed growth and a new middle class.
  • Communal stress: the Mandir movement and the Babri demolition strained the secular fabric and reshaped ideological competition.
  • Coalition maturation: from instability (1989–98) to stable coalitions (1999 onward).
Assessment frame: The balanced verdict: the decade widened democracy and modernised the economy, but at the cost of heightened identity conflict — a mixed but transformative inheritance.

13. Legacy

The settlements of the 1990s still structure Indian politics and economy today.

  • Reservation politics: the Mandal framework and Indra Sawhney still govern reservation debates (creamy layer, 50% ceiling, EWS quota).
  • Reform consensus: the 1991 reforms created a durable, cross-party consensus on a market economy.
  • Coalition norms: common minimum programmes and alliance politics became standard practice.
  • Local democracy: the 73rd/74th Amendments entrenched grassroots government and women’s representation.
GS-I hook: Frame the 1990s as the decade that built the political-economic architecture of contemporary India — every subsequent government has operated within its terms.

14. Contemporary relevance

The 1990s remain the reference point for today’s biggest debates.

  • Reservation: the EWS quota (103rd Amendment, 2019) and sub-classification debates trace directly to the Mandal/Indra Sawhney framework.
  • Ayodhya: the 2019 Supreme Court verdict and the 2024 temple consecration close a chapter opened in 1992.
  • Reform anniversaries: the 1991 reforms are routinely revisited in debates on the “next generation” of economic reform.
Answer value-add: Linking the EWS quota or the 2019 Ayodhya verdict back to the 1990s shows the examiner the continuity from that decade to the present.

15. Prelims practice (PYQ pattern)

The questions below follow the UPSC prelims pattern for this theme. Self-authored practice questions are marked as model questions with no fixed year; only genuinely dated UPSC questions carry a year.

Q1. The Mandal Commission was primarily concerned with:

  • Select the correct option.
  • (a) Centre–State relations
  • (b) Reservation for socially and educationally backward classes (OBCs) — correct
  • (c) Reorganisation of states
  • (d) Electoral reforms

Answer: (b) OBC reservation. Set up in 1979 under B.P. Mandal, it recommended 27% reservation for OBCs; implemented in 1990.

Q2. The Indra Sawhney case (1992) is associated with which of the following?

  1. Upholding 27% OBC reservation.
  2. Introducing the “creamy layer” exclusion.
  3. Affirming a 50% ceiling on total reservation.
  • Which of the statements given above are correct?
  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3 — correct

Answer: (d) 1, 2 and 3. All three are core holdings of the Indra Sawhney (Mandal) judgment.

Q3. The 73rd and 74th Constitutional Amendments deal, respectively, with:

  • Select the correct option.
  • (a) Anti-defection and reservation
  • (b) Panchayats (rural) and municipalities (urban) — correct
  • (c) GST and finance commissions
  • (d) Fundamental duties and DPSP

Answer: (b) Panchayats and municipalities. They added the Eleventh (29 subjects) and Twelfth (18 subjects) Schedules and mandated one-third women’s reservation.

Q4. The 1991 economic reforms were launched under which Prime Minister and Finance Minister?

  • Select the correct option.
  • (a) V.P. Singh and Madhu Dandavate
  • (b) P.V. Narasimha Rao and Manmohan Singh — correct
  • (c) Chandra Shekhar and Yashwant Sinha
  • (d) Rajiv Gandhi and V.P. Singh

Answer: (b) Narasimha Rao (PM) and Manmohan Singh (FM). They launched the LPG reforms in response to the balance-of-payments crisis.

Q5. The “Gujral Doctrine” in Indian foreign policy is best described as:

  • Select the correct option.
  • (a) A policy of nuclear deterrence
  • (b) Non-reciprocal accommodation of smaller South Asian neighbours — correct
  • (c) Alignment with the Western bloc
  • (d) Export-led industrialisation

Answer: (b) Non-reciprocity toward smaller neighbours. Associated with I.K. Gujral; a set of five principles of good-neighbourliness.

Q6. Arrange the following in correct chronological order:

  1. Demolition of the Babri Masjid
  2. Implementation of the Mandal Commission
  3. Pokhran-II nuclear tests
  4. Launch of the 1991 economic reforms
  • Select the correct chronological sequence.
  • (a) 2–4–1–3 — correct
  • (b) 4–2–1–3
  • (c) 2–1–4–3
  • (d) 1–2–4–3

Answer: (a) 2–4–1–3. Mandal (Aug 1990) → reforms (Jul 1991) → Babri demolition (Dec 1992) → Pokhran-II (May 1998).

16. Mains practice + model answers

Model questions in the UPSC GS-I / GS-II idiom, each with a full answer skeleton. These are practice questions with no fixed year unless a genuine dated UPSC question is cited.

Q1. The 1990s remade Indian politics through the simultaneous forces of “Mandal, Mandir and Market.” Critically examine. (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Frame the decade as three simultaneous transformations — caste (Mandal), religion (Mandir) and economy (Market) — that together ended Congress dominance.
  2. Mandal: OBC reservation (1990) and Indra Sawhney empowered backward classes and regional parties — a democratic deepening around social justice.
  3. Mandir (the core): the Ayodhya movement and the 1992 demolition reorganised ideological competition around religious identity and strained secularism — treat analytically and with balance.
  4. Market: the 1991 reforms replaced the statist model with a liberalised economy, creating growth and a new middle class.
  5. Their interaction: the three currents produced a more federal, plural, market-oriented but also more identity-driven polity.
  6. Conclusion: the 1990s built the enduring architecture of contemporary India — simultaneously more inclusive and more contested.

Q2. “The 1991 reforms were a response to crisis rather than conviction.” Discuss their causes and long-term significance. (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Situate the reforms in the acute 1991 balance-of-payments crisis, not merely ideology.
  2. The crisis causes: chronic fiscal/current-account deficits, the Gulf War oil shock, political instability and the collapse of reserves that forced gold to be pledged.
  3. The reforms (the core): LPG — industrial delicensing, rupee devaluation, tariff cuts, disinvestment and financial-market reform.
  4. Long-term significance: a lasting doubling of growth, a new middle class, integration with the world economy, and a durable cross-party reform consensus.
  5. Critical edge: qualify with the costs — rising inequality, agrarian stress and jobless-growth concerns; and note reforms were incremental, not a clean break.
  6. Conclusion: born of crisis, the reforms became conviction — the most consequential economic turn since the Green Revolution.

Q3. Evaluate the significance of the 73rd and 74th Constitutional Amendments for Indian democracy. (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Frame the 1992–93 amendments as the constitutionalisation of local self-government — the “third tier” of Indian federalism.
  2. Key features: mandatory five-yearly elections, State Election Commissions, three-tier panchayats, and the Eleventh/Twelfth Schedules.
  3. Democratic deepening (the core): one-third reservation for women and SC/ST representation brought millions of new participants into elected office — a quiet social revolution.
  4. Limits: uneven devolution of the “three Fs” (funds, functions, functionaries); many states starved local bodies of real power.
  5. Critical edge: the constitutional promise outran its practice — genuine decentralisation remains incomplete.
  6. Conclusion: the amendments were a landmark in deepening democracy, whose full potential depends on real fiscal and administrative devolution.

Q4. Trace the evolution of coalition politics in India from 1989 to 1999. What lessons did it offer for governance? (Model question — no fixed year)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Frame 1989 as the end of single-party majority rule and the start of a decade of coalition experimentation.
  2. The unstable phase: National Front (V.P. Singh), Chandra Shekhar, and the two United Front governments — short-lived, dependent on fragile external support.
  3. The stabilising phase (the core): Rao’s full-term minority government and then Vajpayee’s NDA showed coalitions could deliver stable, full-term governance.
  4. Institutional lessons: the value of common minimum programmes, coordination committees, and accommodating regional parties.
  5. Critical edge: coalitions also brought policy paralysis risks and “coalition compulsions” — a double-edged inheritance.
  6. Conclusion: the decade matured Indian democracy’s capacity for power-sharing — making federal, plural coalition governance the new normal.
