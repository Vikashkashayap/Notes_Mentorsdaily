Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames India 2000–2014

This period is tested as the maturing of two things at once: coalition governance and a rights-based state. Sort your prep into three buckets:

  • Static / factual (Prelims): government terms and PMs (NDA 1999–2004 Vajpayee; UPA-I 2004–09 & UPA-II 2009–14 Manmohan Singh), flagship schemes and Acts (RTI 2005, NREGA 2005, RTE 2009, FRA 2006, NFSA 2013), and the India–US nuclear deal steps (123 Agreement, NSG waiver, IAEA safeguards).
  • Conceptual (governance): "coalition dharma", the National Common Minimum Programme, the National Advisory Council, and the shift from the licence-raj mindset to a rights-based welfare architecture.
  • Analytical (GS-I): how India reconciled high growth with inclusion, why the "growth decade" ended in scams and a slowdown, and what the 2014 verdict said about coalition politics and governance expectations.

Framing note: Do not reduce this to a list of schemes. UPSC rewards the argument that 2000–2014 tried to fuse reform-led growth with rights-based redistribution — and the tension between them defines the decade.

1. Overview & Why It Matters

The years 2000–2014 were India's coalition decade: no single party won a majority, yet the governments of A.B. Vajpayee and Manmohan Singh delivered some of the most consequential policy of the post-1991 era. It was the period when India's growth accelerated toward 8–9%, when a rights-based welfare architecture was legislated, and when India stepped onto the global stage with the nuclear deal — before the decade closed in corruption controversies and a growth slowdown.

  • Two coalitions, one continuity: the NDA (BJP-led) and the UPA (Congress-led) alternated, but both governed through allies and largely continued liberalisation — a bipartisan reform consensus.
  • The defining fusion: market-led growth plus a rights-based safety net (work, information, education, food) — India's distinctive answer to inclusive growth.
  • Why it matters for GS-I: it is the immediate prehistory of contemporary India — the schemes, institutions and debates of today were largely built or reshaped in this decade.
GS-I hook: Read 2000–2014 as the decade India tried to have it both ways — fast growth and guaranteed rights — and judge how far the two reinforced or strained each other.

2. The Age of Coalitions

By 2000, coalition government was no longer an aberration but the norm. The single-party dominance of the Congress "system" had given way to a fragmented, federalised polity in which regional and caste-based parties held the balance.

  • Why coalitions: the "second democratic upsurge" (Mandal, regionalisation) produced a multi-party system where national parties depended on state-based allies to form governments.
  • "Coalition dharma": Vajpayee's phrase for the compromises of running a 20-plus-party alliance — consensus-building, shelving divisive agendas (Ayodhya, Article 370, Uniform Civil Code kept off the NDA agenda).
  • Common Minimum Programmes: both the NDA agenda and the UPA's National Common Minimum Programme (2004) were negotiated documents binding disparate allies to a shared platform.
  • Federal deepening: the era strengthened states and regional parties — cooperative-and-competitive federalism became a lived reality, not a textbook phrase.

3. The Vajpayee NDA Years (1999–2004)

The Vajpayee government proved that a non-Congress coalition could govern stably and reform boldly, combining infrastructure ambition, disinvestment and a bold foreign-policy opening — while managing Kargil and Gujarat.

  • Infrastructure push: the Golden Quadrilateral and the Pradhan Mantri Gram Sadak Yojana (2000) — a highways-and-rural-roads programme that reshaped connectivity.
  • Reforms & disinvestment: a dedicated Disinvestment Ministry (Arun Shourie), privatisation (VSNL, Balco, Maruti stake sale), the Fiscal Responsibility and Budget Management (FRBM) Act 2003, and telecom's New Telecom Policy 1999 — the launchpad of the mobile revolution.
  • Social schemes: Sarva Shiksha Abhiyan (2001) for universal elementary education; the 86th Amendment (2002) making education a fundamental right (Art. 21A).
  • Kargil & nuclear: following Pokhran-II (1998), the Kargil War (1999) was won; the Lahore bus diplomacy and later Agra summit (2001) marked Pakistan outreach.
  • "India Shining": the 2004 campaign slogan celebrated growth but misread rural distress — the NDA lost, a lesson in growth without felt inclusion.
  • Pokhran-II — "Operation Shakti" (11 & 13 May 1998): led by A.P.J. Abdul Kalam, R. Chidambaram and Anil Kakodkar; 11 May is now observed as National Technology Day.
  • Nuclear Doctrine (4 Jan 2003): credible minimum deterrent, No-First-Use, massive retaliation, and civilian control via the Nuclear Command Authority and Strategic Forces Command.
  • Three new states (2000): Chhattisgarh (1 Nov), Uttaranchal/Uttarakhand (9 Nov) and Jharkhand (15 Nov) — the NDA-era reorganisation of central India.
  • Golden Quadrilateral (5,846 km): linking Delhi–Mumbai–Chennai–Kolkata under the NHDP (from 1998), with the PMGSY (25 Dec 2000) extending all-weather roads to villages above 500 people.
GS-I hook: The NDA years show reform continuity across party lines — and "India Shining" is the classic cautionary tale that aggregate growth does not automatically translate into electoral or social inclusion.

4. Second-Generation Economic Reforms

If 1991 was the first generation (dismantling the licence raj), the 2000s attempted "second-generation" reforms — deeper, harder changes in factor markets, infrastructure and governance.

AreaFirst generation (1991)Second generation (2000s)
FocusDelicensing, trade, FDI, exchange rateInfrastructure, fiscal discipline, financial sector, disinvestment
LandmarksIndustrial de-licensing, rupee devaluationFRBM Act 2003, Golden Quadrilateral, telecom NTP-99, VAT (2005)
DifficultyProduct-market reform (easier)Factor-market & institutional reform (politically harder)
  • Fiscal reform: the FRBM Act 2003 committed governments to deficit-reduction targets — institutionalising fiscal prudence.
  • Tax reform: state-level VAT (from 2005) rationalised indirect taxes, a stepping-stone toward the later GST.
  • Financial & telecom: the telecom revolution (falling call rates, mobile explosion) and a deepening capital market powered services-led growth.
  • Unfinished agenda: labour, land and privatisation of banks stalled — the politically costly reforms that coalition governments could not push.
Analytical edge: Second-generation reforms were harder precisely because they touched factor markets (land, labour, capital) with concentrated losers — explaining why coalition India reformed infrastructure and taxes but stalled on labour and land.

5. UPA-I (2004–2009)

The UPA's surprise 2004 victory, read as a verdict against jobless "India Shining" growth, produced a government committed by its National Common Minimum Programme to "growth with a human face".

  • The NCMP: a negotiated platform with Left support from outside, prioritising employment, farmers, social sector spending and public investment.
  • The National Advisory Council (NAC): chaired by Sonia Gandhi, a bridge between civil-society movements and government that drafted much of the rights-based agenda (NREGA, RTI, Food Security).
  • The growth dividend: UPA-I rode the fastest growth phase in Indian history (~9% in the mid-2000s), expanding revenues that funded welfare.
  • The nuclear deal gamble: Manmohan Singh staked the government on the India–US deal in 2008, surviving a confidence vote after the Left withdrew support (see §7).
GS-I hook: UPA-I is the pivot where the social movements of the previous chapter (RTI, NREGA campaigns) entered the statute book — the NAC was the institutional channel that carried movement demands into law.

6. The Rights-Based Welfare Turn

The single most examinable feature of this decade is the shift from discretionary welfare schemes to justiciable rights — entitlements citizens could claim as a matter of law.

Right / LawYearEntitlement
Right to Information Act2005Right to access government information
NREGA (later MGNREGA)2005100 days' guaranteed wage employment per rural household
Forest Rights Act2006Individual & community forest rights for forest-dwellers
Right to Education Act2009Free & compulsory education, ages 6–14 (Art. 21A)
National Food Security Act2013Subsidised foodgrain to ~two-thirds of the population
Land Acquisition (LARR) Act2013Fair compensation, consent & R&R for land acquisition
  • The paradigm shift: from "the state may provide" to "the citizen may demand" — welfare recast as enforceable entitlement.
  • Movement roots: RTI grew from the MKSS campaign, NREGA from the right-to-work movement, FRA from adivasi struggles — the legislative harvest of the social movements (see previous chapter).
  • Aadhaar & delivery: the UIDAI/Aadhaar project (from 2009) began the technology stack later used for direct benefit transfer — a delivery-reform seed planted in this decade.
  • Critique: critics argued rights-based spending strained fiscal balance and that leakages blunted impact — the growth-vs-welfare tension in sharp form.
Analytical edge: The rights-based turn is India's distinctive model of inclusive growth — use it to argue that the decade tried to redistribute the fruits of growth through law, not just charity, while noting the fiscal and delivery challenges it raised.

7. The India–US Nuclear Deal

The civil-nuclear deal (2005–2008) was the decade's foreign-policy centrepiece — ending India's post-1974 nuclear isolation and recalibrating its relations with the world's major powers.

Fig 7.1 — The India–US civil-nuclear deal, 2005–2008.
  • The steps: the July 2005 Manmohan–Bush joint statement → US Hyde Act (2006) → the bilateral 123 Agreement → India-specific IAEA safeguards → the NSG waiver (Sep 2008) allowing India to trade in nuclear fuel and technology despite being outside the NPT.
  • Domestic drama: the Left withdrew support in July 2008; the government survived a confidence vote — the deal became a test of the PM's authority.
  • Significance: a "de facto" recognition of India as a responsible nuclear power, unlocking civil-nuclear commerce and cementing the India–US strategic partnership.
  • Caveat: the Civil Liability for Nuclear Damage Act (2010), with its supplier-liability clause, later slowed the commercial payoff.
GS-I/GS-II hook: Frame the deal as the moment India traded strategic autonomy anxieties for global integration — ending nuclear apartheid while retaining its weapons programme outside the NPT.

8. UPA-II (2009–2014)

Returned with a stronger mandate in 2009, UPA-II legislated its most ambitious rights (Food Security, LARR) but was engulfed by corruption scandals, a governance-paralysis narrative and a sharp economic slowdown.

  • Legislative peaks: the National Food Security Act 2013, the LARR Act 2013 (land acquisition), the Companies Act 2013 (CSR mandate), and the Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act 2013 (in response to anti-corruption protests).
  • The scam cascade: the 2G spectrum allocation, the Commonwealth Games, and "Coalgate" (coal-block allocation) controversies — amplified by an assertive CAG and Supreme Court — framed UPA-II as corruption-ridden.
  • "Policy paralysis": investigation-fear and coalition friction slowed decision-making; investment sentiment soured.
  • Economic reversal: the post-2011 slowdown, high inflation, a widening current-account deficit and the 2013 "taper tantrum" rupee crisis punctured the growth story.
  • Nirbhaya & gender-justice reform (2012–13): the December 2012 Delhi gang-rape spurred the Justice Verma Committee (report in 29 days) and the Criminal Law (Amendment) Act 2013, which criminalised stalking, voyeurism and acid attacks and redefined rape.
Balanced note: UPA-II simultaneously delivered landmark rights legislation and presided over its own delegitimisation through scams and slowdown — a study in how governance perception can eclipse policy substance.

9. Scams, Anti-Corruption & Governance

The anti-corruption ferment of 2011–13 was one of the most significant civil-society mobilisations since the JP movement — and it reshaped the political landscape.

  • India Against Corruption (2011): Anna Hazare's hunger strikes demanding a strong Lokpal drew mass urban support and dominated the national agenda.
  • Institutional assertion: the CAG's audit reports (notional-loss estimates), Supreme Court interventions (cancelling 2G licences and coal allocations), and an activist media created an accountability spotlight.
  • Political fallout: the movement birthed the Aam Aadmi Party (2012), which won Delhi — a movement converting into a party, echoing earlier NSM-to-politics arcs.
  • Legislative response: the Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act 2013 and the Whistle Blowers Protection Act 2014 — belated statutory answers to the protest.
GS-I hook: The 2011 anti-corruption movement links directly to the civil-society chapter — read it as the RTI-era transparency movement escalating into a mass demand for institutional accountability, with lasting effects on 2014.

10. The Growth Decade & Its End

Economically, 2003–2011 was India's fastest sustained expansion, followed by a sharp deceleration — the "boom and bust" that framed the 2014 election.

PhaseYearsFeatures
The boom2003–2008~8–9% growth, services & investment surge, rising savings/investment rates
Crisis & stimulus2008–2010Global financial crisis; India cushioned by domestic demand + fiscal-monetary stimulus
The slowdown2011–2014Growth falling below 5%, high inflation, twin deficits, rupee slide, "policy paralysis"
  • Resilience in 2008: India weathered the global crisis relatively well — a bank-conservative, domestic-demand-driven economy plus counter-cyclical stimulus.
  • The reversal: the stimulus overstayed, fuelling inflation and fiscal/current-account deficits; stalled projects and investor caution deepened the slump.
  • The 2013 rupee crisis: the US "taper" triggered capital outflows and a sharp rupee depreciation, met by RBI (Raghuram Rajan) stabilisation measures.
Analytical edge: Connect this to the economy chapter — the decade shows both the strength of post-1991 India (crisis resilience) and its vulnerabilities (twin deficits, stalled reforms), setting up the 2014 governance-and-growth mandate.

11. Foreign Policy & Security

Beyond the nuclear deal, 2000–2014 saw India deepen its major-power partnerships, expand its economic diplomacy, and confront cross-border terrorism.

  • Strategic partnerships: a transformed India–US relationship, a stable "special and privileged strategic partnership" with Russia, and rising trade with China even amid the boundary dispute.
  • Neighbourhood & "Look East": engagement with ASEAN and East Asia deepened; relations with Pakistan swung between the composite dialogue and rupture after the 2008 Mumbai (26/11) attacks.
  • Multilateral rise: India joined the G20 as a significant voice after 2008, pushed for UN Security Council reform, and shaped BRICS and IBSA groupings.
  • Security shocks: the 2001 Parliament attack (Operation Parakram standoff) and the 26/11 Mumbai attacks (2008) drove counter-terror and coastal-security reforms (NIA, NSG hubs).
  • 26/11 Mumbai (26–29 Nov 2008): 10 LeT attackers, 166 killed, ended by NSG "Operation Black Tornado"; the response created the NIA (Act, Dec 2008), four regional NSG hubs and a restructured coastal-security grid.
  • Kargil Review Committee (K. Subrahmanyam, 1999–2000): its recommendations seeded India's higher-defence reform — the Chief of Defence Staff (created 2019), the Defence Intelligence Agency (2002) and the NTRO (2004).
GS-I/GS-II hook: The decade marks India's transition from a balance-of-payments-anxious economy to an aspiring major power — economic weight translating into diplomatic reach (G20, nuclear deal, strategic partnerships).

12. Assessment & Debates

How should the coalition decade be judged? As a period of real achievement shadowed by governance failure — growth and rights delivered, but trust squandered.

AchievementsFailures / criticisms
Fastest growth phase in Indian history; crisis resilience (2008)Growth ended in slowdown, inflation and twin deficits
Rights-based welfare architecture (RTI, NREGA, RTE, FRA, NFSA)Leakages, fiscal strain, uneven delivery
Nuclear deal & major-power partnershipsNuclear liability law blunted the commercial gain
Deepened federalism & coalition governanceCoalition friction → "policy paralysis" & corruption
  • The central tension: reform-led growth vs rights-based redistribution — the decade proved they could coexist but strained their fiscal and administrative fit.
  • The governance deficit: scams and paralysis showed that policy substance is not enough without credible, decisive governance — the lesson the 2014 verdict encoded.
Balanced verdict: The decade legislated a more rights-secure, faster-growing India, yet its own governance failures — corruption, indecision, a stalled reform agenda — consumed the political capital that success had built.

13. Legacy & the 2014 Turn

The 2014 general election ended the coalition decade: for the first time since 1984, a single party (BJP) won an outright Lok Sabha majority, on a platform of decisive governance and development.

  • End of the coalition era (temporarily): the return of single-party majority reversed three decades of fragmentation — a structural shift in Indian politics.
  • Continuity beneath change: the incoming government inherited and largely retained the rights-based schemes (renamed, redesigned around Aadhaar/DBT) and the growth model — policy continuity within political rupture.
  • The governance premium: 2014 signalled voter demand for effective delivery over entitlement announcements — a lasting reframing of political competition.
  • Institutional seeds: Aadhaar, GST groundwork (VAT), and DBT plumbing built in 2000–2014 became the delivery backbone of the next decade.
  • Telangana — India's 29th state (2 Jun 2014): the culmination of a six-decade movement (TRS founded by K. Chandrasekhar Rao, 2001; Srikrishna Committee, 2010) — the only state formed by sustained mass agitation, reviving the "development-backwardness" basis for statehood.
  • 2014 verdict in numbers: BJP 282 seats on a 31% vote share — the first single-party majority since 1984; Congress fell to 44 (below Leader-of-Opposition strength) at a record 66.4% turnout.
One-line frame: 2000–2014 built the rights-and-growth architecture of modern India; 2014 changed who would run it and how — continuity of substance, rupture of style and mandate.

14. Current Affairs Linkage

The institutions and debates of this decade remain live — UPSC regularly tests the 2000s through their present-day continuations.

  • Rights-scheme evolution: MGNREGA's continuing role in rural distress, NFSA and one-nation-one-ration-card, and RTE outcomes — all trace to this decade's laws.
  • Aadhaar & DBT: the delivery revolution built on the UIDAI seeded here; the Puttaswamy privacy judgment (2017) reviewed its foundations.
  • Transparency debates: RTI amendments and Lokpal functioning descend directly from the 2005–2013 accountability architecture.
  • Land & growth: LARR 2013's consent/R&R provisions frame today's land-acquisition and infrastructure debates.
  • Strategic partnership: the India–US relationship the nuclear deal cemented underpins current Indo-Pacific and Quad cooperation.
Answer tip: For any question on a flagship scheme, RTI, land acquisition or the India–US partnership, anchor the answer in its 2000–2014 origin — historical grounding lifts a routine answer.

15. Prelims PYQs & Practice

UPSC-pattern multiple-choice practice on India, 2000–2014. Attempt before revealing the answer; each includes a one-line explanation.

Q1. The Vajpayee-led NDA government (1999–2004) is historically notable as:

  • The first coalition government in independent India
  • The first non-Congress government to complete a full five-year term ✓
  • The first government to introduce the licence raj
  • The first minority government supported by the Left

Answer: b. The 1999–2004 NDA was the first non-Congress-led government to serve a full term; earlier non-Congress governments (Janata, NF, UF) collapsed early.

Q2. Consider the following rights-based laws and their years:
1. Right to Information Act — 2005
2. Right to Education Act — 2009
3. National Food Security Act — 2013
Which of the pairs are correctly matched?

  • 1 and 2 only
  • 2 and 3 only
  • 1, 2 and 3 ✓
  • 1 and 3 only

Answer: c. RTI 2005, RTE 2009 (Art. 21A operationalised) and NFSA 2013 are all correctly dated.

Q3. The Nuclear Suppliers Group (NSG) waiver relevant to the India–US civil nuclear deal was granted in:

  • 2005
  • 2006
  • 2008 ✓
  • 2010

Answer: c. The NSG granted India a waiver in September 2008, enabling civil-nuclear commerce despite India being outside the NPT.

Q4. With reference to MGNREGA, consider the following statements:
1. It legally guarantees a fixed number of days of wage employment to rural households.
2. It was enacted during the UPA-I government.
Which of the statements is/are correct?

  • 1 only
  • 2 only
  • Both 1 and 2 ✓
  • Neither 1 nor 2

Answer: c. NREGA (2005, UPA-I) legally guarantees 100 days of wage employment per rural household per year; both statements are correct.

Q5. The Golden Quadrilateral highway project is associated with which government?

  • UPA-I
  • UPA-II
  • The Vajpayee-led NDA ✓
  • The United Front

Answer: c. The Golden Quadrilateral (and PMGSY) were flagship infrastructure initiatives of the Vajpayee NDA government.

Q6. The National Advisory Council (NAC) during UPA-I is best described as:

  • A constitutional body under Article 280
  • An advisory body linking civil society to government that shaped rights-based legislation ✓
  • A statutory economic-planning body
  • A wing of the Election Commission

Answer: b. The NAC (chaired by Sonia Gandhi) was an advisory body that channelled civil-society inputs into laws like NREGA, RTI and Food Security; it was not a constitutional/statutory body.

Anticipated themes — high-probability areas: government terms & PMs (NDA 1999–2004 / UPA-I 2004–09 / UPA-II 2009–14); rights-law years (RTI 2005, NREGA 2005, FRA 2006, RTE 2009, NFSA 2013, LARR 2013, Lokpal 2013); nuclear-deal sequence (2005 joint statement → 2006 Hyde Act → 123 Agreement → IAEA safeguards → 2008 NSG waiver); Vajpayee-era schemes (Golden Quadrilateral, PMGSY, SSA, FRBM 2003, 86th Amendment); "coalition dharma"/NCMP; India Against Corruption → AAP (2012) & Lokpal; the 2013 rupee/taper crisis; Aadhaar/UIDAI origins.

16. Mains PYQs & Model Answers

Practice GS-I mains questions with full model-answer skeletons. These are model questions in the UPSC pattern; write your own version in 150–250 words.

Q1. "The decade 2004–2014 marked a rights-based turn in Indian welfare." Discuss its features and evaluate its impact. (15 marks, 250 words)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: define the rights-based turn — the shift from discretionary schemes to justiciable entitlements — and locate it in UPA governance and the NAC.
  2. Features (the core): RTI 2005 (information), NREGA 2005 (work), FRA 2006 (forest), RTE 2009 (education), NFSA 2013 (food), LARR 2013 (land) — welfare recast as enforceable law.
  3. Roots: civil-society movements (MKSS, right-to-work, adivasi struggles) channelled through the NAC.
  4. Impact: expanded entitlements, empowered claim-making, cushioned rural distress; Aadhaar/DBT began improving delivery.
  5. Critical edge: fiscal strain, leakages, uneven implementation, and the growth-vs-welfare tension.
  6. Conclusion: a distinctive Indian model of inclusive growth — redistribution through law — whose promise depends on the quality of delivery.

Q2. Examine the significance of the India–US civil nuclear deal for India's foreign policy. (15 marks, 250 words)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: situate the deal (2005–2008) as the end of India's post-1974 nuclear isolation.
  2. The process (the core): 2005 joint statement → Hyde Act → 123 Agreement → IAEA safeguards → 2008 NSG waiver, allowing civil-nuclear trade outside the NPT.
  3. Significance: de facto recognition as a responsible nuclear power; deepened India–US strategic partnership; access to fuel and technology; enhanced global standing (G20, NSG aspirations).
  4. Domestic dimension: the 2008 confidence vote — a test of the PM's authority and coalition politics.
  5. Critical edge: the nuclear-liability law (2010) blunted commercial gains; concerns over strategic autonomy.
  6. Conclusion: a watershed that integrated India into the global nuclear order and anchored the US partnership central to today's Indo-Pacific strategy.

Q3. "Coalition politics shaped both the achievements and the limits of governance in India between 2000 and 2014." Critically analyse. (15 marks, 250 words)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: characterise 2000–2014 as the mature phase of coalition government (NDA and UPA), governed by "coalition dharma" and common minimum programmes.
  2. Achievements (the core): stable full-term governments, bipartisan reform continuity, rights-based welfare, the nuclear deal, deepened federalism.
  3. Limits: divisive agendas shelved; hard reforms (land, labour) stalled; coalition friction and scams bred "policy paralysis".
  4. The balancing dynamic: coalitions forced consensus and moderation but diffused accountability and slowed decisive action.
  5. Critical edge: the 2014 single-party majority was partly a reaction against coalition indecision — voters demanding decisive governance.
  6. Conclusion: coalitions deepened democracy and federalism yet exposed the governance costs of fragmented power — a defining trade-off of the decade.

Q4. Assess the causes and consequences of the economic slowdown and anti-corruption ferment of the UPA-II years. (15 marks, 250 words)

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: frame UPA-II (2009–2014) as legislative peak yet political nadir — landmark laws amid scams and slowdown.
  2. Causes of slowdown (the core): overstayed post-2008 stimulus → inflation and twin deficits; stalled projects and investor caution; the 2013 taper-tantrum rupee crisis.
  3. Anti-corruption ferment: 2G, CWG, Coalgate; CAG and Supreme Court assertion; India Against Corruption (2011); rise of AAP (2012).
  4. Consequences: "policy paralysis" narrative, erosion of governance credibility, Lokpal Act 2013.
  5. Critical edge: policy substance (Food Security, LARR) was eclipsed by perception of drift and graft.
  6. Conclusion: the twin crises delegitimised UPA-II and set up the 2014 verdict for decisive, corruption-free governance.
