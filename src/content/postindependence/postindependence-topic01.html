Chapter 1: Legacy of Colonial Rule & the Partition of India

India in August 1947 inherited two intertwined burdens — the economic, administrative and social legacy of nearly two centuries of colonial rule, and the trauma of Partition. This book-depth file traces the drain of wealth & deindustrialisation, the institutional inheritance (ICS, army, railways, rule of law), the communal & constitutional road to 1947 (Two-Nation Theory → Cabinet Mission → Direct Action Day → the 3 June Plan → the Indian Independence Act 1947), the Radcliffe boundary award, the violence & the great migration, refugee rehabilitation, and the unfinished business (Kashmir, minorities) — with maps, flowcharts, tables and real dated PYQs + full model answers.

Conceptual Clarity — How UPSC Frames the Colonial Legacy & Partition

This chapter is tested as the starting condition of independent India — the material, institutional and psychological inheritance the new nation had to work with, plus the single most traumatic event of 1947. Sort your prep into three buckets:

  • Static/institutional — the dated constitutional-communal ladder to Partition (Morley-Minto separate electorates 1909, Lucknow Pact 1916, GoI Act 1935, Lahore Resolution 1940, Cripps 1942, Cabinet Mission 1946, Direct Action Day 16 Aug 1946, 3 June 1947 Plan, Indian Independence Act 1947, Radcliffe Award 17 Aug 1947). High-yield factual recall.
  • Economic/social — the drain of wealth & deindustrialisation, commercialisation of agriculture & recurring famines, the modern intelligentsia, English education & the growth of a pan-Indian consciousness. The paradox: colonialism impoverished and unified.
  • Analytical (GS-I) — why Partition happened (Two-Nation Theory vs colonial divide-and-rule vs Congress–League failure), the responsibility debate, the human cost (~1 million dead, ~15 million displaced), and the enduring legacies (Kashmir, communalism, minorities, refugee rehabilitation). Needs idea + example + judgement.

Territorial note: Maps in this file show India with its full official boundaries — Jammu & Kashmir, Ladakh (including PoK & Aksai Chin under illegal Pakistani/Chinese occupation) and Arunachal Pradesh are integral parts of India. Land & maritime frontiers reflect the Government of India's official position.

1. Overview: Two Inheritances of 1947

Independent India was born carrying two simultaneous inheritances — the accumulated legacy of colonial rule (an impoverished economy, a strong steel-frame administration, and a modernised but stratified society) and the fresh wound of Partition. Understanding the Republic's early choices — planning, secularism, a strong Centre, land reform — is impossible without first grasping what 1947 handed over.

  • The material inheritance: a stagnant, agrarian economy with per-capita income near subsistence, a literacy rate of ~16%, life expectancy of ~32 years, and an industrial base narrow and foreign-controlled — the outcome of a colonial political economy designed to serve Britain.
  • The institutional inheritance: a functioning civil service, army, police, judiciary, railways and telegraph — the "steel frame" that, though built to rule, gave the new state administrative continuity most decolonising nations lacked.
  • The political inheritance: parliamentary institutions, a mass nationalist party (Congress), and the practice of representative government — but also the poisonous device of communal representation that culminated in Partition.
  • The Partition wound: the largest forced migration in human history — a demographic, humanitarian and psychological rupture that shaped Indo-Pak relations, the Kashmir question and the secular-communal debate for decades.
One-line frame: colonialism left India poor but administratively equipped and politically awakened; Partition left it wounded but determined to build a secular, plural republic — both legacies drove the nation-building choices of the Nehru years.

2. Economic Legacy of Colonial Rule

The core of the economic legacy is underdevelopment engineered by design — Britain restructured India from a manufacturing exporter into a supplier of raw materials and a captive market for British goods. Nationalist economists (Dadabhai Naoroji, R.C. Dutt) called this the "drain of wealth".

DimensionWhat colonial rule did & its effect
Drain of wealthUnrequited transfer of Indian resources to Britain via "Home Charges", salaries/pensions of British officials, and repatriated profits — theorised by Dadabhai Naoroji in Poverty and Un-British Rule in India (1901).
DeindustrialisationRuin of handicrafts (esp. textiles) by machine-made British imports & discriminatory tariffs — artisans thrown back onto an over-burdened agriculture ("ruralisation").
Commercialisation of agricultureForced cultivation of cash crops (indigo, cotton, jute, opium) for export; peasants exposed to price shocks & indebtedness without productivity gains.
Land-revenue systemsPermanent Settlement (1793), Ryotwari & Mahalwari — high, inflexible revenue demand that entrenched landlordism, sub-infeudation & peasant impoverishment.
FaminesRecurring, massive mortality — culminating in the Bengal Famine of 1943 (~3 million deaths) — a policy failure, not mere natural calamity.
Stunted industryLimited industrialisation (jute, cotton mills, TISCO 1907) concentrated in enclaves; no capital-goods base; foreign-managed "managing agency" system.
Infrastructure with a colonial logicRailways, ports, telegraph built to extract raw materials & move troops — not to integrate a domestic market, yet inadvertently a unifying legacy.

The cumulative result by 1947: near-zero long-run per-capita growth, chronic mass poverty, a food-deficit agrarian economy, and dependence on primary-commodity exports — the material problem the Planning Commission and the Five-Year Plans were built to solve.

GS-I hook: the drain theory is doubly important — it explains India's poverty and it was itself a political weapon that delegitimised the Raj and fed economic nationalism. The colonial economy is why independent India chose state-led planning & import substitution.

3. Administrative & Institutional Legacy

Unlike the economy, the administrative inheritance was an asset. The colonial state bequeathed a centralised, rule-bound bureaucratic apparatus — the "steel frame" — that gave independent India rare continuity of governance amid the chaos of Partition.

  • The civil service: the Indian Civil Service (ICS) — elite, generalist, district-centred — became the model for the IAS. Sardar Patel defended the services as the "steel frame" of a united India in the Constituent Assembly.
  • The army: a professional, apolitical, well-organised force with a tradition of civilian supremacy — a legacy that helped India avoid the coups that afflicted many post-colonial states (contrast Pakistan).
  • Police & law: the Indian Penal Code (1860), the CrPC, the Police Act (1861) and a hierarchical judiciary — a codified, if colonial, rule-of-law framework that independent India retained and adapted.
  • Communications & connectivity: the railways (one of the world's largest networks), the telegraph and the postal system — instruments of extraction that became instruments of national integration.
  • Fiscal & statistical machinery: a land-revenue and census apparatus, an all-India market and a single currency — the administrative sinews of a nation-state.
The paradox: the same "steel frame" built to rule Indians was repurposed to serve them. Patel's decision to retain the services — over the objections of those who saw them as colonial collaborators — is widely judged decisive in holding the new state together in 1947–48.

4. Political & Constitutional Legacy

The colonial period slowly — and grudgingly — introduced representative institutions. Each reform expanded Indian participation but also seeded the communal principle that would end in Partition.

ReformWhat it introduced
Indian Councils Act 1861 & 1892Nominated Indian members; the germ of legislative representation.
Morley-Minto Reforms 1909Elected element — but with separate electorates for Muslims: the constitutionalisation of communal division.
Montagu-Chelmsford 1919Dyarchy in the provinces; expanded franchise; the goal of "responsible government" formally stated.
Government of India Act 1935Provincial autonomy, an all-India federation (never realised), a wider franchise — the single largest source of the 1950 Constitution's text.

Alongside these ran the legacy of parliamentary practice — debate, elections, a professional political class and, above all, a mass party (the Indian National Congress) that had learned to run governments in the provinces after 1937. This is why India could adopt and operate a Westminster-style democracy from day one.

GS-I hook: the political legacy is genuinely double-edged — it gave India working democratic institutions (a huge asset) but also the communal electorate, the device that converted religious identity into political geography and made Partition thinkable.

5. Social, Educational & Intellectual Legacy

Colonial rule triggered a social and intellectual awakening it never intended. Western education, the printing press and the English language created a modern intelligentsia that turned the coloniser's own ideas — liberty, nationalism, equality — against colonial rule.

  • Modern education: Macaulay's Minute (1835) & the Wood's Despatch (1854) built universities (Calcutta, Bombay, Madras, 1857) — producing lawyers, teachers & journalists who led the national movement.
  • English as a link language: unintentionally forged a pan-Indian elite able to communicate across regions — a tool of both administration and nationalism.
  • Social reform: reform movements (Brahmo Samaj, Arya Samaj, Aligarh, the reformers who won abolition of Sati 1829 & the Widow Remarriage Act 1856) reshaped Indian society under colonial law.
  • The press & public sphere: newspapers and associations created modern public opinion — the soil in which organised nationalism grew.
  • The costs: education reached a tiny minority (~16% literacy in 1947); a colonial curriculum bred cultural self-doubt; and the "divide-and-rule" instrumentalisation of caste and religion deepened social fault lines.
One-line frame: colonial modernity was a double gift — it gave India the vocabulary of rights and the tools of nationhood, while leaving a mass of the population illiterate, poor and socially divided.

6. Roots of Partition & the Two-Nation Theory

Partition was not inevitable in 1900; it became so through a long interplay of colonial policy, competitive communal mobilisation and the failure of a power-sharing settlement. Historians debate the weight of each — but all three strands must be understood.

Fig 1.1 — Three converging strands behind Partition: colonial divide-and-rule, competitive communal mobilisation, and the failure of a constitutional power-sharing settlement. Schematic.
  • The Two-Nation Theory: the claim — crystallised by the Muslim League and articulated by M.A. Jinnah at the Lahore (Pakistan) Resolution, 1940 — that Hindus and Muslims were two distinct nations who could not live within a single state.
  • Colonial divide-and-rule: from separate electorates (1909) onward, the Raj institutionalised religious identity in politics, and at critical moments (1946–47) preferred a quick, partitioned exit.
  • Failure of settlement: the rejection/collapse of power-sharing formulas (Cripps 1942, the Cabinet Mission's grouping scheme 1946) hardened positions until Partition became the "least-bad" option for the exhausted actors.
  • Congress vs League: the League's rise after 1937 (feeling excluded from Congress provincial ministries) and its sweep of Muslim seats in the 1945–46 elections gave it a plausible claim to speak for Muslims.
GS-I hook: UPSC rarely asks "was Partition inevitable?" as a yes/no — it wants you to weigh the three strands and reach a judgement. The strongest answers argue that colonial policy created the framework, communal mobilisation supplied the ideology, and the 1945–47 settlement failure supplied the trigger.

7. Milestones of Communal Politics (1906–1940)

The road to the Two-Nation Theory ran through a series of dated milestones — each a step in converting religious community into political nationhood. Memorise the ladder; it recurs in Prelims.

YearMilestone & significance
1906Founding of the All-India Muslim League (Dhaka) — a separate Muslim political platform.
1909Morley-Minto Reforms grant separate electorates for Muslims — the constitutional root of communal politics.
1916Lucknow Pact — Congress accepts separate electorates in a short-lived Congress–League entente.
1932Communal Award (MacDonald) extends separate electorates to Depressed Classes; the Poona Pact (Gandhi–Ambedkar) substitutes reserved seats.
1937Provincial elections: Congress forms ministries & largely excludes the League — deepening League grievance & separatism.
1940Lahore Resolution (23 March) demands "independent states" for Muslim-majority areas — the formal birth of the Pakistan demand.
Prelims trap: the 1932 Communal Award is about the Depressed Classes (Dalits), answered by the Poona Pact; do not confuse it with the 1909 Muslim separate electorates. And note the Lahore Resolution (1940) never used the word "Pakistan" — the press coined that label.

8. The 1945–47 Endgame

The two years from the end of WWII to Independence were a rapid, tragic cascade — a weakened Britain, a resurgent League, a Congress under pressure, and a viceroy determined to leave fast. This is the most heavily tested sequence in the chapter.

DateEvent & significance
1945–46Provincial & central elections — the League sweeps most reserved Muslim seats, establishing itself as the dominant Muslim party.
Mar–Jun 1946Cabinet Mission Plan — proposes a united India with a weak Centre & a three-tier grouping of provinces (rejecting Pakistan). Both parties initially accept, then diverge over the "grouping" clause.
16 Aug 1946Direct Action Day — the League's call triggers the Great Calcutta Killings (~4,000+ dead), spreading communal violence to Noakhali, Bihar & Punjab.
Feb 1947PM Attlee announces British withdrawal by June 1948; Mountbatten appointed the last Viceroy.
3 Jun 1947Mountbatten (3 June) Plan — accepts Partition; provinces/assemblies to decide; a Boundary Commission to draw the lines; date advanced to 15 Aug 1947.
18 Jul 1947Indian Independence Act 1947 (British Parliament) — creates the two Dominions of India & Pakistan; lapses paramountcy over princely states.
14–15 Aug 1947Pakistan (14th) & India (15th) become independent; the Radcliffe Award is published on 17 Aug, after Independence.
GS-I hook: the Cabinet Mission Plan (1946) was the last serious chance for a united India — its failure over the "grouping" interpretation, followed by Direct Action Day, made Partition the accepted outcome. The advancing of the date to August 1947 (from June 1948) is widely blamed for the administrative unpreparedness that magnified the violence.

9. Partition Mechanics: the Radcliffe Award

Partition was executed through a Boundary Commission chaired by Sir Cyril Radcliffe, a British lawyer who had never visited India. Only Punjab and Bengal — the two large Muslim-majority provinces with big non-Muslim minorities — were actually divided.

Fig 1.2 — The 1947 Partition: two wings of Pakistan (West & East) flanking India; only Punjab & Bengal were divided by the Radcliffe Line. India shown with full official boundaries — J&K & Ladakh (incl. PoK & Aksai Chin) integral to India. Schematic, not to scale.
  • The Commission: two Boundary Commissions (Punjab & Bengal), each with equal Congress & League nominees, chaired by Radcliffe — whose casting vote effectively decided the lines.
  • The brief: divide on the basis of contiguous majority areas of Muslims & non-Muslims, while considering "other factors" (a vague clause that invited controversy).
  • Controversies: the award of the Gurdaspur district tehsils and the Ferozepur headworks to India, and the fate of Chittagong Hill Tracts — disputed to this day, with implications for road access to Kashmir.
  • Timing: the award was finalised before but published on 17 August — after Independence — so that Britain would not "own" the ensuing bloodshed; millions did not know which country they were in until after 15 August.
GS-I hook: the Radcliffe Award is the classic case of a hurried, opaque partition of territory — drawn in five weeks by an outsider, published after the transfer of power, and directly implicated in the scale of the violence. Gurdaspur's award to India is the fact examiners most often probe.

10. Partition Violence & the Great Migration

Partition produced the largest and most violent forced migration in modern history. As the boundary became known, a two-way ethnic cleansing convulsed Punjab and Bengal.

  • Scale: an estimated ~10–20 million people displaced (commonly cited ~15 million) and ~1 million killed (estimates range 200,000–2 million) — the exact figures unknowable.
  • Geography: the worst violence hit Punjab (near-total population exchange, organised massacres, train massacres) and Bengal (large but comparatively less genocidal, after Gandhi's Calcutta fast).
  • Gendered violence: an estimated 75,000–100,000 women abducted & raped; the later Abducted Persons (Recovery & Restoration) Act, 1949 sought their return — a fraught, coercive exercise.
  • Gandhi's role: Gandhi's fast in Calcutta (the "Calcutta Miracle") and his tours of Noakhali & Delhi curbed violence at critical moments — his last great act before his assassination on 30 January 1948.
RegionCharacter of the migration
Punjab (West → East)Rapid, near-complete, extremely violent two-way exchange within months; Sikhs & Hindus to India, Muslims to West Pakistan.
Bengal (East → West)Slower, prolonged over years/decades; recurring waves (1950, 1964, 1971) into West Bengal, Assam & Tripura.
Sindh & othersHindu out-migration from Sindh to Gujarat/Bombay; a largely non-violent but total displacement.
Note the asymmetry: Punjab's exchange was fast and effectively complete; Bengal's was open-ended — a continuing refugee inflow that shaped the politics of West Bengal, Assam (the citizenship & NRC debates) and Tripura for generations.

11. Refugee Rehabilitation & Resettlement

The new state's first great administrative test was resettling millions of refugees with almost no resources — a task led politically by figures such as K.C. Neogy and later Mehr Chand Khanna, and organisationally by the Ministry of Rehabilitation.

  • The scale of the task: feeding, housing & employing millions — refugee camps (Kurukshetra), new townships, and the reallocation of "evacuee property" left by departing Muslims.
  • Urban resettlement: the reshaping of cities — Delhi (new colonies like Lajpat Nagar), and the planned new capital of Punjab, Chandigarh (Le Corbusier), built partly to replace lost Lahore.
  • The Punjab model vs Bengal: Punjab's rehabilitation, aided by roughly matching land vacated by departing Muslims, was comparatively successful; Bengal's open-ended influx made rehabilitation far harder & politically explosive.
  • Legal machinery: the Administration of Evacuee Property Act (1950) & rehabilitation loans/grants — an early exercise of state capacity that tested the new bureaucracy.
Assessment: despite chaos and injustice, India absorbed one of history's largest refugee inflows without state collapse — a quiet success of the "steel-frame" administration and a foundation of the new republic's legitimacy.

12. Consequences & Unfinished Business

Partition's consequences did not end in 1947 — several became structural features of independent India's politics and security.

  • The Kashmir question: the lapse of paramountcy & the tribal invasion of October 1947 led to J&K's Instrument of Accession to India — the origin of the enduring Indo-Pak conflict (covered in Chapter 2).
  • Indo-Pak hostility: four wars (1947–48, 1965, 1971, 1999) & a permanent security burden flow directly from Partition and the Two-Nation logic.
  • Communalism & secularism: Partition made secularism a foundational commitment of the Republic — a conscious rejection of the Two-Nation Theory, embodied in the decision to keep India plural.
  • Minorities & citizenship: the status of those who stayed, and of continuing refugee inflows (esp. in the east), fed long-running debates — from the Nehru-Liaquat Pact (1950) to Assam, the NRC and citizenship law.
  • Divided assets & rivers: the division of the army, treasury & assets, and of the Indus river system — later settled by the Indus Waters Treaty (1960).
  • Psychological scar: a collective trauma memorialised in literature (Manto, Train to Pakistan) that continues to shape identity and politics.
GS-I hook: the single most important consequence to assert in an answer is that Partition made secularism a strategic as well as moral choice — India defined itself against the Two-Nation Theory, which is why the integrity of a plural republic became non-negotiable for the founders.

13. Assessment: Who Was Responsible?

The responsibility debate is the analytical heart of the chapter — UPSC wants a balanced, evidence-weighted judgement, not a partisan verdict. The major schools:

School / viewArgument
Colonial responsibilityBritain's divide-and-rule (separate electorates, census categories) created communal politics; the hurried 1947 exit magnified the violence.
League / JinnahThe Two-Nation Theory & the 1946 Direct Action call made the communal rupture unbridgeable; the demand for Pakistan forced Partition.
Congress choicesCongress's exclusion of the League after 1937, and its 1947 acceptance of Partition (to secure a strong Centre & avoid a weak, "Balkanised" India), share responsibility.
Structural / long-termDeep socio-economic divisions, uneven modernisation among communities & the logic of majoritarian electoral politics made some rupture likely.

The mature verdict, widely held by historians: Partition was neither purely inevitable nor the fault of any single actor — colonial policy built the framework, communal mobilisation supplied the ideology, and the failure of the 1946 settlement supplied the trigger. Contingency (personalities, timing, the advanced date) mattered as much as structure.

One-line frame: the best answers distribute responsibility — Britain, the League and Congress each contributed — and stress that the manner of Partition (rushed, opaque, unprepared) turned a political division into a humanitarian catastrophe.

14. Current Affairs Link

Partition remains a live theme in public memory and policy — useful for value-addition in Mains answers and essays.

  • Partition Horrors Remembrance Day: observed on 14 August each year (announced 2021) — a state acknowledgement of the human cost of Partition. check for latest update or data
  • Citizenship & refugees: continuing debates over the eastern refugee legacy — Assam, the NRC, and citizenship law — trace directly to Partition's open-ended Bengal migration.
  • Memory & historiography: oral-history projects (e.g. the 1947 Partition Archive) and museums (the Partition Museum, Amritsar) keep the survivor record alive.
  • Indo-Pak relations: every flashpoint — Kashmir, the Indus Waters Treaty, cross-border terrorism — is a downstream consequence of the 1947 settlement.
Essay/value-add: Partition is a rich source for essays on nationalism, tolerance, the costs of majoritarian identity politics, and the resilience of institutions — pair the human tragedy with India's constitutional choice of secular pluralism.

15. Prelims PYQs & Practice

Note on sourcing: the first question below is a genuine dated UPSC Prelims PYQ. The remaining items are exam-pattern practice MCQs (deliberately left yearless, not attributed to a fabricated year) built strictly on this chapter's high-yield facts.

UPSC Prelims 2014

The "Radcliffe Committee" was appointed to:

  • (a) Solve the problem of minorities in India
  • (b) Give effect to the Independence Bill
  • (c) Delimit the boundaries between India and Pakistan
  • (d) Enquire into the riots in Bengal

Answer: (c). The Radcliffe Boundary Commission demarcated the India–Pakistan boundary in Punjab & Bengal, its award published on 17 August 1947. (UPSC's official key accepted (c); the boundary-demarcation function is the defining role.)

Model question — no fixed year

The concept of the "drain of wealth" during colonial rule was most systematically expounded by:

  • (a) Dadabhai Naoroji
  • (b) Bal Gangadhar Tilak
  • (c) Gopal Krishna Gokhale
  • (d) M.G. Ranade

Answer: (a). Dadabhai Naoroji theorised the drain in Poverty and Un-British Rule in India (1901); R.C. Dutt developed it further in his Economic History of India.

Model question — no fixed year

Consider the following statements about the Cabinet Mission Plan (1946):
1. It proposed the creation of a separate state of Pakistan.
2. It envisaged a union of India with a weak Centre and a grouping of provinces.
3. It provided for a Constituent Assembly to frame the Constitution.
Which of the statements are correct?

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 and 3 only
  • (c) 1 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3

Answer: (b). The Cabinet Mission rejected Pakistan and proposed a united India with a weak Centre & three-tier grouping; it also set up the Constituent Assembly. Statement 1 is wrong.

Model question — no fixed year

"Direct Action Day", which led to the Great Calcutta Killings, was called on:

  • (a) 23 March 1940
  • (b) 16 August 1946
  • (c) 3 June 1947
  • (d) 15 August 1947

Answer: (b). The Muslim League called Direct Action Day on 16 August 1946; 23 March 1940 was the Lahore Resolution, and 3 June 1947 the Mountbatten Plan.

Model question — no fixed year

The Indian Independence Act, 1947 did which of the following?
1. Created the two Dominions of India and Pakistan.
2. Ended British paramountcy over the princely states.
3. Made the Constituent Assembly a sovereign body.
Select the correct answer:

  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 1 and 3 only
  • (c) 1, 2 and 3
  • (d) 2 and 3 only

Answer: (c). The Act created the two Dominions, lapsed paramountcy (freeing princely states to accede), and made each Dominion's Constituent Assembly sovereign to frame its own constitution.

Model question — no fixed year

The Lahore Resolution of 1940, which formally raised the demand later called "Pakistan", was moved in the session presided over by:

  • (a) M.A. Jinnah
  • (b) Liaquat Ali Khan
  • (c) Sir Sikandar Hayat Khan
  • (d) Fazlul Haq

Answer: (a). The Lahore Resolution (23 March 1940) was adopted at the Muslim League session presided over by Jinnah; it was formally moved by A.K. Fazlul Haq. The word "Pakistan" was a press coinage.

16. Mains PYQs — Model Answers

Note on sourcing: the first question is a genuine dated UPSC Mains GS-I PYQ. The remaining three are exam-pattern practice questions (left yearless, not attributed to a fabricated year) on this chapter's core analytical themes, each with a full answer skeleton.

UPSC Mains GS-I 2019

Assess the role of British imperial power in complicating the process of transfer of power during the 1940s. (250 words) 15 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: The transfer of power in the 1940s was complicated less by an orderly British design than by imperial calculations — wartime pressures, divide-and-rule, and a hurried exit — that deepened communal division and magnified violence.
  2. Wartime provocations: the unilateral declaration of war on India's behalf (1939), the failed Cripps offer (1942) and repression of Quit India hardened Congress–Raj relations and left a leadership vacuum the League exploited.
  3. Divide-and-rule (the core): Britain's institutionalisation of communal politics — from separate electorates onward — and its treatment of the League as an equal veto-holder (against its 1937 strength) legitimised the Two-Nation demand.
  4. The hurried exit: advancing the date from June 1948 to August 1947, and publishing the Radcliffe Award after Independence, left the administration unprepared for the migration & violence — a British responsibility.
  5. Critical edge — not Britain alone: League intransigence (Direct Action) & Congress's acceptance of Partition for a strong Centre also shaped the outcome; imperial power complicated, but did not solely cause, the tragedy.
  6. Conclusion: British imperial conduct — manipulative during the war, communal in method, and reckless in timing — turned an already difficult transfer of power into a humanitarian catastrophe, even as Indian actors shared the burden.
Model question — no fixed year

"The economic legacy of colonial rule was one of engineered underdevelopment." Critically examine. (250 words) 15 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: India entered independence as one of the world's poorest economies despite being a pre-colonial manufacturing power — the outcome of a colonial political economy that served Britain, not India.
  2. The drain & deindustrialisation (the core): the unrequited transfer of surplus (Home Charges, profits) and the ruin of handicrafts by discriminatory trade turned India into a raw-material supplier & captive market.
  3. Agrarian distress: extractive land-revenue systems, commercialisation without productivity gains, indebtedness & recurring famines (Bengal 1943) impoverished the peasantry.
  4. Stunted industry & finance: enclave industrialisation, no capital-goods base, foreign managing agencies & drained investable surplus left a narrow, dependent industrial structure.
  5. Critical edge — the ambiguous inheritance: colonialism also left railways, an all-India market, a single currency & a modern administrative-statistical apparatus — unintended assets that aided post-1947 planning.
  6. Conclusion: the balance is overwhelmingly negative — the "underdevelopment" was structural & policy-driven — but the institutional residue gave independent India tools its planners used to escape that legacy.
Model question — no fixed year

"Partition was neither inevitable nor the responsibility of any single actor." Discuss. (250 words) 15 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Partition is best understood as a contingent outcome of converging forces — colonial policy, communal mobilisation and settlement failure — rather than a preordained event or the fault of one party.
  2. Colonial framework: separate electorates (1909), census categorisation & divide-and-rule built the communal architecture within which nationhood was contested.
  3. Communal ideology (the core): the Two-Nation Theory & the League's post-1937 rise — culminating in the Lahore Resolution (1940) & Direct Action (1946) — supplied the driving demand.
  4. Congress choices: exclusion of the League after 1937 & the eventual acceptance of Partition (to secure a strong Centre & avoid Balkanisation) made Congress a co-author of the outcome.
  5. Critical edge — contingency over inevitability: the collapse of the Cabinet Mission over "grouping", personalities (Jinnah, Mountbatten) & the advanced timetable show how differently events could have run.
  6. Conclusion: responsibility is distributed — Britain built the frame, the League drove the demand, Congress conceded the split — and contingency, not destiny, sealed it.
Model question — no fixed year

How did the trauma of Partition shape the foundational choices of the Indian Republic? (150 words) 10 marks

Model Answer — full skeleton
  1. Intro: Partition was not only an event but a formative lesson — its trauma directly shaped the Republic's commitments to secularism, a strong Centre and integration.
  2. Secularism as rejection: India defined itself against the Two-Nation Theory — a plural, secular state became a moral & strategic necessity.
  3. A strong Centre (the core): the fear of further fragmentation drove the Constituent Assembly toward a powerful union, emergency provisions & a single citizenship.
  4. Integration & security: Partition's lesson underpinned the urgent integration of princely states & a permanent Indo-Pak security posture.
  5. Critical edge: the refugee & minority questions it created still shape citizenship debates — an unfinished legacy.
  6. Conclusion: the Republic's core design — secular, unitary-leaning, integrationist — is in large part Partition's institutional answer.

15-Minute Revision Sprint

Colonial economic legacy

  • Drain of wealth — Naoroji (1901), R.C. Dutt; deindustrialisation of handicrafts.
  • Commercialisation of agriculture; extractive land-revenue (Permanent Settlement 1793).
  • Bengal Famine 1943 (~3 mn); near-zero per-capita growth; ~16% literacy in 1947.

Institutional inheritance

  • "Steel frame" — ICS → IAS; apolitical army; IPC/CrPC; railways & telegraph.
  • GoI Act 1935 = biggest source of the 1950 Constitution.
  • Separate electorates (1909) — the constitutional root of communalism.

Road to Partition

  • ML 1906 → Lucknow Pact 1916 → Lahore Resolution 1940 → Cripps 1942.
  • Cabinet Mission 1946 (united India, weak Centre) fails over "grouping".
  • Direct Action Day 16 Aug 1946 → 3 June Plan → Indian Independence Act 1947.

Partition & aftermath

  • Radcliffe Award (17 Aug 1947) — only Punjab & Bengal split; Gurdaspur to India.
  • ~1 mn dead, ~15 mn displaced; ~75,000–100,000 women abducted.
  • Legacies: Kashmir, secularism, refugee rehabilitation, Indus Waters Treaty 1960.
