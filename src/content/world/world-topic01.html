World History · Topic 01

Industrial Revolution (1760–1914): Origins, Technology & Global Spread

The deepest economic and social transformation since agriculture itself. Why England first, the cascade of textile-steam-iron-rail inventions, the rise of the factory system, the human cost paid by the working class, and how the Revolution spread to Germany, USA, France, Russia and Meiji Japan — with real UPSC GS-I PYQs.

Why this chapter matters for UPSC: The Industrial Revolution is the formal starting point of the UPSC World History syllabus ("History of the world will include events from 18th century such as industrial revolution..."). Direct PYQs include 2016 ("Why did the Industrial Revolution first occur in England? Discuss the quality of life of the people during industrialization"), 2014 ("major political, economic and social dimensions of the Industrial Revolution"), 2020 ("socio-economic effects of railways"), 2017 (cotton textile industry locational variations). It is also the causal trigger for imperialism, the World Wars, communism, and decolonization — every other chapter in this series traces back to it.

1. Concept & Periodisation

What is the Industrial Revolution?

The Industrial Revolution is the term coined by French economist Adolphe Blanqui (1837) and popularised by historian Arnold Toynbee in his Oxford lectures (1880-81, published posthumously as Lectures on the Industrial Revolution in England, 1884) to describe the transformation of Britain from an agrarian, handicraft economy into one dominated by machine-based manufacturing.

The defining features of this transformation were:

  • Substitution of machines for human and animal muscle.
  • Use of inanimate energy (water, coal, steam, later electricity, oil) instead of organic energy.
  • Concentration of production in factories instead of cottages.
  • Rise of industrial capitalism — a small class of capital-owners directing wage labour.
  • Sustained increase in per-capita productivity — for the first time in human history, output grew faster than population.

The UPSC syllabus and most standard textbooks (NCERT, Bipan Chandra's Pelican Modern World) focus on the First (1760-1840) and Second (1870-1914) Industrial Revolutions. The Third and Fourth are referenced as continuity but rarely asked.

Why "Revolution" — Was It Really Sudden?

Historians have long debated whether the change was truly revolutionary. T.S. Ashton (The Industrial Revolution 1760-1830, 1948) defended the term — the structural break with all previous economies was real. Critics like Nicholas Crafts have shown that British growth rates 1780-1830 were only ~1.5% per annum — slow by modern standards. But the cumulative effect over 80 years was civilisational rupture; even a 1.5% sustained rate doubles output every 47 years. By 1850 Britain produced two-thirds of the world's coal, half its iron, and more than half its cotton cloth.

2. Why England Started First

This is the single most-asked question in UPSC PYQs on Industrial Revolution (direct hit 2016). The reasons are best grouped under seven heads:

1. Political Stability & the Glorious Revolution

The Glorious Revolution of 1688 established parliamentary supremacy and a stable, predictable rule-of-law state. The Bill of Rights 1689 secured property rights; Hanoverian succession (1714) provided dynastic continuity. Continental Europe by contrast saw repeated wars (Spanish Succession 1701-13, Seven Years' War 1756-63, French Revolution 1789-99, Napoleonic Wars 1803-15). Britain was an island — invasion-proof — and could invest in production while the continent rebuilt.

2. Strong Property Rights & Legal Framework

English common law protected private property and contract enforcement. Patent system (Statute of Monopolies 1624) gave inventors monopoly rights for 14 years — incentivising technological innovation. James Watt's 1769 patent on the separate condenser steam engine was extended to 25 years (1775) by special Act of Parliament.

3. Agricultural Revolution (precondition)

Detailed in Section 3 — surplus food, surplus labour, surplus capital all flowed out of agriculture.

4. Demographic Surplus

England's population grew from 5.7 million in 1751 to 16.7 million in 1851 — providing both labour and consumers. Detailed in Section 4.

5. Natural Resources — Coal & Iron

Britain had abundant coal (Yorkshire, Lancashire, Wales, Scotland) and iron ore often in the same geological belts. Wood (charcoal) had been depleted by 1700 — forcing the switch to coal. Abraham Darby's coke-smelting process (Coalbrookdale, 1709) made iron-from-coke viable. By 1800 Britain produced more coal than the rest of the world combined.

6. Capital — Banking, Insurance, Joint-Stock

Bank of England founded 1694; London Stock Exchange 1773; Lloyd's of London insurance from 1688 (Edward Lloyd's coffee house). Wealth from the slave trade, sugar plantations, and Indian conquest (Battle of Plassey 1757 brought Bengal's revenue under Company control) provided unprecedented capital pools. Eric Williams's Capitalism and Slavery (1944) argued — controversially — that slave-trade profits directly financed industrialisation.

7. Colonial Empire — Markets & Raw Materials

By 1750, Britain controlled major colonies in North America, the Caribbean, and Bengal. These provided:

  • Raw materials — cotton from American South + India + Egypt; sugar, tobacco, dyes.
  • Captive markets — British colonies were compelled to import British manufactures (Navigation Acts, Calico Acts 1700/1721 banning Indian textile imports).
  • Re-export trade — London became the entrepôt of the world.

8. (Bonus) Cultural / Intellectual Factors

The Scientific Revolution (Newton 1687, Royal Society 1660) created a culture of experimentation. The Lunar Society of Birmingham (1765-1813) brought together Erasmus Darwin, Matthew Boulton, James Watt, Josiah Wedgwood, Joseph Priestley — combining science, engineering and business. Non-conformist Protestant ethic (Max Weber's Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism, 1905) and rational this-worldly orientation among Quakers and Dissenters fuelled entrepreneurship — Darby (iron), Wedgwood (pottery), Lloyds (insurance) were all Quakers.

One-line UPSC answer: The Industrial Revolution began in England because no single other country in the world combined — at the same time — political stability + property rights + agricultural surplus + abundant coal-iron + capital from colonies + a vast empire as captive market + a culture of scientific innovation.

3. Agricultural Revolution Background

The Industrial Revolution would have been impossible without the Agricultural Revolution that preceded it from roughly 1700-1850. Key elements:

Enclosure Movement

Common lands ("open fields" and commons) were enclosed into private holdings by Acts of Parliament — over 4,000 enclosure Acts were passed between 1750 and 1850, covering ~7 million acres. This:

  • Consolidated scattered strips into compact farms.
  • Eliminated subsistence rights of small peasants — pushed labour off the land toward cities.
  • Allowed scientific farming, crop rotation, selective breeding.
  • Increased per-acre productivity dramatically.

Norfolk Four-Course Rotation

"Turnip" Townshend (Lord Charles Townshend) from 1730s popularised the Norfolk system — wheat → turnips → barley → clover, on a four-year cycle, eliminating fallow. Turnips and clover restored nitrogen to soil and fed livestock through winter (so livestock no longer had to be slaughtered each autumn).

Selective Livestock Breeding

Robert Bakewell (1725-95) of Dishley applied selective breeding to sheep (New Leicester breed) and cattle. Average weight of cattle sold at Smithfield market more than doubled between 1710 and 1795.

Mechanical Innovations

  • Jethro Tull's seed drill (1701) — replaced broadcast sowing.
  • Andrew Meikle's threshing machine (1786).
  • Iron ploughs from late 18th century.

Outcomes

  • Wheat yield rose from ~10 bushels per acre (1700) to ~30 bushels per acre (1850).
  • Despite population tripling, Britain remained largely food-self-sufficient until the 1830s.
  • Surplus rural labour migrated to industrial cities — providing factory workforce.
  • Agricultural profits were reinvested in industry, mining, transport.
Causal chain: Enclosure → fewer peasants needed on land → migration to towns → labour supply for factories. Higher farm productivity → food for non-farm population → urbanisation became possible. Farm profits → capital for factories, mines, canals, railways.

England's population grew explosively:

Reasons for population growth: declining mortality (smallpox inoculation from 1721, Jenner's vaccination 1796), better nutrition, fewer famines, peace at home. By 1851, England became the world's first majority-urban country.

Capital Accumulation

  • Bank of England (1694) — founded by Scottish entrepreneur William Paterson; financed government borrowing.
  • National debt system — Britain pioneered cheap long-term government borrowing (Consols from 1751); released private capital for industry.
  • Country banks — by 1810 there were 800+ provincial banks lending to local industry.
  • Joint-stock companies — South Sea Bubble (1720) led to Bubble Act 1720 restricting joint-stocks; repealed 1825; Companies Act 1844, Limited Liability Act 1855 created the modern corporation.

Colonial Wealth Inflows

India: Battle of Plassey 1757 → Battle of Buxar 1764 → Diwani of Bengal 1765 → British East India Company gained tax-collection rights. Bengal's annual revenue (~£2-3 million) flowed into Company coffers; "Plassey Plunder" reached London by mid-1760s. P.J. Marshall and K.N. Chaudhuri estimate India's contribution to British capital formation at 1-2% of GDP annually through late 18th century.

Slave trade & West Indies: Britain became the dominant slave-trading nation from 1713 (Asiento under Treaty of Utrecht). Liverpool, Bristol, Glasgow grew on the triangular trade (manufactures to Africa → slaves to Caribbean → sugar/tobacco to Britain). Profits funded factories — though debated, William Darity Jr estimates slave-trade-related earnings at 5% of British investment.

Navigation Acts (1651, 1660, 1696) mandated that colonial trade pass through British ships and ports — guaranteeing Britain a captive shipping monopoly. Calico Acts (1700, 1721) banned import of Indian printed cottons — protecting British textile manufacturers and forcing them to develop their own substitutes.

Infrastructure — Canals & Turnpikes

  • Bridgewater Canal (1761) — Duke of Bridgewater's Worsley-Manchester canal, engineered by James Brindley; reduced Manchester coal price by half. Triggered the "Canal Mania" — by 1830 Britain had 4,250 miles of canals.
  • Turnpike roads — over 22,000 miles of toll roads by 1830 under John Loudon McAdam ("macadamisation") and Thomas Telford.
  • Cheap inland transport was a precondition for moving coal, iron, raw cotton, finished goods at industrial scale.

5. Technological Changes — Textile

Cotton textiles led the Industrial Revolution. India had been the world's largest cotton producer for 2,000 years; by 1830 Britain had overtaken India and was exporting cotton cloth back to India. The cascade of inventions:

The Yarn-Cloth Race

Notice the sequence: weaving was mechanised first (Kay's flying shuttle 1733), creating a yarn shortage. Spinning then leapt ahead with Jenny (1764), Water Frame (1769), and Mule (1779) — creating a yarn surplus. Cartwright's power loom (1785) finally caught weaving up. This back-and-forth dynamic — bottleneck creates incentive for invention to remove bottleneck — is a defining pattern of the Industrial Revolution.

Cotton Output Explosion

  • 1760: Britain consumed ~3 million lb of raw cotton.
  • 1800: 56 million lb.
  • 1850: 588 million lb.
  • 1860: Lancashire mills employed 440,000 workers in cotton alone.

By 1830, cotton textiles accounted for ~50% of British exports. Manchester became known as "Cottonopolis."

The dark side: The American South's slave-based cotton plantations (made viable by Whitney's gin) supplied 75-80% of Lancashire's raw cotton by 1860. The Industrial Revolution in England was thus directly tied to the expansion of slavery in America. Karl Marx noted: "Without slavery you have no cotton; without cotton you have no modern industry."

6. Steam Engine & Power Revolution

From Water to Steam

Early factories (Arkwright's Cromford 1771) used water power — restricting them to riverside locations and seasonal flow. Steam power broke that constraint, allowing factories anywhere coal could be transported.

Watt-Boulton Partnership

James Watt was a Scottish instrument-maker at the University of Glasgow. While repairing a Newcomen engine model in 1763-64, he realised that condensing steam in the main cylinder wasted enormous heat. His separate condenser (1765, patented 1769) kept the main cylinder hot continuously — quadrupling efficiency. But Watt was a poor businessman. He partnered with Matthew Boulton, a Birmingham manufacturer, in 1775. Their Soho Foundry began commercial engine production. By 1800 the Boulton & Watt partnership had sold ~500 engines, dominating the market and earning enormous royalties.

Standardisation: The "Horsepower"

To sell engines, Boulton & Watt needed a way to compare with the work animals customers were replacing. Watt calibrated his engines in "horsepower" (1782) — defined as 33,000 foot-pounds per minute. The unit survives today.

Why Steam Mattered

  • Factories could be built anywhere coal was transportable.
  • Continuous, controllable power — unlike rivers (seasonal) or wind (unreliable).
  • Scale — engines could be enlarged indefinitely; some by 1850 produced 1,000+ horsepower.
  • Enabled mechanical mobility — locomotives, steamships.

By 1850, Britain had ~1,300,000 horsepower of installed steam capacity — more than the rest of the world combined.

7. Iron, Coal & Transport Revolution

Iron Revolution

  • Abraham Darby I — Coke smelting (1709) at Coalbrookdale, Shropshire. Replaced charcoal (wood) with coke (coal) for iron-smelting — solving the wood-shortage bottleneck.
  • Henry Cort — Puddling and Rolling (1783-84) at Fontley, Hampshire. Removed carbon from pig iron in a coal-fired furnace; rolled into uniform shapes. Made wrought iron cheap and abundant.
  • Iron Bridge over the Severn (1779) — the world's first cast-iron bridge, built by Abraham Darby III; still standing as a UNESCO World Heritage site.
  • Bessemer Process (1856) — Henry Bessemer's "converter" turned pig iron into steel by blowing air through molten iron in seconds. Steel costs dropped from £40 to £6 per tonne by the 1880s — the foundation of the Second Industrial Revolution.
  • Siemens-Martin (Open Hearth) Process (1865) — produced higher-quality steel; dominated production for 100 years.
  • Gilchrist-Thomas Process (1879) — allowed Bessemer/Open-Hearth to use phosphoric iron ores (abundant in Lorraine and Sweden) — gave Germany its steel advantage.

Coal Revolution

Britain's coal output:

  • 1700: 2.7 million tonnes
  • 1815: 16 million tonnes
  • 1850: 50 million tonnes
  • 1900: 225 million tonnes

Sir Humphry Davy's safety lamp (1815) reduced colliery explosions; enabled deeper mining. By 1900 the British coal industry employed over 1 million workers.

Railway Revolution

The railway was the defining technology of the First Industrial Revolution — combining iron, steam and coal.

  • 27 September 1825 — Stockton & Darlington Railway opened — world's first public steam railway, 26 miles.
  • 15 September 1830 — Liverpool & Manchester Railway — first inter-city passenger line; first day saw William Huskisson MP killed by Rocket (first railway fatality).
  • 1840s "Railway Mania" — speculative bubble; 6,200 miles of track laid by 1850.
  • By 1900 Britain had 21,700 miles of railway; impact on:
    • Coal and iron demand exploded (Bessemer steel for rails 1860s onwards).
    • Coal could now reach inland factories cheaply.
    • Time zones standardised — Greenwich Mean Time adopted 1880 because of railway timetables.
    • National markets in fresh food, newspapers, manufactured goods.
    • Tourism and seaside resorts.

Steamships

  • Robert Fulton's Clermont (1807) — first commercial steamship; Hudson River.
  • Savannah (1819) — first transatlantic crossing (partial steam).
  • Great Western (1838) by Isambard Kingdom Brunel — first regular Atlantic steamship service.
  • SS Great Britain (1843) — first iron-hulled, screw-propeller ocean liner.
  • Suez Canal opened 17 November 1869 — cut Britain-India journey from ~13,000 km (Cape route) to ~6,500 km; gave steam (which couldn't sail) advantage over sailing ships in eastern trade.

Telegraph

Samuel Morse's electric telegraph (1837); first commercial line Washington-Baltimore 24 May 1844 ("What hath God wrought"). First transatlantic cable 1858 (failed); permanent cable 1866 by Brunel's Great Eastern. By 1900 the world had a copper-wire telegraph network 1.5 million miles long.

8. Factory System & Urbanisation

The Factory as Institution

The factory was a revolutionary social invention as much as a technological one. Defining features:

  • Centralised production — workers, raw materials, machines, and power source brought to one site.
  • Time-discipline — work measured by the clock, not by task; fixed hours; whistles, bells, gates.
  • Division of labour — Adam Smith's Wealth of Nations (1776) had described the pin factory; factories took it to extremes.
  • Wage labour — workers sold their time, not their product.
  • Hierarchical supervision — foremen, overseers, managers.

The first true factory is generally identified as Richard Arkwright's Cromford Mill (1771) in Derbyshire — water-powered, 200 workers, 24-hour two-shift operation.

Urban Conditions

Friedrich Engels in The Condition of the Working Class in England (1845) — based on his 22 months in Manchester — documented:

  • Overcrowding — 8-12 people per room in working-class districts.
  • No sanitation — cesspools, open sewers; cholera epidemics 1832, 1848-49, 1853-54, 1866.
  • Polluted water — Thames a "monster soup" (Punch, 1858); Great Stink of 1858 drove Parliament to fund Joseph Bazalgette's London sewer system.
  • Smoke and soot — Manchester sky black at noon.
  • Life expectancy in Manchester 1840s: ~26 years (gentry) vs ~17 years (labourers' children).

Public Health Response

  • Edwin Chadwick's Report on the Sanitary Condition of the Labouring Population (1842).
  • Public Health Act 1848 — first Central Board of Health.
  • John Snow's cholera map of Soho (1854) — traced cholera to Broad Street pump; founded epidemiology.
  • Public Health Act 1875 — comprehensive sanitation reform.

9. Three Phases — First, Second, Third

First Industrial Revolution (1760–1840)

  • Core technologies: Steam engine, water power, iron, coal, cotton textiles, railways.
  • Lead country: Great Britain — held a near-monopoly for ~80 years.
  • Defining sectors: Textiles (Lancashire), Iron (Wales, Midlands), Coal (Yorkshire, Tyne), Railways.
  • Organisation: Family-owned firms; partnerships; small-scale joint-stock.
  • Energy source: Coal + steam.

Second Industrial Revolution (1870–1914)

  • Core technologies: Steel (Bessemer 1856, Open Hearth 1865); electricity; chemicals (synthetic dyes 1856 Perkin, ammonia 1909 Haber); internal combustion engine (Otto 1876, Diesel 1893); petroleum (Standard Oil); telegraph/telephone (Bell 1876); automobile (Benz 1886, Ford Model T 1908); aircraft (Wright Brothers 17 December 1903 at Kitty Hawk).
  • Lead countries: Germany and USA overtook Britain by 1900.
  • Defining sectors: Steel, chemicals, electricity, automobiles, oil.
  • Organisation: Large corporations, cartels, trusts (Standard Oil, US Steel, Krupp, Siemens, Bayer); scientific R&D laboratories (Edison's Menlo Park 1876; Bayer's chemistry lab); mass-production assembly lines (Ford 1913).
  • Energy source: Coal + electricity + petroleum.

Why the Lead Shifted to Germany and USA

  • Britain's "Burden of First Mover": obsolete plant; resistance to scrapping working capital; complacency.
  • German scientific education: Technical universities (Technische Hochschulen) from 1820s; PhD culture; close industry-university links.
  • American scale and resources: Continental market; abundant coal, iron, oil; mass immigration.
  • Bessemer steel + electricity rewarded fresh investment, which Germans and Americans made; British stuck with iron and gas.
  • By 1900 USA produced 10.2 million tonnes of steel; Germany 6.4 million; Britain 4.9 million.

Third Industrial Revolution (1969 onwards) — Brief Reference

Sometimes called the "Digital Revolution" — microprocessors (Intel 4004, 1971), personal computers (Apple II, 1977; IBM PC, 1981), internet (TCP/IP standardised 1983; WWW invented by Tim Berners-Lee at CERN, 1989; commercial Web from 1993), mobile phones, robotic automation. Jeremy Rifkin's The Third Industrial Revolution (2011) argues for renewable energy + smart grids as the unifying paradigm.

Fourth Industrial Revolution (2010s onwards) — Brief Reference

Term coined by Klaus Schwab, Founder of the World Economic Forum, in 2016. Convergence of AI, IoT, robotics, 3D printing, gene editing, blockchain, quantum computing. India's Niti Aayog and MeitY policy papers reference this framework.

Periodisation caution: Some textbooks compress all 1760-1914 into "the Industrial Revolution" without distinguishing First and Second. For UPSC answers, ALWAYS distinguish the two — the First was British, coal-and-steam, textiles; the Second was German-American, steel-electricity-chemicals, mass-production. The shift is critical to explaining why Britain lost industrial leadership and to understanding the causes of WWI (Anglo-German rivalry).

10. Economic & Trade Impact

Productivity & Growth

  • British GDP doubled between 1780 and 1830, and tripled by 1860.
  • Real wages stagnated from 1780-1830 ("Engels' Pause") then rose ~50% by 1860 and doubled by 1900.
  • Per-capita income in Britain rose from £100 (1750) to £400 (1900) in constant prices — a 4-fold real increase.

Trade Explosion

  • British exports grew from £14 million (1780) to £680 million (1913).
  • Britain dominated world trade through the 19th century — share of world manufactured exports: ~46% in 1880 (UK), falling to 25% by 1913 as Germany and USA rose.
  • Britain ran trade deficits in goods but surpluses in services (shipping, insurance, finance) — the "City of London" became the world's financial centre.
  • Gold Standard adopted by Britain 1821; spread globally 1870-1914 (the "Classical Gold Standard" era).

Free Trade Era

  • Repeal of Corn Laws (1846) by Robert Peel — ended agricultural protection; symbolic victory of industrial bourgeoisie over landed aristocracy.
  • Repeal of Navigation Acts (1849).
  • Cobden-Chevalier Treaty (1860) — Britain-France free trade.
  • Britain pursued unilateral free trade till WWI; most other countries returned to protection (USA: McKinley Tariff 1890; Germany: Bismarck's tariffs 1879; France: Méline Tariff 1892).

Banking & Finance

  • Limited liability — Companies Act 1844, Limited Liability Act 1855, Companies Act 1862 — created the modern joint-stock corporation.
  • Joint-stock banks expanded — by 1900, the "Big Five" (Midland, Lloyds, Barclays, Westminster, National Provincial) dominated.
  • London Stock Exchange handled half the world's listed securities by 1900.
  • British overseas investment reached £4 billion by 1914 — financing American railways, Argentine ranches, Indian textile mills, Russian bonds.

Globalisation 1.0 (1870-1914)

Economists call 1870-1914 the "First Great Globalisation" — the world before WWI was, in many ways, more open to trade, migration and capital flows than the world before 2008:

  • Trade as % of world GDP rose from 5% (1820) to 14% (1913); did not exceed this until 1980s.
  • Global migration: ~50 million Europeans emigrated 1840-1914 (mostly to Americas).
  • Capital flows: Britain alone exported ~7% of GDP as overseas investment 1900-1914.
  • The Gold Standard provided exchange-rate stability.

11. Social Impact — Class Formation

The Industrial Revolution did not just change machines — it created two brand-new social classes that would dominate the next 150 years of world history: the industrial bourgeoisie (factory-owning capitalists) and the industrial proletariat (wage-earning factory workers). Marx called this the defining class antagonism of modern society.

11.1 Rise of the Bourgeoisie

  • Origin: Many came from yeomen farmers, merchants, putting-out clothiers, dissenting Protestant artisans (Quakers like the Darbys, Unitarians like Wedgwood).
  • Wealth without aristocracy: Did not inherit land or titles — built fortunes on coal, cotton, iron, railways.
  • Political demand: Reform Act 1832 gave vote to £10 householders in boroughs — first time middle class entered Parliament in numbers.
  • Anti-Corn Law League 1838-46: Manchester cotton manufacturers (Richard Cobden, John Bright) campaigned for free trade against landed aristocracy. Repeal of Corn Laws 1846 = bourgeoisie victory over Tory squirearchy.
  • Cultural style: Sober, hard-working, evangelical, philanthropic (Lord Shaftesbury, Quaker Rowntrees) — but also ruthless employers.

11.2 Rise of the Proletariat

  • Source: Dispossessed peasants pushed off land by enclosures + Irish immigrants (especially after 1845 Famine) + handloom weavers ruined by power looms.
  • Conditions: 14-16 hour workdays, 6 days a week, wages 10-15 shillings/week (subsistence), no job security, sacked without notice, blacklisted for union activity.
  • Housing: Back-to-back terraces, cellars in Manchester, courts in Liverpool — Engels' Condition of the Working Class in England (1845) documented horror.
  • Diet: Bread, potatoes, tea, occasional bacon — adulterated food (chalk in flour, alum in bread).
  • Life expectancy 1840s: Manchester gentry 38 years; labourers 17 years (infant mortality pulled down the average).

11.3 The Middle Class (Petty Bourgeoisie)

  • New professional middle class: doctors, lawyers, engineers, clerks, shopkeepers, schoolteachers, civil servants, journalists.
  • Distinguished by salary (not wages), Victorian respectability, suburban villa, single servant, Sabbath observance.
  • Created Victorian morality, separate-spheres ideology (man-public, woman-domestic), foundation of mass-circulation newspapers.

11.4 Class Consciousness

E.P. Thompson — The Making of the English Working Class (1963): argued that the English working class was "present at its own making" between 1790 and 1832 through shared experience of factory discipline, Combination Acts (1799-1824 banning unions), Peterloo Massacre (16 August 1819 — 18 killed at St Peter's Field Manchester), and Chartism. Class is not just an economic category but a cultural-political identity.

12. Impact on Women, Children, Labour — Factory Reform

12.1 Women in Factories

  • Textile mills (especially cotton spinning) employed huge numbers of women — by 1830s, women were ~50% of cotton workforce and 65% of power-loom weavers because they accepted lower wages.
  • Wages: women earned roughly half of men's wages for same work; girls earned a quarter.
  • Sexual harassment by overseers was endemic — documented in 1832 Sadler Committee evidence.
  • Pregnancy = dismissal. Women returned to work weeks after childbirth, leaving infants with dosing of "Godfrey's Cordial" (opium-based) → infant mortality.
  • Domestic servants remained the largest single category of female employment throughout 19th century (Britain 1851: 1.3 million women in domestic service).
  • Middle-class women, by contrast, were excluded from work — "angel in the house" ideal (Coventry Patmore 1854).

12.2 Child Labour

  • Children as young as 4 worked in mines as trappers (opening ventilation doors); 6-year-olds picked cotton lint from under running machinery; 8-year-olds worked 12-14 hour shifts.
  • Workhouse pauper apprentices supplied to mill owners under indenture — sometimes 100 children to a single Lancashire mill.
  • Common deformities: spinal curvature from standing 14 hours, knock-knees, stunted growth, white lung from cotton dust.
  • Lord Shaftesbury (Anthony Ashley Cooper) led the campaign for Factory Acts from 1830s.

12.4 Trade Union Movement

  • 1799-1824: Combination Acts banned all worker associations as criminal conspiracy.
  • 1824: Repeal under Francis Place — limited right to combine.
  • 1834: Tolpuddle Martyrs — 6 Dorset agricultural labourers transported to Australia for 7 years for swearing secret oath to form union; massive London protest; pardoned 1836.
  • 1834: Grand National Consolidated Trades Union (Robert Owen) — tried to unite all trades; collapsed after Tolpuddle.
  • 1851: Amalgamated Society of Engineers — "new model" craft union of skilled workers; cautious, benefits-focused.
  • 1868: Trades Union Congress (TUC) founded in Manchester.
  • 1871: Trade Union Act (Gladstone) — legalised unions and protected funds.
  • 1875: Employers and Workmen Act + Conspiracy and Protection of Property Act (Disraeli) — allowed peaceful picketing.
  • 1888-89: Match Girls' Strike (Bryant & May) + London Dock Strike — birth of New Unionism (unskilled workers).
  • 1900: Labour Representation Committee → 1906 Labour Party.

13. Global Impact — The Imperialism Connection

The Industrial Revolution and the New Imperialism (1870-1914) are inseparable. Mechanised industry needed three things from the colonised world: raw materials, captive markets, and outlets for surplus capital. This is the central thesis of J.A. Hobson (Imperialism: A Study, 1902) and V.I. Lenin (Imperialism, the Highest Stage of Capitalism, 1916).

13.1 Raw Materials

  • Lancashire cotton mills needed raw cotton — sourced from American South (slave plantations till 1865), then forcibly from Egypt (Khedive Ismail), India (Berar, Khandesh, Gujarat), and Sudan.
  • British iron and steel needed iron ore from Sweden and later from Indian Bihar; coal stations across Indian Ocean (Aden, Colombo, Singapore).
  • Rubber for tyres, electrical insulation → Belgian Congo (King Leopold's atrocities, 1885-1908), Malaya (British rubber estates), Amazon.
  • Vegetable oils (palm, groundnut) for soap and margarine → West Africa.
  • Jute for sacking → Bengal (Calcutta jute mills).
  • Tea, indigo, opium → India.

13.2 Markets

  • Industrial output outran European demand after 1870 — colonies became dumping grounds for finished cotton textiles, hardware, machinery.
  • India became Britain's largest market: by 1880s, ~60% of Indian cotton-cloth consumption was Lancashire imports — destroying Indian handloom weavers (Bipan Chandra's "drain of wealth" + "deindustrialisation" thesis).
  • Tariffs against Indian textiles in Britain + free entry of British goods into India = classic colonial trade pattern.

13.3 Capital Export

  • British overseas investment rose from £700 million (1870) to £4 billion (1914) — roughly equal to Britain's GDP.
  • Major destinations: USA, Argentina, Australia, India, South Africa — railways, mines, plantations, government bonds.
  • Lenin: capital export = "highest stage" of capitalism because home market saturated, profits falling, monopolies forced to seek colonial outlets.

13.4 The Scramble

  • Berlin Conference 14 Nov 1884 – 26 Feb 1885 (Bismarck) — partitioned Africa among European powers without African presence; established "effective occupation" rule.
  • By 1914, 90% of Africa under European rule (only Ethiopia and Liberia free).
  • Asia: British India + Burma + Malaya + Hong Kong; French Indochina; Dutch East Indies; Russian Central Asia; Japanese Korea (1910) + Taiwan (1895); American Philippines (1898).
  • India's role as "jewel in the crown" — supplied troops for Britain's eastern empire (Sepoys in China 1860, Egypt 1882, East Africa 1914-18).
Link to GS-I Indian History: The colonial drain (Dadabhai Naoroji's Poverty and Un-British Rule in India 1901), deindustrialisation of Indian handicrafts, commercialisation of Indian agriculture, famines (12 million dead 1875-1900), and the railway network designed to ferry raw cotton to ports — all are direct consequences of the British Industrial Revolution needing inputs and markets.

14. Industrial Revolution in Germany

Germany was a latecomer (no political unity till 1871) but by 1900 had overtaken Britain in steel, chemicals, and electrical industries — the heart of the Second Industrial Revolution.

14.1 Pre-Conditions

  • Political fragmentation till 1815 (300+ states) — Vienna Congress reduced to 39 states under German Confederation.
  • Rich coal (Ruhr, Saar, Silesia) and iron ore deposits.
  • Strong tradition of technical education (Polytechnische Hochschulen, research universities — Humboldt model).
  • Disciplined Prussian bureaucracy and military demand pulling industry.

14.2 The Zollverein — 1 January 1834

  • Customs Union created by Prussia, eliminating internal tariffs between 18 (later 25) German states.
  • Created a unified German market of 25 million people — the economic foundation of political unification.
  • Architect: Friedrich Motz; intellectual godfather: Friedrich List (National System of Political Economy, 1841) — argued for "infant industry" protection and national economic development against Adam Smith's free-trade dogma.

14.3 Take-Off 1850-1870

  • Railway boom: 469 km in 1840 → 18,876 km in 1870.
  • Krupp steel works at Essen (Alfred Krupp, "the Cannon King") — pioneered Bessemer process and cast-steel artillery (1851 Great Exhibition gold medal).
  • Borsig locomotives (Berlin), Siemens-Halske telegraph and electricals (1847).

  • To undercut socialism, Bismarck pioneered the modern welfare state: Health Insurance 1883, Accident Insurance 1884, Old Age & Disability Pension 1889.
  • Created model later copied by Britain (Lloyd George 1908-11) and worldwide.
Why Germany overtook Britain: (1) state-led education + research-industry link, (2) cartels and big banks (Deutsche Bank 1870) funded industry long-term, (3) latest technology adopted without obsolete sunk costs, (4) protective tariffs (1879 Bismarck tariff), (5) demographic boom (41 million in 1871 → 65 million in 1910).

15. Industrial Revolution in France & Belgium

15.1 France — Slow, State-Led, Luxury-Oriented

  • Why slow: Napoleonic Wars disrupted commerce 1792-1815; peasant proprietorship (after 1789) kept rural population on land — no labour surplus; banking conservative; coal expensive.
  • Growth driven by railways under Napoleon III (Second Empire 1852-70) — Crédit Mobilier (Pereire brothers 1852) financed railway boom.
  • Saint-Simonian engineers (Prosper Enfantin, Michel Chevalier) provided ideology of industrial modernisation as moral mission.
  • French Industrial pattern: high-quality, low-volume luxury goods (silk Lyon, wine Bordeaux, fashion Paris) — not mass-production cotton.
  • Cobden–Chevalier Treaty 1860 = first major free-trade treaty; began European trade liberalisation.
  • Major firms: Schneider et Cie (Le Creusot ironworks), Peugeot, Renault (1898), Michelin (1888).

15.2 Belgium — Continental Pioneer

  • First country on the European continent to industrialise — head start because of rich Walloon coal seams and proximity to British technology.
  • William Cockerill (English emigrant) and his son John Cockerill set up integrated coal-iron-engineering works at Seraing near Liège in 1817 — biggest industrial complex on the continent.
  • Independence 1830 followed by aggressive railway programme — first European country with state-built rail network (Brussels-Mechelen 1835).
  • Société Générale de Belgique (1822) — first industrial investment bank, model for continental finance.
  • By 1850, Belgium per capita industrial output was second only to Britain.

16. Industrial Revolution in the USA

America began as an importer of British technology (often through industrial espionage) and ended as the world's leading industrial power. By 1900 American manufacturing output exceeded Britain, Germany, and France combined.

16.1 Beginnings 1790-1860

  • Samuel Slater ("father of American Industrial Revolution") — memorised Arkwright water-frame design, emigrated illegally from England, set up first cotton spinning mill at Pawtucket, Rhode Island in 1790.
  • Lowell System (Francis Cabot Lowell, Massachusetts 1813-14) — integrated spinning + weaving in one factory, employed young farm girls in dormitories ("Lowell Mill Girls") — model of paternalistic factory town.
  • Eli Whitney — cotton gin 1793 (revived Southern slavery) + interchangeable parts in musket manufacture 1798 (foundation of the "American System of Manufactures").
  • Erie Canal opened 1825 — opened the Midwest to New York markets.
  • Railways: from 23 miles in 1830 to 30,000 miles by 1860 — most extensive in world.
  • Civil War 1861-65 = industrial war won by Northern industry; created lasting iron-coal-railway complex.

16.3 Labour Response

  • Knights of Labor (1869) — early national union, declined after 1886 Haymarket bombing (Chicago, 4 May 1886) blamed on anarchists.
  • American Federation of Labor (AFL) founded 1886 by Samuel Gompers — craft-union model, "bread-and-butter" unionism.
  • 1 May 1886 Chicago strike for 8-hour day → origin of International Workers' Day (May Day).
  • Pullman Strike 1894 (Eugene Debs) — broken by federal troops.
  • Industrial Workers of the World (IWW, "Wobblies") 1905 — radical syndicalist union.
  • Anti-trust laws: Sherman 1890, Clayton 1914.

17. Industrial Revolution in Russia

17.1 Pre-conditions and Late Start

  • Russia entered industrialisation a century behind Britain. Causes: serfdom (till 1861), autocracy, weak middle class, vast distances, sparse population, capital shortage.
  • Emancipation Edict — 19 February 1861 (Tsar Alexander II) — freed 23 million serfs but burdened them with redemption payments → kept peasants tied to mir (commune) and slowed labour mobility to cities.

17.2 The Witte System 1890-1903

  • Sergei Witte, Finance Minister 1892-1903 — directed state-led industrial drive.
  • Four pillars: (1) heavy state investment in railways, (2) protective tariffs (1891 tariff), (3) gold standard adopted 1897 to attract foreign capital, (4) deliberate squeezing of peasantry to fund industry ("we may go hungry but we shall export").
  • Foreign capital: French (38% of total foreign investment), British, Belgian, German — financed Donbas coal-iron, Baku oil, Polish textiles.
  • Trans-Siberian Railway begun 1891, Moscow-Vladivostok completed 1916 (9,289 km — longest railway in world).

17.3 Industrial Centres

  • St Petersburg: Putilov works (locomotives, artillery — 12,000 workers, biggest factory in Russia by 1900).
  • Moscow region: cotton textiles (Morozov, Prokhorov dynasties).
  • Donets Basin (Ukraine): coal and iron — John Hughes's New Russia Co (1869) founded the steel town of Yuzovka (today Donetsk).
  • Baku (Azerbaijan): oil — by 1900 Russia briefly the world's largest oil producer; Nobel Brothers and Rothschilds dominated.

17.4 Social Consequences

  • Industrial workforce was small (3 million in 1914 out of population 166 million) but concentrated in huge plants — easily organised.
  • 1905 Revolution preceded by Bloody Sunday 9/22 January 1905 (Father Gapon's march at Winter Palace; 200+ killed).
  • Russia's incomplete and uneven industrialisation — pockets of advanced capitalism beside medieval peasantry — explains why Marxist revolution succeeded here first (Trotsky's "law of combined and uneven development").

18. Industrial Revolution in Japan — The Meiji Miracle

Japan is the only non-Western country to complete a full industrial revolution before 1914 — making it the model for all later late-industrialisers (South Korea, Taiwan, China).

18.1 From Tokugawa to Meiji

  • 1603-1867 Tokugawa shogunate kept Japan in self-imposed isolation (sakoku).
  • 8 July 1853 — Commodore Matthew Perry's Black Ships arrived Edo Bay; forced Treaty of Kanagawa 1854 opening Japan.
  • 3 January 1868 — Meiji Restoration: Satsuma-Chōshū samurai overthrew Tokugawa; "restored" Emperor Meiji; capital moved Kyoto → Tokyo.
  • Slogan: Fukoku Kyōhei ("Rich Country, Strong Army") + Bunmei Kaika ("Civilisation and Enlightenment").

18.2 Reforms 1868-1890

  • Abolition of feudal domains (han) → 72 prefectures (1871); abolition of samurai class privileges; conscript army 1873.
  • Iwakura Mission 1871-73 — Iwakura Tomomi led 100-strong delegation (including Itō Hirobumi, Ōkubo Toshimichi) on 22-month tour of USA and Europe to study institutions and bring back models.
  • Land Tax Reform 1873 — converted feudal dues into cash tax, providing 80% of state revenue for industrialisation.
  • Universal compulsory primary education 1872.
  • Bank of Japan 1882 (modelled on Belgian National Bank).
  • Meiji Constitution promulgated 11 February 1889 (Prussian-modelled, Itō Hirobumi).

18.3 State-Led Industrialisation

  • Initially state ran "model factories" — Tomioka silk filature (1872, French engineers), Yawata Iron Works (1901).
  • From 1880s, state sold model factories cheaply to favoured private firms = origin of Zaibatsu conglomerates: Mitsui, Mitsubishi (Iwasaki Yatarō), Sumitomo, Yasuda. By 1930s the four Zaibatsu controlled ~30% of Japanese economy.
  • Textiles (silk + cotton) led the first phase; heavy industry (shipbuilding, steel, chemicals) took off after Sino-Japanese War indemnity.

Why Japan succeeded where China failed: (1) pre-existing literacy and commercial economy, (2) homogeneous society allowing top-down reform, (3) Iwakura Mission's empirical approach, (4) merger of samurai elite with capitalism (not opposed to it), (5) absence of European colonisation (unequal treaties only), (6) state-Zaibatsu partnership compressed 100 years of European industrialisation into 40 years.

19. Critique — Luddites, Marx, Pollution

19.1 The Luddites 1811-13

  • Skilled stockingers, croppers, weavers in Nottingham, Yorkshire, Lancashire smashed wide stocking-frames, gig-mills, power looms in the name of a mythical "General Ludd" (or "King Ludd" of Sherwood Forest).
  • Not anti-technology per se — opposed introduction of machines that produced cheaper, poorer-quality goods while displacing skilled craftsmen.
  • Government response: Frame-Breaking Act 1812 made machine-breaking a capital offence. 17 Luddites hanged at York in January 1813.
  • Lord Byron's maiden speech in House of Lords (27 February 1812) defended Luddites against death penalty.

19.2 Captain Swing Riots 1830

  • Agricultural labourers in southern England destroyed threshing machines, fired hayricks, sent anonymous letters signed "Captain Swing".
  • Triggered by underemployment, rising food prices, hated New Poor Law looming.
  • Government response: 19 hanged, 644 jailed, 481 transported to Australia.

19.3 Chartism 1838-48

  • First mass working-class political movement in world history.
  • People's Charter (May 1838) — Six Points: (1) universal male suffrage, (2) secret ballot, (3) annual parliaments, (4) equal constituencies, (5) payment of MPs, (6) abolition of property qualification for MPs.
  • Three monster petitions 1839, 1842, 1848 — all rejected by Parliament. Last "Kennington Common" rally 10 April 1848 fizzled (rain + Wellington's troops + railway).
  • Long-term: 5 of 6 Points adopted by 1918 (only annual parliaments rejected).

19.4 Marx and Engels — The Communist Critique

  • Friedrich Engels — The Condition of the Working Class in England (1845) — eyewitness account of Manchester slums based on his work in his father's cotton firm Ermen & Engels.
  • Karl Marx & Friedrich Engels — The Communist Manifesto (Feb 1848, London) — "A spectre is haunting Europe — the spectre of communism"; history = class struggle; bourgeoisie has been a revolutionary force but now creates its own grave-diggers (proletariat).
  • Karl Marx — Das Kapital Vol I (1867, Hamburg) — labour theory of value, surplus value, primitive accumulation (Chapter 26-33 on English enclosures and colonialism), tendency of falling rate of profit. Volumes II (1885) and III (1894) edited posthumously by Engels.

19.5 Environmental Critique

  • "Coketown" (Dickens' Hard Times 1854) — fictional Manchester of choking smoke, black canals, monotonous brick.
  • Rivers Pollution Commission Reports 1868-74 — found Rivers Aire, Calder, Mersey, Irwell dead.
  • Great Stink of London Summer 1858 — Thames so foul Parliament suspended; led to Bazalgette's sewer network (1858-75).
  • Alkali Act 1863 — first British air pollution law (regulated hydrogen chloride emissions from soda industry).
  • Conservation backlash: William Morris (Arts and Crafts movement), John Ruskin (Unto This Last 1860 — Gandhi's favourite book), Romantic poets Wordsworth and Coleridge on Lake District.

19.6 Quality of Life Debate

Optimists vs Pessimists: Historians have debated for 100+ years whether the IR improved or worsened living standards for the working class between 1780 and 1850. Optimists (T.S. Ashton, R.M. Hartwell) cite rising real wages, longer life expectancy, more goods. Pessimists (Eric Hobsbawm, E.P. Thompson) cite urban squalor, alienation, longer working hours, loss of artisan independence. Modern consensus (Crafts, Allen): real wages were largely stagnant 1780-1820 and improved only after 1840s; non-monetary quality of life (housing, air, autonomy) clearly deteriorated.

20. Continuity to the 20th Century — The Long Shadow

20.1 Imperialism and World War I

  • Industrial rivalries (German chemical-electrical lead vs British colonial-financial lead vs American manufacturing) hardened into geopolitical blocs.
  • Naval arms race: Dreadnought 1906 (Britain) → Tirpitz Plan (Germany) → industrial competition translated into military buildup.
  • WWI 1914-18 became history's first industrial war — machine guns, artillery shells in millions, poison gas (Haber-Bosch process, 1915 Ypres chlorine attack), tanks, aircraft, submarines.

20.2 Russian Revolution 1917

  • Uneven Russian industrialisation created the explosive combination of advanced industrial proletariat + medieval peasantry that produced the Bolshevik Revolution (October/November 1917).
  • Lenin extended Marx's industrial-capitalism analysis to imperialism (1916) and built the world's first socialist industrial state.

20.3 Great Depression 1929-39

  • Wall Street Crash 29 October 1929 spread globally because industrial economies were now interconnected by trade, finance, gold standard.
  • US industrial output fell 46%; unemployment hit 25% in USA, 33% in Germany — providing the social base for Hitler's rise 1933.
  • Keynes' General Theory (1936) and FDR's New Deal redefined state-economy relationship.

20.4 Second Industrial Revolution Becomes Third

  • Steel-coal-railway complex peaked 1900-1950 and gave way to oil-electricity-automobile complex (Fordism 1908-73).
  • After 1973 oil shock, Fordism gave way to Third Industrial Revolution (information technology, microelectronics, computers, internet from 1969 ARPANET).
  • Fourth Industrial Revolution (Klaus Schwab 2016) — AI, robotics, biotech, nanotech, IoT — now unfolding.

20.5 The Environmental Long Shadow

  • CO₂ atmospheric concentration: 280 ppm in 1750 (pre-IR) → 315 ppm in 1958 → 420 ppm in 2023. Of the cumulative ~2,500 Gt CO₂ emitted since 1750, more than half came after 1990.
  • Climate change is the long-run consequence of the fossil-fuel economy launched at Coalbrookdale 1709 and Liverpool-Manchester 1830.
  • Concept of "Anthropocene" (Paul Crutzen 2000) dates the new human-dominated geological epoch to the Industrial Revolution.
UPSC mains relevance: The Industrial Revolution is the single most important topic in World History because it explains the origins of (1) modern capitalism, (2) modern imperialism and colonial exploitation of India, (3) Marxism and the socialist movements, (4) the working-class welfare state, (5) the two World Wars, (6) the divide between developed and developing world, and (7) the climate crisis. Most other World History topics (American/French Revolutions are the political flank; Russian/Chinese Revolutions are the reactive flank) need to be situated against this economic transformation.

15 Must-Know Facts — Quick Revision

  1. Term coined: "Industrial Revolution" popularised by Arnold Toynbee in his 1884 Oxford lectures; French use earlier (Auguste Blanqui, 1837).
  2. First Industrial Revolution: c.1760-1840 — cotton, iron, coal, steam, Britain. Second: 1870-1914 — steel, chemicals, electricity, oil; led by Germany and USA. Third: from 1970s — IT, microelectronics. Fourth: from 2000s — AI, IoT.
  3. Norfolk crop rotation (turnips-clover-barley-wheat) developed by "Turnip" Townshend ~1730; Jethro Tull's seed drill 1701; Robert Bakewell selective breeding.
  4. Textile inventions sequence: Kay flying shuttle 1733 → Hargreaves spinning jenny 1764 → Arkwright water frame 1769 → Crompton mule 1779 → Cartwright power loom 1785 → Whitney cotton gin 1793.
  5. Steam engine sequence: Savery 1698 → Newcomen atmospheric engine 1712 → Watt separate condenser 1769 → Watt & Boulton rotary 1781 → Trevithick high-pressure 1800 → Stephenson Rocket 1829.
  6. Iron breakthroughs: Abraham Darby coke-smelting Coalbrookdale 1709 → Henry Cort puddling process 1783 → Henry Bessemer converter 1856 → Siemens-Martin open-hearth 1865 → Gilchrist-Thomas basic process 1879.
  7. First railway: Stockton-Darlington opened 27 September 1825; Liverpool-Manchester opened 15 September 1830 — first inter-city steam passenger line; Suez Canal opened 17 November 1869; Trans-Atlantic telegraph 1866; Trans-Siberian completed 1916.
  8. Cromford Mill (Richard Arkwright, Derbyshire, 1771) — first true factory using water-power on a 24-hour shift system.
  9. Factory Act 1833 (Althorp) — banned under-9s; appointed first 4 factory inspectors. Mines Act 1842 (Ashley) — banned women and children underground. Ten Hours Act 1847.
  10. Combination Acts 1799-1800 banned unions; repealed 1824. Tolpuddle Martyrs 1834. Trade Union Act 1871 (Gladstone) legalised unions. TUC founded 1868. Labour Party 1900-06.
  11. Communist Manifesto February 1848 (Marx & Engels, London); Engels' Condition of the Working Class in England 1845; Marx's Das Kapital Vol I 1867.
  12. Zollverein 1 January 1834 (Prussian-led Customs Union); Friedrich List's "infant industry" doctrine 1841; Krupp Essen; Siemens-Halske 1847; BASF 1865; Bayer 1863; Bismarck's Health Insurance 1883.
  13. USA: Samuel Slater Pawtucket 1790; Eli Whitney cotton gin 1793 + interchangeable parts 1798; Lowell System 1814; Carnegie Steel → U.S. Steel 1901; Standard Oil 1870 broken up 1911; Ford Model T 1908; assembly line 1913; AFL 1886.
  14. Russia: Serf emancipation 19 February 1861 (Alexander II); Witte System 1892-1903; gold standard 1897; Trans-Siberian begun 1891; Putilov works St Petersburg; Bloody Sunday 9/22 January 1905.
  15. Japan: Perry's Black Ships 8 July 1853; Meiji Restoration 3 January 1868; Iwakura Mission 1871-73; land tax 1873; Meiji Constitution 11 February 1889; Zaibatsu (Mitsui, Mitsubishi, Sumitomo, Yasuda); Yawata Steel 1901; Russo-Japanese War victory 1904-05; annexed Korea 22 August 1910.
